    Q:

    What is the theoretical maximum size of a terrestrial planet?

    StaysAwakeAllWeek A:

    If you keep adding terrestrial planetary material (mostly iron, silicon and oxygen) to a planet eventually it will resemble a white dwarf star. The radius will peak at only a few times that of Earth, depending on the exact composition, then start decreasing again. When you reach 1.44 times the mass of the Sun, or about half a million Earth masses, the planet/star will collapse, nuclear fusion will ignite and the resulting type 1a supernova will completely destroy the planet/star leaving no remnant.

    ExpectedBehaviour A:

    Depends what you mean by size — mass or diameter.

    Our current models of planet formation suggest that at around ten times Earth’s mass a terrestrial planet would begin to accumulate and retain a dense and thick (in terms of how deep it is relative to the planet’s overall diameter) atmosphere, and begin to look like a small gas giant. Assuming the same density as Earth, such a planet would have a diameter of about 2.2 times that of Earth, around 27,500km – at least for the rocky core.

    Sakinho A:

    It's conceivable that a Neptune-like gas giant could be impacted by a large terrestrial planet, causing the ejection of virtually all the gas giant's atmosphere/volatiles well after planetary formation in a system has finished. If the core of the former gas giant manages to stay in one piece, the resulting trainwreck may look like an enormous terrestrial planet, substantially bigger than what planetary formation models would suggest is possible.

    However, even ignoring gas accretion from space, over geological timescales it would regenerate a large helium atmosphere due to the radioactive decay of its constituent rocks (alpha particles turn into helium). This would eventually make it look like a gas giant again.

    So the answer is: really big, maybe even dozens of times more massive than Earth, if a lot of unusual circumstances add up and you happen to look at just the right time.
