    Q:

    ELI5 What happens to the wax when you burn a candle, and why doesn’t the wick burn to ash?

    PM_ur_Rump A:

    The wax is the fuel. It melts and, well, "wicks" up the wick. The bright flame is the wax burning off of the wick like any liquid fuel would. So the wax is consumed in the burning process. The wick only burns away at the point beyond where the wax burns off, as the liquid wax flowing up it insulates it from the flame.

    Lordxeen A:

    Candle wax is mostly made of a long chain hydrocarbon, a molecule made of hydrogen and carbon, which when burned with oxygen turns into water vapor (hydrogen and oxygen) and carbon dioxide (carbon and oxygen). Solid wax doesn’t burn, unfortunately, but a burning wick will melt wax and draw the liquid up into the wick where it can burn as fuel. The wick mostly is ash, held in shape by the wax in it; as the candle burns lower the waxless end of the wick burns up completely

    Arclite02 A:

    The wick DOES burn to ash, but only the little bit at the top where the melted wax doesn't reach.

    ​

    The rest of it is really just a channel for the liquid wax fuel to... Well, wick up towards the flame, where it gets burned instead of the wick itself. But obviously that only works so long as there's still wax to burn, and the wax only gets so far up the wick before being consumed. So as the wax level gets progressively lower, there's a tiny little bit of the wick, right up at the top, that constantly gets burned away as the wax can no longer reach that high.
