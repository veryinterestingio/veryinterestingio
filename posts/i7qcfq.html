    Many planets have surface temps far too hot due to proximity to s star or lack of protective atmosphere. On these planets beneath a protective layer of crust is the only place liquid water can exist.

    Flip that logic and there are planets with icey surfaces due to extreme low surface temps and atmospheric pressure conditions. Those planets with an active core such as ours may allow liquid water deeper under the surface where the temperature is above freezing point.

    jaminfine A:

    Other comments touch on why surface water is rare. You'd need a very specific temperature and pressure range on the surface of the planet. In our solar system, Mercury and Venus are too hot for surface water and Mars is too cold, although there are some hints that water may have previously existed on the surface of Mars, when it had a different atmosphere.

    But why is water more common beneath the surface? This is because of how planets tend be hottest at their core. If a planet is a little too cold to have surface water, it's very likely that at some point between the surface and the core it is the right temperature for water.

    Of course, just being the right temperature doesn't mean they actually have water. But it makes it possible.

    dumb_ants A:

    Our oceans are also protected by the oxygen in our air. Sunlight breaks water apart into hydrogen and oxygen and the hydrogen can then escape since it's much lighter than oxygen. Over very long time spans this can cause large amounts of water to be broken down.

    On our planet, however, that hydrogen has to get through a lot of free oxygen without turning back into water before it can escape. None of the other planets we can measure have any free oxygen in their air, since this requires life (or some other process we aren't aware of).
