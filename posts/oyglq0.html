  • Astronomy If all of our solar system's planets are orbiting the Sun in approximately in the same plane, what portion of the Sun's energy goes "out of plane" away from all of the planets in our solar system?

    NEW
    Q:

    So the planets in our solar system are pretty close to having all of their elliptical orbits in a common plane ( I understand correctly, Mercury is the farthest out-of-plane about 7 degrees off of the ecliptic).

    It readily follows that some of the Sun's light ends up going out-of-plane from the 8 planets + Pluto. What portion of this energy coming off of the Sun ends up getting just blasted out into the void of space never to go near any of the planets of our solar system, or even anywhere near where they would be at any time in their orbits?

    NonstandardDeviation A:

    Space is vastly empty. The Earth is quite far from the sun, and intercepts 4.53×10^-10 of the Sun's output, or about 1 part in 2.2 billion. I calculated this as a solid angle^[Wikipedia] fraction using the size and orbital distance of Earth^[calculation]. Jupiter intercepts a bit more, at 2×10^-9^[calculation], highest among the planets^[calculation]. Altogether only about 1 part in 240 million of the Sun's energy is intercepted. The rest escapes into space, whether out of plane of the planets or between them.

    If you're interested in the areas swept by all the planets in their entire orbits, which in my opinion isn't really a physically relevant quantity, that's still tiny, hovering for each planet around 10^(-5),^[calculation] Jupiter being highest at 8.986×10^(-5). Sum them all up^* and it's about 3×10^(-4), 1 part in 3328, or .03%.

    ^(Note: Viewed from the Sun, the orbits of the planets do intersect, and their swept regions of sky overlap, but likely not enough to change the total significantly. The inclinations vary by a handful of degrees, while the planet with the largest apparent size, Jupiter, is only 0.01 degrees wide.) ^[[calculation](https://www.wolframalpha.com/input/?i=%282planet+radius%2Fplanet+semimajor+axis%29*180%2Fpi)]

    na3than A:

    What portion of the solar energy is projected above and below 7° from the ecliptic? Picture a cone of light projected above 7° and below 7° from the plane, intersecting a sphere. You're looking for the ratio of the volume of these two spherical sectors to the volume of the sphere.

    Per https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_sector, the volume of a spherical sector, Vss, can be written as (2 π R³ / 3) (1 - cos φ), where r is the radius of the sphere and φ is half the cone angle.

    Since the volume of a sphere is Vs = 4/3 π R³, the ratio of (2*Vss) to Vs is

    2 ((2 π R³ / 3) (1 - cos φ)) / (4/3 π R³)

    which reduces to

    (1 - cos φ).

    If the farthest out-of-plane angle is 7°, φ is (90° - 7°) so (1 - cos φ) = ~0.878.

    tldr; About 88% of solar energy is projected above and below the orbits of the eight planets.

    If my analysis, geometry or math is wrong, please correct me.
