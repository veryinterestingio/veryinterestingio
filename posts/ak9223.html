    BottledCans A:

    The short answer is no, not if you use sunscreen correctly.

    There is a theme in medicine: we never evolved to live past our early twenties. Cumulative UV exposure (not sunburns) makes skin look older, splotchier, thinner, more wrinkly, and also predisposes us to more indolent types of skin cancer (like basal cell carcinoma).

    Cumulative sunburns predispose us to more aggressive forms of skin cancer (like melanoma).

    There's no such thing as a safe tan (except for the one you're born with or the one you spray on).

    For anyone planning on living past 25, your vitamin D should come from your diet, not UV radiation.

    Source: medical student, worked in a dermatology clinic for four years.

    Shyman4ever A:

    Vitamin D biosynthesis in the body specifically requires UVB rays in humans. This is an example where evolution took a crazy turn, because UVB rays are also known to cause various skin cancers, so it’s interesting how we’ve also evolved to need it to produce an essential vitamin. Long story short, studies have shown that wearing sunscreen does not lower vitamin D levels on a long term basis. It might slow it down, but not significantly enough for you to have to go in the sun unprotected. The harms of unprotected sun exposure are overwhelmingly more dangerous than that of slightly lower vitamin D levels anyways. 7-Dehydrocholestereol is converted in to pre-vitamin D via a photochemical reaction that uses UVB as a high energy source, which is then converted in to vitamin D in the skin surface spontaneously. This is then transported to the liver, where it gets oxidized in to calcidiol, and then to the kidneys, where it gets oxidized once again in to calcitriol. This is the active form of Vitamin D in the body, which is responsible for the regulation of calcium levels in the body and a myriad of other key roles. Source: Took a class on biosynthetic chemistry.
