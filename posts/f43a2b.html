  • Biology At what point of human or hominid evolutionary history did short-term (monthly) ovulation cycles become the reproductive norm?

    NEW
    Q:

    I've done some brief reading on the topic, and it appears that bats and great apes (and humans) are the main branches of mammal species that have short-term ovulation cycles rather than just a few fertile seasons per year.

    In our evolutionary history, at what point did this become common?

    As a follow up question, do we have any evidence of a rapid population growth due to increased year-round fertility of a hominid or precursor species?

    atomfullerene A:

    It's not just great apes either, at least some old world monkeys have similar cycling (rhesus monkeys for example).

    Kind of informed speculating here but I think two things are going on. First, most primates live in fairly nonseasonal habitats and food availability is fairly constant year round. Animals in these environments are less likely to have strong breeding seasons. This is likely enhanced by the long parental care primates provide: if you have an infant around for a year, there's less reason to ensure it's born at exactly the right time since it's going to be a baby through all the seasons anyway.

    The actual length of the cycle isn't remarkable for a nonseasonal mammal as far as I can tell.
