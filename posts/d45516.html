  • Astronomy In stars with enough mass to collapse into a black hole specifically, what causes the "shock wave" that blows apart the rest of the star in a supernova?

    NEW
    Q:

    As I understand it in stars that collapse into neutron stars, the "shock wave" is created by the rapid collapse of the stars core into a ball of neutrons and then abruptly stopping once neutron degeneracy pressure prevents the core from collapsing any further into a black hole, and leaving behind a neutron star.

    In more massive stars, neutron degeneracy pressure is overcome and the core collapses into a black hole, but at what point in this scenario does the shock wave happen and what causes it if there is nothing stopping the collapse?

    DAMO238 A:

    This is, in part, due to the Virial Theorem, which states that the half of the potential energy lost is transferred to kinetic energy and half to thermal energy. A lot of this energy is released in the form of neutrinos, (since the matter has heated up so much). Thus we see a neutrino 'wave' approaching the core, which is now incredibly dense since neutron degeneracy pressure is the only thing holding it up. This therefore means the core reflects the neutrinos which then eject the envelope.
