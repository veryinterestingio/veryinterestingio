  • Why do music streaming services' catalogues have a lot of overlap while video streaming services seem to stride to have as much exclusive content as possible?

    NEW
    Q:

    Services like Spotify, Tidal, Deezer, YouTube Music, Apple Music, etc. seem to have overlapping catalogues (for the most part) while video streaming services like Netflix, HBO Now, Amazon Prime Video, Hulu, etc. seem to have a lot less overlap and much more exclusive content. To the point where having exclusive access to certain shows becomes a selling point for the service. Why is that? Is the copyright law different for music and video?

    newytag A:

    Historical reasons. Video streaming services inherited the TV model, where services/channels would compete for exclusive rights to broadcast a series, or invest in producing their own. Whereas audio streaming services have inherited the radio model, where every station acquires rights to a wide catalogue of songs (sometimes focused on specific genres, time periods, popularity etc depending on the station).

    As for why this has historically been the case, I couldn't say for sure, but I suspect it's something to do with the fact that songs are only a few minutes long and listened to in isolation, whereas TV series are 20-40 minute episodes and require a long-term investment from the viewer.

    Copyright laws aren't different for music and video, but certainly the business models built around licensing out those media have evolved differently.

    jappadad729 A:

    All of these answers are sort of correct. It’s actually all about who holds the rights for performance (which is what streaming is considered under the law).

    For video, production companies - basically whoever made the originals- own the rights.

    For music it’s more complicated.

    Video performance is a showing - a movie theater or a tv airing, or a stream. If I’m on a stage acting out a movie, that’s not a performance of that video (I may be performing the script, but I’m not performing the MOVIE).

    For music, if I cover a song in a bar with my guitar, that’s a performance, just like a stream or a playing in a tv show, etc.

    And to deal with that, the music industry has organized rights clearinghouses. These are organizations that offer the rights to big catalogs of songs to all types of buyers - music venues pay for rights, as do streaming services. It’s a flat fee for the whole catalog for most uses, not a per song fee in most cases.

    The two big clearinghouses are called ASCAP and BMI, and the nearly all songs are licensed through them. There are some exceptions, like that Taylor swift album for which she withheld the rights, but for most artists that’s not really an option (for several reasons that aren’t worth explaining here).
