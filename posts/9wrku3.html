    Q:

    ELI5: Why does colored plastic turn white/lighter when you bend/stretch it and stay that way? What happens to the paint/coloring?

    Amphorax A:

    When you bend plastic, it forms teeny-tiny fractures throughout the bent area. These fractures scatter the light that passes through them, causing a surface to appear translucent or even opaque when bent.

    kinda-guy-kinda-sly A:

    Like already mentioned, it’s called crazing, and the voids are the most likely area for fracture to occur. At this site there is a localization of the plastic deformation, and can become the location of a macro-scale fracture if the load continues. The crazies aren’t technically voids or cracks, they actually have 50-55% free space by volume. The rest of the volume in the area is the polymer molecules that have been aligned by the force applied.

    Sulaco1978 A:

    Isn’t this stress induced crystallization? Stretching/bending aligns the polymer chains to pack and form crystalline domains. The crystalline regions scatter light differently than their amorphous (non-crystalline) counterparts.
