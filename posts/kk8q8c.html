  • ELI5 - how do good open world games make us just accept the unrealistic scale of their world?

    NEW
    Q:

    For example, in GTA V, Trevor says it takes about four hours to drive from Los Santos to Paleto Bay, but in the game it takes eight minutes. Yet the journey feels totally normal and the world doesn't feel unrealistically dense. How do games create worlds with highly truncated travel times but which nevertheless feel realistically huge?

    TPSou A:

    It's because they cut down on repetition. In the real world you might have 2 miles of identical residential streets in one housing area, in the game it's just two streets. So you still get the feeling of neighbourhoods, but they're all tiny versions of the real ones.

    Enzar17 A:

    Game designer here.

    In general, the time you want a player to spend in the game should feel meaningful. In an open world, this is REALLY important. I've made the drive from Denver to Phoenix. It's 13 hours and it mostly sucks, but what do I remember? Denver, Colorado Springs, Albuquerque, Phoenix. So if I were to put that in a game, I would make those cities and then cut out most of the travel time in between. That way players would get the memorable parts without the tedium of all that travel time. Boom, now your open world feels big, but also meaningful because your time wasn't wasted.

    The same principle applies to the cities themselves. You don't need a house for every NPC, you just need one residential street and the player will fill in the rest. Give your players all the meaningful utilities (clothing shops, auto dealers, gunsmiths, etc.) and then provide enough space around it to feel memorable without wasting time. Boom, believable city.

    Put those together, and you can even make interstate travel feel reasonable (see Horizon: Zero Dawn or RDR2).
