  • Biology Why is it widely recommended to get 8 hours of sleep when we sleep in 90 minute cycles?

    NEW
    Q:

    Getting 8 hours of sleep is a well-known recommendation and general health goal, yet I've heard from several sources that humans sleep in cycles of 90 minutes, with different "levels" (R.E.M., etc.) of sleep happening within that cycle. Five full cycles would be 7 and a half hours, and six cycles would be 9 hours, meaning that the recommended 8 hours would leave you waking up part way through a cycle, disrupting your natural rhythm of sleep.

    Which, if either of these two points are inaccurate? Is there something I'm not considering?

    MultiFazed A:

    Both the timing of sleep cycles and the optimal amount of sleep are factors that vary from person to person and from day to day. They're all just estimates.

    Coerced_onto_reddit A:

    Though our sleep cycles are about 90 minutes, the cycles are not uniform in structure. Both non-rapid eye movement (NREM) sleep and rapid eye movement (REM) sleep are important. The NREM stages tend to have slow, uniform brainwaves. A key function of deep NREM sleep, which tends to predominate early in the night, is to do the work of weeding out and removing unnecessary neural connections. The dreaming stage of REM sleep, which prevails later in the night, plays a role in strengthening those connections.

    This is taken from Matthew Walker’s “Why We Sleep”

    BronxLens A:

    So much ‘TiL’ in this page:

    “It's important to get enough sleep, but also to get it at the right time. While it depends on each individual's circadian rhythm, in general 10PM to 2AM is when your body secretes the most growth hormone (that is- IF you're asleep at that time).

    The other thing on the sleep to do list is waste cleanup.

    Throughout the course of the day, the brain produces a decent amount of waste.

    The brain handles this waste cleanup task during sleep via something called the glymphatic system in which brain cells shrink to allow for cerebrospinal fluid to flood into the brain and flush out the waste.

    One thing that needs to be flushed out is the compound adenosine. Adenosine is a byproduct of your neurons and other cells when they burn up adenosine triphosphate, the main molecule that our bodies use to store energy.

    As adenosine builds up, you start to slow down and accumulate a "sleep pressure".

    When your adenosine levels reach a certain point, your body sends you signals to go to sleep.”
