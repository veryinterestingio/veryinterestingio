    JohnQK A:

    Your gut uses a lot of resources. Between digestion, management, and exchanging nutrients, the area has a very high traffic of bloodflow.

    The rest of your body also gets the stuff if needs to do things from the blood. To help conserve resources, the body does most of its gut-stuff while asleep (which is why many people poop in the morning). However, the gut does still get a lot of blood while you are awake.

    When something triggers a stress response, your body wants resources in other places. Your body wants to supercharge those arms for punching, those legs for running, and that brain for processing sensory information. In order to supercharge those other areas, the body diverts blood from the gut. Digestion is a much lower priority than not getting digested.

    That's why you feel cold and hollow. Your gut, the part of you that is normally full and warm, has less of the blood that makes it feel that way.

    Meanwhile, our brains haven't quite adapted to social stresses because they are a relatively new invention. Our brain hasn't quite figured out the difference between the different kinds of stresses. Your brain handles bad news in the same way it handles rustling grass; it gets ready to run or fight.

    breathfromanother A:

    When people feel emotional pain, the same areas of the brain get activated as when people feel physical pain.

    This triggers the release of stress hormones (cortisol and adrenaline), which leads to physical symptoms, such as nausea, difficulty breathing, and also a weakening of the heart muscle that doctors called Takotsubo cardiomyopathy.

    Takotsubo cardiomyopathy is also known as stress cardiomyopathy, which there is a sudden temporary weakening of the muscular portion of the heart. This weakening may be triggered by emotional stress, such as the death of a loved one, a break-up, rejection from a partner or constant anxiety.
