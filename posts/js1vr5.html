    SyrusDrake A:

    This thread is a mess. People barely even understand the question.

    To answer the actual question as best I can:
    Color-changing animals have special skin-cells called "chromatophores", which can made to change, either through muscle action (cephalopods) or hormonal signals (chamelon). Those aren't like LED diodes, they can't all just fade through the entire spectrum. Instead, each cell contains a specific color pigment, and depending on how far all cells of this color open, the color of the animal changes. This seems to be a largely involuntary action¹, so the animals don't really "know" when they reached the right color. Instead, there is a stimulus ("the background is black", for example), and the appropriate, involuntary reaction by the cells follows².

    It's like when you trip over a crack in the pavement. You don't "know" what movements to perform to regain your balance. It's just an automatic response to a stimulus that doesn't require your input.³
    Of course, it's possible to get that wrong. But natural selection takes care of that. An octopus whose chromatophores color them bright pink on the sandy ground gets eaten and thus their faulty automatic response dies out. A human who trips and just falls over gets eaten by lions, so their faulty automatic response dies out.

    ¹ Mostly. It seems that cuttlefish can change their color voluntarily, they can be "taught" to change color.

    ² How they perceive that stimulus is a fascinating topic but not part of the question as far as I understand it.

    ³ Of course, there is a feedpack loop to the chromatophore response, as there is to every cell process. So something somewhere "knows", otherwise the cells would just do whatever. But that goes beyond ELI5 and beyond my understanding of cellular biology.
