    Q:

    Why can influenza infect a person multiple times, but once a person is infected with measles, they gain lifelong immunity?

    nice--marmot A:

    Primarily because there are multiple strains of influenza that circulate differentially depending on a variety of factors. Influenza also has a high rate of mutation, which the means the prevalent strains circulating at in given season have changed enough that your immune system doesn't recognize them. Also, immunity to pathogens varies significantly depending on the antigen molecules (usually) on the surface of the virus or bacteria: Some are more easily recognized by the immune system than others.

    iayork A:

    All the answers are going to explain that it’s because influenza mutates rapidly, and that’s part of the answer but not all of it. Unfortunately, the rest of the answer is mainly “We don’t know why”.

    It’s true that influenza viruses accumulate mutations more rapidly than most other viruses. That’s not because they have a higher mutation rate - they’re pretty typical for RNA viruses, all of which mutate fast. Rather it’s because influenza proteins, or at least the ones involved in immunity, are amazingly tolerant to mutations. For other viruses, a mutation that changes the structure of the protein typically decreases the virus’s fitness. Influenza mostly doesn’t care - it can take many mutations in its hemagglutinin without losing fitness, so it can mutate and keep the mutations without being crippled, until it becomes resistant to immunity.

    That’s unique to influenza, other viruses can’t do it.

    But it’s not all the story. The viruses take a while to change, say 3 to 10 years before they change enough that previous immunity doesn’t work against them. (The vaccines contain four viruses, so the vaccines usually need to be changed just about annually. But the individual viruses in there don’t change nearly as often.)

    But even if you get a perfect vaccine match (that is, even if you’re vaccinated and then one or two years later infected with the virus in the vaccine), there’s a chance that you’ll still be infected. And that’s even true (though not as drastically) with natural infections - you can be re-infected after some fairly short period. The reasons for this aren’t clear, but influenza infection, and influenza vaccines, tend to give fairly short-lived immunity.

    Again this is pretty much unique to influenza, and you can use influenza to make predictions about other viruses. Influenza viruses have infected hundreds of millions of people a year for hundreds of years - they may be the most successful viruses out there.

    (In particular, SARS-CoV-2 is very much a generic, ordinary virus in virtually every way. It doesn’t have the extraordinary and unique adaptations of influenza. You can’t look at influenza and draw any conclusions about COVID.)

    Just to be clear - you should still get the influenza vaccine, because getting vaccinated annually helps overcome this short immunity, and even if you get infected after vaccination there’s a good chance the disease will be milder and you will transmit much less, protecting your family as well as yourself.

    wildcatkevin A:

    Interesting and relevant side note: You get lasting immunity to measles, but the infection with measles wipes out many memory immune cells and you actually lose immunity to other things (like influenza!) as a result of measles infection. (Source editorial that cites two primary studies).

    DrDocumentarian A:

    Part of the problem is f*ing ducks. Influenza was originally an avian virus that mutated to infect humans a long time ago, but still circulates in migrating duck population. The problem is that the flu virus doesn't cause the same problems in ducks, and can recombine (a type of sharing of genes, or sexual-esque reproduction), resulting in new combinations of virulence factors that haven't been see before (or at least in a generation or two).
