    Q:

    I am wondering why a lot of psychosis or delusions are rooted in the same topics.

    -religion or god (delusions of grandeur or other religious delusions)

    -thinking you are sick

    -obsessing over people or being in "love"

    -thinking an individual is trying to ruin your life or persecute you

    I'm just wondering why given how complex the human mind is, delusions often take the form of four or five topics as opposed to a very wide array of topics.

    SauronOMordor A:

    Delusions are heavily influenced by the surrounding culture. It is a disease of the mind, but the mind is shaped by experiences.

    So if you grow up in a culture where, say, individual freedom is a central value, you're more likely to experience delusions about your personal freedom being violated. If you grow up in a highly religious community, you're more likely to have delusions around God and religion.

    exeSci A:

    Psychosis is a controversial topic within psychiatry, with different groups advocating for different theories of how it works. Some propose that it's a biological disorder and rooted in genetics and brain wiring, and others argue for a more socially oriented cause. A very high number of people with psychotic delusions have been shown to have experienced one or more forms of trauma in their life, with increasing trauma bringing higher chances of psychosis. (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trauma\_model\_of\_mental\_disorders#Recent\_approaches) (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5440889/)

    The particulars of a person's traumas and experiences, and the way they're taught to relate to them and the resulting mental health issues may have a role in determining what the particulars of the delusion are. But it's still a debated topic. (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biopsychiatry\_controversy) (https://psychology.wikia.org/wiki/Biopsychiatry\_controversy)
