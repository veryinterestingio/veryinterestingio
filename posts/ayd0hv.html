  • Medicine How does the "plug" of skin cut out by injecting someone with a hypodermic needle or IV not clog the needle and then get pushed into the blood stream and cause problems by clogging capillary blood vessels?

    NEW
    Q:

    From my understanding the needle is hollow with a pointed end, and it seems like this would act like a leather punch and cut a disk of flesh out, which would then clog the needle and be forced into the bloodstream when the medicine is injected. How does this not cause problems when it enters the blood stream? Do needles not work that way?

    zetephron A:

    In some ways it's better to think of a needle as a knife, not a punch. It has a beveled end, and a newly uncapped needle is sharp. In fact, in surgeries we sometimes use small (e.g. 30ga) needles as microknives, just to cut something without ever intending to punch into it.

    So ideally the needle splits the skin and enters the desired tissue "clean". You can also help this process, when necessary, by applying slight positive pressure to the syringe that is on the back end of the needle; this pushes material away and stops anything from plugging the tip. This technique is used, for example, in a related kind of procedure called whole-cell recording (where the "needle" is actually a pulled glass pipette, with a subcellular sized tip).

    Edit: fixed minor typos. Also, it's worth mentioning that needles commonly are not bare metal, but have an ultrathin coating that reduces friction, thereby also lessening the chance of material getting stuck to the tip.

    Also, while I'm on a roll, there can be a huge decrease in effectiveness after a needle is first used. They are not meant to be reused.

    Dyalar A:

    The term you're looking for is "coring", and is of real concern in pharma, but more of the potential to do what you described to a rubber stopper. It is a very serious issue, and needles are designed to prevent that from happening.

    synester101 A:

    Needles like that are slanted, and only one part of that slanted needle is actually sharp. The other end is comparatively dull. So, the sharp part cuts a small slice in the skin, then the dull end just pushes it out of the way, like a flap. Imagine the hole as a U shape, but you are putting a rod through it. The rod will just move the U flap out of the way.

    baggya99 A:

    So this is generally not a huge issue for needling vessels, when trying to make bigger holes or going through tougher tissues it can be a problem. In addition to positive pressure and needle tip design, for some procedures, such as spinal procedures you may well have an obturator, which is essentially something which fills the needle lumen to the tip to prevent this problem.

    Even with such things, it still happens that tissue does block the orifice to the needle, or indeed blood does. It is super annoying.
