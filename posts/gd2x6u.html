  • Biology Why do some liquors „burn“ more in the throat / when swallowing than others, even with the same or even less alcohol percentage?

    NEW
    Q:

    Like how can two kinds of Grappa‘s with the same strength burn so much differently while swallowing?

    Portarossa A:

    It's complicated, and seems to have as much to do with psychology as biology or chemistry.

    First of all, the question is why any liquor burns when you drink it. The answer boils down to the fact that it triggers a particular receptor in your mouth and throat. This receptor -- TRVP1, for anyone looking for a little more than an ELI5 -- is responsible for a bunch of sensations. When something in your mouth is too hot, this little beastie tells your body that something is fucky and you need to hashahashafashaha until it cools down. However, it's also triggered by capsaicin (the thing that makes spicy food spicy) and by ethanol (the thing that makes booze feel so good until it suddenly doesn't). Ethanol affects it slightly differently, however; it makes it more sensitive to other stimuli, rather than directly stimulating it itself. All of a sudden, that magic button in your throat that tells you 'Oh shit, this is really hot you guys' at 42 degrees celsius fires off at about 34 degrees centigrade instead. Considering the human body is about 37 degrees celsius at rest? That means you're firing on all cylinders. (This is also the reason that alcohol stings like a bastard when you get it on a cut; it's lowering the threshold for those same receptors, giving you that burning sensation in your fresh new wound.)

    So obviously, the more alcohol -- ethanol -- you have in a drink, the more of these receptors it's going to trigger, and the more of a burning sensation you're going to feel; that's why Everclear hits harder than tequila, which hits harder than wine, which hits harder than beer, which hits harder than a clara. (Sidenote: claras are delicious and refreshing; half beer, half lemon Fanta. Try them this summer. You'll thank me.) So why do you get the burn differently even if the alcohol level seems to be the same? Surely it should be a direct relationship?

    That's where the complexity hits.

    The number on your bottle of hooch is the amount of ethanol in it, so in theory one bottle of 40% spirits should hit the same as another bottle of 40% spirits -- at least if ethanol content is the only factor. At least part of it is psychological in nature; you'd be astonished how much the science of taste comes down to external influences. Take Grey Goose vodka, for example, which has long been held up as a premium product, far superior to other vodkas, which is why they justify the price increase... except that people can't tell the difference between it and others. People expect it to burn less, and so they report that it burns less. Anecdotal evidence is a powerful thing.

    However, there are other things to consider too. Given that this is a temperature reaction, booze served cold -- that is, below the threshold of your TRVP1 receptors -- is going to feel less burny than booze that isn't, largely because it's not going to have a chance to warm up enough to trigger the burny sensation before it makes its way down to your stomach.

    Beyond that? As far as I can tell, you're mostly dealing with speculation; I can't find a lot of studies that discuss specifically how much certain boozes 'burn'. (When you think about it, it would be difficult to do; the 'burn', as we just found out, is the kind of thing that's notoriously difficult to measure.) You'll find a lot of places on the internet talking about the 'heads' and the 'tails' of a drink -- that is, the first part and the last part that are removed, which tend to have more unseemly chemicals in that may affect the taste and are more commonly found in cheaper alcohols -- but evidence for that is pretty inconclusive. (They deal with the taste, but not necessarily the burn.) There's also a longrunning question, especially in home distilling, about whether adding glycerine to alcohol will smooth it out -- but again, it's a bit hit and miss in terms of how much effect it has. Similarly, you'll find a lot of people saying that sweet alcohols burn less, but I can't find any evidence to suggest that that's the case beyond the merely anecdotal.

    In short: there are some things that definitely make a difference (temperature and alcohol content), and some things that may make a difference (additional chemicals and sweetness), but it seems that a lot of it is down to psychology and taste preferences. If you like the taste of the booze at the front of your mouth, you're less likely to notice the burn at the back.

    Hiro-Agonist A:

    Your body has structures in your mouth and throat (known as VR1 receptors) to tell how hot or cold your food is. Pure alcohol reacts with these and makes them more easily stimulated. Suddenly, your normal body temperature is enough to trigger them and create the feeling of heat. The same is true of spicy foods.

    As to why higher quality alcohol doesn't burn as badly, it is often because the alcohol has been aged in wooden barrels. During this aging the alcohol will pull parts of the wood out, and combine with them. This changes the flavour and makes it less likely to react to these receptors.

    Cheap alcohol makers often use flavours such as cinnamon or vanilla; this has a similar effect to aging because it also reacts with the receptors and you get a pleasant taste instead of the harsh burn. Chilling alcohol also helps, as it lowers your body temperature enough to not trigger the receptors.
