    luckyluke193 A:

    There are different ways to make a material opaque.

    If your material is perfectly homogeneous and the surface is perfectly smooth, then a material might be opaque because it reflects light or because it absorbs light. Both depend on whether and how electrons in the material can be moved by light. In a metal, infrared and visible light can make them move around freely, which causes the light to be reflected. In insulators, the difference between two energy levels of the electrons in the material can be equal to a photon of visible light. This is why many transition metal compounds have such nice colours.

    If your material has a rough surface, then it can appear opaque because it scatters light randomly.

    If the material is not homogeneous but contains lots of tiny inclusions that scatter light, it will also appear opaque.

    Some materials like glass are broad spectrum transparent... others are not, some are opaque and block the flow of radiant energy flux.

    When you disperse opaque particles into a transparent matrix at low particle densities it looks like fog and becomes translucent, and/or reduce the ability for the radiant energy flux to penetrate the matrix.

    If you put a lot of opaque material into a transparent matrix, it can become very nearly or entirely opaque.
