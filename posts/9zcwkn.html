    MarijnBerg A:

    Osmosis mostly.

    Osmosis is what happens when two water solutions of different concentrations of solvents are separated by a water permeable membrane. The water moves through the membrane to equalize the concentrations in the two solutions.

    Honey is a solution with a very high concentration of sugars in it.

    Bacteria and other microbes are filled with water that contains a smaller concentration of dissolved substances.

    Cell membranes surrounding microbes are water permeable.

    so when a microbe contacts the honey, a water permeable membrane separates a high concentration solution(honey) from a lower concentration solution(microbe cytoplasm) and the water will move in such a way to equalize the difference. Out of the microbe, drying it out.

    Honey is also slightly acidic and contains some hydrogen peroxide but those are small factors.

    TL;DR: Honey is a desert for microbes that sucks them dry.

    pehrs A:

    Bees are working quite hard to make their honey a very hostile environment for microorganisms. There are, at least, three things that help preserve the honey:

    • The high sugar content makes it very hydrophilic, so it will dry out any microorganism
    • It has a low PH, and the acidity breaks down microorganisms
    • It is mixed with enzymes producing hydrogen peroxide, which has a strong oxidizing effect.

    These three factors combined ensures honey have such a long shelf life. Still, it will spoil, eventually, if exposed to enough moisture. That's how you make mead after all.

    There are a few bacteria associated with honey. One of the more nasty ones is Clostridium botulinum which produces the toxin which causes botulism. This is the reason that small children shouldn't eat honey.

    MrMercedes- A:

    The water content of honey is a key factor in why it doesn't spoil. At 17%, its water content is much lower than that of bacteria or fungi. Honey also has a low water activity; this is a measure of the amount of water in a substance that is available to support microbial growth.

    RetardedWabbit A:

    As others have pointed out honey is monstrously "dry" due to being about 80% sugars by mass.

    I'll add that honey is also incredibly stable over time due to it's composition. There's no fats to go rancid, few proteins to break down, and even the sugars are largely stable simple sugars.

    Honey is basically too dry for microbes, and too simple for time to break it down.
