  • Physics Eli5: How do amateurs keep long exposure astonomy shots from blurring?

    NEW
    Q:

    I keep seeing all these posts "I took this 10 hour exposure of the sky and wow look at many stars:O".

    How do you do that at home and keep it from getting blurry? I mean, the earth (in this case the sky) moves, how do you keep that at a steade pace in the exact right angle over 10 hours so that the picture doesn't blur? I can easily see how a professional telescope can do this, but all the amateurs posting all the time?

    jaa101 A:

    A ten-hour single exposure is difficult, requiring good equipment and experience. These days you can “stack” a series of shorter exposures in software to reach the same total exposure time. This has the advantage of being able to throw away bad exposures so a single adverse event doesn’t ruin everything. The software can also align all the shots, effectively correcting for any long-term drift in the tracking system.

    mmmmmmBacon12345 A:

    Equatorial mounts and good counter weights

    The most important thing for a non-blurry photo is a stable platform so they use heavy tripods with some added weight on them to keep things from wobbling in the breeze. Even a tiny bit of wobble results in a lot of blur when you're looking at something far away

    Next up is using an Equatorial mount rather than an Azimuthal mount. Azimuthal mounts are the simplest to understand, its got two axis that can point the telescope up/down and left/right, unfortunately objects in the sky move in a curve so you need to carefully adjust both the X and Y at different rates throughout the night to stay on target.

    Equatorial mounts are designed with this curved movement in mind. You align it north/south and set one axis which defines how high above/below the equator what you're looking at is, and then the telescope rotates around a curve to line up with the object you want to see. A single little motor scoots the telescope around this curve at a fixed rate(always the same!) keeping the object in the center of view.

    A good quality camera with lenses can be hundreds of dollars, a good equatorial mount and a telescope is going to be around the same. Its an expensive hobby, but not thousands of dollars required expensive

    magnora7 A:

    Software.

    You can use something like the free software "SharpCap" to take the long exposure (which is thousands of images taken at 30 images per second, which is saved in one big .avi file).

    Then you use the free software "autostakkert" to stack the images so they're all aligned perfectly. This then finds the least blurry images out of the image set, and blends them to form one very-clear image.

    Lastly you can do "regiStax 6" to do post adjustments.

    It's all free and basically automated. Then you just hook a webcam up to your telescope, and away you go!

    travelinmatt76 A:

    You don't need a lot of fancy gear to do this. A decent telescope starts around $150, and the motor drive is around $40 or so. The only real challenge is getting your telescope mount properly aligned. Once that is done all you need to do is line up what you want to take a picture of and turn on the motor drive.
