    ELI5: Why are rocket exhaust ports increasing in diameter, while jet engine exhaust ports are decreasing?

    slugmas818 A:

    Wasn't going to answer, but a lot of these answers are missing the main point.

    ​

    The point of an an engine is to shoot gas out the back as fast as possible. The faster the gas goes, the more thrust per mass you get.

    ​

    For gas flows below the speed of sound, increasing the pressure makes them faster. All nozzles, jet or rocket, will include a section that decreases in size. This accelerates the gas until it reaches the speed of sound, and for most jet engines that's all that we do.

    ​

    However, rocket engines really need more thrust per mass of gas expelled, because every gram of gas expelled takes up space in a very limited fuel tank, so they accelerate the gasses even more, using what is called a de Laval nozzle (converging-diverging).

    ​

    For supersonic flows, the effect of pressure changes. Now, low pressures make the gas go faster, so we expand the gas as much as possible as soon as our flow reaches the "throat", where the gas flow reaches the speed of sound.

    ​

    Of course, we can't expand the gas until it has lower pressure than the atmosphere, because then the atmosphere kind of pushes back into the nozzle, and we can get some really weird shockwave-type effects. But that's talk for another day.

    hairthrowaway10oct17 A:

    Aerospace engineer here...

    It has to do with the fact that the properties of air change with its speed.

    Main idea is to get air to move really fast in one direction so the reaction causes the vehicle to move equally fast in the other direction. Both engines do this, using an exhaust port.

    As air starts to move faster through a decreasing port, there comes a point where the air is moving at the speed of sound. Before this point in time, the behaviour of air can be considered compressible - that is, increasing pressure causes air to compress in a smaller volume. At and after this point, air becomes incompressible - meaning increasing pressure will not cause it to get any smaller in volume -- it literally becomes a rock!

    So now imagine air moving so fast, that at the point where it moves at the speed of sound, it is quite literally very, very hard, and the air behind it is slamming into it -- slamming into a rock! Now you have a problem, in that this decreasing port is useless -- you can't move the air any faster.

    So for rocket engines, which need to move the air much, much faster than jet engines, some smart guys way back a few hundred years ago discovered that if you use an increasing duct from the point where air is at the speed of sound, the air velocity will keep increasing, and the pressure drops.

    The rocket engine duct is increasing only in the visible to the eye section. There is a whole section behind it that is decreasing. This is called a decreasing-increasing, or convergent-divergent duct, and it works for rockets.

    For jet engines, the air speed, even at it's highest is nowhere near the speed of sound, so they can keep using the decreasing/convergent duct with no problems.
