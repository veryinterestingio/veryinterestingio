    DarkAlman A:

    The cost of recovery is extremely high. It's much cheaper to just leave it there and build a new one.

    Planes usually disintegrate on impact, meaning that they are in thousands of pieces by the time they hit the bottom. Making salvaging them even more difficult.

    The only reason we spend the money is to determine why the crash happened.

    zgrizz A:

    In August of 2000 the Russian submarine Kursk sank in 353 feet of water after a faulty weld in a torpedo set off a chain reaction of explosions while it was submerged.

    All but the bow section was recovered at a cost of over $100 million.

    Wrecked ships are rare, and occurring in shallow enough water is even more rare.

    Planes are extremely fragile and are generally destroyed beyond repair on impact.

    Edit to add - the Costa Concordia capsized after hitting rocks in shallow water in January 2012. It was mostly above water. That salvage operation cost roughly $1 billion.

    TraceMalin A:

    The cost to recover dead passengers and crew from the Costa Concordia and salvage it was $1.85 billion. Many cruise ships cost as much as $1.3 billion, so that project was in the ballpark of cost. Oil tankers and freighters usually cost $40 million - $120 million. The insurance company usually decides to "total" the wreck much like insurance companies decide that the damage to repair a car would be more than its value. As a diver, I was asked to video a freighter in 250 feet of water that is broken in half, but the company still owns the ship and has to prevent environmental damage.
