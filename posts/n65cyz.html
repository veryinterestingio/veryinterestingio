  • Earth Sciences Why are 75% of the earth's annular lakes north of the 49th parallel when only about 1/8 of the earth's land surface is there?

    NEW
    Q:

    Wikipedia lists 32 annular lakes, of which 24 are north of the 49th parallel. Follow up question: why are 20 of them in just the three countries of Canada, Finland, and Sweden? What makes these countries more prone to annular lakes than others? Do they account for the majority of land north of the 49th?

    Sharlinator A:

    The bedrocks in Canada (the Canadian Shield) and Fennoscandia (the Baltic Shield) constitute some of the most ancient and well-preserved rock on Earth's surface, their formation dating back to literally billions of years ago! They have had a lot of time to collect records of meteorite impacts. These old and stable areas of the lithosphere are called cratons, and they have survived cycles of continental merging and rifting relatively unchanged.

    The ancient bedrock, however, would still be covered in a thick layer of soil and other regolith, but in these areas the most recent glaciation has conveniently stripped off a lot of soil and exposed the bare rock underneath.

    beleaguered_penguin A:

    Likely due to glacier retreat. There's much less land in the south that is near the pole (excepting antarctica, which is one big frozen thing).

    As glaciers retreat they leave their carved shape in the landscape which is hilly and obviously not level, leading to lakes.
