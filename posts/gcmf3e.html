    BitOBear A:

    The filmmakers typically have to take way more film than fit in a normal documentary format. but that normal documentary format was truncated due to attention span that movie theater goers set as standard.

    Now that the media formats like DVD etc don't constrain the movie length limit. Now that broadcasters don't have to block out 1 and 2 hour time slots. Now that people can stream. The documentaries are hitting a more natural length.

    Plus with advertising and all that, a longer show pays better.

    So it's not so much there stretching out the content as it is they are no longer condensing the content.

    jmccrann A:

    It's about money, driven by SVOD (subscription video on demand services, like Netflix) and AVOD (ad-supported video on demand, like YouTube). SVODs count on 'time spent' with their service as one metric to keep subscribers. AVOD uses 'time spent' as a metric for advertisers.

    The craft of well-edited, concise storytelling goes out of the equation when docs are monetized. The longer, the better - and now stuff that once wound up on the cutting room floor is included. They all benefit from bloated content, but not viewers.

    We can complain about the rigid 23- and 45-minute and 3 or 5 arc structure on ad-supported tv, but the editing did cut bloat. Producers/directors had to be disciplined in their storytelling in that model.
