    Q:

    If helium keeps floating up and up, how do we still have helium left on Earth after more than a billion years? Also, helium is a gas. Where do you actually go to find helium on Earth?

    iorgfeflkd A:

    Helium on Earth is typically produced by the radioactive decay of larger elements, which produces alpha particles, which are helium nuclei. A lot of it remained underground, and was extracted as a byproduct of hydrocarbon extraction.

    shleppenwolf A:

    A substance floats up because it's surrounded by some denser medium. Helium is less dense than air, so up it goes. But eventually it reaches the top of the atmosphere, and runs out of air molecules to displace it.

    It can be knocked loose by the solar wind (charged particles from the sun), but this is a very slow process.

    dihydrocodeine A:

    To add on to what others are saying about where helium comes from, you are right to suggest that helium released from the earth will eventually leave the atmosphere. This occurs via a process called atmospheric escape. According to Wikipedia, which is citing this article in Scientific American, "The Earth additionally loses approximately 50 g/s of helium primarily through polar wind escape."

    Helium is essentially a non-renewable resource. As this article describes, given the current rate of depletion, experts believe we could run out of accessible helium in 25 years or so. This would actually be very problematic for some technologies that rely on helium, such as MRI scanners.
