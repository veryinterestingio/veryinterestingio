  • Economics How do alcohol companies, mainly whiskey producers, keep up with demand?

    NEW
    Q:

    For example, Jack Daniels takes about 8 years to age. How would a company be able to create a new product for a current trend or be able to produce the correct quantity of the alcohol?

    volci A:

    In short: they guess well

    Longer answer: they look at past demand, forecasted demand (from a variety of statistical data sets), and adjust production to hopefully have ”just enough” (or slightly ”too much”) product when it eventually becomes saleable

    Yes - it's a multi-year investment

    Yes - it's risky

    But also yes - as long as they guess right enough, it's highly profitable

    And, of course, they can decide to sell something as a ”special edition” if market conditions change

    Jello_hell A:

    They don't always. One of the reasons for the price of Japanese whisky shooting up over the last decade is due to a huge increase in popularity a lot of the older whiskies ran out. That is why you see 'distillers cuts' or 'distillers blends' coming out from Yamazaki and others. It's mixes of what's left while more whisky is ageing. When it comes to Jack Daniels the laws governing Tennessee Whisky require a minimum 4 years ageing. However, Jack Daniels will have reserves of whisky anyway to blend into each batch to ensure consistency of flavour. Basically they make a shitload and anything they don't sell they just keep to blend later.

    51PercentRye A:

    I work in the magical world of whiskey (rye if you can’t tell from my name) and am so happy to have a question I can answer!

    For volume, we look at market trend and operational capacity (how much we can make and store). If the market will demand something above our capacity, we build more warehouses and invest in increasing Distillery capacity.

    For new spirits and trends, we can either innovate at the front end (using different grain and things to change the flavor before barreling) or back end (things to change the flavor after the barrel). Front end innovation relies on future marketing to make it a new product while back end innovation can be more reactive to trends.
