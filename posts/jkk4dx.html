  • Medicine Are there any autoimmune diseases that have been "cured?"

    NEW
    Q:

    All the autoimmune disorders I am familiar with have the same general treatment idea - manage the symptoms. Some types may go into remission here and there but this is unpredictable. Is this correct? Are there any that can be considered "cured" after medical treatment?

    OncoFil A:

    For an IgG mediated autoimmune disease the general treatments are plasma exchange, IVIG, rituximab and corticosteroids. Some other disease specific treatments exist but none are curative. An exciting new treatment is the FcRn inhibitor, which induces IgG degradation. Unfortunately most patients are not cured by these and many are on steroids for life (steroids suck)

    kkngs A:

    Lots of good answers here. I concur that we haven’t cured any autoimmune diseases.

    One positive step that occurred in the last 20 years, though, has been the development of “biologic” drugs. Monoclonal antibodies that can be used to target key proteins in the immune system. For many autoimmune diseases, these are the first treatments that are genuinely effective and aren’t just masking symptoms. For many patients they can halt the progression of their disease for years or even decades.

    Han_Brolo316 A:

    The short answer is no. Think of what it would take to “cure” an autoimmune disease - you would have to eradicate a part of your own immune system. Very hard to do this and not eradicate the good parts as well. Currently, we do not have that specificity or that type of weapon. This is complicated by the fact that the immune system “remembers” what it had attacked in the past (B lymphocytes). These cells survive for decades. This is why it is considered remission and not cured. Same for cancer. It’s impossible to know if you’ve cleared every last cell or not.

    rugglesbee A:

    Autoimmune disorders are a heterogenous group; however, there are curative treatments for a small subset of them. One example is cancers of the immune system, such as leukemia and lymphoma, which can be cured using a bone marrow transplant. Doctors use chemotherapy and radiation to wipe out the dysfunctional immune system and then re-start the immune system from scratch by transplanting healthy immune progenitor cells (which originate in the bone marrow). If the bone marrow transplant is successful, the patient will go into remission with a healthy immune system. While there is always a chance of a relapse, the patient could legitimately be considered “cured.” Bone marrow transplant has significant risks and long term effects which is why it is not commonly used to treat other autoimmune disorders.

    Additionally, new medications exist that help slow the progression of autoimmune diseases. They do not cure the disease, but they do more than manage symptoms. These medications are called immunosuppressants and include prescriptions such as rituximab. Immunosuppressants also have significant side effects including a higher risk of infection, but they have been a game changer for many patients with autoimmune disorders.
