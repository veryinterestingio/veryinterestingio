  • Earth Sciences Is another earthquake less likely to happen after an earthquake?

    NEW
    Q:

    Hello. I live in Catania (Sicily) and we had 2 earthquakes in 2 days. The first one was very weak and short but the other one was quite powerful and it lasted for ~10 seconds (magnitude 4.8 I believe).

    I’d think that after an earthquake most of the energy would be released, making another one less likely to happen, though that doesn’t seem to be correct as in my case it just got stronger.

    I definitely don’t want to die crushed by a building, so yeah. Should I expect another one coming soon?

    KeepThatInMind A:

    Unfortunately, there's no "in general" answer to this question. How a fault behaves after an earthquake is specific to that individual fault and is dependent on several variables, such as the shape of the fault, how much the fault slipped, or how much stress was released versus how much was present before rupture.

    The friction resisting fault slippage is often not the same at all places along the fault. So the smaller foreshock that you experienced may have been a more weakly bound portion of the fault releasing ahead of the 4.8 main shock.

    On the other hand, sometimes foreshocks transfer stress to other parts of the fault, thereby pushing the rest of the fault past the rupture point.

    This ambiguity is why individual earthquakes are often reclassified as foreshocks, mainshocks, or aftershocks in the days or weeks after an earthquake sequence, once seismologists have had an opportunity to fully map the events.

    To answer your final question: again, unfortunately, seismologists can not predict earthquakes. We would like to, but the physics and geology involved are simply too complicated.

    The best advice I can give you is to stay alert, remain calm, and remember your evacuation procedures.

    seis-matters A:

    The earthquakes you are experiencing in Sicily are related to the volcanic activity at Mount Etna. These could be triggered by movements in the volcano, rather than the movements of tectonic plates like most earthquakes. Just because one M4.8 earthquake happens doesn’t mean that deformation cannot continue and trigger more earthquakes. Please be safe, and check for updates on this situation from the INGV.

    In both volcanic and tectonic earthquakes, though, one earthquake occurring does not necessarily relieve all stress on a fault. You would need to have thousands of moderate M3-5 earthquakes to relieve the same area as ruptured in a large M7+ earthquake. Foreshocks and aftershocks around a mainshock show that stresses are relieved and transferred in different ways during an earthquake sequence, and parts of faults can also be re-ruptured. It is unfortunately a very difficult problem to untangle and we are still learning a lot about earthquake behavior and their interactions.

    turtley_different A:

    Anyone who gives you a certain answer (definitely yes, or definitely no) is wrong.

    There is, sadly, no way to be certain if a single quake has acted to release stress from a fault system, such that it is less likely to quake again, or if a single quake has placed some of the fault under high stress such that it is more likely to quake again, perhaps causing a large quake.

    Knowing which is the case for a particular seismic event would require a higher deal of knowledge about small scale structure and stress maps of the crust than we can possibly know with current methods

    In your specific case, a 4.8 in Sicily could either be the largest quake of a series, or the lead up to a larger quake. Both are plausible given the tectonic setting.

    Stay safe, learn your local evac procedures and sign up for earthquake alerts.
