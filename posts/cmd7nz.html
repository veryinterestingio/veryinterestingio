    Q:

    Why does putting a leaf between pages of a firmly closed book prevent the leaf from decaying?

    ethornber A:

    Dehydration; by applying pressure and absorbent layers you're accelerating the removal of water from the leaf. Once it gets below a certain threshold, microorganisms don't have enough free water to function and reproduce, and decay is prevented.

    thwi A:

    Any leaf that dries first, doesn't decay afterwards. A leaf in a book is not so fundamentally different from any other leaf stored in a dry place. If you pick a leaf from a tree and put it right next to the heater or even just on the table in your living room (unless you live in a very damp climate), you'll find the results are very similar to the leaf in the book in terms of decay. The only thing that a book does, is speed up the process by pressing moisture from the leaf quicker than a natural drying process would, reducing the amount of contact with oxygen in the air and flattening it.
