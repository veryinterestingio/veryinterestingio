    Q:

    ELI5: How is the human body/liver capable of identifying/filtering almost endless possibilities of different things that could enter the body?

    maximuse_ A:

    That's because the human body doesn't filter individual things, rather it's more broad. An example:

    Acidic things get neutralized by sodium bicarbonate. Alkaline things get neutralized by the stomach acid. Alcohol (-oh compounds) get broken down by alcohol dehydrogenase into aldehydes (-cho compounds). Aldehydes get broken down by aldehyde dehydrogenase into carboxylic acid (-cooh).

    There are lots of enzymes and tools that the body can use to act on a given substance entering the body, without needing to "identify" the substance.

    HeavyPettingBlackout A:

    This will only partially answer your question but the permeability of the glomerulus of the nephron (the functional unit of the kidney) is responsible for the removal/excretion of many substances from the body. Desirable substances are selectively reabsorbed. The nephron is also the site of a large amount of protein catabolism. After small proteins pass through the glomerulus, most are reabsorbed in the proximal convoluted tubule and end up in lysosomes, where they are degraded.

    ​

    Edit: I forgot this is ELI5.

    Short answer: You pee out small molecules and a part of your kidney sits there and goes, "Wait, I need that" and pulls important things back in.

    Maoman1 A:

    You know how sometimes you see something you don't recognize and you're like "I don't know what that is, but it's definitely not a cat"? Like, you know what a cat is, and even though you don't know what this other thing is, even though you might not know anything about it at all... you can still tell that it isn't a cat.

    Well your body is sorta like that. It doesn't know how to identify all the millions of different things that might get into your body, it just knows the things that are supposed to be in your body, and anything that isn't that is bad.

    But then, sometimes things can be really sneaky or really tiny and your body won't notice them get in, or maybe it looks kinda like a cat, so your body doesn't immediately recognize it as bad, and its only once it starts barking that your body is like "whoa, that ain't no a cat." This is how you get sick.
