    iayork A:

    Before anyone can get a vaccine, it has to be approved by the appropriate regulatory agency (in the US, that’s the FDA). In general, the FDA approves vaccines that have safety and efficacy data, and that generally comes from Phase 3 clinical trials. The two vaccines that have been approved authorized so far (Pfizer/BioNTech and Moderna) were tested in people 18 and up, and Pfizer added 16-18-year olds partway through their trial with the FDA’s approval. That’s why those vaccines are approved authorized for 18-plus (Moderna) and 16-plus (Pfizer).

    So the vaccine will be available for kids under 16 when the FDA approves it, and that will be once the trials are reviewed. Both Pfizer and Moderna have started trials for kids 12-16, but only recently (December/January), and Pfizer only just completed recruiting at the end of January. So we are probably several months away from even starting the approval process for them, and the others are further back (Johnson and Johnson, which just submitted its vaccine data for approval, only included 18-plus in that trial).

    So if all goes well, we can probably expect FDA approval for vaccination of 12-plus in mid-summer, ish, for Pfizer, maybe a little later for Moderna, and even later for J&J.

    I don’t know much about the status of vaccines approved in other countries. AstraZeneca’s vaccine was approved in the UK for 18-plus; I believe they had a trial planned for younger children but haven’t started it. I don’t know about the Russian or Chinese vaccines.
