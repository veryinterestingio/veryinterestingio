    catwhowalksbyhimself A:

    They did a study on this recently, and it turns out a variation of sleeping cycles is not only normal, but potentially a survival adaptation. When studying hunter-gatherer tribes, they found that because of this, there was always someone awake in the tribe they were studying, protecting the entire tribe from attack.

    So no, it' not necessarily better. Everyone has a slightly different rythm and that variation is actually a good thing in a hunter-gatherer tribe.

    Bang_Bus A:

    Because you're probably not doing it very often.

    Quality of the sleep is determined by brain chemistry; brain releases chemicals that make us sleepy, or it doesn't. Lack of light, habits (including what time your dinner is, what's on TV and when, etc), etc all tie in to this - make brain decide when to make you aware and awake or sleepy. There's more noise during day, more light, more warmth, all your sensory organs are getting more signals, etc. So clearly brain is more overloaded and considers all this a possible threat or opportunity and keeps you awake.

    But it doesn't mean you can't re-configure your body -- there's no night and day on ISS for example. People work night shifts all over the planet and still sleep normally, etc. Airline staff switches time zones constantly and still doesn't go mad. Astronauts on ISS see 16 sunrises and -sets (If I remember right) during time we know as single day. Brain has sort of priority system if it comes to sensory input: living in very loud city you might be okay with most of the noise and sleep well, but nursing mothers can instantly wake up to even quietest of noises a baby could make, even if they're horribly exhausted. Brain just decides that it's important enough and instantly wakes mothers up. That's also why many people find it hard to sleep while visiting someone - brain is way more alert than usual, because sounds, smells, geometry, everything's different.

    So, if you train your body with different schedule and acceptable irritant level, sooner or later it'll become the norm. We all turn clocks twice a year and moan long and loud about messing with rhythm, but forget all about it in just a week or two. Only to start whinging again after half a year.

    So your body doesn't really know the difference. It feels like it knows, but it's just a sum of all the other things™ and thus, looks like a grand, weirdly clever design of nature.
