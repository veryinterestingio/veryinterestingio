    TournantDangereux A:

    You’re biased because you know and care about pathogens.

    If you lacked the knowledge of pathogens or the ability to correlate illness with them, then you’d merrily drink from puddles or eat raw meat that you killed or came across. Later when you were suffering from these choices, you’d just accept that you don’t feel well or would blame some unknowable outside agency.

    Wild animals carry all kinds of pathogens. Wild animals often suffer from them. Without knowledge of “hook worm” or whatever, they just accept the itchiness, diarrhea or whatever effects manifest.

    For examples, look at many “medical” procedures before the industrial age. If you don’t know what caused you to get sick, you can end up doing many strange things to “avoid” or “cure” yourself.

    Xenton A:

    A lot of animals still get quite sick quite often. Pet animals often live significantly longer than their wild brethren for that reason.

    Beyond that, a lot of illnesses aren't fatal to us, but just make us unwell for a few days and similar to animals. They may not get sick from every drink at a stream but when they do they spend a few days unable to eat and feeling unwell and either they die or they avoid drinking there next time.

    Similarly, you'll find that most of the time you CAN drink from streams and puddles and be relatively fine. There's huge groups of humanity that do exactly that to survive, many of them do end up with minor or major parasites and other conditions but for the longest time that was normal for humans, just as it is for wild animals.

    So I guess the answer to your title question is: we're not.
