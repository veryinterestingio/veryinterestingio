    Q:

    So i get why IPv4 will be obsolete soon, we need more then 2^32 address spacess, sure, but why do we need a whopping 2^128 addresses (basically billions of billions for every human).

    Wouldn't it be enough to have 2^64 possible addresses and therefore they are still relatively readable and it will still last for a few thousand years from now, or am I missing something out?

    ChopBustin101 A:

    They are attempting to future proof the protocol because we really don't want to change protocols again in the future. Since we have no idea what is going to be connected in the future we can't really say that we only need X amount per person (but I agree several octillion per person does seem a bit overkill), or how many people there are going to be. Why make a new protocol that has a chance of running out of addresses when it is just a couple more characters to ensure we won't have that issue?

    There are also some other nice benefits of IPV6, my biggest one is your ISP will give you a block of IPs and each device would get a public address, no more dealing with port forwarding settings in routers to get a game working or a VOIP phone working.

    TheThiefMaster A:

    Other answers are going on about "future proofing" and so on but that's not the reason.

    Actually, only 64 bits of the ipv6 address are required to get to an "edge router" (the thing that would traditionally get the public ipv4 address) - so the public address in the ipv6 protocol is actually the size you suggest - 2^(64).

    The other 64 bits are for a "device identifier" inside the network. Effectively a local address.

    Think about it like this: in IPv4, you have a 32-bit external ip, and a 32-bit internal ip. In IPv6 that was effectively made 64-bits each, and then both were put into the IPv6 packet as a single 128-bit address to allow traffic to more easily traverse an edge router without NAT.

    In IPv4 you have to deal with NAT and port forwarding, which doesn't allow multiple devices to use the same port because you only have one public address. In IPv6 because the internal part of the address is in the packets you can "pinhole" the port in the router's firewall to every device that needs it with no problem.
