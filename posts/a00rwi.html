    baldengineer A:

    The most common metric is how many “first stream” views the show gets. “First stream” counts the first piece of content someone views after subscribing.

    As with most services, the “cost of acquisition” for new customers is the most critical metric. How much do you have to spend to get someone to sign up? The lower that number, the sooner they are a profitable customer.

    For example, let’s say a show’s production and marketing costs are $100mil, and it brings in 100,000 new subscribers. Again, that “new subs” is determined by how many people stream that show first after subscribing. So in that case, each new sub cost them $1,000. That’s a very high cost of acquisition. At $10 a month, it would take 8 years to become profitable on those customers.

    Change it up and say $100mil cost and 2,000,000 new subs. Now you’re at $50 per customer and that is a far more reasonable acquisition cost. In a few months the subs will be profitable.

    Now, this model ignores a number of other revenue streams and costs, but it effectively determines the “profitability” of a new show.

    Different streaming companies will have different targets, I just used these numbers as an example.
