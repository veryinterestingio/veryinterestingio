    the_narrator42 A:

    Surface tension itself is a measure of the strength and sum of all intermolecular forces at an interface. A molecule of water in the bulk phase of a solution is experiencing a uniform attraction to all other water molecules around it. This is a highly favourable state and the molecule is of 'low energy'. In contrast, a molecule of water at the interface is no longer experiencing a uniform attraction and is being 'pulled' towards the bulk phase - the system is trying to reduce the number of molecules at the interface. The stronger this pull (intermolecular attraction), the higher the surface tension. When we raise the temperature of a system, the molecules gain kinetic energy and as a result spend less time at the interface and the intermolecular attraction is weakened, which in turn reduces the surface tension.

    radicallyhip A:

    When the temperature of water decreases, the molecules have less kinetic energy. A solid (in this case, ice) has particles which do not have enough kinetic energy to overcome the forces of intermolecular forces (hydrogen bonding, for example).

    As liquid water cools, the kinetic energy of the water molecules have a harder time overcoming those hydrogen bonding forces, and so the surface tension increases.
