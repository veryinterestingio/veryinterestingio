    Q:

    ELI5: Why does it take a lot less work and time to build your muscles back to their former strength if you take an extended break from working out than it does to get more strength on top of that?

    Bergerac1982 A:

    Muscle cells (myocytes) have multiple nuclei. This is a unique adaptation of muscle cells, and each myonuclei has an area of the cell it controls, called a myonuclear domain. These domains are limited in size; if a muscle cell is to grow, it needs more myonuclei.

    So imagine a muscle fibre with 10 nuclei. You work out, and using donated nuclei from satellite cells (a kind of stem cell) the fibre adds 3 more nuclei. Good job bro! You got 30% bigger!

    Then you stop working out (we've all been there) and lose your muscle development. However those nuclei remain, so that muscle fibre already has the myonuclear apparatus it needs to regain dem gainz.

    In order to grow beyond that requires more satellite cells to create more myonuclei, and is therefore a more biologically complex and demanding process.

    TheSoup05 A:

    Basically, when you work out you’re pushing your muscles until the cells are a little damaged. During your recovery, other cells will come in to repair this damage. They’ll make it bigger than it was before though, and therefor stronger.

    Cells need something to control them though, so they have what’s called a nucleus that’s like a tiny cell brain. One nucleus can only control so much cell though. So as muscle cells continue to grow, they’ll eventually need more than one nucleus. So the cells responsible for repairing your muscles will often donate their own nucleus to the muscle cell.

    Eventually if you stop working out the muscle cells will begin to shrink again, but they won’t lose the extra nuclei they’ve accumulated. Or at least not any time soon, they might go away eventually but not for a very long time. If you start working out again after that, your cells are already good to go. They’ve already got enough nuclei to support growing up to their previous size. So they’ll jump back with less work than it took the first time around. But after that, you’re building it back up the old fashioned way.
