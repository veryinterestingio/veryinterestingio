    mapsandclocks A:

    Some misinformation here.

    First, the acoustic reflex. It is true that for most people, two muscles in your middle ear space flex when exposed the loud sounds, pull the middle ear bones inward and reducing the level of sound that gets transmitted to the inner ear. Is it protective? Maybe, but not very much. First, it’s frequency dependent, and most people don’t have the reflex above 4 kHz. Second, it decays, and releases after 10-20 seconds. Lastly, the effect is small, on the scale of 3-5 dB. The pain you get after noise exposure is not due to this but to excitotoxicity in the inner ear.

    More specific to your question, the biggest adjustment our ears make to sound level happens in the cochlea, where outer hair cells modulate the level of neural transmission based in the input level of the sound. At very low levels, out hair cells are highly activated, essentially amplifying the signal on its way to the brain. It’s compressive, so at high levels the outer hair cells are much less reactive. This affects not only threshold and level perception, but also filtering, so at lower levels we are much better at picking out all the different frequency components of a sound than at high levels.

    As mentioned, there are many other nuances to auditory perception- masking, squelch, masking release, streaming, object perception, localization, figure ground. All of these things depend on the signal and the hearing status of the listener. It’s interesting and complex stuff!

    Source: am a hearing scientist.
