    Leucippus1 A:

    The air is not as dense in the summer and is more humid, so the droplets tend to fall to the ground faster and get burned up in the sun. Keep in mind, as disease spread tends to lessen in the summer, it never actually goes away. People still get colds, flu, and COVID in warm weather.

In moister air, the epidemic struggles to build momentum, whereas in drier conditions it spreads like wildfire. And comparing 30 years’ worth of climate records with health records, Jeffrey Shaman at Columbia University and colleagues found that flu epidemics almost always followed a drop in air humidity.

The finding has now been replicated many times including analyses of the 2009 Swine flu pandemic.

    dreamrock A:

    Among other reasons, noses are naturally runny in colder weather because the cilia stop performing the duty of sweeping mucus back into the nasal sinuses. Infected people tend to wipe their noses unconsciously, and then touch such surfaces as shopping cart handles. Subfreezing temperatures, like those in a grocery store's parking lot are not an ideal biological temperature for reproduction, but they are an ideal temperature for preservation. So the shopping carts are brought indoors, people touch the infected handles, and when they go back into the cold their nose starts running. They then touch their noses and the cycle of life begins again.
