    Q:

    ELI5: Why do humans seem to be so much weaker (physically) than nearly all animals that are around our size?

    Nagisan A:

    In addition to what's been mentioned so far, we don't have high strength for our size, but we have one of the best capabilities for endurance among all animals (not just those our size). About the only animal with higher endurance than us are the dogs often used in sled racing.

    So we are "weaker" in terms of raw strength, but we have a scary potential for endurance compared to most animals.

    Part of that endurance comes down to our bodies only maintaining enough strength for our recent needs, which is why we need to train to gain and maintain strength.

    BafangFan A:

    https://youtu.be/xAWReEm4l0w

    This is a great lecture that touches on the subject.

    There is something called the "expensive tissue hypothesis". In essence, the brain consumes a large number of calories compared to other organs. In humans it seems the brain consumes around 300 calories per day.

    The human brain is larger in relation to the body mass of humans, as compared to any other animals (we have much larger brains compared to other animals, as a proportion of body size).

    There is a trade-off in other organs, to reduce the total calorie demands that we need to take in each day. We have smaller digestive tracts that other primates. And we also have less muscle mass. And yet we thrived as a species. Better than any other mammal.

    It's suggested that because early humans were able to scavange fat from bone marrow of large animals killed by other predators, we were able to obtain a lot of calories for minimal effort. Fat has 9 calories per gram, compared to 4 calories per gram for carbohydrates and protein. So eating fat gave us the most bang for our buck. And we evolved by getting better at finding fat (became better scavengers, and better hunters).

    No other animal could access the fat in bone marrow because they couldn't figure out how to use tools to break the bones (actually, a couple types of birds are known to drop bones from great heights).

    gamejunky34 A:

    The actual strength difference between us and say a chimpanzee is very exaggerated actually, yes they are slightly stronger pound for pound but animal muscles aren't automatically twice as strong as humans. Usable strength is a function of many things, the most influential of which except size are muscle structure (penate, parallel, fusiform) and skeletal structure (where the muscle actually attaches) it simply comes down to geometry in most cases. Things like muscle type and neurological differences are valid too, but none of them will effectively double your bicep curl quite like relocating your bicep insertion point 2 inches closer to your hand. As another commenter said, we really are built for efficiency, our relatively spindly structure gives us many advantages such as flexibility, limb speed and reach. If you took a chimpanzee and a human of the same weight and had both of them deadlift something, the chimp would likely be able to lift more, but the human would also have to lift it 5x higher, literally doing more work. There's a reason a 500lb squat is more impressive than a 700lb calf raise. To answer your question though, in most cases animals outperform humans in terms of maximum force output purely because of leverage or just shear size.
