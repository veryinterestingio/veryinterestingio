    PickledPokute A:

    I think you're fooling yourself if you think you can imagine something perfectly in your head.

    Imagine someone who has a wide and open smile. It's pretty easy, but your mind fills in the blanks and deliberately obfuscates the fact it doesn't know everything. How many of the front upper teeth are visible in that smile. What's the size ratios of those teeth? Were the incisors the 3rd or 4th from the center? What's the 3d shape of the incisor and in what angle is it positioned in the mouth?

    You want to draw a human body? How many muscles do you know the shape of in the legs or the back? These are crucial for realistic depictions and the best way to know these is through studying - either by drawing people or reading literature. It's no surprise that medical books about anatomy are a staple reference material for artists.

    Even drawing something as "simple" as hands is really difficult. Do you think you could accurately remember the amount of folds in the knuckles and at what point they visibly appear? Does everyone know realise that while fingers are of different lengths, they don't start from the same "line"? That fingers are attached to the palm differently than from the backhand?

    Imagine your favourite pair of shoes that you wear. You surely must remember everything about them. If they have laces, how many laceholes do they have? How are your shoes laced - when the laces cross which side is on top?

    when people do art, they imitate reality. They base it on items and shapes people find familiar. Few artists just improvise everything and gain fame. So one good way to get better is just to copy, evalute, copy, evaluate and copy and evaluate.
