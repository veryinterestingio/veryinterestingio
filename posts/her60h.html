  • Why are there business suit and jewelry stores in airports? Do people really casually buy a +$600 suit or $1,000 ring before they get on a plane?

    NEW
    Q:

    I never understood this. Ive never seen someone come on the plane with a brand new suit. How are these stores still in business? I could understand a little if the airport was part of a mall and these stores were before the security line.

    Do people really go the airport just to buy a suit?

    test_user_200 A:

    There’s multiple things going on. One is that buying something in an airport’s international area can be much cheaper for residents of certain countries. International terminals are sort of like international waters so there’s no import duties or taxes. Luxury goods tend to be taxed at a higher rate so that’s what’s at the airport. (That’s less of an issue now because of decades of trade agreements lowering import duties but some products are still heavily taxed in some countries.)

    The stores are also sometimes just there for promotional reasons. People in an airport terminal are typically wealthier than average and are often killing time waiting for a flight. They might not buy anything at the airport but maybe they wander in, see something they like, and buy it from another location (or online) later.

    amg_v A:

    Airport retail space is very expensive (because space is limited and they're provided a valuable, steady supply of above-average income customers). As a result, everything in an airport is either priced higher than normal (food), or is a super high margin item like jewelry, cosmetics, cologne, designer clothes, alcohol, handbags, etc. Some of the super expensive, high margin items make so much profit off just a single sale that it only takes an occasional random purchase to keep them going. Notice how the super high end shops in malls (outside airports) are rarely crowded - they sell an "exclusive" product to the few people willing to part with so much money.

    read_it_0n_reddit A:

    An example might be a watch. Just like it was suggested, business travelers and tourists a like may want to take advantage of tax and or currency advantages while crossing boarders. Depending on the country in which you live, luxury goods may be taxed heavily, so on a 10k Rolex, a 10% reduction in sales tax or difference in currency exchange rates vs. local pricing, you are seeing a 1k savings, if not more. Same logic goes for high end luggage like Tumi where saving taxes could mean a nice meal out at your destination or when you return home.

    That being said, you are still held to declare what you've purchased abroad and would be responsible for paying your own countries duty as you are importing goods. Some will skirt this by not declaring and managing the evidence of purchase, but that would be illegal.
