  • Medicine Mechanistically, why is benzene carcinogenic?

    Q:

    On paper it seems to me that benzene shouldn't be carcinogenic, since it isn't especially reactive.

    greyham11 A:

    While benzene isn't particularly reactive with most biologically relevant molecules, our enzymes are very effective catalysts and the oxidation products of benzene in the body are potent carcinogens. Cytochrome P450s in the liver and lungs are thought to initially convert it to benzene oxide, which then spontaneously rearranges to phenol. Another P450-dependent oxidation converts it to hydroquinone, and then reactive oxygen species oxidise this to benzoquinone within the bone marrow, where it can cause leukemia. There are alternate pathways from many of these intermediates, leading to other toxic and carcinogenic substances.

    https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3271273/

    MaynardTheMoose A:

    It’s an intercalating agent.

    Basically, your DNA is a series of Adenine/Thymine, Guanine/Cytosine base pairs. These bases have pretty flat 3D structures, so benzene, a similarly flat molecule, is able to stack in between two bases. This causes your DNA to form a bulge, preventing the replication machinery from working properly.

    Life has ways of getting around DNA bulges and similar deformations (otherwise we’d be screwed). We have polymerases that can handle these situations and still read through, but they’re error prone and more likely to introduce a mutation.
