  • Biology If insects can grow larger by being raised in an oxygen-rich environment, can animals like crabs and lobsters also grow larger in oxygen-richer water?

    NEW
    Q:

    There have been experiments showing that insects grow a bit larger when raised in a high-oxygen container. Does that carry over to sea arthropods too? What would the other limitting factors be? How could an experiment be designed and performed in order to test this hypothesis?

    Sigroc A:

    There's actually a phenomenon called polar gigantism, where marine invertebrates (mostly arthropods) in the high oxygen-low temperature polar environment in tend to be quite a bit larger than their warm water counterparts. It's a really interesting subject! It's believed that the higher oxygen levels in the cold water plays a role in the size difference, but overall it's not 100% known as to why there is such a difference.

    NezuminoraQ A:

    Insects have holes called spiracles in the surface of their bodies to expose the interior of their bodies to oxygen. Therefore insects can't be very large because the smaller their surface area to volume ratio, the harder it is for a simple mechanism like that to allow all the interior parts of the body to receive adequate oxygen. Increasing the availability of oxygen in the environment will go some way to reducing the limiting factor of size.

    Sea creatures exchange gases using entirely different systems because water is such a different medium, so not sure this same limiting factor of size would be an issue.
