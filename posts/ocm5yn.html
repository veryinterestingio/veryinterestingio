    Notably:

    It has been known for a long time that women have lower plasma glucose and higher plasma FFA concentrations than men after short-term fasting (1, 4–6). However, despite extensive research on gender-related distinctions in glucose and lipid metabolism, these differences in plasma glucose and FFA concentrations have not been explained in full detail so far (7–16).

    But:

    We hypothesized that the relative protection from FFA-induced insulin resistance during fasting in women results from lower muscle ceramide or glucosylceramide levels due to lower muscle FFA uptake, subsequently resulting in higher muscle glucose uptake with lower plasma glucose concentrations.

    Cleistheknees A:

    Lot of talk about differences in energy accessibility in here, and I would just like to remind everyone that the total amount of glucose in the bloodstream is tiny, generally between 3.5-8 grams total. A blood glucose measurement doesn’t say anything about the total storage (ie available energy). Glucose is not static in the blood. Like minerals in the bone it is constantly moving into the bloodstream from the liver and digestive tract, and moving out, primarily into skeletal muscle, the brain, and a minority of tissues which cannot oxide fats. The serum level is the just the steady state between those ins and outs.

    In fact, if anything you would probably evolutionarily expect the sex with higher energy requirements to have lower serum glucose levels because of a higher ability to remove glucose out of the bloodstream and transport it into the brain and muscles.
