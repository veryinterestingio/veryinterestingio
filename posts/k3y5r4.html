    Q:

    Do humans get Antibodies against various pathogens that infect cattles via their milk, based on the same principle that human babies get antibodies against several pathogen from their mother's breast milk?

    Bag_of_Flesh A:

    Babies have a receptor called RnFc in their inestines' lining. That receptor attaches to the antibodies in the gut and passes them to the blood. This receptor is also present in the materno-fetal interface (the placenta) and allows passage of antibodies to the fetus' blood. In humans over 3 months old, this receptor can be found in the lining of our vessels (the endothelial cells). This serves as a way of recycling IgG (a type of antibody) extending its plasmatic half life from 6 to 21 days.

    RnFc bounds to the Fc portion of the antibodies, which is species-specific. So it's unlikely one could absorb bovine antibodies by drinking milk.

    KingHenryXVI A:

    The antibodies babies get from breast milk are not part of the immune system in the blood. Those antibodies don’t actually make it “into” the body. They stay in the digestive tract. The antibodies in the blood are produced in the bone marrow. So in short, no it doesn’t really work like that.
