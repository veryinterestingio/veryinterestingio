    Q:

    Why are horns / trumpets shaped the way that they are? How does the flair increase volume?

    hwillis A:

    The "optimal" horn grows exponentially in diameter. An exponential horn creates an impedance match between the small diameter tube and the open air. It's effectively a hydraulic link- the small end of the bell has higher pressure (which you can push easily with your lungs) but low airflow (so you can blow longer). The big end of the horn has lower pressure but moves much more air. In the end this lets you create much more sound.

    Most instruments don't have an exactly exponential horn, but it's close. The length of the horn might be shorter or longer- this changes the frequencies that get amplified the most, changing the sound of the horn. Long horns, like the winding, gradual increase in a sousaphone, cause longer wavelength, deeper sounds to be amplified the most. Relatively short bells like that on a trumpet amplify higher pitches.

    MysteriousHo-Oh A:

    The flair doesn't actually 'increase' the volume in a conventional way. See, we all know how sound works: when we speak, the pressure produced by the larynx or by an instrument causes the air molecules in the air to vibrate back and forth and reach the other person's eardrums. Now when we normally speak, the sound-waves move around in several directions and our mouths themselves are small so the eardrums, so not only do the sound-waves spread out like ripples in a pond, they don't cause our eardrums vibrate as vigourusly. This is rectified by having a large curvy shape at the end of the horn. The curvy end of the horn is essentially not only forcing the sound-waves to move in one direction(which is also why when we cup our hands, we hear stuff to be 'louder') but it also acts as a more effective interface of the sound-waves crashing into the molecules and does causes us to hear it to be louder.

    ​

    Essentially, the curvy shape conserves the sound rather than amplify it.

    ​

    Now the shape of the flare impacts the pitch of a sound(whether our brain perceives it to be sharp sounding or deep sounding). Larger flares give more mellow and lower pitched sounds as they allow for lower air pressure while smaller flares give more high pitched sounds as they allow for greater air pressure. So musicians usually look for the perfect flare shape which will allow for both high and low pressure so as to get high and low pitched sounds.

    ​

    samueljsb A:

    The horn shape doesn't increase the volume, but it does conserve the sound so less of it is lost. It's to do with impedance matching, basically reducing reflections and allowing the energy to transfer more efficiently.

    The problem with the closed-end instruments is that the notes are very far apart. Without moving fingers, the harmonics would be almost an octave and a half apart!
