  • Physics What makes a metal an ohmic conductor or a non-ohmic one?

    NEW
    Q:

    I've learnt that metals like copper is ohmic while tungsten is non-ohmic, but is there a reason behind these properties?

    (Also, out of curiosity, are gold and silver also ohmic conductors?)

    ksblur A:

    An ohmic conductor is simply one that follows Ohm's law: V=IR. Any conductor that does not is non-ohmic.

    Tungsten only allows a certain amount of current to pass, even if you increase the voltage significantly, and therefore is non-ohmic. This is mostly due to the proportional temperature increase when current passes through it. The increase in temperature causes an increase in resistance, and therefore it cannot satisfy Ohm's law.

    calite A:

    I would not say that "copper is ohmic while tungsten is non-ohmic". Either metal (and any metal) has an increase in resistance when warmed. Tungsten is special in that you can heat it to a higher temperature without melting.

    But increases in resistance upon warming is not what we normally refer to as non-ohmic behavior.

    A non-ohmic device is something that exhibits non-linear behavior without requiring an accompanying temperature rise. Examples: a semiconductor diode, an oxidized joint.

    robbak A:

    Nothing is a truly ohmic conductor. But if you keep the temperature under control, most things are pretty close. Gold an silver would melt before they got too hot - as would copper. Tungsten is an exception because, at least in an oxygen-free environment, it gets very hot without melting (or burning),

    soullessroentgenium A:

    Electrons moving through the overlapping valence and conduction bands in a metal tend to be described well by the free electron model, which has the founding assumptions that there are very few interactions going on between the electrons and everything else, much analogous to the a perfect gas. As such, there is very little to make the process anything other than tediously linear in most respects.

    aortm A:

    Think of Resistance as a function of current. Now expand it out as a taylor series.

    R(I) = R(0) + R'(I)I + 1/2 R''(I)I^(2) + ...

    The ohmic region is where R'(I) and all subsequent derivatives are small, and negligible. So Resistance is constant, and V=IR holds regardless of I.

    When I is large, R'(I) might still be small, but the total product R'(I) and I might no longer be negligible, and Resistance may increase with current. This is where it begins to be non ohmic.

    Diodes are most obvious example of non ohmic conductors. for V<Vd,~0.6V, no current passes. V>Vd, current traces out an exponential.
