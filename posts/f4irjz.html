    Q:

    Why does plastic turn white when you bend it?

    ccdy A:

    When subject to tensile stresses in excess of their yield strength, amorphous and semi-crystalline polymers form crazes, which are microscopic planar voids normal to the stress direction bridged by a network of highly aligned fibrils connecting both faces of the craze. Craze planes reflect light due to the difference in refractive indices between uncrazed and crazed material; if a high density of fine crazes is present, crazed regions will appear white.

    Despite their visual similarities, crazes are emphatically not cracks: being partially filled with polymer, they are not true voids, and moreover can support tensile loads. That said, crazes will eventually transition into cracks if loading is continued.

    References:

    1. Kambour, R. P. (1973). A review of crazing and fracture in thermoplastics. J. Polymer Sci. Macromol. Rev., 7(1):1–154. doi:10.1002/pol.1973.230070101
    2. Deblieck, R. A. C.; van Beek, D. J. M.; Remerie, K.; Ward, I. M. (2011). Failure mechanisms in polyolefines: The role of crazing, shear yielding and the entanglement network. Polymer, 52(14):2979–2990. doi:10.1016/j.polymer.2011.03.055
