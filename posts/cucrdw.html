    Q:

    Wouldn’t it make more sense to give them downers to slow their brains to a normal level?

    Optrode A:

    Neuroscientist here.

    It's more complicated than that (as everything brain related always is). The other commenter's response about needing stimulation isn't really accurate.

    First, ADHD isn't really about being overstimulated / overactive in general. It's more about having difficulty deciding which things to do, especially deciding which things to do based on what you're supposed to be doing / future consequences. This is why one of the hallmark features of ADHD in teens and adults is a long history of failing to get important things done, and why suffering consequences as a result often fails to have an effect on future behaviour.

    As for how stimulant medications address this: We aren't sure. Most likely, though, the stimulant medications are somehow stimulating the brain circuits involved in using information about future consequences to guide behavior, or other brain circuits involved in "executive control" of behavior (basically, exercising deliberate control over your actions).

    feeya7 A:

    Also neuroscientist here, specifically focusing on neuroimaging of ADHD and autism.

    The ELI5 version is that stimulant medication alters dopamine that effect the reward pathways in the brain. Effectively this means that it helps decrease the salience is distracting stimuli which ultimately allows you to focus.

    As others have said the exact mechanism is not well understood. However for a good introduction scientific reading I'd start with the following article:

    https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC24401/
