  • Medicine Why can the birth control “Nuvaring” be removed for up to 48 hours/month without decreasing efficacy, but “Annovera” can only be removed for 2 hours/month, even though they are both vaginal rings releasing the same hormones?

    NEW
    Q:

    I understand that since Annovera lasts for a full year (compared to Nuvaring that only lasts a month) it would have more restrictions, but 2 hours vs 48 hours is a very drastic difference.

    Annovera does release slightly less estrogen than Nuvaring (13mcg vs 15mcg), but it also releases more progestin (150mcg vs 120mcg), so I don’t feel like those slight variances would make that much of a difference.

    Is Annovera just being overly conservative with the adherence guidelines since it’s new? Or is there really that much of a difference between the two of them, and if so, why?

    EDIT:

    First, when I say “can be removed for x hours/month,” I meant during the 21 day period you are supposed to have it in. I am aware you are supposed to remove it during the break week, but I am not talking about that.

    Second, Many of you pointed out that on the Nuvaring website it states it cannot be removed for >3 hours, however, both Planned Parenthood and UC Berkeley state that it is still effective for up to 48 hours. So a follow-up question would be why do both Berkeley and Planned Parenthood (both reputable sources) state 48 hours as the limit?

    EDIT 2: Okay I’m tired of people saying “are you sure you interpreted Planned Parenthood/Berkeley correctly?” So here is a direct quote from the Berkeley source listed above:

    >> [for] delayed insertion of a new ring or delayed reinsertion of a current ring for <48 hours since a ring should have been inserted • Insert ring as soon as possible. • Keep the ring in until the scheduled ring removal day • No additional contraceptive protection is needed

    And a quote from Planned Parenthood:

    >> ...sometimes the NuvaRing might slip out of your vagina. If the ring has been out of your vagina for less than 2 days, rinse it in cool water and put it back in right away. If you put it back in within 48 hours, you’ll still be protected from pregnancy.

    EDIT 3: CDC and WHO (see page 52) also recommend the <48hr rule

    Cheesecake_fetish A:

    It is likely these are the testing conditions it has been approved under, and so that is what they must say on the box, despite it being potentially safe to remove for longer. Safety and effecacy testing is expensive, so if it doesn't add value then they will go with the minimum test conditions for approval. Using it outside of what is stated would be using it 'off label', which is common for many pharmaceuticals, but means that the company is not liable if it fails when used inappropriately.

    For example, the Mirena hormone coil has been shown in research studies to be effective for up to 7yrs, where as it is only licenced for up to 5yrs, as these are the testing conditions it was approved under. The company wouldn't make more sales by paying for expensive testing to extend use for another 2yrs, so using longer is off-label but likely safe and still effective.

    Birdbraned A:

    TL;DR: Not all hormones are made the same. That 3 hours vs 48 hours isn't a typo.

    This is in the realm of pharmacokinetics, if you ever want to google or do a literature search "how long does it take a drug to enter/stay/leave my body for"

    You know how you can have vitamin D supplements in tablets ( calcitriol ) vs liquid ( Colecalciferol ), and they can both give you different amounts of vitamin availablility because (in short) they get digested differently?

    Ignoring the filler ingredients, they both will deliver vitamin D to your body that you can use, but in different ways.

    The way your body processes the actual molecule affects how much usable Vitamin D you get and how long it can hang around for.

    Nuvaring /Vs/ Annovera: Etonogestrel and ethinyl estradiol // segesterone acetate and ethinyl estradiol

    If you have a look at the actual ingredients, while the type of estrogen molecule is the same, they have different a progestin molecule that "delivers" the progestin to you receive.

    The progestin in Nuvaring has a much longer a half life (that is, time until you only have half the initial dose left in your system)https://www.drugs.com/ppa/ethinyl-estradiol-and-etonogestrel.html than the progestin in Annovera which is roughly half that https://www.drugs.com/ppa/segesterone-acetate-and-ethinyl-estradiol.html.

    (*I'd prefer to be able to compare from the same site, or a handy dandy comparison graph, but I'm not going for precision here. Edited with links to sources with numerical half life values.)

    This means that without it there delivering the hormones, your hormone levels drop faster. On the flip side, once it's in, it looks like it kicks in sooner, but that's a separate property and I haven't looked in depth at that.

    If you were wondering why Annovera can be used for a year and Nuvaring is monthly then it most likely has to do with the exact composition of the vaginal systems/duration of use, both use Ethinyl Estradiol but Annovera has Segesterone Acetate and Nuvaring uses Etonogestrel.
