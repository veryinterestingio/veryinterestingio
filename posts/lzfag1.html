  • Chemistry Why is wasabi and horseradish a different spicy than peppers and hot sauce?

    NEW
    Q:

    Wasabi burns in the sinuses. Hot sauce hurts everything. Why is that?

    Tgs91 A:

    Just looked it up to try to understand. Peppers and hot sauce have a chemical called capsaicin. Capsaicin is water soluble , which means it can mix into water and get carried around your mouth. It also interacts with a special protein in the nerves of your mouth that is designed to sense heat. So the nerves send "hot" signals to your brain, even though the food isn't hot.

    Wasabi and horseradish don't have capsaicin, they have allyl isothiocyanate. Unlike capsaicin, allyl isothiocyanate tends to form a vapor. So instead of mixing around in your mouth, it floats up into your nose.

    plaid-lemming A:

    Your tongue has cells (neurons) with proteins that sense heat. But there are several different proteins that can sense heat. One protein is called TRP-V1, and the other is called TRP-A. They both do the same basic thing, they sense heat and tell your brain "this part of the body is hot!".

    Hot peppers and and wasabi both trick the proteins into thinking it's hot, when it's not. But they each activate a different protein. Hot peppers activate TRP-V1, and Wasabi activates TRP-A. So you get a similar sensation, but slightly different, because these proteins are found in different parts of your mouth and nose. there's a lot more TRP-A in your nasal cavity, so you feel the wasabi in your nose a bit more. the stuff in Wasabi is also more volatile, so the vapors get up in your nose more.
    Cinnamon, by the way, activates both of them, but not as strongly.
