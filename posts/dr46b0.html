    VehaMeursault A:

    UV-A radiation—that's the answer no one has given in this whole thread.

    TL;DR at the bottom.

    Over time you and your skin are exposed to all sorts of radiation, of which the sun's is most prominent. Of all the radiation it blasts at us, most is blocked by our lovely atmosphere. The little UV radiation that does make it through hits your skin and slowly alters it. Specifically the UV-A type radiation is what affects the aging of the skin most.

    How you wonder? Simple. Your skin cells reproduce, and as they do, their offspring is passed along the blueprint of how to make the next generation of cells, and so on. Radiation damages that blueprint, and although most cells will have a perfect blueprint to pass along, over time the number of cells that pass along imperfect ones become so numerous that we start seeing it with our bare eyes: wrinkles.

    They'll contain less fat, manipulate water less well, etc., all because the blueprint they're passing along is getting blasted by light from the sun.

    UV-B in particular is a contributor to cancer—damage to the blueprints such that the cells that read it think their instructions are to multiply fast and indefinitely.

    TL;DR: the sun is blasting the DNA stored in our skin cells such that their offspring knows less well what to do over time, resulting in wrinkles, and in some cases even cancer.

    Edit: "hur dur stay inside infinitely healthy skin." No. It protects you from a lot of radiation, but not all of it. It helps a lot to stay inside, yes, but so does cream with an SPF of more than zero.

    The easiest way I believe to describe it is to think of a rubber band, over time after being stretched time and time again it begins to lose its elasticity. The rubber band will begin to sag and not snap back into place. Think of your skin being stretched and moving back and forth, your cells do not produce collagen at the rate they once did so it begins to wrinkle and not snap in place.
