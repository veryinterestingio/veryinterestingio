    shuvool A:

    You know how you can blow over the top of a soda bottle and make a whistle sound? You know how doing it with a bigger bottle makes a lower sound? Ok, so imagine that as far as the air around the car going down the freeway is concerned, the car is just a big bottle and the open window is the hole at the top of the bottle.

    All sound is just vibrations of the medium you're in, so in most cases for us humans who need to breathe air pretty constantly, we're going to be most familiar with the air transmitting these vibrations. The measurement of these vibrations is done in frequency for pitch and in a pretty wide assortment of pressure units for intensity, but the one you're likely most familiar with is the decibel (which leads to a whole other discussion that's beyond the scope of this question). Frequency is measured in cycles per second, called Hertz. The higher the frequency of the sound, the bigger the number. Make that frequency low enough and you can actually start hearing the space between those cycles. A good example of this would be an engine running. At a high rpm, an engine sound like it's whining or buzzing, but slow it down and it sounds more like a rumble. Slow it down enough and it sounds like a series of pops as the fuel and air mixture lights off each cycle making a small explosion.

    The technical term for that soda bottle effect is Helmholtz cavitation, but you can think of it as just a harmonic resonance
