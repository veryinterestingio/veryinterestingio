  • Biology How do marsupials clean their pouches? Do they smell terrible?

    NEW
    Q:

    I was thinking about how disgusting a human pouch would be. Can someone elaborate?

    TheWrongSolution A:

    Much like grooming other parts of the body, they lick them clean. The frequency of pouch grooming has been observed to increase when the joeys are in the pouch.

    Source: Russell, E. M., & Giles, D. C. (1974). The effects of young in the pouch on pouch cleaning in the tammar wallaby, Macropus eugenii Desmarest (Marsupialia). Behaviour, 51(1), 19-37.

    Lunar_Magpie A:

    Kangaroos clean their pouches with their tongues. If they have a joey in the pouch, it will defecate in there, so the mom has to clean it. I imagine it still smells kind of bad after cleaning. Not sure about other marsupials, but I guess they do the same thing.

    LD-help A:

    Opossums also clean their pouches by licking. Something worth noting is that, during certain months of the year, there is a dark orange/dark red colored discharge present in the pouch, at least in the tropical species Didelphis Aurita. Usually during or soon after the female's reproductive cycle.

    ​

    I'm unsure -why- exactly it happens, or even if there's a purpose for the discharge at all, but the good news is that the discharge is almost entirely odorless. It might be sort of a "self-cleaning" fluid to prepare the pouch for babies somehow, sort of how human lady-bits do loads of fluid exchange along the cycle.

    ​

    As someone who regularly rescues opossum (and cares permanently for a disabled female), I must say (clean) opossum smell is quite pleasant and reminds me of that soft, comfortable smell that most clean cats have. Even their pouches smell perfectly fine :) Wild opossums sometimes get a bit dirty due to being a perianthropic species, but they're still very tidy animals who frequently bathe themselves like felines and rodents do.
