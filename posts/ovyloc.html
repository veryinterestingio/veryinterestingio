    katinla A:

    A couple of good answers already in this thread covered the "always day" part. What hasn't been answered so far in this thread is the second part of the question: always night. I think the answer is no.

    The closest to it would be the L2 point. It's similar to the L1 that one of the other comments described, but on the other side, i.e. farther away from the Sun, at about 1.5 million km from Earth in the outwards direction. From this point, if you do the math, the Sun is supposed to look slightly bigger than the Earth (in angular diameter terms - of course the Sun is a lot bigger but it doesn't "look" that big as we're so far away). So, if you were observing from that point, the Sun would look like in a constant annular eclipse.

    So, this isn't exactly a "constant night", but the amount of sunlight is significantly reduced by Earth's shadow.

    Just like L1, it takes a lot of energy and time to reach L2, so also in this case don't expect any space stations to be put there, only satellites. An additional difficulty specifically for L2 is that power for solar panels is reduced. You could, however, orbit L2 in a halo orbit to get constant sunlight.

    (That wikipedia link also contains a nice diagram showing the locations of L1 and L2).

    Sharlinator A:

    No, the ISS as it currently exists cannot do either. Its ability to change its orbital height and/or inclination is very limited. But in theory we could build a new space station in an orbit that's high enough and inclined such that Earth would not eclipse it on every orbit, just like the moon is not eclipsed by Earth every month even though it goes "behind" Earth from the sun's perspective.

    Being constantly in Earth's shadow is much more difficult. Basically it's only possible at the L2 Lagrange point, which is unstable, so propellant must be expended to stay there for longer periods of time. It would, of course, also make it impossible to use solar panels to generate power for the spacecraft's systems.

    the_agox A:

    The interesting thing about this question is that both the "yes" and "no" camps are equally right. Yes, there is a specific kind of sun-synchronous orbit that would work, as might the L1 and L2 points, sorta. No, the ISS cannot reasonably move into those orbits. Yes, given infinite fuel, it would eventually be possible. No, it would be more efficient to de-orbit the ISS, build a new one, and launch it into an always-day orbit.

    bendvis A:

    Yes, it could. There are points in space where a spacecraft needs minimal propulsion to stay 'still' relative to the Earth and Sun called Lagrange Points. L1 is a point where the gravitational forces of Earth and Sun are almost completely cancelled out by each other.

    NASA's DSCOVR satellite is at this point now along with a handful of other satellites. The ISS could sit at this point and always be in sunlight.
