    SirMarglar A:

    The US National Cancer Institute has published long-term data showing the risk of lung cancer in ex-smokers comes down to baseline in 15-20 years. So it takes that long for ex-smokers to have the same lung cancer risk as never-smoker.

    More info: https://www.health.harvard.edu/lung-health-and-disease/as-an-ex-smoker-am-i-still-at-high-risk-for-lung-cancer

    BobSeger1945 A:

    Smoking produces inflammation in the lungs. Inflammation activates enzymes (MMP) to destroy the walls of the alveoli. Alveoli are tiny air-filled sacs. When the walls are destroyed, many alveoli fuse together into a single large alveolus. This is called emphysema, and it's part of the disease COPD.

    COPD belongs to the group of obstructive lung diseases. The primary problem is exhalation, not inhalation. Forced expiratory volume (FEV) is reduced, meaning you can exhale less air. This is measured in a test called spirometry.

    In theory, lung tissue can be repaired. The alveoli contain stem cells which can regrow the damaged walls. In practice, emphysema is irreversible. The alveoli do not grow back. It is believed that cigarette smoke prevents repair.

    PussyStapler A:

    In general, smoking related lung disease does not heal. After reaching adulthood, we all lose lung function at a steady rate. For those who have smoking related lung disease, they lose lung function more rapidly. If they quit, they lung function generally goes back to declining at the original rate, but doesn't recover.

    Mucosal cilia will grow back, but the amount if air you can effectively move with each breath does not. For those who have emphysematous lung disease rather than COPD, the same thing applies.

    In short, what lung function is lost will not be regained, but quitting smoking may still have benefit in that the rate of lung function decline goes back to normal in most people.

    The problem is most people don't quit until several decades of damage is done.
