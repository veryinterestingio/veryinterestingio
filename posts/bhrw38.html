    Q:

    I have heard of other people who moisturize so often that their skin no longer moisturizes itself enough on its own in the absence of moisturizer as it has become accustomed to the moisturizer's assistance. I am in a similar situation as I have used aquaphor (petrolatum) on my lips constantly for the past few years, and now I can't go much longer than a few hours without it lest my lips become chapped. It's almost as if my lips are more prone to becoming chapped ever since I started constantly moisturizing/protecting them. Why does this happen?

    Aleutienne A:

    There are products that introduce moisture into the skin, like water soaking into cloth, and products that help your skin retain water, like a wax on top of cloth.

    Vaseline/aquphor/petroleum jelly doesn’t introduce moisture to your skin - it sits on top of your skin and helps preserve what is already there. So if your skin is dry, you’re just adding a greasy layer on top, and once it wears off, your skin feels dry again. You need to find an alternate product that actually sinks into your skin and adds moisture and use that before aquaphor.

Burt's Bees lip balm thing is apparently a good thing to try instead. Personally I think there is more to the issue than saying bad things on your lips will harm your lips.

    When you start regular running, your feet build up calluses which makes your feet able to take more exposure to pressure and friction. The same thing happens to your hands as you work with tools like shovels or hammers. Calluses make them less susceptible to damage but it takes time to build them up, and the process of building them can be painful. In college I learned that the feet and hands have what is essentially a special extra layer of skin which I makes that possible. (If I remember that correctly.) Now, maybe you want your lips to be soft, but maybe you also want to let them be tough enough to keep their own moisture without cracking or feeling sore.
