    RHK_Ezze A:

    For small molecules microwave spectroscopy is sufficient. Microwaves excite the rotations of the molecules. From quantum mechanics we know that the possible rotational energies depend on the inertia tensor - from this, the geometry can be calculated.

    For large molecules x-ray christallography is a good method, here you basicially see the molecules themselves.

    Another thing I should add: quantum chemical calculations are accurate enough today for small and medium molecules. If I wanted to know the geometry of let's say acetone, I'd definitely do theoretical calculations. Quicker, easier, and much cheaper than experiments.

    UnclePat79 A:

    With nuclear magnetic resonance it is possible to directly measure the distance between two atoms as well as angular information (e.g. dihedral angles between 4 atoms) based on the magnetic dipole-dipole interactions between nuclear spins. This works irrespective of the size of the molecule/crystal.

    ​

    Since the dipole-dipole interaction between two magnetic moments depends on the inverse cube of their distance, very precise information can be obtained even systems lacking long-range order.

    EdibleBatteries A:

    Bond distances from heavier elements can be measured “directly” using EXAFS (extended x-ray absorption fine structure). Periodic back scattering of emitted core electrons on nearest neighbors leads to wavelength dependent interference, which can be Fourier transformed to a distance domain. Peaks in this distance domain relate to nearest neighbor distances. This requires broadband x-rays, which are only available at synchrotron sources.
