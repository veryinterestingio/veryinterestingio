  • Physics Why does light diffused from laser (pointer) look grainy, but other sources look smooth?

    NEW
    Q:

    I'm a physics phd, and playing with my laser pointer today, pointing at objects that diffuse the light made me remember this question again. Why does this happen? I assume this is some coherence effect, but I can't come up with any train of thought into why all diffused light from laser pointers have the same grainy noisy effect while uncoherent light never displays this characteristic.

    EDIT: Confused the words collimation and coherence

    I_Cant_Logoff A:

    It's not an effect of collimation, it's an interference effect from coherent monochromatic light. It's called a speckle pattern and it appears when the light is reflected either off a surface or scattered by some medium.

    Kurai_Kiba A:

    When a laser beam of monochromatic light strikes the surface of something, some portion of the light will be reflected, depending on the object it hits. At the micro/ nanoscale , all things are “rough” and have variations in their structure which are greater than the wavelength of visible light . Polished surfaces with variations in their surface of less than few hundred nanometres would not show a speckle pattern for example .

    The reason the speckle arises is that these variations alter the relative phases of the reflected light beams by more than 2pi, and according to diffraction theory, each component of this reflected field acts as its own new point source of light of spherical waves. These all interfere with each other to produce a complex random interference pattern which you observe when you look at the reflected laser beam.
