    Q:

    Is it actually possible to create artificial gravity in a spacecraft through a spinning fuselage?

    zekromNLR A:

    Yes, it is. The problem with doing it in practice is that, in order to produce earth gravity without spinning so fast that everybody gets sick, you need a very large radius of rotation - on the order of hundreds of meters, far larger than anything we have put into space so far.

    The basic principle has already been proven though, beyond the fact that centrifuges used in research and industry rely on the same physics. The Skylab space station had a jogging track along part of its inside wall, and the astronauts were able to move on it fast enough to provide sufficient centrifugal force to keep them on that wall (from the ~10 rpm rotation rate in that clip, and the 6.6 m diameter of Skylab, the astronauts would be experiencing about 0.37 g at their feet and about 0.17 g at their heads).

    bencbartlett A:

    Yes. The equivalence principle states (loosely) that gravity and acceleration are indistinguishable by a local observer. If you launched Apple's new headquarters, which has a radius of 230.5m, into space, you could generate Earth's gravitational acceleration by spinning it with a period of about 30 seconds.
