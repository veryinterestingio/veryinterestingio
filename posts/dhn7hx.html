    Hattix A:

    To get pithy a little (it's Venus from Mercury if we use a strict definition) and relax the requirements to include moons, it's Jupiter from Metis. Jupiter would subtend 68 degrees, covering 18% of the sky and would be 4% as bright as the Sun. That's enough light to read by.

    Edit: No it isn't! The brightest body (not the Sun) from the surface of any other is Mars from Phobos. It subtends 42 degrees, and is 2,500x the brightness of the full moon from Earth.

    In terms of pure surface brightness, Earth from the Moon takes it. Earth is much larger than the Moon and has a much higher albedo. Earth is 1.9 degrees across and fifty times brighter than viewing the Moon from Earth.

    Earth from Venus is another great contender, but you have to be in Venusian orbit, as its atmosphere is opaque. Earth is magnitude -6.6 at opposition, and is accompanied by a -2.7 moon. Add these and you don't break -7.0 but do come close.
