    Q:

    I'm not fully informed on the topic, but to my understanding, EU banned certain pesticides and the US have not.

    What's the 2021 situation on pollinators in the EU vs the US?

    NickWarrenPhD A:

    Neonicotinoid pesticides are seen as the most problematic for bees. The EU banned the use of neonicotinoids on pollenator-attracting crops in 2013, and in open fields in 2018. However, there have been some significant loopholes, thus the risks to bees are persisting, but the population-level benefits of the bans are unclear. I unfortunately haven't seen post-2018 data on bee populations in the EU.

    Botanybert A:

    Pesticides are probably the least important factor in honey bee colony collapse. The use of neonics isn’t well correlated with poor survival of honey bees. Eg, in Canada the numbers of bee hives increased greatly after neonics were introduced. They continue to be used in Canada. You can download data for hive numbers from FAOSTAT.

    The science has become highly politicized, which makes things more difficult to judge. But there’s some funny stuff. See this Science article. Both positive and negative effects are observed in hives near neonic treated fields. Note Fig. 2 - the country with the most positive effects has the x axis compressed two fold!

    https://science.sciencemag.org/content/356/6345/1393

    Also see Prof Lin Field’s take on the subject

    Geeky_Nick A:

    I'm not up to date on the latest ecology data so I can't weigh in on that. But regarding the EU versus US restrictions (and more generally) it is worth knowing that these are on some level political decisions.

    The ag industry do a lot of testing to evaluate the ecological impacts of pesticides. However that's just data at the end of the day. How you interpret that and weigh it up against other factors is much more complicated.

    Glyphosate usage in the US is a particularly interesting case. Proving or disproving a link between a particular compound and long term, multi factor conditions such as cancer is quite difficult. At that stage you are more in the realm of epidemiology than chemistry.
