    itdood A:

    Some oil wells also produce natural gas (ng) as a byproduct when the area is tapped with a drill. This NG is highly flammable. It is either captured for distribution or it is burned off. I don’t know about this well but they were capturing the NG and sending it through a pipeline. Perhaps the well was a NG primary purpose well or it was a byproduct of oil capture. Either way they were piping the NG back to either an onshore or nearshore facility. The pipe broke and vented the gas. There was an oil rig very close by. This occurred during a thunderstorm. Either the ignition source was from a spark on the oil well close by or lightning from the storm. As the gas continued to bubble up from the pipe it burned. The gas could have bubbled closer to the oil rig so the spraying of water was just a precaution to keep it away from the oil rig. The fire was put out by turning off a valve on the pipe and stopping the flow of the NG.

    whereisthenutella A:

    Besides what has already been said, about the ignition source, the means to put it out is to just close the isolation valves. However given the distances involved it takes some time for the gas to stop flowing once you cut the supply.

    These isolation valves are remotely operated either pneumatic or hydraulic and will close once a emergency shutdown is initiated or if the air supply/power is cut.

    Frequently on many occasions if the pipeline is closed on one end, due to some emergency then it's automatically closed on the other end as well. Although this might not have happened automatically here it will have once emergency was initiated.

    The nearest isolation valve might be on the top of the platform or at the other end of the pipeline or even underwater if the pipeline is very long.

    The boats are there to mainly intervene in case the wind changes direction and the fire gets routed towards the platform structure not actually put the fire out.
