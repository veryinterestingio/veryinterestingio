    Q:

    From my little knowledge about biology I know vaccines are given before you get infected with a disease so that your body already has the respective antibodies in case you do get infected. Are there any vaccines that can be given when the person is ill already? To help maybe boost the process of making antibodies. Additionally can the COVID-19 vaccine be given to people already suffering from the virus? Will there be any ill effects?

    Niccolo101 A:

    For the most part, not really. As u/captglasspac says, post-infection administration of a vaccine may encourage a faster immune response.

    There is one very notable exception - rabies. Because the rabies virus has a really long incubation period (around 12 weeks), post-exposure vaccination is an effective treatment against the only disease with a mortality rate of over 99% if left untreated. Check the CDC statement.

    ... Actually, there is no other known treatment for rabies. It's a scary disease.

    mfb- A:

    The Rabies vaccine still works if it's given soon after an infection - but it needs to be given before the virus reaches the brain.

    Looked around a bit more: In mice, an Ebola vaccine works up to a day after infection.

    Note that these are relative to the infection time, not the time when people (or mice) feel ill.
