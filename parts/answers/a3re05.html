kmoonster A:

They can't sell their product, BUT because farming as a business is prone to this "wipe out in one event" type stuff on a regular basis AND it is critical to society AND your average farmer only gets one or two crops a year; the Federal government carries a variety of subsidies. You spend a year or so of work getting your stuff done only to have it all wiped out by a single event.

Hail, tornadoes, too much rain [or too little], floods, plant disease, human health/contamination, insect swarms...It's easy for a farm to lose an entire year's effort in one single event, and if that happens twice in ten or fifteen years you can go bankrupt very easily. A store or a restaurant that is gutted by fire can re-open and be selling again in a couple weeks. A farm that is wiped out has to literally start from the ground up AFTER they wait until next growing season to start.

Today a lot of "farms" are corporate and held by a company that owns properties all over the place, so there is less risk to the company as a whole, but that doesn't bring the risk down anywhere close to what it is for other non-farm businesses.

The subsidies vary a lot on type and amount, but they do exist and for good reason. If our farms are constantly fighting bankruptcy it makes grocery costs much more volatile.

edit: in the US the legislation in question is the Farm Bill, though I'm sure other countries have similar measures

edit2: insurance may also be an option for a lot of farmers.
