Humans have been making bread considerably longer than that.

Only 0.7%-1% of people actually have some form of gluten intolerance which at worst causes mild discomfort or diarrhea like symptoms, signifcantly fewer have full blown celiac disease in which gluten is a serious problem.

Much of the rise of the fear of gluten in the past decade is complete nonsense. The vast majority of people eating gluten free food have no intolerance for it and there is no scientific evidence that gluten causes weight gain.

Like many other allergies there are several theories.

  • It's likely that they have been common place for thousands of years but it's only now that we understand and diagnose them. Childhood death rates have dropped drastically in the past 100 or so years and part of that is identifying and treating seriously allergies in children.

  • Cross breeding and creating 'super wheat' and other plants, or mass produced industrial food may have unintended side effects. But the jury is still out on that one. There is no significant evidence at this time that GMOs are harmful in any way.

  • There are studies trying to link the rise hygiene and allergies. anti-bacterial chemicals and drugs, baby wipes, hand cleanser and other products may be having unintended side effects to human development. Much of it linked of all things to gut bacteria.

barbarian818 A:

An important consideration is that modern grains used to make breads and pastas are much higher in gluten content than the strains used even 60 years ago. Selective breeding, hybridization and optimum growing conditions produce flours higher in gluten. Modern breads use higher gluten to produce doughs that rise further and more consistently. So the mixing and baking techniques are all aimed at making the most of that fact.

Another factor is that modern serving sizes tend to be bigger than what our ancestors were accustomed to. (Especially in North America) So total carb intake is higher.

Finally; one theory about the increase in gluten sensitivity (which is lower than the public thinks) is that the changes in our intestinal flora by having so much meat and processed sugars in our diets have sacrificed gluten digestibility. Basically, if we have so much readily digested sugars, simple carbs and proteins, the gut doesn't need to break down gluten and other complex carbs for the intestinal bacteria to thrive.

There are several theories about why gluten intolerance is on the rise.

1 - We don't live with the same bacteria, worms, etc. that we evolved with. While these may seem like undesirable things, in underdeveloped countries rates of allergic reactions to certain foods/intolerance to certain foods has been markedly lower than in developed countries, and a lot of that is because we've become used to clean environments and clean food where these useful bacteria are just absent.

2 - There's just a lot more wheat in our modern diets than what our ancestors ate. A researcher of the USDA claimed that the US has tripled its amount of wheat gluten consumption in the past 40 years, and the thought is that it is just too much and it is overwhelming our body's ability to digest it.

3 - Antibiotics are being prescribed more and more, even when they aren't really needed. These are generic kill-all medicines that also destroy healthy microbes in our digestive system, which could impact our ability to process wheat.

4 - The theory that the rise in gluten intolerance is more or less a hoax or over-exaggerated issue, and that people are quick to claim they are gluten intolerant or even possibly be diagnosed as such even when they're not.
