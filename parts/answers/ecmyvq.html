To get rhe answer you are looking for, you'd have to ignore gravity, the massive energy that would be released as heat as the earth slammed into air at 110k km/h (I think that's our linear speed), ignore the effects of the spin of the earth, etc.

There's an excellent comment already which kinda explains what would happen if you didn't ignore those things.

But if we assume the earth is just as massive smooth ball in standard sea level air, and classic fluid mechanics still works... It's complicated.

Here's a primer on drag on a sphere...

https://www.grc.nasa.gov/www/k-12/airplane/dragsphere.html

The upshot is thst the drag has to be determined experimentally. And since we would need to know the drag as the speed dropped.... This becomes difficult if that drag Vs speed number is not easily fit to some polynomial we can integrate.

But to get an idea where we are in that chart,

  • Air viscosity: 20 x10e-6 kg/ms
  • Air density: 1.225 kg/m3
  • Velocity: 110,000,000 m/h
  • Distance: using diameter: 12,700,000m

The Re number is roughly 2E+16.

I searched for data on "High" Reynolds numbers to see what's out there, and NASA has data... Up to about 10E+7.

So the truth is that we probably can't even estimate what would happen in our overly simplified case. I imagine someone has a theoretical model for this, but nothing that would go that high, because we're already beyond the bounds of std physics.
