Unique_username1 A:

Not only can birds eat them— and they do— birds are better at spreading the seeds. They don’t chew and destroy them, and they poop them out over a large area because they fly.

So capsaicin is thought to help the peppers proliferate because stopping mammals from eating them leaves more available for birds to eat, and they’re the ones that help the plant spread the most.

Of course humans got around this by deciding they liked eating the peppers anyways, but on the other hand being domesticated by humans is a huge win in terms of the plant being protected, nurtured, and spread to a wider area, so I’d argue it was an excellent evolutionary trait.

This is why birdseed may have capsaicin added to it. It's for bird feeders that might be in a place that could get raided by squirrels. The squirrels will feel the heat and be deterred, whereas the birds won't care, and be able to comfortably eat the birdseed.
