tealchameleon A:

Someone mentioned binding water and fats together and what happens is this:

True ELI5: Imagine the egg yolk as a piece of fabric but one side is the rough side of a piece of velcro and the other side is the soft side of a piece of velcro. One side binds to water really well and the other binds to fats and when we mix up the egg into our recipes, we can bind to both fats and water at the same time. If we dont use eggs to bind them, they repel each other and we end up with a much less dense product that is often tougher and doesn't have as nice of a texture.

ELI12: Egg yolks contain phospholipids and the phospholipids in egg yolks have hydrophilic (water-loving) heads and hydrophobic (water-repelling) heads and together they work together to bind water and oils/fats together to create a lighter more flavorful dish.

Eggs are amazing, they foam (increase volume/rising agent ie. Pavlova, souffle),

coagulate (when heated over 60degrees Celsius begins to set, perfect for creme brule),

and emulsify (lecithin in egg allows two liquids, that usually separate, hold together ie. The oil and vinegar/mustard in a mayonnaise) Which has many many uses in cooking/baking

isabelladangelo A:

There are other things you can use other than eggs as a binder. Using apples - peeled, sliced, boiled for 10 minutes, and then pureed- works really well if you know someone that has an egg allergy. Bananas work as well - you just mash them- but they make everything taste like...banana. Works well in chocolate chip cookies or in a chocolate cake but not much else. Apples just add a hint of sweetness but not an "apple" taste.
