MusicBandFanAccount A:

The voice is a harmonic sound, meaning it produces frequencies in a harmonic series. Lots of sounds are harmonic, so we've learned to hear the entire harmonic series as "one sound", since it all probably comes from a single sound source, and the pitch is the frequency the series starts on (also called the fundamental frequency.)

itsmemarcot A:

Very few sounds have a single pitch. Maybe monotonous "beeps" from a computer, but that's it. Most other sounds, including by instuments and voices, are a moltitude of frequencies added together. The pitch you perceive comes from the dominant one, the strongest (loudest) in the bunch.

In harmonious sounds, most other frequencies are multiples of that dominating frequency. In cacophonic (bad sounding) sounds, that's not the case.

(This, by the way, is what makes a note from, say, a violin different from the same note from a trumpet. If a sound was just a frequency, they would be the same.)

The note we hear is described as the "fundamental pitch." There are multiple overtones that sound along with the fundamental that end up giving the sound its unique timbre. A flute sounds like a flute because it has few overtones. A saxophone sounds like two trains crashing because it has many overtones.

The pitch we perceive is usually the one with the most power. You can view this in a spectrogram, where the brightest lines are the perceived pitches. When the harmonic series of a fundamental tone is played without that tone, we still perceive that tone because our auditory systems fill in the gap.

Different frequencies are what gives everything "timbre". I.e, it is what makes a guitar sound like a guitar, a flute a flute, and Bob like Bob. The base, root frequency is what we perceive when we hear a "pitch". But it is the unique combination of harmonics on top of this frequency that makes things sound differently from each other. A pure sine wave frequency, which can be produced on a computer, sounds very robotic without any characteristic
