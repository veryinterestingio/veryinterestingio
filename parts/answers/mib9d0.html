Back in the days of CRT monitors we had a problem called burn-in where if you left the screen on the same image for too long it could get burned into the screen permanently as a shadow.

From then on you would always see the outline of that image or text burned into the screen.

Screen savers as the name implies prevented this by making most of the screen black and have pictures or moving lines to constantly have something changing on the screen.

Nowadays with LCDs this can still happen but to a much lesser extent. Instead Monitors will typically turn themselves off automatically after a certain period of inactivity to prevent burn in from happening.

So why do we still have screen savers? It's mostly out of habit, people expect to have a screen saver on a computer because it's one of those things we expect.

In the enterprise it doubles as the locking mechanism to automatically lock machines if users step away from them for too long.

Belazael A:

Originally it was designed to prevent damage to old monitors. If old monitors sat on one image unchanging for extended periods of time, it would result in phosphor burn-in (basically discoloration) on the display.

Newer monitors fixed that issue, and now it basically works as a privacy measure for when you’re away from your computer so other people can’t just walk by and read everything.
