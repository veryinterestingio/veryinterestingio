My rule of thumb is how many bonds are you breaking vs how many are you making, given that breaking bonds will consume energy and making bonds will release it. Hess's Law is what you'd use to get exact numbers, but you can get a very rough ballpark by knowing the general strength of certain commom bonds

Expert physical chemists can estimate enthalpy change for most reactions quite quickly and accurately. If you memorize the approximate bond energies for all common bonds and roughly calculate enthalpy of bonds broken minus bonds formed, it's not too tough.

KonstantinGr A:

Use Hartree-Fock approximations to give an estimate of bond energies if you know the reactants and products, and take the difference.

You need experimental data to determine the bond structure, but no need to directly measure it.
