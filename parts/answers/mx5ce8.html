It's actually not a very easy infection vector. Spread by respiratory droplets or through contaminated water is an easy infection vector, requiring one animal to have to actually get physically bitten by another is actually a pretty difficult infection vector, since most animals will go out of their way to not get bitten. Furthermore, in any aggressive alteraction there's a significant chance that either the rabid animal or the bitten animal will die outright of other causes, which reduces further spread.

It makes more sense to approach this backwards: you know that rabies has a pretty stable prevalence since it still exists (i.e. it is in some kind of equilibrium), it takes between 2 and 10 days to kill its host, and it is effectively 100% fatal. Based on this knowledge, you could model the infection rate of rabies using a Kolmogorov equation.

The answer is that it is contained by being exactly as infectious as it needs to be in order to be contained. Overly-infectious strains theoretically kill all their hosts and die out.
