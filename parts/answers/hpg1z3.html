man773 A:

tl;dr: no and no.

longer version:

more likely to destroy any kind of pathogen?

Your body has two kinds of immune responses. One could be considered as the front lines defense and fights anything it thinks shouldn't be there. Think fevers. The other is more like special forces that goes after a specific target. When you hear about antibodies, you're hearing about the second one. This second response learns as it goes; when you encounter a new pathogen, it takes time for this system to learn how to recognize and fight it.

A vaccine prepares the body to be ready to execute the second type of response faster, so it can hit the ground running when you come across that specific pathogen. The first will generally react as soon as it recognizes that there's a threat in general and doesn't need to be primed in the same way, so there's no real vaccines to help it.

So no, it will only prepare the body to destroy one kind of pathogen.

​

does my immune system become more active for a short time

Sometimes, but not in the way that you think. When you feel sore after getting your flu shot, that's your immune system responding to the pathogens in the vaccine. The first immune response activates because it detects a threat, but once the second response removes it, it settles down. It's not active in a way that would make it strategic to take several vaccines to keep your immune system at the ready.

The effects of the vaccine on the second immune response; however, lasts for a very long time. In fact, the main reason you need to get a flu shot every year isn't because last year's wore off, it's because the flu virus has mutated again. If you ever come across that previous strain again, your body would be ready and you'd probably never even know about it.
