Back in the day, a person's last name was used to identify their occupation. Over time, the last names shortened.

Blacksmith ( a person who makes and repairs things in iron by hand.) -> Black.

Brownsmith ( deals with brass and copper.) -> Brown.

Whitesmith ( a person who makes articles out of metal, especially tin.) -> White.

There's no such thing as an Orangesmith.

CriticallyAlmost A:

Most last names come from people who had never thought of themselves as having a last name being asked to pick one. When asked to make up a last name, people typically choose an occupation, or some defining feature of their appearance or location.

Black likely comes from Blacksmith

White from Whitesmith, someone who works with tin

Gray and brown come from hair colours

Green likely comes from a variety of different sources, perhaps someone lived in the village green, or a has a penchant for green clothing.

Blue didn't really feature in people's lives much back then, so wouldn't have been good way to identify someone.

And "orange" wasn't even a word in old English, around the time most last names were defined.
