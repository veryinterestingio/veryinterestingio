The recipient is also a factor; what is their overall health, do they take their meds when required, and diet are also factors.

CalEPygous A:

Probably the most useful way to answer this question is to look by organ.

For liver the 18 year survival after transplant was 59% compared to the actuarially expected value of 49%. However these numbers are hugely influenced by a) reason for transplant (malignancies do much worse than cirrhosis); b) age of recipient (younger is better); c) when transplant was performed (recent is better).

For heart, most people are very sick and the 1-year survival rate is about 90%, the 5-year rate is about 70%, but only about 20% survive 20 years or longer.

For kidney the average transplant only lasts 10-15 years. Selena Gomez already needs a new kidney after only about a year.

Therefore, the issue is that the age of the organ is almost irrelevant since most people end up surviving less time than the actuarial tables would suggest from a healthy donor (i.e. if you were donating at 30, you would have expected to live to close to 80 and the organ recipient is not living 50 years with your organ). This is largely because people getting organ donations are pretty sick already and so have shorter life expectancies. As pointed out by u/d0rf47 the liver is unique. There has actually been a successful liver transplant from a 92 year old donor.
