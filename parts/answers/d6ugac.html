They're programmed by their genetics to respond to the prevailing weather conditions.

Shtercus A:

Abundant foliage (whether evergreen or deciduous) has two main drawbacks:

  1. it "costs" the tree energy to keep alive. During summer months, the energy cost of having leaves is more than balanced by the energy from photosynthesis with sunlight (or artificial lights etc) and so provides a net benefit. However during winter, blight, drought etc the leaves are more a drain than a benefit.

  2. thick leaves/needles etc also provide a larger sail area, which during heavy winter winds can cause the tree to be uprooted.

Way back in evolutionary history, a freak plant* had a weird mutation that let it reduce its leafage (or lose it entirely in deciduous varieties) - luckily enough, this proved beneficial and the plant survived through more winters, allowing it to drop more seeds and multiply, sharing this mutation with its offspring. As plants with this mutation proved more able to survive than other plants, they rapidly grew to dominate the plant world.

Today, the hormone auxin has been identified as the trigger for plants to lose their leaves in autumn, which they then stop producing in spring, and starting the regrowth.

This mutation also has an energy cost, so plants whose environments don't change substantially between seasons are less likely to exhibit this behaviour than plants that are native to higher latitudes.

*more than one probably, not sure there is a tree version of adam and eve!
