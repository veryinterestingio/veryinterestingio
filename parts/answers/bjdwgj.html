Chicxulub crater is 150 km in diameter, or about 17671sq/km. If we assume every bit of atmosphere in this diameter was totally lost to space (I doubt that's a good assumption but we'll roll with it), that's 17671/510,100,000 (the surface area of the earth) or 0.000035 of the atmosphere. Or 0.0035% drop in atmospheric volume. So I think it's safe to say there would be an insignificant drop in air pressure, even if 100x that much air was lost you'd see less than a 1% change in the atmosphere.

"Blasted into space" is an interesting phrase, because most things, if hurled into space by some natural process, will still fall back down (because accellerating large masses above escape velocity is hard), and that includes air.
