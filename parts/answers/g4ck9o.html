lifestream87 A:

So to answer your question directly, from what I understand the 1918 Spanish Flu eventually mutated to be less virulent in that the Infection Fatality Ratio dropped from what people think to be in the 3% to far lower. It became the primary strain for seasonal flu until the 1957 Flu Pandemic. That strain subsequently became the dominant strain. This is why people freak out when new flu strains develop - the new strains have potential to be more lethal than a strain that's been with us for a while because we've developed some level of resistance to the old strain vs. a new strain. To put into context the 1957 Pandemic killed approx. 1-2million worldwide & 70,000-116,000 in the USA. https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/1957%E2%80%9358_influenza_pandemic

Infectious Disease Epidemiologists are hoping the disease gets less virulent over time. I have read that the basis for many diseases we have are hundreds of years old, like the common cold, and some believe this could have started as a much stronger illness that eventually lost its virulence and became endemic.

I don't think anyone is sure about the seasonality aspect but it's possible. The issue with the novel coronavirus is that it's novel, so it's really hard to say what will happen with it. There are too many variables that rely on human factors, but those are some thoughts on the topic.
