It's a logical fallacy because it allows you to evade the argument by defining it away.

Let's say we have an argument. I say that it's okay to wear underwear under a kilt if you feel more comfortable that way. You say that it isn't, that in Scottish culture it's completely unacceptable, and nobody would wear underwear under their kilt. I respond with an example, maybe many examples, of Scottish people who say they wear underwear under their kilts, and as a counter argument you say no true Scotsman wears underwear under a kilt.

The problem here is essentially that you have now redefined the word Scotsman. It no longer means a Scottish person, or perhaps a Scottish man. Now it means a Scottish person who doesn't wear underwear under their kilt. But that's at odds with the common understanding of what a Scotsman is. and you only came up with this definition because I provided examples that ran counter to your argument. if somebody is allowed to redefine what words mean in the middle of an argument, the other party can never win, because they will never be able to meet the shifting definition of whatever word is at issue.

Chlorophilia A:

The "No True Scotsman" fallacy is not a logical fallacy. It's what's known as an informal fallacy, which is essentially a problem with reasoning rather than logic.

This fallacy occurs when somebody arbitrarily defines a group to support their argument, rather than on the basis of the generally accepted definition of that group. For example:

Person 1: All Scotsmen love Haggis

Person 2: I'm a Scotsman and I don't love Haggis

Person 1: You're not a true Scotsman then

Person 1 has therefore defined "loving Haggis" as a condition for being a Scotsman, which means their argument is irrefutable. This is fallacious because, of course, "loving Haggis" is not generally regarded as a condition for being a Scotsman. But note that this isn't a logical error. If you define "loving Haggis" as being a condition for being a Scotsman (which Person 1 is doing), then if Person 2 does not like Haggis, Person 1 is correct that they are not a true Scotsman under their definition. It isn't a fallacy due to logic, it's a fallacy because Person 1 is arbitrarily redefining a group to make their argument irrefutable.

This raises an important point though - since definitions are often subjective, the "No True Scotsman" fallacy is also subjective to some extent. How subjective it is depends on how well defined the group in discussion is. The example above is fairly uncontroversial - very few people would agree that "loving Haggis" defines a Scotsman. But consider this example from the Wikipedia article of the fallacy:

Person 1: No pacifist stabs people to death

Person 2: I know a pacifist who stabbed someone to death

Person 1: They're not a true pacifist

This may not be fallacious because most people would agree that "not stabbing people to death" is a condition for being a pacifist (but even that is potentially subjective).

It's therefore important to be careful with this fallacy, because accusations of this fallacy can often themselves be fallacious. Without wanting to get into an argument, the example /u/blindeey chose is a poor choice to illustrate this fallacy because what defines a "Christian" is itself a highly debatable question (even though they may not agree). The "No True Scotsman" fallacy only works with groupings that are very well defined.
