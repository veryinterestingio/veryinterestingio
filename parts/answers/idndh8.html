caturday_drone A:

Antibiotics treat an infection. When you start feeling better, you know they're working but likely not all the germs have been killed yet. If you stop before the end of the course, you leave a few alive, which your body can probably/usually fight off okay. Those few bugs left behind were the ones most resistant to the antibiotics and they're the ones that will multiply. Sometimes people get a second infection because of this, or they pass it to someone else. Every time this happens, the drugs have less of an effect because the infection was built off the strongest bodies of the last infection.

Eventually, this leads to the "superbug" problem where no antibiotics will treat a particular strain of infection effectively anymore.

white_nerdy A:

Antibiotics kill germs.

When you get sick, some of the germs making you sick might be lucky. In the sense that the lucky germs have genetics that make them able to somewhat resist antibiotics.

When you feel better, 90% of the germs are dead and 10% survive.

  • Keep taking the antibiotics: Eventually 100% of the germs will be dead and you will be cured.
  • Stop taking the antibiotics: The 10% survivor germs breed back up to 100% numbers and make you sick again. Since the lucky germs are more likely to be in the survivor group, they pass on their lucky genes to large numbers of children / grandchildren / great-grandchildren germs. And some of those descendants might have even luckier genetics, and antibiotics might not work on them at all.

Taking all your antibiotics makes you better.

Stopping early risks you getting sick again from the survivor germs. And it also risks you breeding resistant germs that antibiotics can't fight. That's a serious problem, both for you and for anyone you pass your germs on to.

You should always take the full course of any antibiotic.
