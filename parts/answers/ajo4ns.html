GESTICULATING_WILDLY A:

There's one interesting aspect I haven't seen mentioned. If you remember enough of your high school chemistry class, you probably remember learning the Pauli Exclusion Principle - when electrons take their places around the nucleus of an atom, they show up in pairs, and always with opposing spins. Each slot around the electron, called an orbital, fills with one pair of electrons, and no more can fit because no two can have the same quantum numbers. Muons can slide in as extra members of the same slot, though, because they have different numbers by default, so they don't really "notice" the electrons and just go wherever they want, but they have identical charges so are still attracted to the nucleus.

You might be thinking that this could have all kinds of cool implications for chemistry, and you would've been right, but muons live for a very short time - on average, their lifetime is only around 2 microseconds. That's actually a long time for a particle, but not enough to use for any chemical applications that I know of. There's a lower-energy configuration that can conserve the quantum numbers of a muon, and if a particle has that opportunity, it'll take it. The only reason electrons don't do this too is that no lower-energy options conserve their charge.
