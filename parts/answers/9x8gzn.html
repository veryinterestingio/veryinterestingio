JakobWulfkind A:

Most water has oxygen, carbon dioxide, and other gases dissolved into it, but its ability to hold these gases decrease as its temperature goes up, so the bubbling you hear before the boiling begins is all of those gases being liberated from the water and forming bubbles that float to the surface and then pop. Near-boiling water has no more dissolved gas and thus the bubbling stops.

If you used distilled water to make tea, it wouldn't bubble until it actually boiled, but I strongly recommend against this, as it could potentially not boil and instead become superheated and explosively evaporate.
