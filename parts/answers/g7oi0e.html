The Radio Data System (RDS) uses a frequency modulated wave to carry a small amount of information along with the primary carrier wave without interfering with the analogue broadcast. Your radio decodes this digital information if it can, and if it can't, it doesn't even know it's there.

narcoleptic_kitty A:

The text that shows up on your screen is digital data, just like this comment you're reading. Your computer knows how to decode those 1s and 0s into text for you to read.

Radio stations use clever techniques to transmit this digital data along with the analog signal. For your car radio it's just two signals. If your radio understands digital encoding, it will know the text is there and display it. If it doesn't then it won't even notice the text.

zap_p25 A:

FM works on a concept called deviation. Unlike AM, (which modulates the signal's amplitude thus changing the power levels) FM works by broadening the width of the transmission (overall transmitted power stays the same). In the land mobile radio industry (commercial two-way radio) the maximum modulated frequency is typically around 3,000 Hz meaning that the information (audio or data) of a FM signal can be +/- 3 kHz from the center frequency. With broadcast radio, maximum deviation is 75,000 Hz so information can be encoded +/- 75 kHz.

If you generate a 1,000 Hz tone into a FM transmitter, you can analyze the signal (in the frequency domain) and see the bandwidth +/- 1,000 Hz in the spectrum. The big thing that makes this all work, humans can hear approximately 3,00-24,000 Hz. Anything generated above or below that we can't typically hear. Old LMR systems used to send slow speed data ~(600 bps or less) below the audible range of human hearing (sub-audible data). RDS works the other way, by sending it above the range of human hearing (47 kHz). You should be able to identify these blips on a spectrum analyzer. On a spectrum analyzer, flat signals on the outermost limits of the broadcast channel will also typically suggest the radio station is running HD digital as well.
