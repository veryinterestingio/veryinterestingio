This paper from 1982 (!) looked at childhood development of (traditional) gender identity and how it related to gendered language, specifically comparing English & Finnish (non-gendered language) with Hebrew (strongly-gendered language). They found that children attained gender identity earlier in Israel than in the US and Finland. That result implies that it is at least plausible that the native language spoken by individuals could impact gender identity in adults as well.

Guiora, A.Z., Beit-Hallahmi, B., Fried, R. and Yoder, C. (1982), LANGUAGE ENVIRONMENT AND GENDER IDENTITY ATTAINMENT1. Language Learning, 32: 289-304. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-1770.1982.tb00973.x

scubasue A:

Seems unlikely to me, given that the presence of gender markers in language isn't correlated with gender equality. The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis (that language shapes thought) is appealing, but it's pretty much disproved except for some very subtle effects right at the edge of detectability. Read 'The Language Hoax' by John McWhorter for more.
