Wormsblink A:

The government being in debt is different from a person being in debt.

The government can issue treasury bonds to investors, in order to raise cash to fund larger programmes. This is recorded as debt, which is different than a person owing money to the bank debt because they overspent.

Government debt does not exist in a vacuum, the government is spending it on something. If the government spending stimulates the economy to grow faster, the increase in tax revenue will offset the debt.

The government does not ever need to pay back the debt, they just need to service the debt (ie pay off interest). If the interest rate on debt is lower than the growth rate of GDP, there is no issue. Even if it exceeds the growth rate temporarily, there is no issue.

Government “shutting down” is a political weapon, not an economic reality. It is also very unique to the US, which for some reason has a manually-set debt ceiling at an arbitrary amount.

ttlyntfake A:

Debt can be a very valuable financial tool.

Suppose you work a crappy job. If you could drive half an hour to the next town, you could get a much better paying job but you don't have a car. You go into debt to get the car and then earn a lot more money. That's a productive debt.

Or if you're a property developer. You want to build an apartment building but don't have fifty million dollars lying around. You go to a bank and borrow the money to build the apartment building, then rent it out. The bank makes money on their loan, income from the apartment building pays for operations and pays back the loan. That's all good for everyone.

So just because the US federal government is in debt, does not mean it ran out of money. It can, and is, repaying the loans exactly on schedule and has never missed a debt payment. It also cannot default unless it chooses to, since it can issue new money (which has other consequences).

People disagree on whether the government is wasting taxpayers money which the government took under threat of violence, or if the government is investing in a productive population and bettering people's lives. Between inflation and GDP growth, the government seems to get a great return. It is super cheap for the US government to borrow money because people trust it, and GDP growth over the past many many decades suggests those were good investments. Also, normal inflation makes it cheaper and cheaper to repay the loans. Again, people disagree - some people say that the productive economy is tied to government spending (something like 20% of GDP, half of all healthcare, infrastructure, etc) and some people say that the economy would be even more robust if the government got out of the way. Or that local governments should be in charge and less centralization.

Your job as a citizen is to learn how these things work, what you think about them, what the trade-offs are, what's true vs who's scamming you for personal gain, and how you think your country should be run - then help make that happen. :-)
