"Price gouging" by definition happens only in a crisis, like a natural disaster. It's taking advantage of a sudden shortage in a critical good. Like if your city's water system blows up, and suddenly somebody starts selling bottled water for $300 a bottle. That's price gouging.

Any other changes in price, in any situation that isn't a crisis, or in any good that's not strictly critical, is not price gouging. It's other changes in price resulting from other changes in supply and demand.

That's not to say that rising prices are never a problem, as long as they aren't price gouging. They could very well be a problem! They could be a result of rampant inflation like in the 1970s, or of a financial bubble like real estate in 2008, or of a company attaining monopoly power in its industry. Those are all very bad market conditions. But they're not "price gouging".

Price Gouging is not the act of raising your price as demand goes up. Price Gouging is the act of drastically raising your price on an essential good or service during a period of emergency when there is little or no competition and/or no time for people to find competition that has price you prefer.

The specifics of what is and is not price gouging is set in law in most States/Countries. Not all agree, but they do typically roughly fit the definition I gave you above. The specific scenario you ask about is not price gouging because it is not a sudden increase in pricing, by an extremely large amount over a short amount of time, it is not on a good or service that is of essential need, and it is not during a time of emergency.

The price of cars and housing is typically a long term supply and demand trend, heavily influenced by the price of money, with a lot of slow moving long term factors influencing the prices.

The recent gas shortage in parts of the US - this is a specific event driven shortage that everyone knows is going to be in place for a very short time, but affects a commodity that a lot of people have an acute need for. Gouging prices for a necessity that is going to be going back to normal prices in a few days is regulated, in no small part to reduce the risk of civil unrest that might develop in response to a very temporary set of circumstances.
