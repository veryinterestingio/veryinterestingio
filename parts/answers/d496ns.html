fiendishrabbit A:

It's because of Glow in the Dark paint.

In 1908 a scientist named Sabin Arnold von Sochocky invented radioluminescent paint. Ie, paint that glows in the dark due to low-level radiation. That paint consists of two components.

  1. Something slightly radioactive. von Sochocky used Radium 226, but today we use tritium (in those cases where we use radioluminescent paint)
  2. Something that glows when exposed to such radiation. The cheapest such paint (and therefor the most common) was Zinc Sulfide with just a tiny bit of copper in it. That paint glows green. Radioactive green. Other slightly more expensive versions can glow blue-green or orange.

So when people needed to show that "This is radioactive!" they automaticly went "Well, it should probably glow like this radioactive paint. Green!"
