darther_mauler A:

The posts talking about the mass of Br are wrong. At the atomic scale, the gravitational forces due to mass are vastly outweighed by the electrical forces.

Let’s look at the trend in boiling point:

Cl2, -100C

Br2, 59C

I2, 184C

HCl, -85C

HBr, -66C

HI, -35C

HCl, HBr, and HI all follow a pretty clear trend. Cl2, Br2, and I2 also follow a pretty clear trend. Both trends are driven by dispersion forces, where the higher amount of dispersion forces (due to more electrons) results in a higher boiling point.

In this trend HCl/Cl2 is actually the odd one out, as it’s the only pair where the acid has a higher boiling point than the diatomic molecule. If we look at the position of Cl, Br, and I on the periodic table, the reason becomes clear: Br and I have electrons in their d-orbitals, which vastly increases the number of electrons around atom, which increases the strength of the dispersion forces due to an increased chance at a spontaneous dipole. It is at Br where the dispersion forces of the diatomic molecule are stronger than the dipole forces of the acid.

If you consider the size difference between Br and H, the question is kind of like asking why Br2 has a higher boiling point than Br. Br2 has twice the chance of a spontaneous dipole than Br does.

So the idea that dispersion forces are generally weaker than a dipole-dipole interaction is true, the case of Br2 and HBr actually breaks away from this trend.

Note: I’ve intentionally left out F2, HF as HF can hydrogen bond.

usernumber36 A:

because your teachers are lying when they say dipole-dipole forces are always stronger than london dispersion forces.

Think about it. I2 is solid at room temperature yet is non-polar, whilst water is liquid. London dispersion forces get stronger when the molecule is (a) larger or (b) more polarisable. Both size and polarisability increase down the periodic table - basically what happens is the electron cloud is bigger, looser and floppier and is more susceptible to forming the temporary or induced dipoles that explain dispersion forces and their strength.

the hydrogen atom is absolutely tiny. What's more, if you're bonding it to a bromine, the hydrogen has less electron density anyway due to the electronegativity difference and the more ionic character of the bond due to poor orbital overlap.

The overall effect of this is that Br2's dispersion forces are stronger than the forces going on between HBr molecules.

EDIT: commented this below, but will copy it here.

The idea that dipole-dipole interactions are generally stronger than dispersion forces is actually false.

What people forget is that *every* molecule can do dispersion forces. The polar ones can just do dipole-dipole PLUS dispersion forces, where a non-polar one only has dispersion. It isn't that dipole-dipole is stronger, it's that it's an *extra* force of attraction on top of what's already there.
