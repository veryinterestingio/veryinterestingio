ExpectedBehaviour A:

The bigger issue for bats is not destroying their own ears when they emit echolocation calls, as these are amongst some of the loudest sounds generated by any animal. This is accomplished by having a well-developed stapedius muscle in their middle ear, which prevents the ossicles (collectively the bones of the middle ear, the malleus, incus and stapes) from moving when tensed, and thus blocks the conduction of sound from the eardrum to the cochlea. Bats have literally evolved selective deafness to protect their own hearing.

What we call sound waves are vibrations or rapid movement of atoms. When you drop a ball it will hit the ground. The ball will vibrate once hitting the ground causing the atoms in the air to also vibrate. These vibrations form waves that travel in all directions through the air, including the air in your ear. Once a wave reaches the ear drum your brain then works it’s magic and process the amplitude and frequency of the sound wave.

Sound waves have 2 main factors: Amplitude and Frequency. Amplitude is basically the volume. Higher amplitude will sound louder and vice versa.

Frequency is the key here though. Frequency AKA pitch(soprano vs bass singer) is the number of waves produced per 1 second. Humans hear frequency at a range of 20-20,000 Hertz. A bat’s frequency range is 1,000-150,000 Hertz.

ELi5: they can hear high frequency sounds that humans can’t.
