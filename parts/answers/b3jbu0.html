byodiftera A:

When a seed is shed from the parent plant, it's in the state of dormancy; contains very little water and is metabolically inactive ( no respiration and thus no energy ), so it doesn't grow. However, when it's put in soil where there's water, it basically absorbs the water which stimulates the production of plant hormone called gibberellin. This hormone, in a series of steps, allows starch to be broken down into maltose. Maltose is then converted to glucose which is respired producing energy; allowing seed to germinate and grow.

Seeds have many different germination triggers. For example, some require scarification ( scratching or thinning of the seed coat). Others are triggered by chemical compounds- for example - chemicals produced by wood smoke are triggers for proteas. Temperature can also be a trigger.
