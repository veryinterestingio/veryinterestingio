Loki-L A:

If you negotiated something before publishing the video with the holder of the copyright to whatever it was you were using, that could be how it worked, if you are caught after using it without permission it isn't.

Think of it like using public transportation without a valid ticket. When you get caught riding the subway/train/tram/whatever without a ticket, they don't make you pay the regular fare price, they make you pay a fine so expensive that it really hurts. If you just had to pay the normal price, there would be no reason for you not to risk it. Making the fine high enough to not be worth the risk is the point.

Copyright claims can get really bizarre.

One of my favorite examples is the song "Bittersweet Symphony" by the Verve. It opens with a very short sample taking from a Rolling Stones record and because the Verve failed to get proper permission, they ended up having to forfeit all profits made from the song to the owner of the Rolling Stones record copyright. The song was a big hit that sold over a million of records and is till being played today and for over two decades the band that made never saw a single penny from it. It was only earlier this year that they got a new deal and finally started seeing some money from it.

Of course just because your work features a short excerpt of another copyrighted work, doesn't automatically mean that you need the owners permission. There are all sorts of exceptions to those rules, but the algorithms checking for copyrighted work on yourtube aren't programmed to understand context and to recognize when something is a parody or other fair use exception.

vomit-gold A:

In an ideal world, yes. and there was a time you could get away with pretty much anything on YouTube unless you were reuploading complete media.

However, around 2007, right after Google bought YouTube from it's original creators, Viacom filed a huge 1 billion dollar lawsuit, just as YouTube was gaining steam. It wasn't YouTube's first lawsuit, and losing to Viacom possibly meant the end of YouTube all together. Viacom accussed that YT was not doing enough to stop copyright infringement. The case settled in 2013.

This was also during the ad boom on YouTube. In order for YT to keep growing they kind of just... gave in. It was easier to let the creator lose out on revenue then getting sued. When something is manually claimed by a company, if the content creator tries to appeal, it's sent to the COMPANY, not YouTube. YouTube wants to stay out of copyright disputes no matter what the cost.
