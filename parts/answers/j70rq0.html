If you've ever seen those "grandmaster plays 100 opponents simultaneously" stories, it's because the past moves and mind games with the opponent aren't much of a factor. They make a move, forget what happened, and go to the next board.

A given board state often has a small handful of "correct" moves, so playing both sides yourself is an exercise in solving board states rather than exploiting an opponent's tendencies.

MiscThoughts123 A:

Chess is a game of perfect information. Both you and your opponent can see the exact same state of the board. Like another post said, there’s usually a number of “correct moves” to make. So theoretically since you have the same information and there’s usually a few “correct moves” you should be able to figure out what your opponent might play even if there is another person playing.

So when you play by yourself, you kind of just attempt to figure out and work through the possible correct moves you can make for either side, similar to how a computer might analyze a chess position.

Contrast this with a game with hidden information like Poker or Stratego, knowing your opponents information completely ruins the dynamic of the game.
