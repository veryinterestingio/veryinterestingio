A lot of people here are talking about more oxygen. This is a good thing for bigger bugs, but for everything else, the answer is actually more related to CO2.

Unlike bugs dinosaurs don’t have their size limited by oxygen because lungs are way better at getting the gas exchange needed. What did limit their size was the availability of food. Dinosaurs weren’t always colossal: coelophysis, one of the earliest dinosaurs, was actually quite small (it was very long for balance but weighed less than a Labrador).

As the age of dinosaurs progressed and CO2 levels rose the amount of plant life exploded as the planet became hotter and wetter. All the extra food meant prey Dinosaurs could bulk the hell up. Now they were getting bigger and could resist predation, right? Sort of.

As prey got bigger to resist predators, predators got bigger to take down prey. This is what’s called an evolutionary arms race. Eventually this reached a point where you have carnivores like Giganautosaurus and herbivores like Argentinosaurus which could have weighed as much as 100 metric tonnes.

Nowadays mammals are the dominant life form and the largest is much smaller. Why? Because mammals have other answers to being hunted than just “getting bigger.” Mammals care for their young, work together, run away or just hide. These strategies require bigger brains but are just as effective

TL:DR/ELI3: there was a ton of food so everyone got bigger. Nowadays we have better ideas than just getting bigger to eat or be eaten

Griffisbored A:

I'm assuming you mean land-based giant animals, because the blue whale is the largest animal in history and can be found swimming today. After the dinosaurs went extinct, other animals evolved to fill the same roles and reached similar sizes to the dino counter-parts. There's multiple reasons there aren't many of these giant land animals around today, but the cause many point to is the rise and dominance of humans.

In the case of large predators, humans most likely started taking such a large share of their food source they were forced into extinction or adaptation. For example, the massive dire wolves were replaced by the smaller grey wolves who require far less food to survive in response to this shortage.

Large herbivores were most likely hunted to extinction by humans as they improved their group hunting tactics and tool use. An example is how species of ground sloths on human-free islands continued to exist long after the ones living on the mainland with humans are believed to have gone extinct.
