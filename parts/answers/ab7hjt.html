Short answer: The magnetic field doesn't have mass; it is a massless field. However, the energy stored in the field is very real and can contribute to the overall effective mass of the star.

Long answer: Recalling general relativity, mass and energy both curve spacetime, meaning that they contribute to the strength of gravitational fields. For example, a charged battery would register as ever so slightly heavier on a scale than an uncharged battery. This doesn't mean the battery is more massive or that there are more particles of matter present, it means something very different. It means the energy stored in it and that energy is gravitating too. This has funny consequences, like a kugelblitz- since light has energy it will curve spacetime, so it would be possible to create a black hole without any matter by just shining enough light in one spot.

A strong magnetic field is a bit like this battery too. So, as you say, the very very strong magnetic field of a neutron star results in an energy density higher than the energy density of matter on earth. That's pretty wild.

For comparison though, a neutron star is about one hundred trillion times the mass density of lead, so even though this magnetic field has an enormous energy density by earth standards (and by objective standards, these are the strongest magnetic fields in the universe), their energy density still pale in comparison to the energy density of neutron star matter.

As for your last question, the magnetic field energy of neutron stars can be released, and such things have been observed. A handful of phenomena have been linked to rapid releases of magnetic energy from neutron stars, such as soft gamma repeaters. Hypothetical events include neutron star merger crust shattering flare, and explanations for fast radio bursts. All these phenomena use the reservoir of energy in the magnetic field to create high energy astrophysical phenomena, like snapping giant magnetic rubberbands.
