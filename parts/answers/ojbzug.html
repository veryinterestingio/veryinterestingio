saywherefore A:

There are (at least) two factors at play. Firstly what you describe is largely a US issue with much less correlation between faith and political leaning in other countries.

Secondly, some of this stems from the conflating of social and economic issues in two-party systems. A high proportion of religious people are socially conservative, especially as a result of religious focus on the importance of family and a certain type of community. In the US that social conservatism also comes with e.g. opposition to gay marriage and some other things that are less prevalent in European Christianity. In modern western democracy social conservatism and economic conservatism (a belief in the free market, desire for small government) go hand in hand.

In theory it is possible to combine social conservatism with big spending on social welfare programmes and suchlike; indeed this is what the current Conservative UK government of Boris Johnson is [somewhat] doing, in contrast to the austerity of previous Conservative administrations.

Edit: I appreciate that the current Tory government is not spending big in all areas, I merely wish to draw a contrast with previous administrations to show that social and economic conservatism do not have to coincide.
