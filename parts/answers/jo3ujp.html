Evolution just sticks with whatever it finds that works. The reason why we stopped at the double blink is twofold:

  • Most situations where you need to close your eyes for protection, you need to close both of them. So the instinct to close both eyes at once in response to a blast of sand in the face of something exists, and it’s what our body uses for lubrication too.
  • So far the genes for a single blink reflex either haven’t appeared by mutation, or they haven’t provided enough reproductive advantage to propagate

makss_f449 A:

From an evolutionary standpoint - blinking with each eye wouldn't benefit an individual any more than blinking with both eyes at once, since blinking takes such a small increment of time, therefore the individual who blinks with both eyes survives and passes on it's genes at the same rate as the one who blinks with one eye, then the other.

From a nervous system point of view - blinking is "automatic" - you don't really do it consciously (most of the time). Also, both of your eyes work in a similar manner - look at the same position, focus on the same object, get exposed to the air for the same amount of time, therefore they get lubricated/moistened at the same time.
