I’m in Houston and currently without power. It has nothing to do with the customers. It’s all about our electrical grid generator sites going down due to sustained freezing temperatures. A large portion of our generation capacity was not properly winterized for that level of cold.

In Texas, especially on the south coast, we never see temperatures like this... not even close to this. These are record setting highs of 17F, when a typical February ranges from 38 to 68 (all above freezing). So, our power plants are designed for a temperature range above freezing. Literally, pipes at the plant are freezing up solid, shutting down the generators.

Also, on top of that, the supply of natural gas comes from Texas oil wells. Most oil wells rely on electricity to pump the wells. Without electricity, those pumps (rod pumps, electric submersible pumps, etc.) can’t run. Now the supply of gas to the power plants is slowing down. Now we have a viscous cycle starting.

So, as you can see, this isn’t an increased demand issue, it’s a lack of supply issue. In fact, heating units use less power than AC. Our demand today is still less than the hottest day in August.

himeylo A:

Our power systems aren’t well suited for the cold, in particular. Texas is experiencing the coldest consecutive days in a century, in large part.

Some folks here have natural gas heating and some have electric heating; both are common. We’re underdressed and cold, so demand for both electrical power and natural gas heat is very high... trying to support all those heaters working overtime, plus all the normal stuff.

Typically, a significantly large portion of our electrical power comes from natural gas.

Right now, a really big chunk of the natural gas supply is being used to support the enormous spike in demand for natural gas heat, leaving much less than what is sufficient to support the also very high demand for electrical power.

Icing on the power shortage cake: Natural gas production is taking a big hit because of freeze-offs. Freeze-offs happen when temps drop low enough to freeze water and other liquids contained within the natural gas mixture, blocking the flow of gas out of the wellhead.

In the summer (and most of the year), we don’t need natural gas for heating, so all of that is available to support our electrical power demands (keeping those power hungry A/Cs running), and natural gas flows just fine in the warmer temps.
