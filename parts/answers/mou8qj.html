CrustalTrudger A:

There is a decent amount of paleo environmental reconstructions for the Beringia land-bridge. From pollen assemblages, we know that there was a relatively varied set of landscapes including woodland, brush, steppe and tundra (e.g., Young, 1982). Especially toward the end of the existence of the landbridge, there was a decent amount of wetland as represented by peats and thermokarst lakes (e.g., Lozhkin et al, 2011). In many ways, these areas would not have been terribly distinct from other arctic environments, though as highlighted in Young, the land bridge area likely had relatively warm summers (for that latitude, during a glacial period at least).

The newly exposed land was moist and fertile, and likely grew new grasses and plants. These new grazing lands attracted heards of grazing animals, which the nomadic people of East Asia were hunting and following.

They might not have even realized they just discovered a new world. They just hunted the heards across the northern Pacific.
