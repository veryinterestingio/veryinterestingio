Coronaviruses aren't new to us. This is just the latest one. It isn't even the deadliest compared to SARS and MERS, it just spreads easier.

But we commonly deal with 4 coronaviruses (HCoV) that cause common cold, these circulate constantly.

We know how these viruses work in general. It isn't really even the virus itself which seems to kill people, but the immune response which is so powerful that it can damage the body whole, and we really don't understand why yet. (Tho research and data about this changes so quickly that you probably can't keep up unless you are infectious disease specialist). E: I'm not downplaying the dangers of the virus, and the virus does play a part in the complicated equation that can be fatal

Maybe Covid-19 can cause deadly complications that come to light in 10 years, only time will tell. But what caused that damage probably wasn't the virus, but our immune system trying to deal with the virus.

This is one of the reasons current treatment is supportive care and steroids. We try to control the immune system. Since it would appear there isn't a antiviral that works on it.

DankNerd97 A:

I know a bit about RNA viruses since I work with parts of them (not live viruses thank God). HIV (the virus responsible for AIDS) is a retrovirus meaning it not only inserts a copy of its RNA into the host cell, but it also alters the genome of the host cell. So it could be in your system for years before symptoms show.

SARS-CoV-2 (the virus responsible for COVID-19) is a coronavirus which is a different family of RNA viruses (and a different order, class, phylum, and kingdom) than retroviruses.
