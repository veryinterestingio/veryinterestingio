GeorgeTheNerd A:

Pulling all the actinides together makes it more proliferation resistant. The vast majority of actinide isotopes are unsuitable for weapons and must be purified out to make a warhead.

Reprocessing has traditionally involved separating out each actinide and thus inadvertently creating a process and a store of material from commercial sources that could be used for weapons.

This process, by not separating all the actinides out prevents that issue. It can't be used to create weapons nor does it create stores of weapons capable material.

But it still separates the fission byproducts. And that is enough to create a product that is useable for commercial plants. It may not be as useful as new enriched uranium, it depends on the mixture. But there are methods that make a full actinide reprocessing possible with light water reactors with enough wait time (50+ years) between use and each reprocessing. (been awhile since I worked with this, will hunt for the paper if you are interested) Something that might actually be feasible as our oldest fuel approaches that age.

Guessing this article. https://phys.org/news/2020-05-reveals-single-step-strategy-recycling-nuclear.html

When in a reactor, uranium is bombarded with neutrons. The uranium both undergoes fission and absorbs neutrons to become heavier atoms (plutonium, neptunium and americium). The fission products undergo further decay, creating more elements and radiation. Some of the elements easily absorb neutrons, greatly decreasing the nuclear reactivity of the fuel.

As a result, when the fuel gets to about 70% uranium, the spent fuel is no longer useful and is classified as waste.

used fuel rods contain fission products, leftover uranium and small quantities of plutonium, neptunium and americium

To reduce the mass of nuclear waste, and recoup the unused fuel portion of the spent fuel, the spent fuel can be processed to separate out the components. However the plutonium in the spent fuel can be used as a nuclear fuel for both reactors and nuclear bombs. To reduce the spread of nuclear bomb material (non-proliferation), regulations are in place to not separate out or enrich plutonium.

This new crystallization process claims to separate the uranium easily to greater than 90% purity without overly enriching plutonium. Basically, the spent fuel is dissolved in nitric acid, and uranium rich crystals are formed and separated from the other elements.

The idea is that this process would separate the U and TRU (transuranics) together. That's what makes it "proliferation resistant" compared to methods that extract nearly pure Pu and U separately. This U+TRU fuel could be burned further in thermal or fast reactors. It's unlikely that such recycled fuel would be cheaper to make than fresh enriched U. The tiny amount of fuel needed to run nuclear power plants is well established.
