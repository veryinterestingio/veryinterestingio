iayork A:

It depends on your definitions of "know exists" and "isolate". To a large extent, advances in genetic sequencing have made traditional "isolations" obsolete. A huge amount of modern virology is done without "isolated" viruses, using sequence as the basis. It's very common to identify a virus from sequence and never bother trying to "isolate" it; or to use alternate molecular approaches (synthetic DNA cloning, protein expression, etc) to study the virus.

That's not to say that isolating a virus (which probably means finding a cell type it will grow on in culture) is not helpful. It opens up a lot of experiments you can do on the virus. But it's much less useful and interesting than it used to be, when isolation was the only way to identify viruses; now, this kind of isolation is just another, fairly clumsy and insensitive, way to look at viruses.

A good example would be the bat influenza viruses. They were first identified in 2012 (A distinct lineage of influenza A virus from bats), based on sequence information. There are probably 100 papers discussing these viruses, their infection patterns, their receptors, etc etc, but they still haven't been traditionally isolated:

Two unusual H17N10 and H18N11 influenza viruses have been found in New World bats. Although neither of these viruses have been isolated, infectious clone technology has permitted significant progress to understand their biology, which include unique features compared to all other known influenza A viruses.

--Bat Influenza Viruses: Current Status and Perspective

I am surprised that the expression metagenomics has not popped up here.

But the answer is, yes. You can take samples (such as a wastewater sample) and analyse all the genomes in the samples (using sequencing), and when you find new genomes you can give them a name, and you can perform phylogenetic analyses to correctly classify them. However, there can be many thousands of different genomes in the samples analysed, and you have not directly observed the species of which you have sequenced the genome. These newly identified species are given the prefix candidatus (Ca.).

Once the candidatus species has been isolated and cultured, it's name can be upgraded to its final form, and the Ca. is dropped.
