veemondumps A:

Most food animals are raised in feed lots which run at 100% capacity and are constantly cycling in new animals as old animals are slaughtered.

You can't really stop the new animals from coming in unless you want to give every animal in your heard an abortion. So your only option is to just continue slaughtering them as normal.

You can't donate the meat because you don't have the facilities to process the meat. Its not like meat comes premade as hamburgers or steaks. It takes a huge amount of work and specialized equipment to turn a cow into a hamburger or a pig into a strip of bacon. Unless you have the machinery to lift a 1500 pound cow you're not going to get very far just having a cow carcass out in a field somewhere.

There are only a handful of meat processing plants in the country that can actually turn a dead animal into useful cuts of meat. If those close - which some have - then there literally is no one who can use the meat so you just have to dump it in a ditch somewhere.

shag377 A:

I have butchered large animals, not cows but bison and elk.

Getting the animal to a location to start processing is difficult. We used a front end loader.

Hoisting a creature that is 1,500 lbs or more is a real chore, and without a decent hoist, it is all but impossible.

So, let's say you have your cow up and hanging. You skin it. What are your plans with the skin? Is there a tannery close that could use it?

Now comes the fun part. You have all sorts of parts - head and the brain, hooves and the offal - lungs, heart, liver, kidneys and the like. Edible, but highly unlikely anyone would want. This, btw, accounts for about 40-60 percent of the carcass.

What to do with this?

Okay. So now we have a hanging carcass. You need to split it. Industrial processors use specialized heavy duty chainsaws. More than likely, you may have a sawsall, and this is not big enough.

Well, we got the carcass split. Now is not the time to break it down. It needs to hang for a while - we went two weeks. Do you have a cooler large enough?

If you cannot see the issues by now, consider one more thing: Even if you manage to get the carcass ready to butcher, you cannot sell any of the meat. It has not been processed in a USDA approved facility.

TL;DR - Breaking down a cow for steaks is much more involved than most think.
