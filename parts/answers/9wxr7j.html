It's not really the same kind of uncertainty relationship, since you are correct that time is not an operator.

Instead think more as a time evolution on states. If a state is an eigenstate of the Hamiltonian, it is stable in time. If a state is not an eigenstate of the Hamiltonian, it can be written as a linear combination of Hamiltonian eigenstates, with a time dependence determined by the difference in energy levels from the spectrum of the Hamiltonian. It is from this time dependence that the time energy uncertainty comes from.

CentiMaga A:

Because it's not really a time-energy relation.

Uncertainty principle

The fundamental form of the uncertainty principle is:

  1. σ_A^(2) σ_B^(2) ≥ (〈[Â,ˆB]〉/(2i))^(2)

(Because the commutator of two Hermitian operators is anti-Hermitian, both sides are always real and positive.) Substituting [x̂, p̂] = iħ gives the familiar position-momentum form, σ_x σ_p ≥ ħ/2. Or with sloppy notation, ∆x ∆p ≥ ħ/2.

The "time-energy" form

The "time-energy" relation is obtained by picking A=H and B=Q, where H is the Hamiltonian (think "total energy") and Q is any generic observable. So,

  1. σ_H^(2) σ_Q^(2) ≥ (〈[Ĥ,ˆQ]〉/(2i))^(2)

However because "i ħ ∂Ψ/∂t = ĤΨ", it follows that "d〈Q〉/dt = i ħ 〈[Ĥ,ˆQ]〉 + 〈∂ˆQ/∂t〉", and the above simplifies to:

  1. σ_H^(2) σ_Q^(2) ≥ ((ħ/i) d〈Q〉/dt/(2i))^(2) = (ħ/2)^(2) (d〈Q〉/dt)^(2)

and taking the square root,

  1. σ_H σ_Q ≥ (ħ/2) |d〈Q〉/dt|.

Ergo for a given state, the variances of H and Q are related to how fast the expectation value of Q is moving (and from (2), if H and Q commute, then 〈Q〉 is constant and Q is conserved in this sense).

Abusing notation, an analogy

To obtain the familiar form, define ∆t = σ_Q/|d〈Q〉/dt|, the linear approximation of the time for 〈Q〉 to drift one standard deviation, and ∆E = σ_H. Then, ∆E ∆t ≥ ħ/2. But beware: the ∆'s no longer mean the same thing!
