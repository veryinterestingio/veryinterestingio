Jiriakel A:

Let's say we discovered 500 new species of large fish between 1800-1850, 400 between 1850 and 1900, 300 between 1900-1950, and so-on..

We can then model that we're unlikely to discover more than ~50 new species in the future, so the discovery rate is about 97%.

Of course, in real life, data is messier, but the same sort of general analysis can be made to estimate how many species we'll discover, based on how many we have discovered so far.

That_Biology_Guy A:

One way this is sometimes estimated is using something called a species accumulation curve. Essentially, this is just looking at how many species have been discovered over time, adjusting for how much effort has been put into looking for them. Since there is some finite but unknown number of species, we expect this curve to slow down over time, getting closer and closer to the true number.

However, these methods are usually more suitable for estimating total number of species within a given ecosystem rather than the entire world. To do the latter, it is also common to simply use more general estimates or extrapolations. Stork 2018 discusses various different estimates of the total number of insect species for example, and settles on an average of about 7 million (of which just over 1 million have actually been officially discovered).
