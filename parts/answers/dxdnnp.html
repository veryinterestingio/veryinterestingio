oyman A:

Inhalation is the process whereby both the diaphragm and the intercostal muscles engage in order to increase the volume of the thoracic (chest) cavity. More interior volume lowers the air pressure within the lungs until it is below that of the surrounding atmosphere. At this point the surrounding air moves into the lungs as you "Take a breath"

Basically, the body contracts a series of muscles in order to make the space within the chest larger. This draws air into the lungs in the same way that pulling back on the plunger of a syringe would draw in fluid.

Having "the wind knocked out of you" usually refers to a kinetic trauma, one that places force upon the chest and diaphragm (which is the major muscle used to expand the chest cavity during inhalation). When this outside force hits just right, it can reduce the volume of the chest cavity. As it gets smaller, the air pressure within your lungs begins to increase and, once it is greater than that of the surrounding atmosphere, it will expel the air from your lungs - which results in a forced exhalation.

Basically, if you fall and land on the plunger it depresses and will squirt out any fluid that had been drawn into the syringe.

Attempting to inhale immediately after having the wind knocked out of you is mostly a contraction of the intercostal muscles which, without the assistance of the diaphragm, feels like you are breathing, but no air enters the lungs.
