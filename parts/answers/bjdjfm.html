bencbartlett A:

There is no fundamental reason preventing sound from traveling faster than light in some contrived medium or structure (although I don't know of any experiments where this has been demonstrated), but you need to be careful about what you define "sound" and "light" to be.

In Lene Hau's famous paper, light was slowed to 17m/s in a sodium BEC. BECs are typically opaque, but the setup used electromagnetically induced transparency to allow light to propagate through the BEC; the incident photons coupled with the sodium atoms to form massive quasiparticles called polaritons, which account for the slowdown factor of \~20M. (For those asking, the speed of sound in a similar sodium BEC is about 2mm/s, so light still travels faster than sound here. Also, as pointed out in some other comments, introducing sound to the BEC might add enough energy to alter its state.) It is morally correct but technically nondescript to simply say that light slows down in their setup.

Sound generally travels through solids at a rate of thousands of meters per second. The only problem I foresee is that a compression wave being introduced to the system may add enough energy to the system to affect the conditions used to slow the light.
