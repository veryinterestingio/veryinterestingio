djublonskopf A:

The tobacco ringspot virus—an RNA virus infecting many different kinds of plants—has jumped from the plant kingdom to the animal kingdom, infecting the European honeybee. Various genera of rhabdoviruses are able to infect vertebrates, invertebrates, or plants, implying that both cross-kingdom and cross-phylum jumps have occurred within this virus family. Both groups are RNA viruses that—in their plant-infecting forms—use animals to spread from one plant to another...I imagine (but have no evidence) that this combination helped facilitate the jump between kingdoms.

Edit: I just remembered...there was also one documented case of plant-to-fungus virus transmission, where the cucumber ringspot virus was found to infect Rhizoctonia solani, a fungus that causes cucumber belly rot. The linked paper shows that the virus can actually move back and forth from plant to fungus to plant, making it a single multi-Kingdom virus.

behemuthm A:

This was covered fairly recently in David Quammen’s book The Tangled Tree which follows scientists Carl Woese and Lynn Margulis among others in their discovery of Horizontal Gene Transfer, or HGT. Basically what Woese discovered is that there’s actually three domains, not two. And viruses have moved between them, throughout history. Many of our ancient ancestors caught viruses that not only remained in their bodies throughout their lives, but were also passed on to their offspring. And when I say ancestors I mean way, way back, millions or even billions of years. And those viruses have jumped between species and our ancestors so much over the years that roughly 8% of our DNA is viral in origin.

Hattix A:

Yes, routinely!

The Orthomyxoviridae (of which influenzavirus is a member) infects salmonids, ticks, mosquitos, mammals, amphibians, etc.

Influenza-B to give one simple example, infects humans, seals and mustelids.

Single strand RNA (ssRNA) viruses tend to be specific to just a few species, but they do not care how closely related those species are. As far as a virus is concerned, all eukaryotes are the same family!

musicsavage14 A:

Yeah tons of them. The big type is arboviruses, meaning viruses that infect insects. The whole class of flaviviruses, which includes yellow fever, dengue, chikungunya, Zika, west nile, etc, are all (almost all? not 100% sure) arboviruses. For example the reservoir for West Nile is birds, it infects mosquitos that feed on infected birds, and can be transmitted to humans, other mammals, reptiles, and amphibians.
