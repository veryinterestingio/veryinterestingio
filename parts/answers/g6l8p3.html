Yes, it is possible. And unfortunately, the first cases that come to mind are other human coronaviruses that cause common colds - you can be infected by the same virus every couple years. The general trend is that if it doesn't elicit a strong immune response, there is not a strong memory immune response, so virus-specific antibodies and T cells decline in the span of months.

This doesn't rule out a vaccine though. With a vaccine, you can use adjuvants to trigger a stronger immune response alongside the antigen, and provide longer-lasting memory.

PirateOfUmbar A:

If you're thinking about viruses that we usually clear our own, RSV is a major one (fortunately, not as much of a big deal for most people since symptoms are mild for people >2 years old, but some kids can get it multiple times per season).

There are also other viruses that we don't necessarily clear; HSV, and pox viruses, for example, live in parts of the body forever despite having "immunity" after getting it.

Getting even more complicated, people respond differently to some viruses like HBV -- some people clear on their own, some don't, and some never develop immunity even after clearing or getting vaccinated.

Some viruses just don't provide lasting immunity; for instance, other coronavirus that cause the common cold are often thought to only provide transient (a few years).

Also remember that HIV actually attacks your immune system itself...

speter_shiggs A:

Immunity lasts different degrees. For SARS, you have antibodies in your blood for 3-5 years. After that, you're not "not immune" anymore, because the immune system has some sort of memory and can build new antibodies faster than if you had never had it. I think, the viruses of the common cold don't give you such a long immunity. Tetanus is an example where the immunity "expires" and you have to renew your vaccines.

Herpes or HIV are also examples of "a viral infection [providing] no immunity".
