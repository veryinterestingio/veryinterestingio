piousflea84 A:

I would caution against taking a single paper as conclusive evidence of harm from low level CO2 exposure (900-1,400 ppm). To the best of my knowledge, it's still very controverisal how well humans tolerate low-level CO2 (less than 5,000 ppm), and large evidence-based guidelines have not yet adopted a lower limit.

Regarding the Allen 2016 paper specifically, it has some pretty glaring methodological problems:
1) The experiment ran five different atmospheric conditions, yet all of their cognitive function analyses only included three of the five experimental conditions. This by itself is only mildly problematic, but...

2) The Figure 1 analysis compared "conventional", "green", and "green+" while excluding all data from the "Moderate CO2" and "High CO2" conditions.

3) The Figure 2 analysis compared "Green+", "Moderate CO2", and "High CO2" while excluding all data from the "Conventional" and "Green" conditions.

4) The "Editor's Note" shows that massive changes were made to the published p-values during the revisions process.

Taken together, this is strong evidence of p-hacking... suggesting that the manuscript authors were unable to reach the conclusion they wanted to by doing their analysis as-planned, so they performed a large # of unplanned subgroup analyses (Many of which were negative, a few of which were positive) and only published the positive ones.
