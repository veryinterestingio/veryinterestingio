I assume you mean the physical feeling of getting choked up during crying.

This happens because when your fight or flight response activates, the part of your throat that lets air into your lungs expands. When you try to swallow before you speak or whatever reason, you're fighting against the same muscles that are keeping your glottis (airway hole) open.

So the conflict there makes you feel like you're choking and can't get anything out.

This may not be exactly the same as what you're asking about, but if someone is trying to verbalize a particularly painful memory or experience, it is actually quite possible that the language center of their brain isn't functioning at full capacity. I only know of this phenomena in the context of PTSD and recalling traumatic events, but I imagine that it may apply to recalling events that are just genuinely upsetting if not entirely traumatic (I'm not a neuroscientist at all though, so I may be off base in that assumption).

Brain scans have consistently shown reduced in activity in the Broca's area of the brain, which is involved in our ability to use language, in people with PTSD when they are experiencing a flashback. The amygdala (the emotional center of the brain) shows drastically increased activity at this time, meaning when a person is reliving a traumatic memory their capacity to verbalize it significantly decreases while they're experiencing heightened emotions.

So again, I'm in no way an authority on the topic, but based on that evidence as well as personal experience with both myself and others without PTSD having difficulty speaking when particularly upset, I've come to assume that the increase in activity in our emotional brain naturally decreases our capacity to put words to the experience. This is actually key to EMDR therapy, which uses various techniques to keep both the right brain (typically associated with emotion) and the left brain (associated with language) stimulated while recalling upsetting memories in order to help reprocess them.

I first read about this in Bessel Van Der Kolk's "The Body Keeps the Score", but here is a link to an article that also describes this phenomena.

https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/the-british-journal-of-psychiatry/article/neuroimaging-findings-in-posttraumatic-stress-disorder/156E2575B0C6B0132B4BCF95302A976B

Think of our emotions and thoughts as a sea, and our logical mind as a ship. When we have intense emotions they're like big waves.

Basically, if you don't know how to navigate the giant amount of emotions and thoughts you're having at the same time, it is easy to get swept up into those sensations and shut down/crash from being overwhelmed.

It takes training to learn how to steer when the waters are rough.
