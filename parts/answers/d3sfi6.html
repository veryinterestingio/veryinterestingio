REO_Jerkwagon A:

I don't think the "why"is well understood yet, but researchers are getting there.

Your brain has special neurons that tell you when you're thirsty. Think of them as "hydration fire alarms." When you're thirsty, these alarms are going off in your brain, prompting you to seek out water. So you start to drink, and eventually these alarms shut off.

UCSF researchers discovered that when the fluid is colder, these alarms shut off faster. They still shut off with warmer fluids, but the colder it is, the faster they shut up.

That is what is happening, but again, the "why" is something I don't think is known yet. (I didn't look too hard though)
