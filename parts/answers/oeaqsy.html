BiAsALongHorse A:

I read an /r/askscience thread (that I can't find) a while back focused on why humans can't regrow teeth like sharks. People have covered why rodents have teeth that grow constantly, but I haven't seen a good take on the human end of this. Basically, you develop your second set of teeth before your first set are even in use, and only switch once your entire life. Developing a "tooth assembly line" like sharks have would be a huge evolutionary hurdle, not just on a scale like what would matter to our hunter-gatherer ancestors or other great apes, but something that would be incredibly significant on a scale of 10s to hundreds of millions of years. The issue is that on that time scale there's a good chance that any given evolutionary line needed to be pretty damn good at grinding down leaves for a significant chunk of their total calorie intake. If you're pretty damn dependant on grinding leaves with your teeth, having a tooth or two out of place can have a horrible and potentially fatal effect on the end to end efficiency of your digestive system. Each generation of teeth you grow risks having your teeth so misaligned that you starve, and some intermediate system to having more than 2 generations of teeth would be especially error prone.

Granted, early hominids were not dependant on grinding up grass and leaves to survive, but if your evolutionary line might have needed to depend on that food source more several times in the past 65 million years, it's kinda hard to get a shark-like system up and running.
