Know_A_Veil A:

First, this depends on the vessel size and where it is. If a large blood vessel in your chest, like your aorta, were to rupture it can leak a lot more blood in less time than a number of smaller vessels in your limbs put together. Second, your body has the ability to “shunt” blood from the outer parts of your body to your core to save the most vital parts of you. When you cut off a limb, if it is relatively clean, the vessels themselves will constrict to help slow down blood loss and the body will use that “shunting” ability to slow down blood movement to your extremities, keeping blood for your heart and brain. This shunting ability is also the reason why most bicyclist and motorcyclists quickly come to know that the warmest gloves in the world will not keep your hands warm and nimble, unless your core is also warm. The body will always redirect blood flow from your hands to keep your core warm, unless those body parts are addressed first. Lastly blood loss from your extremites is more likely to be quickly detected and controlled. Internal blood loss from a vessel can go on for awhile before producing shock and any physical signs. In fact, most of the signs will be fairly general. When my wife’s appendix burst, she initially reported general abdominal pain and a feeling of uneasiness. Obviously, if her arm had been missing she would have called for an ambulance a lot sooner. Most of the bruising that appears from an internal rupture is considered a late sign of internal bleeding.

The same reason it's "more effective" when you cut your wrists - cutting straight across allows the vasculature to temporarily constrict, so if the vessel is cut open like a canoe it's unable to constrict effectively. Once your blood pressure drops low enough you won't be able to get enough blood to your brain or your heart.
