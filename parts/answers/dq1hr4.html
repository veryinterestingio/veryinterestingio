The feeling of travel sickness is a response by the brain to competing signals that don’t make sense. For example; you’re reading a book on a coach going up a mountain. Your eyes see you as not moving, as you are looking at a static book, but your inner ear tells your brain that you’re moving left, right, accelerating and decelerating.

In most circumstances encountered by humanity and it’s ancestors, this missmatch can be caused by one thing; poisoning. So the body has evolved a vomit response to try and get the poison out.

Now the exact mechanism is unclear and your question about why certain people are more affected has not been conclusively answered. Motion sickness is more common in women and those who suffer migranes, and there seems to be a genetic component too. We still have a lot to learn before we know for sure.

spndd A:

Motion sickness is basically because your brain is confused. Your inner ear plays a big role in the motion and balance of your body. When those signals are combined with sight and movement of your muscles, your brain says “Yes we are moving.” However when you’re in a car/plane/boat, your brain is only receiving movement signals from the inner ear. The muscle movement isn’t happening nor are there any visual signals of movement (especially in big ships and planes). This causes your brain to say “What the fuck is happening?!” and translates that into nausea.

As far as whether or not you’ll experience it, it’s just a matter of how sensitive your brain is to those discrepancies. For people who get car sick it’s usually recommended that they sit in the front seat and simply watch the road (rather than looking at their phone/book/video game, etc) because then their brain can see the movement and that’ll ease the sickness. There isn’t much you can do for plane/ship sickness aside from taking anti-nausea drugs.
