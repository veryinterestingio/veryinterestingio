However, it is important to note that shellshock was far less well understood than our current, nuanced understaning of PTSD, and as a result some people were called shellshocked when they were physically injured or had disorders other than PTSD.

The symptoms of shell shock are still seen at times. But the kind of trauma that soldiers are in is not the same today as in WWI. They are in war zones far shorter periods of time now, they are in direct combat for minutes or at most hours not months at a time, and they are not under constant bombardment being subjected to the pressure waves of dozens if not hundreds of explosives a day.
