CrustalTrudger A:

They did not test tubers or similar plants, but there was a study recently (Wamelink et al, 2019 or pop-sci write up of the same paper) that grew a variety of other crops in artificial versions of both Lunar and Martian regolith. The only crop that they tried and didn't work out was spinach. As mentioned in the Wamelink paper, there were actually quite extensive and direct (successful) attempts to grow plants in returned Lunar regolith from the Apollo missions as well and a variety of other tests done on plants with Lunar regolith, e.g., abrading of plant leaves with it, and these studies generally found that growth in Lunar regolith did not imbue the plants with any toxicity (e.g., the summary in Ferl & Paul, 2010).

EDIT: Going back to one of the original Apollo papers (Walkinshaw et al, 1970), it seems they were at least able to germinate potatoes in returned Lunar regolith.

I'm sure it has minerals/nutrients but we would need to create a soil "ecosystem" first before plants would easily survive. Many plants can't just take up minerals unless processed by bacteria/fungi first.

You'd need an initial source of organic matter, starter samples of soil to seed the microbes and start by growing plants just to compost and work your way up from there building the soil until vegetables would survive.
