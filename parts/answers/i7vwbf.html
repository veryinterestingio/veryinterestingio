truthrises A:

Sound is just wiggling air.

Wiggles travel fast through air, but they travel very very VERY fast through the ground.

When you explode something, it wiggles everything around it.

The wiggles that go through the ground get to you faster than the wiggles in the air.

If the ground is wiggling hard enough, it makes more wiggles in the air close to you that sound like a boom.

These extra wiggles that came through the ground get to your ears sooner than the wiggles that just went through the air.

analoguewavefront A:

The Beirut blast apparently caused a pressure wave, not a shockwave. Pressure waves travel slower than sound and build up pressure gradually (but still very quickly), which gives lungs and enclosed spaces a fraction of a second to equalise before reaching the peak pressure. Whereas a shockwave travels as fast or faster than sound and the pressure increase is instant, which leads to much more damage to people and objects.

So the vibrations of the sound travelled quickly through the ground, the sound followed soon after, and very quickly afterwards the pressure wave of the explosion hit.
