Astrokiwi A:

In a sense, the Earth already is a giant eyeball. By placing telescopes on opposite sides of the Earth, you can combine the data using interferometry to get the same resolution you'd get with an Earth sized telescope.

However, this is easiest to do at longer wavelengths - like radio waves or even microwaves. It's much harder to do with infrared or visible light, and we can only do that on a small scale - like two telescopes on the same mountain. So an Earth-sized eyeball would have a similar baseline to a worldwide radio telescope interferometer, but at shorter wavelengths than we can currently manage. Also, shorter wavelengths are inherently "less fuzzy", so a world-sized optical telescope (or eyeball) would have higher resolution than a world-sized radio telescope.

But in addition to resolution, a world-sized eyeball/telescope would have incredible sensitivity. We can use interferometry to combine multiple telescopes to make a high resolution image, but for sensitivity you really can't beat just having a huge collecting area.

The night sky actually has lots of nebulae and galaxies that are big enough that you could make them out with the human eye - but they're too dim to be seen well at night. Sensitivity is what makes the difference here: if the human eye was bigger, you could pick up a lot more light, and see lots of galaxies and nebulae in the night sky that aren't normally visible.

Currently, the biggest visible light telescopes being built have a mirror 30m in diameter. If the pupil of the world-eye is even 1% of the diameter of the Earth, that's an aperture of ~130 km, and would collect about more than 10 million times as much light, and have thousands of times better resolution.

TheOtherHobbes A:

Resolving power scales linearly with reciprocal diameter.

The Earth's diameter is around 12.47 million meters, which would give a maximum resolution of 2.5 E-12 degrees at 550nm.

That would be amazingly good at resolving astronomical objects, especially if the full area of the Earth was used for light capture.

But resolving power is not the same as magnification. You would need Earth-sized optics and/or some unbelievably advanced computing power to turn an Earth-sized sensor into useful human-viewable images.

For stars, you'd probably need optical filtering to prevent sensor white-out. For optics - building Earth-sized glass lenses might be a challenge. A hypothetical quantum gravity civilisation might have more elegant solutions.

There would be some interesting engineering issues. You'd have no problem viewing the surface of remote planets, but tracking the surface of a remote planet as it moved through space would be a much harder challenge.

There are also practical limits set by dust and microlensing events. Anything between you and your objective could potentially reduce resolution.

Bottom line - not even remotely practical with current technology. But if it could be built, it would absolutely transform astronomy and SETI.
