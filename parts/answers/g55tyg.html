The closest thing I can think of at the moment is a thresher shark. They have impressively long tails which they use to stun fish. Their tails can apparently reach speeds of up to 30 m/s and accelerations of \~30 g while doing this (figure from Oliver et al. 2013), which is pretty amazing considering they live in water. If you mean using a whip of their tail as a defensive mechanism?

A lot of modern large lizards will do that. Green Iguanas are well known for it, or as you can see in this example from YouTube, monitor lizards.

Size limitations keep the speeds much lower than those estimated for dinosaurs, but they are still quite capable of breaking skin, even at their reduced size.

Dracorexion A:

Several lizards, especially Iguanas, use their tails in self defense, though I should clarify something. Just because computer models show that some sauropods' tails could have physically moved that fast, performing such a thing would actually hurt the tail, and probably broken bones. More likely their tails would have been used like a bat or club rather than a whip.

AdmrScheer A:

Not via a tail, but the pistol shrimp has a specialized claw that when it snaps creates a cavitation bubble which moves away from the claw at speeds of 97 km/h (60mph) and generates a sound reaching 218 decibels.

The bubble collapsing also produces light. Though the light is mostly inconsequential... The shockwave is the important part.

Still, the little dude basically has a lethal flash bang.
