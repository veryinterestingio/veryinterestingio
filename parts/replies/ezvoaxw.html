    I agree with what you've said. A crowd can smooth out very minor fluctuations in pitch (the chorus effect), but if you're actually off key, it still stands out unless the crowd is so large it drowns out your voice. But as you stated...it's not an "averaging out" but a "merging".

    In my experience, group singing sounds good for 3 reasons (2 of which you pointed out):

    1. Good, confident (and on-tune) singers sing the loudest. If you have even a few of those it can really tie the sound together.

    2. Joining with a existing pitch is really powerful. If you have the first condition, people can join with the confident singers. Even if not, if the instrumentation is playing the melody, turning the volume up on it can cause people to find and match that tune. As the majority of people find the melody, the few who cannot are drowned out and not heard (as long as you're not standing like right next to them).

    3. Kind of going along with your last paragraph, I think bad technique goes unnoticed because people just can't hear it. If one person in 50 runs out of breath in the middle of a note, people aren't going to hear it, let alone in a group of hundreds. But a single singer who makes that mistake would easily be heard.

    Source: sing with an amateur choir twice a week
