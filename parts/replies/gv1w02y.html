    Yes, exactly! You said it all -- except that Liletta (a lower cost version of the Mirena IUC) has been nipping at Mirena's heels for years. Liletta was approved for 6 years last year and, surprise surprise, Mirena quickly followed suit and Mirena is now FDA approved for 6 years.

    This is one of many examples of contraceptives under promising and over performing. Other examples include the Nexplanon implant lasting for at least 4-5 years (FDA approved for 3) and Depo Provera lasting up to 16 weeks, but we tell folks to come back 11-13 weeks.

    It makes sense that the FDA approved package statements would be conservative as pregnancy prevention is pretty high stakes. After a product comes to market, independent organizations can do further testing to challenge the stated assertions, and often find that the methods out-perform their stated claims. Then reputable, evidence-based organizations like Planned Parenthood bring those changes to clinical practice -- while hopefully explaining why what they're recommending is different than what it says on the box.
