      A material that has a lattice is a crystalline material, they are synonymous in this context. Polymers can be amorphous or crystalline, it depends on the monomers used (large, bulky monomers are harder to pack while smaller monomers can often pack) and on other conditions (speed of the polymerization, presence of impurities, etc.).

  • MiffedMouse

    In the science texts I have read, polymers are not classified as a separate category from crystals and glasses. Instead, polymers can crystallize as solids or glasses (or a mixture of both).

    Crystals refer to any solid with a regular lattice. Some polymers have this. Glasses refer to any solid that are locally amorphous. Some polymers are glassy. Some can be both, depending on temperature and history. Fancy recent research has investigated block copolymers, which have molecular bonds between crystalline and glassy polymers, creating closely linked glassy and crystalline regions.

    Finally, the concepts of crystals and glasses is actually quite general. It can be used to describe lattices of any object, including molecules, buckminster fullerene complexes, magnetic moments, and cloth fibers. The idea that a substance isn’t a “crystal” just because it is made of molecules, not atoms, is a misunderstanding of what a “crystal” is (at least, as the term is used in contemporary science literature).

  • sBoon_

    There's lots of strange misinformation on crystallinity in polymers here.

    >99% of polymers are either semi-crystalline or amorphous. Semi-crystalline polymers are polymers where either intermolecular bonding is favourable or where alignment is favourable (ie very rigid polymers). With these conditions in mind, as molten polymer cools it can crystallise. However, at the border of two crystalline regions (look up spherulites) the polymers cannot align to both directions of crystalline growth and be trapped in an amorphous (disorganised) state.

    Crystalline polymers can be synthesised through slow, controlled reactions. Each monomer aligns at the surface of one crystalline region during polymerisation.
