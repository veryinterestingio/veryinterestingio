    • Runiat

      That sounds fascinating, so when it comes to cancerous cells you have multiple mutation, some mutation have to do with division and others have to do with the ability of the cell to travel, right?

      That's the thing, since all the genes required for a cell to travel is already in it before it becomes cancer, you don't need a mutation in the traditional sense to set it off, just a slight change in how the DNA of the cancer is balled up.

      Why a cell arrives to a different organ, it starts to divide and grow a tumor all by itself inside that organ or does it mutate the other cells of that organ in order for the tumor to grow?

      A bit of both.

      Cancer cells can give off various chemicals which cause nearby healthy cells to behave a certain way, and they can multiply unendingly.

    • Bax_Cadarn

      Mutate is a bad word. Mutation is a natural process that occurs when copying dna is flawed. Cancer cells use their enzymes to cut the tissues to infiltrate it, their growth factors to create pathways supplying them with food and oxygen and generally mold their environment. But they don't per se change the programming of the cells, just how it's executed.

      Oh and no topic: I work at a pneumonology ward, and most of our patients die of pulmonary embolisms(cancer makes faulty blood vessels, which cause clots; it can also introduce more clotting factors) or respiratory failure but cancer can kill someone by for instance "eating their food" or growing on a wall of a big blood vessel and rupturing(it's not build to create solid tissue, due to how random it is) or growing enough to obstruct blood flow or the intestines.

      So yeah, many many ways.
