    • Harsimaja

      For shapes embedded in 4D it can be possible to imagine animations. Beyond that, you can think of cross sections (which may even be 3 dimensional) or abstractly imagine it as having properties analogous to others, and still calculable. The 3-sphere (embedded in 4D), or a 4-simplex (next up from a tetrahedron, with 5 tetrahedra as its facets), or a hypercube. The animations can even be fairly easy in these cases.

      In my area some ‘6 dimensional’ manifolds are very important, and where possible we have rather sophisticated ways to characterise them by special ‘subsurfaces’ within them and certain other invariant properties. We can get a qualitative feel for what their topology is like to guide us.

      A lot of the time, infinite dimensional spaces and ‘surfaces’ can also be very important.

      But throughout, you can describe these all very rigorously with mathematics. Complete ‘visualisation’ is not realistic (our eyes ‘see’ 2D projections at any one time, after all) but also not necessary. If anything relying on visualisation can lead to misconceptions.
