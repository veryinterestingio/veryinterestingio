      As long as they're mixed thoroughly enough that the different dyes are less than a few hundred nanometers apart, there's no distinguishing them. If the dyes are dissolved in water, for instance, this happens.

      • aminy23

        A pigment is typically a finally ground powder. This is suspended in the liquid, and can easily sink over time.

        A dye is a colored liquid. A dye can be a powder, but this powder will dissolve in water.

        As a dye is dissolved, it should be mixed well enough that a microscope that can see color won't detect the difference.

    • dbdatvic

      Basically, it's "mixed well" if you can't see individual speckles of blue or yellow on the scale you're looking at. So you'd have to mix much more thoroughly (or, like, use blue and yellow dyes that melt into each other and absorb each other, becoming 'green dye') to have it look homogeneous under the microscope than with the naked eye.

      (And if it's not dyes, but tiny plastic blue and yellow particles, there's gonna be a magnification after which it doesn't look well-mixed at all, it just looks like blue and yelow baseballs in a pile.)
