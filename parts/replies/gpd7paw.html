      • AimlessZealot

        Yes, but there's a few things in there that deserve to be addressed:

        1) What does "less deadly" mean? Do we mean less likely to produce immediate life-threatening symptoms? Less likely to have long-term impact on the lifespan of the infected? Less likely to compromise you to other deadly illnesses? Because all of these might be described as "deadly" but are very different aspects of how a mutation might distinguish itself. As a result, avoiding all of them becomes an increasingly more unlikely event.

        2) As it becomes more transmissible, you are now even more concerned about comorbidities: What else does it show up with? Even if it is not deadly, it might demonstrate a significant comorbidity with other, far more dangerous illnesses. An example of this is that some forms of vertigo have a surprisingly notable comorbidity with heart diseases. While the relationship may not be causal, until the relationship is understood, these increased transmissibility represents a new potential danger.

        3) What does the immunity mean? Chicken pox in childhood usually gives an immunity to future cases of deadly adult chicken pox, but as any older adult who has suffered them can tell you, having your latent infection flare up during times of stress and illness as Shingles is absolutely debilitating!

  • Italiancrazybread1

    You forgot there is another variable that leads to more mutations and that is time spent infected. Something like HIV has lots and lots of mutations that appear with a relatively small population of infected because the virus multiplies in the body for a very long time. The more time it takes to clear an infection, the higher the probability of a random mutation taking hold. Some of these coronavirus patients are sick for over a month. During that time the virus is multiplying exponentially. That's how many of the experts believe the South African variant came about, from a single person who was sick for over a month.

    A billion people that clear an infection in a few days has way less of a chance of mutation than a million people that have the infection for 1 or more months. That's partly what makes vaccinations so important. Even if the vaccine isn't 100% effective, cutting that amount of time spent sick is critical.
