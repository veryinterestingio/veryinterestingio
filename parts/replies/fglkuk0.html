      But you need to give them a push, don't you?

      No, the combustion of hydrogen, like many chemical reactions, is spontaneous.

      My physical chemistry professor used the combustion of hydrogen to demonstrate the point that "spontaneous" doesn't mean "fast": at room temperature, the spontaneous reaction has a half-life of 10^26 years, but with a spark, the non-spontaneous reaction has a half-life of 10^-6 seconds.

      So... You don't need to give it a push unless you want it to react before the heat death of the universe.

    • Mr-Thumpasaurus

      I don't really think the other comments addressed this well, so here's another stab at the explanation:

      There's an electric field created by the chemical potential difference between the two sides of the plate. This is the "Nernst potential", which functionally is trying to pry electrons away from the hydrogen, providing your "push".

      A portion of this available voltage is used to overcome the activation barriers of the reaction, which is one of the reasons why the operating voltage of the fuel cell is lower than the theoretical Nernst potential.

      You can reduce the amount of voltage lost to overcoming this activation barrier by introducing catalysts to the reaction sites, and/or by increasing the reaction temperature
