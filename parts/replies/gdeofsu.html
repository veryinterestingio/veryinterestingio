    • wildcatkevin

      SARS-CoV-2 isn't that specific to humans, it can infect a variety of mammals, like minks and cats and bats and civets etc, but happens to be particularly harmful for us. We're still not really sure why but a lot of the thinking is that our immune system overreacting is often the reason for severe disease. Many animals have similar enough ACE2 receptors to also get infected. (Side note, I also don't know that key and lock is the best descriptor, since the viral proteins undergo confirmation changes as part of infection so induced fit might be more accurate in this case. But that might occur after internalization in the low pH of endosomes, hopefully a virologist will chime in and correct me if I'm wrong)

      As for transfection of a huge range of cells, we can do that because scientists and engineers have done huge amounts of work designing special materials for these LNPs, to make them work so well. This comes with some tradeoffs like poor stability, which is why both leading mRNA vaccines need to be frozen, and also why this would not be a viable path for a virus to evolve towards (although never count out evolution, who knows maybe we'll find one someday that does this). In the lab when you use these tires of materials, you mix the nucleic acid with the lipid/polymer and then have to add it to the cells within tens of minutes, so stability is a key limiting factor.

      For a little more clarity, it is actually believed that these gene delivery methods likely bind to cell surface receptors as part of the process to get internalized, it's just not a cell type specific receptor. I believe for example that polyethyleneimine (a cationic polymer used commonly in some transfections) binds to sialic acid receptors that's are on the cell surface of pretty much all cell types (didn't confirm this in literature, just writing this based on my experience in lab, I'm a phd chemical engineer who's worked a lot with mammalian cells and RNA). It then gets internalized because of that interaction. (Interesting side note, I believe the sialic acid receptors are also targets of influenza for infection, although I hope some other redditor virologist can confirm that, not my specialty)

      Of course, the materials used for the leading vaccines are proprietary so don't know the exact details, but I would expect they use similar gene delivery methods in their liposomal particles (see lipofectamine, which is a lipid and cationic polymer material commercially available for gene delivery). Whatever they're doing it seems to work thankfully!
