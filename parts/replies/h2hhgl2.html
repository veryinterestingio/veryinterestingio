    • Sluisifer

      You can buy tint but that stuff isn't cheap and the smallest you can get is like a quart.

      As for 'pure' blue, you'd be talking about reflected light of a single frequency you define as blue. That's not really what paint is. You can get a single pigment like aquamarine or cobalt blue, but even those have a characteristic spectrum.

      Realistically, just find the paint chip you want.

    • MayorOfBubbleTown

      Depends on what you consider to be pure blue. If it's phthalo blue or aquamarine blue you can get those colorants. If it's not, you will have to add another color to nudge it over to the color you need BUT the more of other colors you add the less bright or saturated the resulting color will be. Like if you mix red and yellow together you get orange but it is duller, sadder looking orange than if you used an orange colorant.

        Hey, sorry for misrepresenting your position! I didn't realize that Home Depot paint departments did in-depth colour matching. It's fun, right?

        And you're right, it would be difficult to write a program that was able to translate hex into a colorant formula for many reasons. A lot of people here have already said that hex's RGB is problematic, so a CMYK system would work better. Still though, you also have to take into account the base paint (white/medium/clear) and all the different ways to achieve similar colors with different pigments (like using Burnt Umber vs Brown Oxide with Lamp Black and Yellow Oxide to make a neutral taupe). It might require someone sitting down and actually matching by eye a bunch of core colors and their codes to colorant formulas and then writing an algorithm to match the rest. Would probably take a lot of time and cost a lot for only a small benefit.
