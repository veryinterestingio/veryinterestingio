    "However if consumers stop buying plastics then manufacturers will notice that plastic products aren’t turning profits and will in turn switch to materials that consumers are willing to buy."

    This may have been true a hundred years ago when we had actual competition and economies of scale that made sense to an ordinary person. We've far outgrown that world. A hundred years ago, markets were smaller, there were many more companies, there was real competition, and it was possible for individual consumer choices to influence companies. But over the last century there has been so much conglomeration and consolidation that individual consumers don't even remotely have the capability to influence multi-national mega-conglomerate companies. They're not just companies anymore, they're systemic, structural entities baked into our economic system.

    Individual consumer choices will never solve this problem. Coca Cola sells 1.9 billion servings of Coke products a day. A day. And that's just one company. You will never, ever, ever, ever in a million years get enough people to boycott the world's beverage companies to make them take enough of a loss to change their business practices. Even if you convinced 10 million Americans to give up plastic tomorrow, it won't make a difference.

    We are facing a structural, systemic failure of our the free market. Only a structural, systemic solution can solve it. This is why we have governments and why we need governments that aren't run "as a business," that don't look to profit motive, but that exist to make good long-term calls. This is the same reason we have anti-trust laws and a commonly-shared belief, even among right-wing economists, that unregulated, unfettered monopolies are actually bad for the economy and our communities, even though monopolies arise out of unregulated capitalism.

    This is the exact same reason that over 2/3rds of major advancements in US medical treatments and technology come out of government-funded research, not private enterprise. This is a very similar line of thinking as to why individual employees will never be able to negotiate effectively on their behalf when dealing with multi-national mega-conglomerates like Xfinity and we need strong unions that can help leverage collective action and consumer protection bureau's to hold megacorporations accountable for violating the law and cheating their customers.
