    No, you get changes in frequency if you move the sound source or microphone (Doppler effect), but reflections in a room (in this case the environment acts like a REALLY big room) filter a signal by boosting and cutting frequencies (affecting their phase), but not shifting nor creating frequencies

    Edit : ie if you play a 1kHz sine wave in a room, you’re going to record a 1kHz sine wave of potentially different amplitude and phase, but not frequency.

A room dampens most frequencies except for some, and you if run that enough times, eventually you should pretty much only hear the few frequencies it doesn’t attenuate, and everything else will have been attenuated so many times it won’t be present in the recording anymore, which is essentially what you’re hearing.

        The tone is ringing because different reflections at different points in time will tend to “smear” a signal over time.
