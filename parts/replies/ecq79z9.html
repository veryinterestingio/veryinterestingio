    • Dantes111

      Rampant speculation here:

      Doubling is easy for living things to do, but tripling is hard. When we grow, our cells divide into two, cloning themselves. The "directive" from our growing bodies of "You, little cell, are going to become the eyeballs. Split yourself into two and have each half go over to those places and keep growing." is simpler to manage than any kind of tripling would. Simpler/easier leads to less mistakes, which leads to an increased likelihood of the organism surviving to adulthood to pass on genes. Something like 4 would be more likely than 3 because it's two sets of two, which is repeated doubling. Note that there are some creatures that sort-of have 3 eyes, but generally it's two eyes and 1 "eye" that works slightly differently.

      In addition, there's the issue of efficiency. If two (eyes/legs/arms/etc) are mostly enough, are the added energy and resources it takes to make a 3rd one worth it in the overall scheme? If a 3rd eye makes you 10% more efficient at looking, but costs you 10% more calories, is that a net positive or negative in your chances of survival? Basically there's diminishing returns.

      EDIT: Note that some animals like certain species of frog and lizard do have a "3rd eye". The Parietal Eye in short just judges the amount of light that's out and helps the animal regulate it's rhythms based on the day/night and seasonal cycles. It doesn't see detail, just general amount of light. So in cases like this, the caloric tradeoff is "worth it" to the animal, so the eye gets passed on.

        I think this is the crux of the answer. Every attribute of our bodies has a biological cost and a reproductive benefit (however obliquely -- it is the benefit to reproduction that determines if a trait is likely to be passed on). If the cost outweighs the benefit, then even if a mutation occurs, it is likely not to thrive in the face of competition.

        Stereoscopic vision and being able to judge distance is a great boon compared to a single eye. And while a 3rd eye might improve our vision, the reproductive benefit (ability to hunt / avoid predators) is probably much less than the 50% increased biological cost over 2 eyes.
