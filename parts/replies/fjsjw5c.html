    Actually, a single horn thrust directly in a straight line thrust into a body has better force distribution than two horns ( which are frequently angled outwards )thrust into a body. There is less chance of actually breaking a single straight horn, thrust straight into the enemy than there is of breaking one of a pair of horns ( usually set at an angle to the motion of the thrust). Similarly, straight horns would be better suited to actually impairing enemies than curves or branching horns. On a curved horn the tip off the horn is not actually in line with the source of the thrusting force ( you see this when competing curved swords to straight swords ).

        I do actually think it has much more to do with bilateral symmetry than anything else. I’d also say that, in a great many cases, horns evolved as a mating display; starting out small with no real value in a fight. Things like deer use them to duel over breeding rights but, even that usage is kind of ritualized. I don’t think the horns on most land animals took their bilateral form because it was better for combat.
