    There is a little bit of cause-and-effect confusion with inflation from time to time.

    The central bank (Federal Reserve) does not directly cause inflation. No one ever issues a proclamation that says "Everyone raise prices!" which then somehow stimulates the economy.

    What happens is that central banks make loans cheaper and easier to get. This stimulates investment in the economy which boosts employment. This in turn can lead to inflation as a side effect because an overheated economy can bid up the price of things.

    What happens is that the central bank can choose not to worry about this side effect. They can decide that the boost to the economy is worth the small increase in prices. At some point, if prices keep going up, the central bank will start worrying about inflation again. They'll decide they need to make loans more expensive and more difficult again -- slowing down the economy in order to control prices.

    Where this point is located is always up for debate but many feel that low-but-nonzero inflation is where the balance is. But it's not the inflation which directly causes the change in investment, it is the relative ease and availability of credit.
