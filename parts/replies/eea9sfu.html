    • tea_and_biology

      Good question! Short answer; yup. Multiple studies have reared insects in oxygen-enriched environments - notably dragonflies and fruit flies - and have observed up to 15% increases in body size. Granted, there's still an obstacle course of physiological and developmental constraints in the way of us growing bird-sized dragonflies in the lab - there's plenty for evolution to overcome in that regard - but as far as normal day-to-day developmental plasticity is concerned, there's a fair amount of leeway in how large (or small, in oxygen-deprived environments) you can rear modern creepy crawlies with respect to atmospheric composition.

  • atomfullerene

    This is true but there's one other component: competition from vertebrates and specifically birds. Oxygen level places a limit on insect size, and up until birds start to become common flying insect size tracks oxygen concentration closely. But after that point, insects decrease in size and stop tracking oxygen levels...they can't get big even when the atmosphere would allow it because they can't compete/deal with birds in those size ranges.

    You can still see this phenomenon in some islands (eg, New Zealand), where lack of vertebrate competition and predation (in this case terrestrial) allows insects to fill some larger niches (but not get as big as they could if oxygen levels were higher).

    Environmental and biotic controls on the evolutionary history of insect body size Matthew E. Clapham1 and Jered A. Karr https://www.pnas.org/content/pnas/109/27/10927.full.pdf

    I recall scientific studies that sampled air bubbles trapped in amber indicating oxygen levels as high as 35%; and at the time critics claiming these were unreasonably high. It was suggested the amber matrix would preferentially allow other gases to diffuse out while retaining oxygen in the bubble. Certainly it appears there were higher concentrations of oxygen in the atmoshphere in previous eras than exists today, but the maximum level is still subject to some debate.
