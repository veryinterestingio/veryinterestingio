    I would like to add to this comment that phosphorylation of a protein is not a binary on always. It can function as a on or off switch and single phosphorylation events do not necessarily cause the intended affect. Sometime multiple phosphorylation events (and in specific sites) are needed to activate or deactivate and a combination of the sites can lead to different functions.

    ATP is used by the body for energy by being dephosphphorylated (removes a phosphate) becoming ADP (adenosine diphosphate).

    Or two phosphates when necessary, generating AMP.

    This allows it to be used as a tool to give up one of the phosphate groups to a protein as needed (phosphorylation, the “on switch” for tons of proteins), the breaking of this unstable bond provides the energy needed.

    Phosphorylation of proteins is an important use for ATP, but it's also used in lots of other reactions. And it belongs to a general pool of NTPs - GTP, UTP, and CTP are all used for more specific things. There are enzymes converting between these to balance out all four "energy currencies."
