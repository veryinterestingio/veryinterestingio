      That's an interesting question, I'm not super sure because it's probably partly a social phenomenon, but here's my shot.

      There's this postmodern take on language which roughly says that words only have meaning with respect to each other, and the connection to the rest of the world is done by each individual. It kinda resonates a lot with the foundations of math in the style of Russel and Whitehead, which just crank up the axiomatic style to 11.

      Electrical charge is defined with respect to a bunch of other words that represent things we learn about from a young age and continue seeing. Wacky hair if you rub a balloon, lightbulbs, getting electrocuted, seeing lighning. We have this very visceral understanding of all these consequences of charge, so this makes us feel less confused (sorry for the arm chair psychology, but I warned you!). The charge is the thing that feels like a tickly pinch, by definition, or the thing that causes the blast of the bolt.

      With spin... not so much. We know magnets, but that's kinda about it. Also, charge is just a quantity. However, the consequences of spin many times have to do with the direction it points to. That kind of adds another layer of complication in the way of some kind of visceral understanding.

      Last, I also want to mention science journalism. It's cliche but still true: quantum sensationalism is a problem. You, as the general public, will commonly be confronted with half-assed analogies for spin that do more to confuse than explain, and just generally cloud spin and anything quantum in arcane mysticism. It just sells to ponder on turning an electron a full circle and it ending up with its ass up.

    • avcloudy

      Charge is also a macroscopic effect we can build intuition about. Intuition is not understanding; we can think about charged foam balls repelling and attracting one another, but that doesn’t give you the exact understanding of how charge works.

      Spin violates intuition; it shortcuts our epistemology. We intuit things about angular momentum that don’t apply to spin. So even though we have the same understanding of both concepts, because one maps easily to our experience we think we understand charge better even though we know electrons are not tiny foam balls bouncing around.
