      There're definitely researchers incorporating spatial dynamics into these disease models in all sorts of ways, with varying degrees of complexity (everything from just adding an constant external force of infection from migration, to using agent-based models where you simulate movements of every person in a population). In general the task goes from having a simple set of differential equations that you can write out by hand and for which you calculate the stable equilibria to having simulations that quickly become too much to run on a single laptop. I can't actually speak to what folks have done for this outbreak--I'm guessing there are many, many people trying to model the spatial spread/risk of nCoV-2019 using a bunch of different methods, including using data sources you mention. Here's a recent review paper that summaries common methods for modelling pandemics. One other thing--it's worth keeping in mind George Box's quote "All models are wrong, but some are useful." There is no amount of data you could throw into a model that will tell you exactly how something like this is going to play out. You don't necessarily need an insane amount of granular data to model where public health resources should be best deployed. Heck, you probably don't even need a mathematical model for that.
