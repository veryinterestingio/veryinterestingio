    • stinkylittleone

      Not who you asked, but I think they’re talking about this:

      The spine has three major curves: it goes in at the neck, out through the upper back, and the lower back curves back in again. Commenter above is saying that usually where we feel like we need a pop is where those curves “switch” from in to out, that is, where the curvature is more or less straight. However, because we’ve got all those nice convenient curvey curves, you’re more likely to get your pop at the most bowed part of the S (especially ime the neck and low back) rather than where you wanted it. Those parts are so good at curving they think they should be doing it all the time. It’s a lot more challenging to keep them stable.

      This is also why backbends are hard: your low back is so good at curving it wants to do the whole job, but if it does you’ll end up injured. The key is keeping the low back stable so that the upper back can do the opening instead.
