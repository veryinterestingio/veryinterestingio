  • CrustalTrudger

    It is important to clarify that the process of rifting involves both ductile deformation of the lower crust and portions of upper mantle (for which taffy is a decent analogue) and brittle deformation of the upper crust through the formation of discrete normal faults. Even with significant crustal thinning, not all rifting leads to magmatism and the development of sea floor, e.g. the Basin and Range is a classic example of a 'wide rift', which has accommodated significant amounts of extension and thinning, but within which deformation was never localized enough to develop into a spreading center.

    It's also important to clarify that you're sort of describing rifting via one particular model that has been proposed for rift geometries, specifically the 'pure shear' or 'McKenzie' model for which there is no through going 'master structure'. The alternative end member 'simple shear' or 'Wernicke' model of rifting does argue for a lithospheric scale structure that could be considered a 'single crack' (although again, deformation will be accommodated via both brittle and ductile means along this structure, whereas at the word 'crack' kind of implies brittle behavior), but certainly not one that develops quickly or results in sudden separation of two continents. For a run down on the pure vs simple shear models of rifts, check out these set of notes.
