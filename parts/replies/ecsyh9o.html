    • TheFacilitiesHammer

      Hi there. Blood type does play a role, and having type O blood does increase your odds of being able to donate to someone else. However, a donor and a respective recipient must also be tissue typed to make sure their kidneys are a match. Without this, the recipient’s antibodies would immediately recognize the new kidney as foreign, resulting in rejection. This is actually the more important of the two tests in recent years, as several treatments have been developed to allow for mismatched blood type donations.

      Your other kidney will have to work harder to compensate, but a healthy kidney is fully capable of compensating with minimal-to-no deleterious effects. There’s research indicating that having one kidney (medically known as solitary kidney) may increase your chances of high blood pressure later in life, but most people live normal, healthy lives with no dietary restrictions. The NIH states as much, though I’d need to look around for a primary source other than what they link on their site.

      *Edit: In response to a few other comments I’ve seen on this thread. Your risk of complications due to something that affects one of your kidney (like physical injury or an infection) is higher if you only have one kidney, but your chances of receiving an injury or infection itself is no higher than the general population.
