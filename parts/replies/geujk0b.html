      There are lots of strains of measles actually.     • mom_with_an_attitude

      The name that describes how viruses mutate is called antigenic drift. I am guessing that the reason why the flu has more antigenic drift than measles is because influenza is a zoonotic disease. In other words, it is a disease that is found in animals and can be transmitted to humans. And as such, it has a huge reservoir, which is primarily found in China, in the form of poultry and pigs. A lot of flu strains start in China and then spread to the rest of the world, and the reason why is because there are so many people living in close proximity with poultry and pigs (i.e., lots of people in rural communities have a few animals in their backyard being raised for food). Those animals give the virus a lot of room to mutate.

      To my knowledge, measles is not zoonotic. So, there is not this vast reservoir of animal hosts where it mutates and then infects humans. There is just not as much measles in the wild as there is influenza.

      • nice--marmot

        You're right about the antigenic drift: Measles has a highly conserved, stable genome with a low rate of mutation and only one serotype. Influenza, on the other hand, has a high rate of mutation, four serotypes (A, B, C, D) plus, it has significant seasonal antigenic drift as well as less frequent antigenic shift (abrupt, significant genomic change). Influenza also has two antigenic surface proteins: Hemagglutinin, which has 18 subtypes, and neuraminidase, which has 11. These are the basis for the influenza naming convention, thus H1N1 (2009 pandemic), H5N1, H3N2, etc. There are 120 or so known combinations.

        Both measles and influenza are RNA viruses, which have high mutation rates in general because they lack the proofreading capabilities of DNA viruses, but it's not clear why there is such variability in mutation rates between different RNA viruses.

        Finally, you're on the right track regarding zoonosis too, with a couple of caveats: Both measles and influenza are of zoonotic origin. Influenza A has various animal origins, primarily birds, pigs, and horses but also bats, and even marine mammals. Measles is closely related to the cattle pathogen rinderpest virus, so cattle are the suspected source. Most human pathogens - 60% or so - are of zoonotic origin, primarily the result of human domestication of livestock and poultry. The percentage of novel emerging diseases that are zoonotic is much higher, something like 75%. Asia is the primary geographic source of avian flu strains, but North America is the source of most swine flu. There are geographic hotspots, but zoonosis is globally distributed.
