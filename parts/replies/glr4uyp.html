    More importantly, if they have $100,000 in stocks, they might not be able to sell it for $100,000. The "value" of a stock portfolio is based on what price the last persons agreed to buy and sell it at.

    Let's say I have 1000 of something.

    Let's say I sell 100 of the something to people for $10 each. They like the something so they tell their friends, and I have a bunch of people knocking on my door asking to buy the something. Its so good, so I offer to sell them for $20 and I sell 400 more.

    You'd say, at this point that, because I have 500 left and I sold them for $20 I have $1000 of value, right? Sure.

    Well, in the meanwhile, one of my somethings broke. I guess the quality wasn't there. So all of the 500 people who've bought my somethings for $10 or $20 no longer think it was worth $10 or $20. So people stop knocking on my door. So I go to the town square and offer them for $20 each and no one offers. Finally, I find someone willing to give me $5 for one.

    With that one sale, my $1000 lost 3/4 of its value and is now worth $250 - $5 = $245.

    That is how stocks work. Your portfolio says its "worth" $X but that is only derived from the last sale price. You need to find a buyer at that price, and if no one is buying, you need to lower your price to finish the sale.

    There is a reason when businesses are accounting for inventory that it is accounted for at the cost basis, not its value basis. Value is in flux, cost is finite.
