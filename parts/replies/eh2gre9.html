    • nlfo

      With water, pumps (or gravity wells) supply positive pressure, and it is possible to provide more water pressure than is needed and have water shoot out of a faucet like a water jet or blow up pipes if too much pressure is supplied.

      With electricity, it's quite different. A generator source can potentially supply much more electricity than is needed, but the device on the receiving end can only draw so much current. You can not force more current to flow than is demanded by the circuit. That's not saying that more current can't flow through a circuit because of a malfunction, such as an electrical short, because it certainly can.

      Think of it in terms of water is pushed and electricity is pulled. Electricity is pulled by the consuming device because the consuming device provides a pathway for the voltage to go to its ultimate destination, ground. If a short does occur which allows more current to flow than the circuit was designed to carry, then wherever the current flows the most (across the short to ground), then that part of the circuit will basically turn into a heating element and burn up or melt. That's actually the point of circuit breakers, not to protect the device pulling power through them, but to protect the wiring so that the wiring does not become a long heating element and catch on fire in case too much current flows through it.
