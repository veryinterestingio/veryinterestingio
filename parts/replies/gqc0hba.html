    • Grabcocque

      Yes, and that’s one reason why none of the new strains have yet been able to escape the wrath of the vaccines. The spike protein can’t change via mutation too much, because if it does, the critical binding sections of the protein will no longer be able to bind to its binding sites on the host cell, or activate the cell membrane fusion, and the strain won’t be able to reproduce or survive.

      Any new strain that manages to escape into the wild must therefore use a spike protein that's very similar in its binding sites to existing strains.

      So any antibody that binds to those precise sections of the spike protein should be effective against all strains that use the same mechanism of infiltration.

    • aziridine86

      Only a portion of the spike protein interacts with ACE2, its receptor expressed on human cells.

      Mutations that occur outside of this interacting region might not effect its ability to bind to ACE2. Furthermore some mutations within this interacting region also may not block binding with ACE2 while also still causing problems for antibodies depending on the exact change.

      So overall its true that the need to bind to ACE2 limits how much the spike protein can mutate, it doesn't mean it can't mutate at all without losing its ACE2 binding ability.
