    • GrandpaChew

      Aquifers are kind of like sponges—rocks in random orientations that have enough space between each other to allow for water to fill them up. As we drain them too quickly, the rocks will collapse and reorient themselves and become way less permeable to future water filling in the space.

      Obviously we can't go down there and reorient them ourselves, so once they've collapsed, that's it. The aquifer is ruined.

    • Darth_Mufasa

      As I understand it the two big issues are land subsidence and saltwater intrusion. Land subsidence is where the land above collapses down into the aquifer. Without water filling the space dirt and rock fills in the aquifer. Saltwater intrusion is a particularly nasty one for California and other costal regions. If the water table drops low enough we have a whole damn ocean of salt water that will seep into the aquifer. Thats why we have those desalination and reclamation plants I mentioned, to pump freshwater back into the aquifers and keep the sea at bay.
