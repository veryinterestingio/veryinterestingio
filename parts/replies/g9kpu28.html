    Even single-celled organisms work together, sometimes in very complicated ways. That means one cell doesn't have to do everything.

    Probably the most relatable example of this would be that slime and gunk you see growing in a drain or damp area that hasn't been cleaned for a while. This is called a biofilm and is a complex web of proteins that are produced by different species to provide a safe area to grow, among other reasons.

    The coolest example is probably that micro-organisms can actually share and swap DNA, even between species. This is actually one of the methods originally used to make GM crops.

      Right. In school you usually learn about one or two interdependent relationships. In the wild there's a complex ecosystem of cooperation and competition in nearly every microscopic environment.
