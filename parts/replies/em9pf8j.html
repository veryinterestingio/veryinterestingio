    Interesting question.

    Moving electrons do indeed have momentum proportional to their velocity. However, electrons in an electrical circuit do not move as fast as you might think.

    For a DC circuit, when you close a switch to turn on a lamp the lamp turns on very quickly. This is not because electrons have quickly flowed from the switch to the lamp though. Instead, it's more like a bicycle chain where all of the electrons move together.

    For an AC circuit this is perhaps more obvious as the electrons don't flow at all but instead oscillate back and forth.

    Another thing to note is that momentum must always be conserved. So, in order for a wire to jump (or gain momentum) an equal amount of momentum must be given to something in the opposite direction.

    For electrons this would mean that electrons would have to be ejected. Cathode ray tubes (electron guns) exist and probably generate a measurable thrust.

    Maybe not the answer you were looking for but I hope it's interesting all the same.

    Sure! Electrons absolutely have mass and, in the case of DC current, do flow through wires much like water through pipes.

    However, they drift very slowly. Check out a quick calculation here. When working out the momentum of the electrons in that example, it comes out to a paltry 1.81x10^(-6) kgm/s. When you consider that the wire itself has a comparatively large mass, I don't think any motion would be visible. However, this does sound like something worth looking more into! Maybe under ideal conditions (vacuum, very sensitive equipment) it may be possible to detect!

  • Neotheo

    There is absolutely a phenomenon which is similar to the water hammer in electricity. Only it happens with a different medium. Water flowing through a pipe will want to keep flowing because of their momentum. When the flow is suddenly constricted, pressure builds up on the blockage, causing the pipe to burst around it.

    Electrons behave the same way. Once they start to move, they want to keep flowing indefinitely. When you disconnect a very long wire where a lot of electrons are flowing through, you would most likely see a spark appear between the disconnected points. The momentum of the electrons (their magnetic fields) create a high pressure area (voltage) on the points of disconnection. If the voltage is high enough, the air starts to ionize and create a sort of electron bridge (spark), which reallows the electrons flow through the wire until all of the momentum (magnetic field) is gone.

    You should check out power station switches to see this phenomenon on a large scale
