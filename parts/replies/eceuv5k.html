    The largest horses become prone to leg and hoof issues from holding up all that weight, which begins to impose a limit on how large they can get before they're no longer practically useful (or really ethical) to produce.

    Also, while riding horses are still fairly common, there's far less use for and desire for large draft horses anymore. What draft horse breeding is left is mainly intended for show, so they're breeding for traits like being pretty and fitting within the established breed characteristics for tradition and history.

    Back in the day, there would have been incentive for breeders to produce the biggest, strongest draft horses possible ... but today, such a horse would only really be useful as an oddity at fairs and shows to be ogled and perhaps to have the notoriety of breaking a record. Not really worth the immense cost of breeding and caring for such large horses for most people. Nor is it really worth the risk of breeding animals that might have short, painful lives because their legs and hooves struggle to support their weight.

    (Even back in the day, it would often be more practical to have a team of smaller horses rather than one gigantic one. They'll be easier to feed, house, and care for. They'll be easier to replace if necessary. They'll be able to use standard-sized harness, shoes, and other gear, rather than needing custom-made items of everything.)
