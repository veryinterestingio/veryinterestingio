    • lifeguard29

      You get the temperature of the glass, or something like that as you'll have to dial in your emissivity. Expensive flir cameras also have settings like reflective energy if you are shooting into hot spaces (furnace) with lots of radiative energy around the object you're shooting. It's a bit of an art. As to the glass of water, you could shoot that since the water and the glass will probably be the same temp after some normalizing time. For calibration I often use black paint or electrical tape on the object I am measuring to get rid of the effects colour can have on my temperature reading. Paint the glass and now you have an emissivity of 0.99. Point your gun at it and you'll get a pretty close reading. That being said a thermocouple in the water would be better here, all about the right tool for the job.

    • LtTallGuy

      IR is a wide spectrum, so it can depend a lot on what your camera is calibrated to "see." The near visible IR can go through glass but the thermal spectrum generally can't. When using the Thermal Imaging Camera pointed at glass I usually see a reflection as if looking in a mirror. So you are not seeing the temp of the object or of the object behind the glass, but rather the reflection of the IR energy. This isn't perfect, some energy goes through the transparent object and it goes both ways so you can end up with a slightly distorted or out of focus reflection. Again, will depend on camera calibration. Just my experience working with TICs.

    • Rail_Control

      When I was shooting IR film, it was fascinating how different materials reflected in the IR band.

      The emissity of an object has very little to do with it's visible emissity. Black cotton t-shirts would be IR reflective, White leather would be IR absorbing.

      One exception I noted was US army combat fatigues. The camo pattern was effective into the near IR range!

      Granted, this is near IR, not far IR, but the color of an object has little bearing on it's IR reflectivity.
