    Smell definitely matters with some. If a ewe lost her lambs and you want to give her some others to raise an ancient trick is to tie the dead lamb's skin on the ones she's supposed to adopt, or at least vigorously rub the new lambs down with the dead one's wool. The scents mingle, which seems to be enough. Sometimes. A few ewes will take on whatever lamb you pair them with, others won't take any replacement ever, or not even their own.

    Mice are more "generous", any baby that smells like the collective nest is fine. They'll even steal each others pinkies. The individual doesn't seem to matter, they just grab any baby and carry it to their own spot in the nest. Some collect ALL the babies, others just want the number to be satisfying again. Any mouse that doesn't smell right gets fought mercilessly. Accepting babies at all for mouse mothers is mostly a matter of stress and age. Too young and/or stressed and some eat their young, sometimes right as they're born. Recycling at its grossest.

    A friend did a study on whether pig litter mates recognize each other even after they were separated for some time, and yes, they do. It was about reducing aggression when raising piglets for meat. But that might be smell or sound. Pigs and sheep recognize each other's voices.
