    • Pro-Karyote

      Great explanation! We do absolutely utilize fats for glucose, but only a small portion of the fat molecule is actually glucogenic (the glycerol). The rest of the fat molecule gets converted to acetyl-CoA, which is ketogenic. Most of the actual glucose we produce when fasting comes from protein degradation since many are glucogenic. Muscles, specifically, produce a lot of alanine which the liver can convert to glucose via pyruvate.

      Related to this, there are actually two long term fasting states that can happen: Marasmus (which is complete energy intake reduction - everything is low) and Kwashiorkor (consumption of carbs but without protein which leads to severe muscle wasting and complications). If you find yourself in an extreme situation, it’s actually better to totally fast than eat things which are purely carbohydrates without any protein since it allows your body to convert to a ketogenic state. If you continue to consume carbs, your body has trouble changing it’s day-to-day use of protein since the insulin release in response to elevated blood glucose maintains an “anabolic” state of metabolism and prevents total conversion to a fasted state. Basically, your body treats its protein stores as if everything is business as usual and will degrade protein like normal, despite no nutritional protein. You end up with sever muscle wasting, bloated belly (like you see in those starved African children pictures), and other problems.

      Note: this is for extreme situations (stranded on an island, trapped on a mountain, etc). Even then, if you have access to a protein source then by all means go ahead and eat something. Just be careful if all you can find are some sweet berries or fruits since those alone can lead to problems.

  • alsips

    You are correct, but since some other answers are not completely correct I want to reiterate that the body uses a mix of the energy sources, with the ratios depending on demands. Fat is not at all just emergency energy reserve.

    For example, for physical activity in fasted state: At low intensity (25% VO2_max, e.g. walking), mostly fat from blood triglycerides fatty acids are used. At medium intensity, muscle glycogen is added, with some increase in muscle triglycerides and blood glucose. At higher activity (85% V02_max), the largest part comes from muscle glycogen, with further increase in blood glucose at less fat used. In the feed state, carbohydrate use is larger. Also, on prolonged exercise, carbohydrate use drops and fat use increases. Eventually, glycogen from both muscle and liver are depleted.
