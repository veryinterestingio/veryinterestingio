    • BurnOutBrighter6

      Yes that would (kind of) matter, because the remaining water's freezing point would be getting lower as it gets more concentrated. In fact seawater freezing can lead to wacky things like brinicles aka sea stalactites - hollow tubes of ice freezing downwards from the underside of the sea ice surface layer.

      But both of the cases in OPs question (and my answer) are talking about fresh water, so I didn't get into that. Lakes are freshwater of course, but so are the pockets of water under the Antarctic ice. Antarctica is glacial ice on top of land, and in some places there's liquid water under the ice but above that land, or just within the ice. But it's fresh water not salty sea water. OP was not (I don't think) asking about oceans freezing under surface ice like in the Arctic (which doesn't have land under the ice).
