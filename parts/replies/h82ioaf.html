      It's probably worth remembering that polio is actually a relatively mild virus given the cultural boogeyman it has become. Only about 0.5% of people who are infected with polio develop any kind of paralysis at all, and a very substantial fraction of the people who have any degree of paralysis fully recover. About 75% of infections are asymptomatic, and about 25% have generic symptoms similar to those of a cold (fever, sore throat, headache, etc.). Polio is an enterovirus, closely related to a bunch of other viruses that cause disease in humans, mostly limited to colds (for example, rhinoviruses are in the same genus) but occasionally more serious. The fact that it gets into and damages the nervous system is sort of an accident, and different strains of polio vary widely in their ability to damage neurons.

      Thus, the infamous propensity of poliovirus to invade the central nervous system and specifically target motor neurons is rare and accidental and it is neither a prerequisite nor does it present a benefit for its normal life cycle in humans. Poliovirus is a neurotropic virus by mistake; its CNS invasion is mostly a chance event and largely independent of the age, gender, or socioeconomic position of the infected person.

      Hygiene has undoubtedly saved far, far more lives than it has negatively impacted.
