    Since we only have so many neurotransmitters, we have to use them carefully. As a result, serotonin does much more than simply improve your mood, much like dopamine does more than simply make you euphoric. In fact, too much serotonin will kill you: it's called seratonin syndrome, and it's caused by a toxic overproduction of serotonin. You'll recognize the symptoms as the side effects of an anti-depressent, but with serotonin syndrome, they're far worse: shakes, sweats, vomiting, fever, hyperflexia... eventually, serotonin syndrome leads to seizures and rhabdomyolysis, or muscle breakdown. That alone gives us a clue about serotonin's multitool existence, but it's far from a complete picture.

    It shouldn't surprise you to learn that the way SSRIs work, their "mechanism of action," is complicated. However, it might surprise you to learn that SSRI's mechanism of action is poorly understood.

    We do know that, if mood improvement only required an increase in serotonin at the synapse, SSRIs would be effective in days, not weeks. One theory calls out the down-regulation or decrease of 5HT1A receptor sites, gradually reducing the level of re-uptake over several weeks of physical adaptation. Other lab studies showing that SSRIs reduce inflammation might be the answer. Or maybe SSRIs fire the sigma receptors, and we don't fully understand those either. Brain science has come so far, and it still has so far to go. It's an exciting time.

    Today, you can get genetic tests to try and predict which psychiatric medications you'll respond best to, but we can't say for certain why they work... not yet, anyway.

  • ThaBigSean

    So I’m a pharmacy student and I’m going to be a little pedantic here. So the serotonin receptor that causes nausea/vomiting is the 5HT3 subtype. Activation of this receptor by serotonin causes the vomiting.

    Zofran technically does not decrease total serotonin levels in the synaptic cleft. It is actually a 5HT3 receptor antagonist. So it blocks the receptor and prevents serotonin from ever causing the nausea/vomiting.

    I believe the reason many side effects tend to decrease/go away with time is because of desensitization of involved "systems".

    This may not be the case in this specific situation with ssri/nausea, but the body has the ability to adjust to new normality. An example would be how it builds tolerance to certain medications, such as opioids/opiates. In that specific scenario, the body both adjusts (lowers) its endogenous production of substances, as well as gets better at breaking down the active substance of the medication.
