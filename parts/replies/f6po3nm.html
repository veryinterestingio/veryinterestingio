    but it can only take so much per unit of time

    Good answer overall, but that part is inaccurate. The issue is actually static vs dynamic friction. To keep it ELI5, friction is stronger when an object is at rest than when it's moving. Think about pushing a heavy box across a carpeted floor. It takes more force to start moving the box than to keep it moving once it gets going.

    To bring it back to swivel chairs, the stronger jerking motion overcomes the static friction and gets you moving, while the slow turn back doesn't generate enough of a reaction force to overcome the static friction.

    Also, I see several other comments mentioning the ability to change your moment of inertia which is a factor, but the static/dynamic friction dichotomy plays a bigger roll in all but the swivel-est of swivel chairs.

    • Vitztlampaehecatl

      Good answer overall, but that part is inaccurate. The issue is actually static vs dynamic friction.

      That's a separate idea.

      When you swing your legs to one side, you're generating momentum, and since momentum is conserved, something else must gain an equal amount of momentum in the opposite direction.

      The chair's bearing has a certain coefficient of friction, which means that it can exert a certain amount of force to keep itself from turning. Momentum is mass times velocity, while force is mass times acceleration, and acceleration is velocity over time, so the friction of the chair's bearing absorbs a certain amount of your momentum per unit of time.

      The chair only turns when you break that limit by generating more momentum (and thus exerting more force) than the static friction can handle, and that's when dynamic friction comes into play.
