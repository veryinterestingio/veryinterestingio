    Mobile phones neither use Time-Division Multiple Access (TDMA) or Frequency-Division Multiple Access (FDMA).

    FDMA was use for the old analog system and TDMA was used for 2G. Today it is Code-division multiple access (CDMA) that is used.

    It is a bit complex to explain but multiple phones can transmit on the same frequency at the same time the way the signal is encoded make it possible to pick the signal apart and get the data each device transmited.

    • _craq_

      There's a limit, but it's more complicated than "traffic per square metre". Engineers would talk about "Signal to Interference and Noise Ratio" SINR.

      If two towers are transmitting at the same time, frequency and code, then they will interfere and the signal will be garbled. But if you space the towers out, then a signal from a tower further away will be weaker, and you can treat it as "noise" on top of the "signal" you're getting from a nearby tower. If it's far enough away, the SINR will be high and everything works. "Far enough" depends how powerful each of their transmissions are. You can pack towers closer together if they all use lower power, and that will increase the amount of data you can transmit per square metre. That means installing extra towers and cabling, and it means more handovers between towers, which are an additional complexity. So it becomes a question of cost. Can you get a return on investment before you have to upgrade to the next generation (3G, 4G, 5G...)?
