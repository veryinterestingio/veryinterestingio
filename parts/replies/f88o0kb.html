      You seem to be confusing the Turing test, Turing completeness, and computability.

      Turing completeness is a property of models of computation - anything which can compute at least as much as a Turing machine can is Turing complete.

      Computability (or being computable) is a property of functions, which is that for any input a Turing machine can calculate the corresponding output.

      It's pretty hard to design from computable components a function that's not computable, and natural examples tend to involve reflection on computational models themselves, because of Rice's theorem - in colloquial terms, all functions that tell you whether or not programs behave in a certain way aren't computable. This includes properties we really want to know, like "does this program reach an invalid state" - we can check this for individual cases, but not in general.

      The Turing test (which you seem to allude to with 'high chance of failing to pass') is entirely separate - it's about a machine's ability to fool a human into thinking it's also a human. It has nothing at all to do with computability, since we know human conversation is computable - it's more to do with the complexity of human speech and thought, and the relationship between the computational models of Turing machines and human brains.
