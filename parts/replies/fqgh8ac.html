    • setecordas

      There is an effect called coriolis deflection which affects objects in free fall. The helicopter in your example is not in free fall, but in controlled flight, so iy can land anywhere the pilot directs. If the helicopter were to drop out of the sky, then, barring air resistance, you can use the coriolis deflection equation to tell you about where it will land.

      The coriolis deflection equation is (ω/3)(cosφ)√(8h³/g), where ω = Earth's spin rate ≈ 0.000073 radians/sec, φ = your latitude, h = height above ground, and g = acceleration due to gravity ≈ 9.8 m/sec.

      If you are at 40° North and are falling from the edge of space, assuming no air resistance, you will land about 657 meters west of the point over the ground you started at.

      If you were 500 meters high, you would land about 19 cm away.

      The deflection is highest at the equator and goes to 0 at either pole.

    • Fredissimo666

      The question is not how high, but how long.

      What u/inlarry omitted is that the real reason you land on roughly the same spot is inertia. Simply put, when you are on earth, you move at the same speed as the earth rotation. When the helicopter takes off, it continues going at that speed, plus some vertical speed.

      ​

      On earth, the air moves at roughly the same speed as the earth, so the helicopter would pretty much stay in place however long you wait (winds may move it but that's beside the point).

      If there were no atmosphere, it would be different. Since the helicopter now doesn't touch the earth, it now goes on a straight line. This means it would move away from the earth as time goes by.
