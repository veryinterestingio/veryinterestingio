    • alexander1701

      Fire consumes oxygen while it burns. A room that's on fire is almost completely without oxygen. Sometimes, oxygen becomes the limiting reagent, where there's plenty of stuff that's hot enough to light on fire, but can't find the oxygen to make that happen. So, things don't burn completely. The smoke still has flammable material in it, the fires are hot and low. But it's all hot enough that the second oxygen hits, that fire is going to pick up and blaze through all the smoke and fuel as fast as the oxygen comes in.

      They call that a 'backdraft'. You open the door, all of that hot air comes rushing out the roof, hot enough to spontaneously combust anything it touches, like a sheet of flame overhead. Air rushes in along the ground, and any flame it touches leaps and grows, billowing up.

      It's bad.

        So, things don't burn completely. The smoke still has flammable material in it, the fires are hot and low.

        A lot of things that tend to be burning are wood or woodlike. Wood, when heated in the absence of oxygen becomes charcoal and gives off liquid tar and what is known as "producer gas" or "wood gas" which has several components, the main one of interest is hydrogen.

        The exact composition depends on the conditions, but the result is the same.
