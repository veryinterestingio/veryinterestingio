  • mfb-

    If you expose the nuclei to the right kind of radio frequency waves, some of the nuclei at the lower energy level will get excited to the higher energy level, and when they relax back down, you'll get a signal. This is what's being measured during the imaging.

    That's a confusing description I think. The protons in the lower state are brought into a superposition of the two states - which corresponds to an average direction orthogonal to the magnetic field. It is then measured how that state evolves over time (it "rotates" around, staying orthogonal to the magnetic field, emitting radiation in the process). The protons in the higher state emit waves opposite to the signal, so they weaken the overall signal. A stronger magnetic field leads to a stronger difference in occupation of higher and lower state.

    • ConanTheProletarian

      Yeah, that's more like it. The relaxation doesn't shape the signal itself - that's just the free precession of the bulk magnetization vector. The relaxation shapes the decay of that free induction signal. On the other hand, relaxation is extremely important in MRI, since weighing your measurement differently for spin-spin or spin-lattice relaxation can give you different information.
