      • TheDarkFiend

        Like haplo said here, all nuclear reactors are able to shut down if something happens. In particular, the control rods’ default position is to be 100% inserted; if the power plant lost power for whatever reason, these rods immediately go inside the reactor and suck up neutrons, preventing criticality from occurring.

        Like haplo also mentioned, this doesn’t mean we are out of the frying pan. Really large reactors (think commercial reactors) have so much decay product, the heat alone from these products can lead to Fukushima if they aren’t cooled (hence why reactor need backup diesel generators if the lights were to go out). What Rapierian might be referring to here (and correct me if I am wrong) is that there are several modern reactor designs that combat this excess decay heat. One such design is present in micro and small modular reactors. These reactors are small enough that the heat generated by the decay products can be regulated by more passive systems, such as heat pipes.
