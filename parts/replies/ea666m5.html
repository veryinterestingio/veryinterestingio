    My interest in the mountain and in the science around it was all tied up together. I love the mountains and I like climbing, but when I was younger Everest seemed big and crazy and I never considered climbing it. It was being part of the 60th anniversary celebrations in 2013 that really inspired me. I became fascinated with the mountain when I realised how much science and technology have an impact in these kind of things, and yet we just take it all for granted. So then I became interested in the mountain and at the same time, as I gained climbing experience and met people who had climbed Everest, I realised that I probably could climb the mountain. And then I couldn't really not do it; I couldn't let that opportunity slide away. There's more about this in a blog post I wrote: http://melaniewindridge.co.uk/science-exploration/how-meeting-the-queen-inspired-me-to-climb-mount-everest.html But I didn't do any cutting edge scientific research on the mountain (except I did collect snow samples from each camp and the summit to send back to a lab at Lobuche for research into atmospheric pollution). The project was more to do with outreach and highlighting the science around summiting Everest. There is a whole series of Science of Everest videos that I made that you can find on the Institute of Physics YouTube channel: bit.ly/EverestVids
