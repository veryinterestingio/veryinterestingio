    • AcornWoodpecker

      That's a good question, and I'll share what I know as an amateur blacksmith less a metallurgist.

      TLDR: Yes the oxidation should slow down if it's in contact with virgin material if an alloy mixed for rust inhibition, similar to aluminum or titanium.

      Steel alloys are typically blends of materials and these days they tend to be very consistent. The different elements attached together at specific places and in different amounts depending on the alloy. When the billet is formed, the impurities, excess of some ingredients, are pushed out and you're left with the consistent block of raw stock.

      This is in contrast to the smelting of yore. I have run Japanese, Viking, and Archimedean furnaces and the alloy, or bloom, is inconsistent and all over the place. So you might get iron, mild, high carbon, and tool steel all in a little block of 5"x5". This is actually very useful for blacksmithing but not for many industrial processes.

      Your success depends is on how you make your stuff. IF you just mill (cut into) billet, or block, material, you're going to have a consistent material across every surface. With SS, that's probably good enough if you can ignore some surface discoloration and oxidation. Carbide cutting tools are so hard, I doubt they really deposit material and tend to fail completely or are replaced before failure. And the chromium is something like 30% of the mix.

      IF you forge the SS, be it drop forging with dies or hand forging, you can expect a change in raw material since it's being worked in a big way, at least on the surface. You have to work the face to remove the materials that could cause problems, physically removing material or chemically (passivation) though mostly for customer satisfaction. These parts are like your cold/hot drip forged wrenches and tools and hand forged knives.

      Not alot of people I'm familiar with hand forged SS, but I've seen lots of eating utensils made from it and you have to treat it like cast iron because there's lots of iron from the anvil and hammer worked into the surface. Typically the utensil is polished as best as possible but has to be treated like it isn't SS. Even SS knives are testy, mostly because they are thin enough you can't loose too much material without failure. But again, probably the biggest thing is customer satisfaction, no one wants a rusty fork or knife.

        There is not a lot to think about: it's all about the surface. Is the surface protected with a coating of oil, paint, plastic (powder coating) or etc. Is the stainless mirror polished (exceptional corrosion resistance) vs grain finish (weak resistance). Has the surface been scratched, if so how deep? And what temperature is it stored at (hot = fast corrosion, cold = slow corrosion).

        To clarify Stainless Steel "rusting". The primary ingredients of Stainless Steel are iron&carbon, chromium and nickel. A common grade of Stainless used to be called 18-4 which references its chromium-nickel content. Higher grades of Stainless also have molybdenum and sometimes vanadium too but these are exotic alloys for performance. "Rust" is iron oxide (oxised iron), and Stainless Steel doesn't give up free iron particles to Rust without a fight. If you really want to understand the chemistry of Stainless you should understand the crystal structures of austenite and martensite.

        I don't know much about blacksmithing but I understand why you wouldn't blacksmith with Stainless. Stainless is a pain to weld because of its thermal properties. It doesn't conduct heat much so parts where you've welded will warp if you don't properly sequence your fabrication. Stainless is also be a pain to Machine because it's so hard.
