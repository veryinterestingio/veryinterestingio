    • RobusEtCeleritas

      You generally have a dedicated location, called a beam dump, where the beam is dumped. You don't just let it hit the walls anywhere/everywhere throughout the beamline. The reason is that dumping the beam will result in some residual radioactivity, and you'd rather have that in a concentrated location rather than distributed around. Depending on the intensity of your beam, the beam dump can just be a bunch of material (metal, concrete, water, etc.) placed in the path of the beam, or if your beam is very intense like at the LHC, they have to be engineered with cooling systems and other bells and whistles.

      • mfb-

        The LHC beams have as much energy as tens of kilograms of TNT (hundreds of MJ). Just letting them hit the wall at a random spot wouldn't just make things more radioactive. It would burn holes into the walls.

    • mfb-

      The LHC beams have so much energy that this needs quite a large dedicated system. Each direction has its own beam dump: A 700 m long tunnel leading to what's basically a big graphite block. The beam is spread out over that block and widens while traveling along the tunnel (that's why the tunnel needs to be so long) in order to avoid burning holes into the graphite block.
