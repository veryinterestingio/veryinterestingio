    Negative mass violates General Relativity.

    In a freely falling reference frame in a gravitational field, the laws of physics are the same as in empty space (according to GR), therefore no experiment can determine whether you are in an elevator falling down or an elevator outside the Milky Way. Suppose that you could drop a ball with negative mass, if you were outside the Milky Way in absence of a gravitational field, the anti-mass ball would not move. If you were in free fall on Earth, the anti-mass ball would fall up. Therefore the existence of anti-mass would break Einstein's equivalence principle and therefore General Relativity.

  • Tonexus

    You need to make a distinction between inertial mass and gravitational mass. Inertial mass is what is referred to in Newton's laws of motion whereas gravitational mass is what determines force due to gravity. While experimentally the two have been shown to be equal, there is no reason that they must be. If we were to find some exotic material with negative gravitational mass, we may very well discover that inertial mass is better described as the magnitude of the gravitational mass.
