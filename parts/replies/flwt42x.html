    Do the common cold viruses like rhinovirus and adenovirus not mutate to avoid population immunity?

    • iayork

      Not really. Adenoviruses are DNA viruses that are quite stable. They have their own ways of avoiding immunity that don’t involve antigenic variation. Rhinoviruses also have their own ways of avoiding immunity, including multiple strains and induction of relatively weak, short lived immunity. I already answered for coronaviruses.

      Every virus has ways of avoiding immunity, because every virus around is the result of an unbroken string of thousands or millions of generations that did successfully avoid immunity long enough to replicate and transmit. But each virus lineage has come up with its own solutions, and you can’t extrapolate from influenza to adenovirus, or from HIV to coronavirus. They are different viruses with different answers to the same questions.

    • iayork

      HIV does vary in its host to avoid immunity, but it’s very different from influenza in many ways. For one, the immune evasion variants of HIV tend to be profoundly damaged, replicating much worse than “normal” HIV that doesn’t have to deal with immunity, whereas influenza can blithely incorporate mutation after mutation without showing any detrimental effect.
