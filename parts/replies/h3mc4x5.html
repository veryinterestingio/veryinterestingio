    pH is the logarithmic measure of concentration of protons in a given solution

    This is the definition for p[H]. There's nearly no distinction between pH and p[H] for aqueous solutions, but it may not be what the OP had in mind. The pH can be used to quantify the chemical potential of the proton in any environment, which is a broader definition. (The chemical potential is like a sophisticated version of concentration that incorporates interactions. It's the true determiner of how matter responds.)

      To add to this: Beyond pH 0 and 14, even strong acids/bases tend to not completely dissociate anymore. Take concentrated hydrochloric acid for example: it's 37% HCl in Water. Much more then 1mol/L. But because of that, it's not completely dissociated.

      pH can also be very different in organic solvents, but here measuring it with traditional methods doesn't always work anymore. Often it's just theoretical and comparative measurements.

      All that said, the strongest (non hypothetical) acid known to man is CH5+ (which is just Methane bombarded with protons) which has a pka of around -50. The strongest bases on the other hand are substances like n-Buthyllithium or t-BuLi which have a pka of around 40 if I'm not misremembering.
