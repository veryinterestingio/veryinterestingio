      Well, psychological traits can be inherited, yeah. Behaviours can be innate, meaning they are not learned, and it is known that mental disorders such as schizophrenia or depression run in families, even in family members separated in birth and raised by different people, even different cultures. I don't know exactly the mechanism, but my best guess is genes that regulate the expression of certain chemicals in the brain, and genes that affect the physical distribution of neural pathways in the brain.

      This all makes sense, if you think of it. You share 50% of your genes with mom, dad and your siblings, as much as with any of your children. So say you have some genetic mutations that make you more predisposed to be empathetic with them, feed them when they are hungry, care for them when they are sick. You have 5 kids that carry your genes, and 6 siblings that do too, and with your nurturing behaviour all survive. Your genes have spread far more than your 5 kids, as each of your siblings, that survived thanks to you, have their own kids, who share some of their dna with you. These nurturing genes clearly gave you an advantage.

      Another example can be innate fears. Spiders and snakes cause fear. At some point in evolution, the people who had some genetic traits that made them more likely to flee snakes and spiders survived far more than their fearless counterparts, and their genes survived with them. The colour red raises alertness, is the colour we detect the fastest, which, considering red is the colour of blood and ripe fruit and of genitals ready for mating, is no wonder we're so fast to see it. Sucking milk for infants, or not breathing when underwater for aquatic and semiaquatic mammals.
