    • ColossalCretin

      Probably kind of like this.

      We know water is on the surface of the planet, so whatever is deeper is probably denser than water.

      Of the densest common elements, iron is a good candidate. Iron is roughly 7.8 times denser than water. So that's probably around the limit.

      So it's safe to say Earth is most likely between 1-8 times denser than water.

      Then you add some other information, like the density of common rock being somewhere around 3-5 times that of water and estimating the gradient of change as you go to the core and eventually you arrive at the 5t/m^3 , that is five times denser than water estimate.
