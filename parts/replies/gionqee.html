      "Breaking" time-reversal symmetry (or any symmetry really) is a statement about specific states of system. It's saying that the interactions between the particles favour a set of configurations (i.e. they have the lowest energy) that are not symmetric under some symmetry of the world.

      E.g. the interactions between atoms favour crystals, which pick out a particular set of spacing between the atoms. There are an infinite number of ways to do this -- since space is isotropic I can set up the origin for my crystal anywhere (similarly it's orientation) -- the energy is the same.

      If that's the case then if you lower the temperature enough (+ a few other technical caveats) then the system strongly prefers to be in one of those states, so much so that at some point even an infinitely small perturbation (a stray electric field, an imperfection in a container, etc, etc) will be enough to pick out one of those states. (that's the transition temperature).

      At that point you might why isn't the system moving between these states? They cost the same energy (since they're related by symmetry), so it should be easy, right? While that's true, but these are states that involve the correlated behaviour of many many atoms -- for a solid this is like \~10\^23 atoms. So the probability of some random fluctuation or something act in such a way that it moves all the atoms coherently becomes vanishingly small.

      So the system gets "stuck" (kinematically) in a state that isn't invariant under the original symmetries that we started with. This is what is called "spontaneously broken symmetry". Technically it's a break down of ergodicity and leads to a (mild) breaking of statistical mechanics (due to the subtlety of taking the number of atoms to infinity).

      The argument for time-reversal is the same; magnetic moments or whatever line up due to their mutual interactions. Time-reversal symmetry says there are at least two ways to do this -- e.g. say all "spinning" clockwise or anti-clockwise -- at the same energy. But if a macroscopic number of degrees of freedom are involved them it'll get stuck in one of those two states "breaking" the symmetry.

      So now the physics inside that object effectively doesn't obey time-reversal -- if you lived in there you'd see everything around you spinning clockwise, but never anti-clockwise. You'd have to flip all those ordered spins at once -- an exceedingly (like wait >> the lifetime of the universe) rare event.
