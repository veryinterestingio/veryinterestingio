  • Drakk_

    If you have warm brine beneath colder fresher water, both salt and heat will diffuse across the boundary, but heat diffuses faster than salt. This means that the fresh water right above the boundary will be warmer than usual, so it will rise into the freshwater above. Contrariwise, the uppermost part of the brine will be cooled, and sink into the brine. This creates strong mixing within each layer, but sharpens the boundary between them.

    • agate_

      I have actually seen this phenomenon demonstrated in a beaker, not with salt and heat, but with sugar and salt. (Dissolved sugar diffuses across the boundary 3 times slower than salt does.) It doesn't work well with heat because heat can conduct out through the walls of the container.

      The demo I saw was also the other style of double diffusion: in which "warm, salty" water overlies "cold fresh", leading to rising and sinking "fingers" of the the two fluids which interleave with each other, as seen in the figure here. This style erases sharp boundaries rather than strengthening them.

      There's a photo of this demo in this review article, see Figure 3. I can't find a recent photo or any detailed recipes, unfortunately.

      And yes, the demo I saw put food coloring in the upper layer, so the fingers were easily visible. It looked really cool, but it's apparently tricky to get the concentrations exactly right.
