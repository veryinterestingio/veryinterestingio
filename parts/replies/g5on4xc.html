      • HopefulGuy1

        Any random decay process can in theory take arbitrarily long- the half-life is just the mean time taken for half of the thing in question to decay, and there isn't an upper limit on how long it can take. The radioactivity of a sample is modelled as decaying exponentially because that is its average behaviour- if you run the same experiment on more and more samples, the average number of undecayed particles would follow the curve. At large time t, your number of undecayed particles would average to near 0, but in any given experiment it could be 0, or 1, or 1000, or whatever you like (just with extremely low probability).

        Of course, exponential decay in velocity is also not something that can last forever because you can't ever just create a situation where only the force you're modelling is acting, and there will always be external forces influencing the motion. That said, it can last far longer than exponential growth, which gets too big too fast in general,
