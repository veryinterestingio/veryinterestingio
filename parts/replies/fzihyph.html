• ImplicitEmpiricism

    Animals usually bite off the cord. It’s easier to hold/manipulate/feed a baby without a cord and a placenta attached.

    Among primates, Jane Goodall witnesses that chimps would leave the cord attached and it would dry and fall off within a day of birth, but apes will bite it off.

    From this we can extrapolate that early man would either leave it until it dried and fell off naturally (4-5 days? That’s how long the cord stump needs) or they bit through it so they wouldn’t have to carry it around.

    Medical doctor, not an anthropologist, but I did some reading on the topic in med school because I thought it was interesting.

    There's a more or less recent trend called Lotus Birth, which leaves the placenta attatched to the baby until it falls of (because it's decomposing.) There's obvious heavy health risks for the baby though.

    Cutting the cord early and not clamping it could be a risk of bleeding out for both mother and baby I think. It's generally clamped on both sides and cut between the clamps.

    Cutting and clamping is usually done after the cord has ceased beating to give baby the benefit of added iron and oxygen. (source: am third time mom with four week old baby)
