    • tuekappel

      CO2 is really hard to measure, because it will differ from body part to body part.

      Even O2 is hard to measure; most O2-meters are placed on the fingertip. During a dive, outer extremities are vaso-constricted, meaning that blood flow is much lower in fingers, hand, arms. So O2 level needs to be measured close to the inner organs or brain, since that is where the body tries to keep all the oxygenated blood.

  • Dr__Chris

    You don't significantly increase your blood oxygen content by doing this. Haemoglobin in your blood can't go beyond 100% saturated which is only a very small change from 97%-99% breathing air. The way that breathing oxygen for a while works is that you replace the air (78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen) which normally fills your lungs with pure oxygen. As your body uses the oxygen from your blood you continue to absorb the reserve in your lungs.
