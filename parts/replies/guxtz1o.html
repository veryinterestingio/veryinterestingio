    There's no good reason for shorting to not be a thing, but plenty of good reasons why naked shorting is already illegal, albeit a mostly unenforceable legality.

    If you allow short positions to be opened when the shares to cover them don't exist, you provide a perverse incentive for anyone with a short position to do whatever they can to try to find a way to end that company as a going concern. That's the GME situation in a nutshell, a short interest over 100% (over an extended period of time, not just due to reporting fluctuations) indicates that some subset of short positions were taken with the assumption that the price would go to 0 or even lower.

    That's not price discovery at work; prices for equities don't go below zero. It's a scam.

        Simple, you depress a company's stock price long enough that it either defaults on its loans, is de-listed from the exchange, or one leads to the other.

        In the case of GME, both were very likely scenarios prior to January. In 2020 it was trading at around $4 a share and at serious risk of de-listing from the NYSE. Actions were taken by the company to prevent that, namely a share buyback to stabilize the price, but they weren't a guarantee; if GME had been de-listed, or just remained at $4 a share for the last 6 months, they were almost guaranteed to default on bonds coming to maturity in 2021.
