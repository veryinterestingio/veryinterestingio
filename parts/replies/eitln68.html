    Horizon distance = sqrt[(Moon Radius + Eye Height)2 - (Moon radius)2] = approx. 2.64km.

    A consequence for the curious: if you were to stand at the center of any of the larger craters (diameters from tens to ~100km), you wouldn't know you were in a crater. The crater walls (mountain ranges) would be far beyond the horizon.

    For reference, here's Plato. The four larger craterlets on the crater floor have diameters of about 2-2.5km (so the crater wall would be discernible in those).

    • svenson_26

      Sure. Using the same method but plugging in the earth's radius of 6371km, and adjusting your eye level height up in order to get a curvature of over 1cm across a meter stick held at arms length, you would need to be at a height of 2300m.
      That is high up, but not crazy high. It's taller than any man made tower, but there are mountains that are certainly that tall. If you could find a mountain that has a flat horizon you can see, then you should be able to tell (maybe looking out over the ocean on a clear day from Mauna Kea at 4200m).
      Airplane cruising altitude is at least four times the height you need. At a cruising altitude of 10,000m you would see over 2cm in deflection across your meter stick.
