• Therese_bird_brain

    That's a great question, and exactly what we have collectively dedicated years to figuring out! We study zebra finches specifically, and they learn when they are young by forming a memory of the song of their father, then practicing their song until it matches the memory of the father. They have dedicated circuits in their brains to calculate how close or different their song is to the example in their minds. Other songbirds share similar learning systems, but they aren't all exactly the same. Hummingbirds and parrots are other classes of birds that also learn their songs/sounds, but their strategies can be very different from songbirds'.

      • Therese_bird_brain

        That is an excellent question and a very hot area of research right now! It is hard for us to calculate the distance between two songs, which makes it hard to figure out how the birds are doing this. We know that there are parts of the brain that fire more when the songs are very similar or very different, but we don't know where in the brain or how these 'error signals' are calculated yet. People are looking at what brain areas send information to the areas with 'error signals' to try to work backwards and find this sight of comparison, but that is very much a work in progress.
