      So I'm going to have to disagree with /u/ExtonGuy with the caveat that I may very well be totally wrong and after such a huge supernova (enough that iron was actually the endpoint of nuclear fusion) everything is atomized....

      Well, let's think about it.... There's likely enough energy around that iron exists with a number of electrons (all?) stripped off. That would leave each iron atom with a hefty positive charge....

      It may very well be that the result is an atomic mist that expands somewhat spherically from the central supernova...

      But I'm going to guess, and I'm thinking of the Crab Nebula and its composite image, that as this atomized stellar dust starts to cool, the various speeds of the ejecta start to cause bottlenecks, you're going to start to see "ribbons of matter coalesce around the shock waves (physical, EM, gravitational?) in a somewhat spherical shell outward bound".

      These are huge stars with lots of matter. Just as smoke from burning hydrocarbons (basically soot) can form as the particles in the smoke cool and coalesce, I'm guessing that supernova ejecta will similarly cool and coalesce.

      If there remains a neutron star or black hole at the center, then you may see an "inward light shining out" effect as the EM radiation beats the "soot" and makes it glow.
