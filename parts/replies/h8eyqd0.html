  • AceJohnny

    Each of those lines can be represented as a binary digit, a 1 or a 0, depending on whether it's black or white.

    This is incorrect, UPC codes don't encode values directly as the black-or-white bars. For example, 0xFF would a lot of white (or black bars) but you never see barcodes with extra-super-thick lines. You'll only ever see "single thickness" or "double-thickness" white or black lines.

    The reason UPC codes don't just use black and white as 0 or 1 is to make it easier to read with the bardcode is at an angle or on a wrinkled wrapper (like on bread packaging), where a fold at the wrong place would transform a single 0 into multiples (or make it disappear).

    Here's more info on how UPC barcodes are actually encoded: https://www.cut-the-knot.org/do_you_know/BarcodeEncoding.shtml

    • just_some_a--hole

      Bar codes used the SME patterns to determine how to read the bits. The S and E patterns are 101 and the M pattern is 01010. By reading these three patterns (at the Start, Middle, and End of the code) the system can tell where each bit begins and ends, which is how it handles reading the code at an angle. It can read the code upside down because of the parity bits added to each encoded number (odd parity for the left, even parity for the right).

      And yes, they UPC codes do use black for 1 and white for 0. The encoding is unique, though, so you can't compare it to ASCII or any other encoding value.
