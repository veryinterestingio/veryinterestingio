    If you're looking at ionic conductors, it only really matters what the net charge is of the ion. For example silver iodide is the classic example of ionic conductors, and it's the positively charged silver ion that can readily diffuse in the alpha phase. It's actually kind of cool, where the silver sublattice essentially melts at elevated temperatures and can flow through various sites within the BCC iodine lattice. Fuel cells though typically rely on protons diffusing through the electrolyte to fully complete the circuit so you actually have charge balancing, so I wouldn't say it's uncommon even though fuel cells still use electrons through whatever circuit it's powering.
