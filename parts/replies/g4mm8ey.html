    As I scroll through the answers here, yours is the closest that I have seen, but needs a little more. As you break the milk fat globules down into smaller and smaller pieces, their surface area increases and you no longer have enough phospholipids to encase them, leaving hydrophobic patches exposed which interact with the protein casein to stabilize them. Also your terminology is a little off. The milk fat globules are not micelles as it is a lipid bilayer that surrounds the triglycerides, so it is a membrane structure (in fact it is directly derived from cellular membranes during production of the globules). It is the casein that is generally referred to as micelles, intrinsically disordered proteins with their mostly hydrophillic parts on the outside and hydrophobic parts on the inside. Interactions with the disrupted globules after homogenization causes rearrangements in this allowing the casein to act as an emulsifier.
