    Why does cavitation eat away at the internal components of, eg, vacuum pumps, but doesn’t eat away at the inside of tea kettles?

    • The_Fredrik

      The cavities are created in the lower pressure at the suction side of the impeller blade (not by heating the water, but by lowering the pressure. You might know that water boils at a lower temperature on Mount Everest? Same thing!), and as the next blade’s pressure side “catches up” with it the pressure increases again and the cavities collapse.

      Thing is, now the cavities are really close to the blade surface, and they collapse inwards on themselves in kind of doughnut shape (think smoke rings). This creates a powerful water jet through the center of the cavity-doughnut of up to a 1000 times the ambient pressure. This water jet hits the blade (or whatever part it comes into contact with) and literally rips materials away.
