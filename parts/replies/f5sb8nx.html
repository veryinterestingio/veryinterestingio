    The mechanical and electrical properties of copper are very dependent on these defects, with lots of effort put into processing the material to control them.

    The properties are also dependent on having enough atoms together for properties like the electronic band structure to emerge. A few atoms of copper isn't nearly enough to show the presence of a conduction band.

    For that matter, if you were to somehow build a single unit cell and isolate it, it would probably spontaneously rearrange into something unrecognizable.

  • racinreaver

    A good way of thinking about it is the ratio of surface area to volume. Atoms away from a surface have no idea how far they are from a surface, and behave like "bulk" materials. Atoms on and near a surface have distorted bonds, giving them different properties than the bulk. Of you have enough surface that a large percent are now surface atoms, you suddenly get very different properties. This is why nanostructures can become interesting.
