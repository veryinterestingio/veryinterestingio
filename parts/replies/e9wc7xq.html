    This is a good answer. It depends how you define acid.

    Arrhenius: MUST BE IN AQUEOUS STATE (dissolved in water), and must increase the [H+] in solution. Is it at acid before it is dissolved? Perhaps a semantic issue here.

    Bronsted-Lowry: Must donate a proton (H+). Could be any state in theory.

    Lewis: Must accept electrons. Could be any state. We don't even need to think of water as the medium or solvent.

    ​

    Another note for OP, who I believe is probably thinking of Arrhenius acids: liquid is not the same as aqueous. Liquid particles can freely flow past each other in pure form; but Arrhenius acids are not liquids per se, they are in an aqueous solution. This means that they are dissolved within a liquid. For instance, HCl is a molecular gas in pure form, but when it is dissolved in water, the H+ and Cl- dissociate, giving the solution more H+, thus making it acidic. Water (H2O) can be thought of as H+ and OH-. When there is exactly as much H+ as OH-, the water is neutral, but when there is more H+ than OH-, the solution is acidic.
