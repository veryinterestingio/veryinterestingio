      It's because there were different strains of influenza in the area you moved to compared to where you used to live.

      Every time you get a cold or flu it's a different or varied version of a previous one caused by random mutations and genetic reassortment of the viral code.

      When you stay in 1 location every cold or flu you get is usually quite similar to a previous cold/flu so your immune system quickly detects the virus and fights it. When you move to a new area you're being exposed to a completely new family of viruses your body hasn't seen before. Meaning you're Ill more often with higher severity.

      Influenza typically infects better during cold weather, however this doesn't completely prevent its spread.

    • duglarri

      Flu is much more transmissible when it's damp. That's usually winter for temperate climates, which is why we Northerners associate flu with winter. Things dry out more quickly in summer, so a sneeze doesn't stay virulent on a doorknob as long.

      In a hot, humid climate, the transmission vectors would be present on warm days as well.

      Diseases generally do perform better in warmer environments. Much of the effect of higher transmission during winter in non-equatorial regions comes from people spending more time inside in closer proximity, not from a more direct effect on the disease itself.
