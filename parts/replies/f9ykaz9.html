  • pagethirtyfour

    Yes, your body tries to actively dispel food and drink if it winds up where it shouldn’t, but sometimes the body just doesn’t work the way it’s supposed to. In people with dysphagia, the process of swallowing is impaired and so they may not have a coughing reflex. People who silently aspirate are more at risk for pneumonia because they’re not always aware that their food/drink has gone down the wrong way.

    You’re right to not suppress a cough! Also take a break from eating/drinking after choking or coughing, don’t just try to wash it down with more water!

    • DieCastRN

      Remember there are two pipes - the esophagus and the trachea. The trachea serves the lungs while the esophagus serves the stomach. If you’re not coughing and having trouble breathing, feeling like food is in your windpipe, etc..., you’re likely getting food stuck in the esophagus. With the esophagus you want to get food down, with the trachea you want to get food up and out.
