    Correct! Let's do a simple calculation.

    A server has 1000 players. Let's say every player moves 1 time per second. This means that they send a message "I moved here" to server 1 time per second, and server in response sends message "This guy moved here" to everyone who can see you.

    If all 1000 players are spread out in small groups 10 players each, every player will make the server send 9 messages every second to the people who can see you. This results in 9000 messages every second.

    If 500 players are in one huge group and 500 are in separate groups 10 players each, then we have a different scenario.

    500 of them are responsible for 9 messages per second, and 500 of them are responsible for 499 messages per second.

    This results in 254000 messages per second. This is 28x more messages to process and send.

        The other side of this issue is that combining systems to handle more connections scales in the opposite direction, logarithmically. This is known as Concurrency.

        Each process ends up having to wait for others to finish what they are doing to ensure one process isn't using information while it is being rewritten by another process. They end up spending a significant amount of time just waiting or "blocking". Not to mention the job of overseeing who gets to access what information when takes additional computation work. Writing concurrency code is a massive cluster fuck compared to linear computation.

        So doubling players makes more than double the work, while doubling your hardware gives diminishing returns the larger a system you are trying to split a task across.

        This outlines how important it is to keep the scope of your problem small enough that it can be done on fewer physical machines, the ratio of work required and cost to acheive that work gets unreasonable very quickly
