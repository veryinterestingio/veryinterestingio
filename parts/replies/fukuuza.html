      I'll give it a try for capacitance:

      You know what a capacitor is: In the simplest case, two plates of metal with some insulator in between.

      Let's first imagine the plates to be far away from each other. We connect a battery, which then charges the two plates. What does this mean? The battery (remember: an electron pump) pulls electrons from one plate (the one connected to its plus terminal) and pushes them onto the other plate.

      Now remember that there are not only moveable electrons in the metal plates but also the rest of the atoms: the whole atom, including it movable outer electrons, the immovable inner electrons and the positively charged atomic nucleus, is electrically neutral. However, once the battery pulls electrons off the plate, the charges are no longer balanced and the whole plate is now positively charged. The other plate, with the extra electrons pushed onto it, is negatively charged.

      This is why the battery will only be able to pump for a brief moment. Then, the positively charged plate will pull back the electrons that the battery tries to pull off, and the positively charged plate will push away the electrons that the battery tries to push onto it. Our circuit is not closed, so everything comes to a standstill after only a very little bit of current has flown.

      But now, we bring the two plates really close together. Now, the electrons that the battery tries to push onto the negative plate will not only feel the repelling push from the electrons that have already been pushed there but also the pull from the positively charged plate which is now so close by.

      And the electrons that the battery tries to pull from the positive plate will get a little extra push from the negative plate that is now right next to it. So, even though the two plates are not connected, they are so close that the electrons feel the charge from the other plate.

      And hence, things can move again and more current can flow before the system comes to a standstill again. With the plates together, the battery can pump more electrons from one plate to the other than before, when the plates were far apart. We say that the "capacity" of the two plates to take up charge has increased because we moved the plates together.

      A capacitance of 1 Farad means that a battery with 1 Volt will manage to pump 1 Coulomb of charge (i.e., 6*10^18 electrons) from one side of the capacitor to the other before current flow stops. To get a high capacitance, you need to make the plates big (to offer room for the electrons) and get the plates close together (so that the electron can feel the charge on the other plate). This is why the capacitance of a plate capacitor is proportional to the area of the plates divided by distance between the plates.
