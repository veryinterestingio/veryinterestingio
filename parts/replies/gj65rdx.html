    • Unicornmarauder1776

      The answer to your question is that the temperature steam can reach is generally dictated by the pressure it is at. In very low pressures or even vacuum, steam can form at extremely low temperatures. In very high pressures, water won't even boil at 100 C. So, in a sense, you are correct that steam won't reach above 100 C at sea level under normal circumstances. We heat the steam by restricting or containing it and it generates the pressure as a result of adding more heat (raising the temperature).

      If you heat water to boiling and put a lid on, the steam will get hotter than 100 C because it generates its own pressure
