  • AmberVeil

    So basically does it mean that asymptomatic patients either have a strong pathogen able to suppress the immune system or weak pathogens that does not do that much damage to trigger symptoms?

    • Handsome_Claptrap

      "Strong" and "weak" don't make much sense in this context.

      Some pathogens can reduce the immune system effectiveness, but generally speaking they can't completely suppress it. This however depends on the virus species, some viruses just don't have any weapon to do so.

      As for "weak" pathogens, some are really weak and tend to cause asymptomatic infections, such as many Herpesvirus species. In other cases it's more correct to talk of a weak infection, either because the amount of viruses that first infected you is small or because your immune system reacted more effectively against it.

      Let's say we have two healthy adult, an elder and an unlucky dude with severe AIDS that come in contact with a person with a cold.

      The first adult simply talked a brief time with the infected person, so few virus copies entered his airways. His immune system is strong and he will likely get an asympomatic infection.

      The second adult instead stayed in the same room of the other person for some hours, he came in contact with a lot of virus copies. Despite his strong immune system, he develops symptoms.

      The elder talked a brief time but his immune system is weak. He developes symptoms.

      The dude with AIDS didn't even talk with the infected person, he just walked by him, but it was enough to get in contact with a minimal amount of virus copies. His immune system however is severely damaged by HIV, so he gets a very bad infection.
