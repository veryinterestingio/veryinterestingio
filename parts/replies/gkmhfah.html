    You're correct, however it's important to not devalue reducing your vices. For things like eating healthier and cutting consumption of alcohol it's absolutely awesome to simply reduce it at first.

    One reason that so many people fall back into their vices is going from a very unhealthy lifestyle to immediately trying to live how their goal lifestyle lives. If your goal is to only eat home cooked meals and drink no alcohol, that progression will absolutely take months of effort and preparation, changing it overnight is the wrong approach for the vast majority of people. Although it's great to have a long streak without a vice, that's not the starting point for quitting something, the starting point should be a small change that's doable. If you smoke 15 cigarettes a day, try smoking only 12 per day for a week. Then 9, reducing it slowly.

    The same goes for beginning to exercise too, too much too early is detrimental. You cant change a habit overnight and you cant create an entirely new big habit overnight either.

    I think OP chose a poor example to illustrate the question. The question is about streaks.

    An apples to apples comparison would be a 6-month streak of no fast food VERSUS a 7-month period in which 3 months were no fast food, one month with the same amount of fast food consumption as before this exercise, and then another 3 months with zero fast food.

    So the question remains. Why is the former more satisfying than the latter, even if they’re pretty much the same amount of fast food consumption?

    I think an explanation is that we see each “good” day as multiplicative rather than additive. We don’t take each day one at a time, except when we’re bad. Then it’s just “let me have a cheat day” and then hope that tomorrow is better.
