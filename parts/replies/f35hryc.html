    While I cannot contribute much more information. I feel it is worth noting that rayleigh scattering alone provides a brighter horizon with the sun overhead (e.g. midday hours). Mie scattering also contributes to a brighter horizon (e.g. a hazy day) and the white halo around the sun.

    Comparison of computer sim sky @ midday, Rayleigh vs Rayleigh+Mie from a fantastic article on CG skies. Full article here

    Another effect that contributes to the lighter horizon is ground albedo. Light reflected from the ground bounces back into the atmosphere and gets scattered all over again. This results in a not-insignificant lighting of the sky near the ground (intuitive, if you imagined the ground being lit like under midday sun but no sun in the sky).     • qwiglydee

      Ther are two types of scattering.

      One of it is Reyleigh scattering in air. It scatters blue light more than other. And it is responsible for blue color of the sky (scattered light) and red color of sun at dawn/dusk (sun light with all blue scattered away).

      Another one is Mie scattering in haze. It scatters all light equally, including the light that has been already scattered. It is responsible for spreading light near horizon.

      There are links in another comment.

      The OP asked about "lightness" near horizon. Which is commonly understood as "whiteness". And that is result of Reyleigh scattering.

    But when you look to horizon your look goes through about 5 km full of the haze

    Most of the haze and dust in the atmosphere is troposphere. The top of the troposphere/ bottom of the stratosphere is at about 33k feet / 10km. So when you look straight up, you're looking through ~ 10km of haze. When you look at the horizon, you're looking slantwise through that 10km, far enough that the curvature of the earth comes into equation. Bottomline though, it's in the 100's of km, not 5 km.
