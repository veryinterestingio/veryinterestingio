    • Nemo222

      Altitude data is absolutely used to supplement GPS data. GPS is relatively accurate in x and y on the surface of the earth but is pretty garbage for altitude/elevation. Adding a barometric altitude changes how location is calculated and gives better positional accuracy. More simple GPS uses a lookup table for elevation but that limits the accuracy to how fine a resolution that lookup table has. Local weather data is available with calibrated barometric pressure so variations in weather can be compensated for.

      It turns +/- 50ft into +/- 5ft which is about the limit where you need to do the crazy spectrum analysis to compensate for error caused by signals through the atmosphere.

      • TheBunkerKing

        The GNSS calculations aren't exactly rocket science: the satellite sends the user data of its position and current time of its clock. More advanced receivers also read the phase of the signal wave to enhance accuracy, but phones of course don't.

        The elevation error is due to receivers measuring the ellipsoidal elevation, while Earth isn't a perfect ellipsoid. But still, in normal conditions you're getting a lot more accurate data even on cheap receivers than you do on some clock or phone barometer. At least I've never heard of such barometers getting 5ft error or even close to that.

        If the satellite horizon is poor, your accuracy will suffer to all dimensions - vertical is usually about double the error horizontal is. But phones aren't very good at this in general - at work we usually get to less than 5cm elevation errors with our receivers.
