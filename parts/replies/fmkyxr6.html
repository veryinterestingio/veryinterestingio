  • fm1185

    Biology prof here. While what you said is largely correct, to assume that by becoming avirulent (causing less harm to the host) a virus will always increase lifetime reproductive output is erroneous. There is often a trade-off between virulence (causing harm to the host) and transmission. For example, viruses replicate by hijacking host cells. This is bad for the host but increases the transmission of the virus.

    We often see parasites and pathogens evolving to an intermediate level of virulence and there are a number of small changes to the environment of the parasite or pathogen that can influence how virulent the PP is. For example, if a pathogen is spread through the bite of a tsetse fly, it may make evolutionary "sense" to incapacitate the host in some way which makes them less likely to swat or resist the fly. We see this with African trypanosomes which cause sleeping sickness.

    The classic example of an intermediate virulence level is seen with myoma virus in European rabbits. Initially, the virus killed 99% of rabbits infected. Within just a few years the lethality dropped because dead rabbits cant transmit viruses. However, the lethality stayed relatively constant over time after the initial drop. Basically the virus keeps its host alive just long enough to be transmitted.

      • fm1185

        The consensus is correct and we are full of harmless viruses. Selection pressure is focused on increasing lifetime reproductive output. Sometimes this means being innocuous, other times the PP can get away with being more virulent.

        Bacteria are famous for swapping genes (this is called horizontal gene transfer) but viruses can as well (rarer) if two viruses infect the same cell.

        You mentioned coinfection which is a fascinating topic! Theory dictates that if two parasites coinfect the same host at the same time, evolution favors increased virulence (what good is keeping a host alive if the other parasite is just going to harm them anyway). Escalating virulence appears to be the only solution to "within-host selection". On the other hand, if the two different parasite species rarely infect the same host, and the host is long-lived, then lower levels of virulence are selected for (as /u/jdlech mentioned total reproductive output depends in part on keeping the host alive longer so that you can get more out of them). The "optimal" virulence of a PP can rise or fall over time depending on the relative frequency of each parasite or pathogen.
