    To add to that, those hundreds or thousands of km make a huge difference on gravitational effects during the near-pass portion of an orbit. The trajectory of the asteroid will be bent slightly each time it passes near a massive object, and that 0.001 degree error on the first pass could mean a thousands of km closer pass the next time around, making the following pass even more uncertain.

    This is the reason that when you hear news about an asteroid that may hit earth, it's almost always "not this time, but X years out". It's also why if you read a little further, you may encounter the term "gravitational keyhole". This is basically saying that given all of the uncertainty figuring out where an asteroid might go is really hard, so it's easier to figure out just the orbits that will lead to a collision, and then check the measured trajectory against those values.
