    • Sevardos

      The schwartzschild radius is defined as r = 2 G * m / c\^2. If the mass m is within the radius r, its a black hole.

      So in principle you could have an arbitrarily low mass to form a black hole, you just need to put the mass (= energy) into a small radius. But there are at least two limits:

      1. hawking radiation: black holes "evaporate". smaller ones faster, so very small black holes are very short lived.
      2. quantum physics. When the scale is small enough, physics starts doing weird stuff. One example of this is that mass cannot be precisely localized. So the mass cant be confined to an abitrarily small radius, which puts a lower limit to black hole mass and radius. This mass limit equals roughly the planck mass, or 2.2e-8 kg
