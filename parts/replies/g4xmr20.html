      And to add to your note, because gravity falls with the square of distance, it won't take too large a distance before the lack of gravity becomes significant.

      A simple equation to find the distance to the Earth's surface D for any given strength of gravity relative to Earth's surface is:

      D = 3960/√(G%/100) - 3960

      The distance at which Earth's gravity is 50% of surface gravity is 3960/(√.5) - 3960 ≈ 1640 miles, or about 6.5 times the distance of the ISS.

      100(3960/(D + 3960))^2 = G%

      Conversely, the above equation gives you the strength of Earth's gravity for any distance D.

      At the distance of the Moon, Earth's gravity is 0.014%

  • CrustalTrudger

    At the center of a spherical Earth of uniform density, you would accelerate equally in every direction.

    This is true even if a spherical Earth didn't have uniform density, as long as the density variations were concentric spheres. The non homogeneous, but still perfectly concentric spherical layers, density case changes what the gravitational acceleration at points throughout the sphere would be and deviate from what you would calculate for a uniform density sphere, but at the center, everything would still average out to 0, i.e. you are being 'pulled' equally in all directions so experience no acceleration.
