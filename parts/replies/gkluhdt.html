    • Teanut

      About half of the heat is from radioactive decay/fission (~21 TW per https://newscenter.lbl.gov/2011/07/17/kamland-geoneutrinos/)

      The rest is primordial heat: heat leftover from the formation of the planet, from the kinetic energy of meteorite/comet impacts, and also from friction when iron rich material sinks in the mantle (and also silica rich materials rise.) Other things, too, and it's still an active area of research. Some are looking into if there's other fission and even fusion reactions going on in Earth's interior.

      Also, remember the Sun still dominates Earth's heat budget. The Sun contributes about 173,000 TW of solar radiation, while the heat "loss" from the Earth's interior (i.e. the flow of heat from the interior to the surface) is somewhere around 44 TW to 47 TW depending on your source. A drop in the bucket in terms of influence on surface temperature. Rocks and space are pretty good insulators.
