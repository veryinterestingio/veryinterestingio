  • sbdeli

    There’s a lot more causes than just this. At any given time there are far more people who own homes than there are people looking for homes.

    Our public policy historically has been more concerned with ensuring that homes are increasing in value (and therefore that homeowners are happy) than ensuring that new housing is being created. Existing homeowners are usually entrenched in their communities and have more political power to influence local government than “potential” homeowners, and use bureaucratic measures like zoning hearings to protect their interests in property value. Even leaving aside that people are generally resistant to the idea of outsiders coming into their community, it’s basic supply and demand. Limiting new supply is good for existing owners.

    One of the policies that’s been historically passable is increasing the availability of credit, either directly or through tax breaks for mortgages. In the short term it keeps everyone happy when buyers have access to money to buy, but in the long term it leads to artificial price inflation.

    Long term its been great for anyone who owns land. The losers are: younger generations, who are unable to buy homes like their parents did, and “stuck” renting later and later in life, forfeiting a large share of their income to a landlord rather than into home equity. It’s also hurt minority groups who’s parents and grandparents weren’t given access to credit (due to blatant institutional discrimination).

    In a broader sense, it’s been economically inefficient. By artificially inflating the return on real estate investment, we’ve encouraged investors to sink money into real estate rather than in other more productive investments.
