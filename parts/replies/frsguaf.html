    • fluffyrhinos

      Sorry I wasn't clear. You are right, TFs are indeed proteins. When I talk about dynamic activation, I'm not referring to the genes being expressed, I'm referring to the protein itself being activated. How this happens depends on the specific protein.

      For example, NF-kB is normally bound by an inhibitory protein that keeps it in the cytoplasm, away from the DNA. Upon activation, this protein is degraded and NF-kB translocates to the nucleus where it can regulate gene expression. p53 on the other hand, gets degraded when it is supposed to be inactive, and that degradation is inhibited when it is supposed to be activated. That way, there are only high levels of p53 when it is supposed to be active.There are other mechanisms as well.

      Many transcription factors are regulated in these post-translational ways, because it means they can react faster. NF-kB, which regulates many immune genes, can react in a matter of minutes to a stimulus, which wouldn't be possible if the cell had to express the NF-kB proteins from the DNA when it needs to respond.

      I hope this answered your question!
