    That’s really not true.

    Arthropods display a wide range of nervous competency, just like vertebrates, with eusocial Hymenopterans and jumping spiders being the pinnacles of arthropod intelligence. As a few notable examples:

    Bumblebees can learn and improve upon new skills that wouldn’t be used in the wild by watching humans or other bumblebees.

    Honeybees can learn to count to 4, and, more impressively, even understand the concept of 0 as a numerical integer, which otherwise is something only humans and dolphins are known to be capable of.

    Spartaeine spiders, which prefer to target other spiders, approach prey in complicated detours; they display an understanding of object permanence, and the ability to plan in advance and memorize attack routes.

    Intelligence is a spectrum; some arthropods are more intelligent than others, just like how dolphins are more intelligent than koalas, and the most intelligent arthropods are absolutely not reducible to “reaction machines”.
