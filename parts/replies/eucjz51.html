  • Nagisan

    The US government operates a huge one.

    Not entirely. In short, NIPRNet has it's own dedicated lines and is connected to the public internet through DISA, all traffic is encrypted before traveling to the public domain. SIPRNet either has dedicated lines (airgapped), or is encrypted and then tunneled over NIPR. JWICS (next level above SIPR) can again have it's own dedicated lines (airgapped), or encrypted and tunneled over SIPR.

    So in practice, US government networks are not airgapped by default, but the connection to the public internet can be severed by DISA, making it airgapped.

    EDIT: As for OP - Companies do use airgapped networks, the problem with them is if they are airgapped you severely limit availability, part of the CIA triad (a model used to design information security policies). While you do increases confidentiality (keeping information secret) and integrity (making sure you data hasn't been changed by unauthorized people), limiting availability may mean your employees have difficulty doing their job. Truly airgapped systems are the most secure, but also the most difficult to work on, especially for a geographically diverse company.
