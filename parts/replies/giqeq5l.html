    • FogeltheVogel

      Small correction:

      We don't have cell walls. We have cell membranes. Bacteria (mostly) do have cell walls, in addition to their cell membranes.
      This is one of the key distinctions between us and bacteria (there are others) that antibiotics use to be lethal to bacteria, and harmless to us: They (some of them) attack the cell wall, which we don't have in the first place.

      Note that this doesn't matter for chlorine and iodine, which attack indiscriminately.

  • Gastronomicus

    The mechanism by which halogens such as chlorine or iodine kill microbes is not fully understood.

    It's not very complicated - they're strong oxidisers and non-specifically break down organic materials at the molecular level by ripping out electrons, breaking bonds and forming new molecules. Since microbes are simple single-cells and lack the more sophisticated multi-cellular structures and biochemical defences that eukaryotic cells possess, they're more susceptible to this damage.
