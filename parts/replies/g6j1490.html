    • setecordas

      There are fundamental limitations in DNA synthesis, and especially RNA synthesis, that prevent the synthesis of very long stretches of nucleotides. With DNA, you reach a limit of around 200 to maybe 245 nucleotides. Each base has its own coupling efficiency (probability that it will couple to the next base in sequence), and the synthesizer, method, and reagents used can have a large impact as well. Overall coupling efficiency is back calculated once you've synthesized crude oligonucleotide by a simple equation: E = P^(n-1) where P is your average coupling efficiency and n is the length of the sequence. The solid support onwhich synthesis occurs normally has the first base attached, so you use n-1 in the exponent.

      After cleavage and deprotection, you can run some of your crude sample on an LCMS to get a purity estimate of your full length product (FLP), and you can see how efficient or successful the synthesis was before you take it through purification. If you synthesized a 100nt strand of RNA and find 30% FLP purity through LCMS, then

      0.3 = P^(99)

      So your coupling efficience is 98.79% which is not great, but is not totally unexpected. If you are able to bump up your coupling efficiency to 99.5%, then you would expect a doubling of FLP to 60%, which is much, much better.

      As you can see, small changes to coupling efficiency lead to substantial increases in purity. At the same time, increasing and decreasing strand length has a huge impact on yield. For RNA, an average coupling efficiency of 99.5% is very good.

      If you took the method that gave you a 98.79% coupling efficiency and tried to synthesize a 200nt length oligo, you would have less than 9% crude FLP and 91% truncations and branchings. In comparison, a 99.5% average coupling efficiency would theoretically allow you to synthesize a 200nt oligo, but beyond around 150nt, coupling efficient will tend to drop off precipitously due to packing/stacking effects of the oligo on the solid support.

      Techniques to get really long gene sequences involves enzymatically ligating short sequences until the desired length and sequence is aquired.
