    However, you shouldn't be worried about any health effects. A small amount of leakage isn't any different from the WiFi signal itself. RF radiation at the frequencies that microwaves use just does what it does inside a microwave: heat things up. It doesn't cause cancer, because it is non-ionizing radiation.

    I just want to re-iterate this part for anyone who's concerned.

    "Radiation" is a scary word, but we're all exposed to all kinds of radiation all the time, and our bodies typically deal with it just fine.

    Ionizing radiation is the problem--radiation with so much energy that it can strip electrons from the molecules in your body. That includes the kind of particle radiation that results from nuclear fission and decay, as well as things like X-ray band radiation.

    If you look at any of the typical ways we introduce the electromagnetic spectrum to learners, you'll notice that X-rays and gamma rays are past the "purple" end of the visual light spectrum--they carry more energy than visible light, which is how they can strip those electrons and damage your body.

    Microwaves, radar, radio waves, and a few other things are past the "red" end of visible light. In ordinary exposure amounts, these do not pose health hazards. (That is, if you fall asleep naked two feet in front of an operating radar antenna, this might not apply, but in most real-world circumstances you're fine.) Those kinds of radiation don't have enough energy to do anything to you--your body is less likely to be damaged than it is by visible light.

    A leaky microwave is less efficient while it's operating, and it can interfere with some consumer electronics. But it's not going to give you cancer.

        The intensity of the radiation is not a factor of causing or not causing health problems.

        Yeah, I was trying to plot a middle course in my post between ignoring the potential complicating factors and getting lost in the weeds. I might not have hit the right balance.

        But obviously your statement here isn't strictly true. If you did something really dumb with an infrared cutting laser, you could cause some superficial burns.

        And that illuminates a more general rule: the risk of tissue damage and other adverse health effects from non-ionizing radiation is proportional to flux (power per unit area). The more power a device puts out, and the more directional the array, the more health risks there are.

        All of the reliably demonstrated effects are due to heating, and in order to see them, you have to have so much incident energy that you exceed the body's ability to dissipate it. That is, the risk is apparently lower, as well as categorically different, from what you encounter with ionizing radiation. So usually moderate engineering controls are all you really need: you add an interlock to make sure the cover of the cutting array won't open while it's in operation, and you put a cheap faraday cage across the microwave door.

        So. If someone asked me to stand naked two feet in front of an operating 60kW radar antenna, I would politely decline. But from a consumer standpoint, the risks are negligible--radio waves are really, really safe. Unless someone is working in laboratory or engineering jobs with specific kinds of equipment, they should literally never spend a moment worrying about it.
