    Looks like NotAPreppie nailed it. Octyal seems to be a nonstandard name for 2-ethylhexanol. So this is a nonstandard name for the ester NAP linked to. It's a soap derivative and it's used as an emulsion stabilizer.

    An emulsion (or a colloid) is a situation where you have substances that won't dissolve in one another (like oil and water), where one of the substances has been broken down into small droplets and suspended in the other.

    Normally, suspensions separate quickly (again, oil and water, or sand in water), but emulsions are somewhat stable. Through various methods, it's possible to make a situation where the droplets of substance A remain small and scattered uniformly in substance B. You can typically recognize an emulsion because it looks white. Fog and clouds are both emulsions of water in air, mayonnaise is an emulsion of oil in vinegar.

    Emulsions have varying degrees of stability. It's possible for the suspended droplets to come together, aggregate, and separate out of the suspension. That's why you have to shake a can of spraypaint, or just stir a regular can of paint, to get the separated parts to mix back together long enough for you to paint with them. By contrast, mayonnaise is quite stable.

    In order to make an emulsion, you might have to use an emulsifying agent, something that will break up substance A into those small droplets and then keep those droplets from coalescing. Soap does this with oils and grease on your skin. For mayonnaise, the emulsifier is the proteins found in egg whites.

    And that's what the octyl stearate here is doing. Cosmetics are primarily oils, but they have small amounts of water suspended in them carrying something they want in the cosmetic that won't dissolve in oil. The stearate is keeping the water suspended in the oil and not separating out.

    There's no need to be concerned about applying it to your skin; it's harmless.
