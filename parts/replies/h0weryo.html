    • r3dl3g

      Like, why not just give each note it's own letter?

      Because within Western musical conventions, each scale only ever has 7 notes, no more, no less. Thus, you will always have an A, B, C, D, E, F, and G tone, and the only way to shift those notes in order to make it fit within a given mode (e.g. major or minor) is to give them some kind of mark to shift them up or down a semitone from their nominal notes.

      For example, for a C-major or A-minor scale, you just have the nominal notes (A-G), but if you move to say G-major or E-minor, you end up needing to shift the F-key up a semitone, thus your notes are now A, B, C, D, E, F#, and G.

      This also results in a pretty self-consistent musical ruleset, in that you only end up with 12 discrete tones, but you can use those 12 tones to create any 7-note scale.

      As for why they're specifically called sharps and flats; that I don't know.
