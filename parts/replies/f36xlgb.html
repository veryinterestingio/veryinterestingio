    Definite merit. Paleontology has trends just like any field. In the middle of last century, there was a trend for a lot of things to be oversplit, and that included mistaking different ontogenetic stages (stages of development through an individual's lifetime) for different species. One of the clades I study, Lystrosaurus, was a victim of that. There were several dozen species named, and now we're down to four in Gondwana. I've looked at the Chinese Lystrosaurus and reduced that number as well in an SVP abstract, but haven't published it yet. I'll actually be hopping back on that paper when I return from Australia to push it out.

      Partially. It has more to do with inherent preservation potential. Tiny bones are more fragile, and therefore more likely to break and be ground into unrecognizeable fragments and dust.
