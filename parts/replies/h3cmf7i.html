    • dnesting

      Because AM relies on the radio's ability to distinguish between a station's quietest amplitude and its loudest amplitude in order to shape the resulting audio wave, and the strength of the wave depends on the distance to the station (inverse square law), radios have to be able to scale the wave in order for you to hear far away stations just as well as nearby stations.

      With loud, nearby stations, the background noise is such a small fraction of the wave's energy that you can't hear it, even when you listen closely when the radio broadcasts silence. With stations farther away, the radio has to scale up the wave in order to interpret the full range of its amplitude, at which point the random background noise becomes an appreciable fraction of the waveform used to shape the sound, and the quality of the sound starts to go down until the radio can't tell the difference between changes in signal amplitude, and background noise. Your ear might still pick out some patterns, but eventually the farthest signals can't compete at all.
