    This is the right answer. Ultimately, the phone only has to listen for transmissions from the tower, not actually ask if there is any data for it - when any data comes it will be announced to the phone. If the phone misses the announcement for whatever reason, the tower will send it again next time it has a chance until it is received or the whole communication breaks down. And, to save battery, they agree on the exact times data will be sent, so the phone doesn't even have to be listening most of the time.

    Once your phone falls off the network though, it has to repeatedly check to see if it sees any towers, and if it does it has to speak first to "log in" again and set up this entire communications scheme again. This is why sometimes having no coverage can drain your battery faster, and why having poor coverage that comes and goes is the worst case.

      • a_cute_epic_axis

        In many cases this is also because the phone is designed to increase or decrease its transmit power so that the signal it sends out is at the right power level for the tower. This is not only typically deployed as an energy saving mechanism, but in cases like CDMA, it's required to prevent other phones from being drowned out.

        When not connected to any tower, the phone will transmit at a higher power level to see if it actually is in range of a tower, but just unable to reach it at lower power levels.
