    One clarification- it's true that most uranium ore is <1% uranium, but most of the ore for the Manhattan Project came from Shinkolobwe in the (then Belgian) Congo. This ore was ridiculously pure, at 20-65% uranium. Some sources say that if we hadn't had this source of Uranium, the Manhattan Project would have been a failure, because we lacked the technology to refine less-dense ore.

    • restricteddata

      If you see my response in the thread, they definitely had the technology to refine ore of lesser quality — they used some really low-quality stuff (like 0.25% U content) during the war, in their desperation. But they didn't have enough raw material to use it. They used about 3,000 tons of oxide to make the weapons in World War II. They only got 2,756 tons from non-African sources by the end of 1946 — despite processing some 384,000 tons of low-grade ore (and some of that was as high as 30%) to get that amount.

      It's possible they could have made use of the non-African sources but the importance of the African sources becomes really clear when you look at the numbers: they got most of the oxide from them, and they needed a lot of oxide.

      In the 1950s, fearing uranium scarcity, the US government set an artificially high price for uranium ore from the US Southwest, setting off a "Uranium Boom" that found lots of low-grade ore. By the 1970s, the government had enough uranium to do whatever it wanted with.
