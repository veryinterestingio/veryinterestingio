    Professional diver here, you're definitely on the right track. Couple of nitpicks though. Submarines aren't really pressurized, they maintain normal atmospheric pressure inside. The hull is incredibly strong to prevent it from being crushed: every 33ft of depth is equal to another atmosphere worth of pressure. This means that at 100ft, a submarine is experiencing 4x normal atmospheric pressure. Current military subs can routinely operate at 600+ feet. Maintaining internal pressure at the same as normal atmospheric pressure leaves them free to change depth or even rapidly surface whenever they need to, without worrying about barotrauma or decompression sickness.

    The little hole in the inside window pane of the airplane isn't for pressurizing the cabin, it's for pressurizing the outer window pane. The outer window pane is the one designed to take the load of the cabin pressure. The inner window pane is for us to look at, and to stop us from damaging the critical outer pane. The little hole allows cabin pressure to also fill the air space, preventing the inner pane from having to handle the stresses. And, if the outer pane blows out, the little hole turns a gaping death portal into a tiny, very loud, annoyance.
