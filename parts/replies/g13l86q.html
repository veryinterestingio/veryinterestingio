      Heat within planets can come from a few different sources. Earth, for example, is heated by two sources: radioactive deccay, and heat leftover from the formation of the planet.       The kinetic energy released during a planet's formation takes the form of heat which is largely retained. The heat differences also cause certain parts of the planets interior to move around and convect, which creates more heat. There is also the decay of radioactive materials within the Earth which creates heat. This heat doesn't last forever and will continue to slowly dissipate as long as the Earth exists.

      • migmatitic

        Pressure isn't the source of the energy. The energy initially exists as potential energy in a dust cloud. There is a potential relative to the center of mass. When that dust falls towards each other while forming a planet, it becomes kinetic energy. When the dust collides as the planet coalesces, the kinetic energy is converted into thermal energy. A lot of the heat of our planet was this original residual heat from planetary formation.

        But there's also a lot of radioactive materials in the core too--think about it, the densest stuff sinks to the bottom, and what's denser than uranium? So the radioactive decay produces a lot of heat--without uranium Earth would be solid or mostly solid by now, but the uranium decay keeps the mantle convecting and plate tectonics active... for now at least...
