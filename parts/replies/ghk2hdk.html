    Have a more general question about using bacteria to produce dna:

    However, wouldn't the production of the target sequence (even just copying the dna) be an extra "cost" to the cell?     • Yoojine

      However, wouldn't the production of the target sequence (even just copying the dna) be an extra "cost" to the cell?

      Correct. In the absence of antibiotic selection, non-transfected cells will rapidly out-compete transfected cells.

      Hence, any cell that loses the target sequence (but retains the antibiotic resistance) be fitter and reproduce faster?

      Also correct, but this is unlikely. You put everything on the same plasmid so the odds of some mutation or replication anomaly causing you to lose your gene of interest (GOI) while keeping the antibiotic resistance cassette is minimal.

      Hence, how can companies continue to ensure the target gene is being produced in growth vats?

      This is going to be dependent on the application, but we can make some guesses. The classical way is to do RFLP analysis with restriction digests, where your GOI will generate known fragment lengths. This is usually accompanied by sequencing since it's so rapid and cheap nowadays.

    • ineffablepwnage

      As /u/spanj said you start from a frozen stock every time, ensuring your starting material is good every time. In practice, there's few enough generations and little enough stress that you're not likely to see a loss of your target gene. But 'probably' isn't good enough, so you would normally add some antibiotic and purify it out later.

      The regulatory agencies don't like that anymore though and over the past few years have been pushing back more on any use of antibiotics, so a different method is needed. A current popular method is to use auxotrophs and use the knocked out gene as the stabilizer on the plasmid instead of an antibiotic resistance gene. i.e. instead of making it resistant to being killed off and then trying to kill everything, you make it so that it can't grow without the plasmid and you don't have to do any extra work.
