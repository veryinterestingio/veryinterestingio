    Play is present in many animal species. It is a characteristic of the ability of the brain to project hypotheses and then use the error feedback in order to correct its internal models. I strongly believe in the importance of play in the classroom, precisely because it naturally calls upon two of my four "pillars of learning"

    1. Active engagement
    2. Error feedback

    There is growing evidence that playing educational games can facilitate the acquisition of math. See e.g.

    Siegler, R. S., & Ramani, G. B. (2008). Playing linear numerical board games promotes low-income children’s numerical development. Dev Sci, 11(5), 655–661. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-7687.2008.00714.x

    Dillon, M. R., Kannan, H., Dean, J. T., Spelke, E. S., & Duflo, E. (2017). Cognitive science in the field: A preschool intervention durably enhances intuitive but not formal mathematics. Science (New York, N.Y.), 357(6346), 47–55. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aal4724
