      Tg means glass transition, so when a polymer goes from soft(souple) to hard, so yeah. Crosslinking happens in any "solid" polymers, it's the bonding between the chains (formed with the monomer(s)). It can be of different nature (covalent, ionic) depending on the type of polymer.     • News_of_Entwives

      Yes I think you understand it :)

      Crosslinks are formed by linking two polymers in the kiddle of their chain. Sometimes it's done after the polymer chains are already mixed (like with vulcanized rubber) but other times they're formed as the plastic polymerizes.

      Polymers are made of monomers, and at the end of each monomer there's a functional group which allows it to link to another monomer, like a key and a lock, to form that polymer chain. As the monomers keep reacting the entire molecule keeps getting bigger and bigger. To crosslink that polymer chain to another, you need to introduce a monomer with two functional groups, so it can react on either side of itself, bridging the gap between the two chains.

      You can also use even higher functionalities to get even more crosslinking. Tetra, hexa, peta functional monomers all exist, and so do dentritic polymers (with 10s of functional groups). This allows the chemist more control over how tight the crosslinking actually gets, and thus allows for a wide range of properties.

    • News_of_Entwives

      Not necessarily tuned to a specific temperature. The usual case is to make the application temperatures all on one side of the Tg, in the ski skin case, maybe the Tg is at like -60, who knows?

      Another poster has mentioned tackifiers, which could also be useful in the skins. They're mostly inert big molecules that have very low Tgs. Essentially they drag the Tg of the whole mixture down to where it needs to go.

      I'm saying a lot on Tg, but it's still only part of the adhesive equation. The polymer's structure is just as important, or maybe even moreso, it's just so much easier to adjust the Tg than to change the whole molecule.
