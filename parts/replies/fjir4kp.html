      • mygrossassthrowaway

        It depends on what you’re looking for.

        For 99% of cases, if you’re doing an at home sleep test, you’re trying to see whether or not you have obstructive/possibly neurological sleep apnea.

        Those results are sent to a polysomnographer (someone who is trained to read the data of a sleep study), and a report is sent out with your statistics. From that, the clinician can make a reasonably accurate guess as to what was going on.

        For me, it turned out I did not have obstructive sleep apnea, but neurological sleep apnea (ie something in my brain was telling my body to stop breathing a few times an hour).

        But we tried the cpap anyway, and it helped.

        The at home test is faster, cheaper, and less cumbersome, but obviously at a cost of potentially missing something, because you don’t literally have electrodes glued to your head.

        But for 99% of us, it’s fine.

        Former sleep clinic admin, AMA.
