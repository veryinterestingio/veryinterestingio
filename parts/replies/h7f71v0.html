      • OlympusMons94

        Radiogenic heating (by uranium, thorium, potassium-40) is mainly in the mantle and crust, not the core. The core is generally thought to have very little of these radiogenic elements, which chemically favor incorporation into rock rather than metal. (There may be some contribution to core heat from radiogenic elements, but even then it would be a small component of the core's energy.) Most of the energy in Earth's core is leftover from its the formation (kinetic energy of accreting planetesimals, and friction from dense iron sinking to form the core). The gradual cooling of the molten core led to the formation and growth of the solid inner core ~0.5-1.5 billion years ago. Dense iron (and nickel) freezes to grow the inner core, while buoyant lighter elements (e.g. sulfur, oxygen, silicon, hydrogen, etc.) also present in the outer core rise. This releases gravitational potential energy, which is converted to heat by friction. The freezing also releases latent heat. This energy and the rising and sinking due to compositional and temperature differences (i.e. convection) powers the dynamo for Earth's magnetic field.

        Plate tectonics (which is important for life) is also a feature of the crust and mantle (for which radiogenic heating is important), and not a part of the convection in the outer core that drives the dynamo. However, they are interrelated. The core supplies some heat to the mantle, while the rate of mantle cooling (e.g. by plate tectonics, and mantle convection in general) constrains the rate the underlying core cools.

        The degree of importance of an intrinsic magnetic field for protecting the atmosphere is complicated and has been increasingly called into question over the past few years. A magnetic field protects from direct erosion by solar wind, but drives some escape (polar wind) and does not affect thermal escape. Planets without their own dynamo develop a weak induced magnetosphere in response to the solar wind that provides some protection. In short, an internally generated magnetic field is neither necessary nor sufficient for maintaining a thick atmosphere (as demonstrated by Venus)
