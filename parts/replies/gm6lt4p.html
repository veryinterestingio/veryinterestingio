    Amazon as a company has a goal of like 10% employee turnover every year. But sometimes one department in a location is expected to take it on the chin and get 15 or 20% turnover to protect a different department.

    I have a lot of coworkers from AWS,(and worked there briefly as a tech too) and they were frequently asked to sacrifice workers from Ops because RH was understaffed. Or an individual building had to sacrifice to protect another one (with a more popular manager). Following year, the manager who had to lose 40% of his workforce gets a bad review because his building:

    1. Went overbudget on overtime. (They didn't have enough workers to keep the building staffed otherwise.)

    2. Had too many outages. (Not enough people to do the day-to-day and do rounds to find issues.)

    3. Outages that weren't resolved fast enough. (Not enough workers to do all tasks to support a casualty. Especially when they're all half-trained. During my brief time there I was literally required to choose between a rack level outage and a lost of cooling to an entire data center.)

    That sets off a chain reaction where senior management are taking super-close looks at his building and its paperwork. Usually results in the manager and lead engineer(s) getting fired.
