    When photographing moving objects, it is also possible to "pan" the camera, change the angle of view to follow the object. That technique is more often used for terrestrial photography like racing cars.

    It's also extremely common on space telescopes. Since you have conservation of angular momentum without any external friction, reaction-wheel systems can achieve extremely precise continuous rotation of the telescope to follow remote objects. Hubble, for example, has a rated precision of 10 milliarcseconds (i.e 3 x 10^-6 degree), with a rotation rate of up to 6 degrees per minute.

    To add a few things here, amateur terrestrial telescopes do this with an "equatorial mount". The axis of the mount is pointed towards celestial North, close to Polaris. A synchronous motor then gradually moves the telescope about this axis to compensate for the Earth's rotation.

    In the old days, you'd hold that exposure for hours and occasionally check it against a guide star using your spotting scope to make sure it wasn't drifting too much.

    We've come a long way. The modern technique is to take a number of shorter digital exposures throughout the night. Then you process all those exposures with software that throws out the defects.

    Pictures on /r/astrophotography/ today look like what I used to see printed from multi $million research telescopes in the '80s.

    edit -- to get to the heart of OP's question though, and others have mentioned this, the motion of the planet against the stellar background is negligible during a photography session vs. the rotation of the Earth.
