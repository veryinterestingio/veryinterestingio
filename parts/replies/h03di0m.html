  • NotTooOrdinary

    Say if they only have 1 unique stretch identified for a given virus, then the virus mutates somewhere in that stretch, would that mean the tests wouldn't detect it, and they also wouldn't be aware of the new mutation?

    • AWormDude

      Yes. The tests wouldn't detect it if it's within the stretch you're testing for. And they may not be aware of the mutation.

      However its more complex than that. When a mutation occurs, it could infect many people and not be detected anyway. But as it spreads, people would come down sick with the symptoms. Logically - of you've got people sick with the symptoms, and the tests say negative, you'd suspect something is wrong with your tests and start doing extra checks.

      And of course in your scenario, you're only testing for that one stretch. In reality we aren't testing for that one. I did see another comment around that did name the 3 things that are being searched for.

      So the simple answer to your question is yes, it wouldn't be detected, but the more in depth answer - we might not notice at first, but we'd notice fairly quickly.

    • ShirtedRhino2

      We can actually use a real world example to show how this would work. The B.117 (British) variant had mutations in the S gene. This ended up being useful - the S gene was one of the three in the PCR test used in the UK, so S gene drop-out could be used as a proxy for B.117 proliferation, and gave faster data than genetic sequencing (which is still important, as other variants could also cause S gene drop-out).

      Imagine now that the PCR assay only tested for the S gene. Anyone infected with B.117 would return a negative test, and cases would appear to fall. After a few weeks, when hospitalisations (and later deaths) start to increase, it's likely people would put 2+2 together, but this would delay the response, and allow the variant to gain a much bigger foothold than otherwise.
