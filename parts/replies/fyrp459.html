    • a_trane13

      Because the vacuum of space is never a true vacuum, and there are always a few molecules around, the thermometer would eventually reach equilibrium with those molecules. Then it measures the temperature of those molecules, which would be typically very, very low (-200 to -400 F, depending on the region of space).

      So the vacuum itself has no temperature. Temperature is by definition the average energy of particles.

      Now, in a perfect vacuum (which can't really exist in practice and arguably in theory either), technically there is NO temperature. But your thermometer would measure something very close to absolute 0 (-459.67 F) if you let it sit there for a very, very long and radiate away any energy it has (and the thermometer doesn't stop working or break down chemically in that very long time).
