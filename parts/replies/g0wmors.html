      • americanroundabout

        You are partially correct in that plants do not have adaptive immune system. However, they have innate immune systems which are similar in many ways to the innate immune systems of animals. In addition to allowing parts if the plant to die (hypersensitive response), plants can also synthesize a variety of compounds to damage parasitic organisms. The plant immune system is also not even close to fully understood. There have been huge advances in the field of plant-parasite interaction in just the past 5-10 years. I wouldn't write off the possibility that plants have some kind of adaptive immune system that has not yet been discovered.

        Innate immunity plays a role in plant defence against pathogens, insects and parasitic plants.

      • Ilaro

        Plants can utilize a lot of different defense mechanisms upon infection. They have anti-microbial proteins like defensines and chitinases, can strengthen their cell walls, and destroy their own cells as you said (only if the pathogen is not necrotrophic). In fact, the plant immune system works so well that many pre-treated/primed plants are completely immune to most pathogens. Pathogens almost never try to fight the immune system, but try to evade it altogether. The immune system is usually "off" in plants, as it costs a lot of energy, which is why recognition of the pathogen is so important and is so effective for the pathogen to not be recognized.

        Plants recognize pathogens not much different than the innate immune system of animals. They even utilize most of the same pathways (like mapk pathways). They recognize microbial patterns (PAMPs) with pattern-recognition receptors (in plants these include RLPs, RLKs, lectin receptors, etc which have a similar function as TLRs in animals) on the pathogens to activate their immune response.
