    This is it; artists learn to see things much more detail in a way that allows them to translate it into a medium like a painting or drawing.

    That’s the first step; being able to remember all that detail and turn it into art later is another skill which probably requires some natural ability; some people don’t seem to be able to imagine a scene and visualize it in their head.

    • PickledPokute

      I do think it's the same answer - they don't actually imagine with precision at all. They might imagine something like a beautiful blond with a bombshell body, but don't actually have a complete picture when asked to describe them. Shape of the eyebrows, the hairstyle or even basic body proportions that are essential for drawing them. If they had a precise mental image, they would be able to answer questions like "what is the ratio of their height to their hip width". But people don't image complete pictures in their mind - instead they more likely have a list of characteristics.

      If I asked someone to describe those characteristics with detail in words, they would probably give very undetailed descriptions because they never though about the details. Neither they could accurately describe the relative positions and sizes between those characteristics.

      Someone might even have a good mental image of a big pair of titties from the side, but their most familiar imagination of a torso might be from the front and those are difficult to mesh together.

      As for line drawing, it's a skill of simplification or reduction. We don't see nor often remember people as line drawings. Someone might draw many different kinds of mouths with just a single line in different shapes, but that's also a learned skill with a lot of trial and error. While practically everyone can see a cartoon characters or 16-bit pixel art and extrapolate the missing details (our eyes have a lifetime of training for completing fractured data), selecting which details to retain when simplifying is not an innate skill.
