      Overall the catholic church tried to oppose superstition. That included the belief in witchcraft.

      Yes, there were individual popes that believed in witchcraft (for example Innocent VIII.*) but witchtrials were a much later thing (a full century after the great black plague) and mostly a protestant thing (since the various inquisitions had a much higher standard when it came to trials and evidence. For example any confession extracted under torture was inadmissable as evidence).

      *Innocent VIII was also a compromise pope where the election politics were vicious and devisive, so everyone basically agreed to elect a pope that wasn't a political threat (ie, not very bright). The 15th century was also the most corrupt century in church politics, with for example Callixtus III, pope 1455-1458 and Alexander VI, pope 1492-1503. You might recognize them more easily by their pre-papal names. Alfonso and Rodrigo Borgia.
