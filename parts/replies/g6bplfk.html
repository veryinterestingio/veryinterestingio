  • LouPeckOfficial

    Thanks so much, u/VeryLittle for your question.

    Rather than being dependent on the subject area, you may find it is publisher-specific. A number of publishers are running innovative peer review initiatives (I've added some examples below) that they are piloting or have now rolled out. Those publishers may be multi-disciplinary or subject-specific. The multi-disciplinary publishers might have a preference for which journals they include in the pilot - faster publication times, higher impact etc. Science related titles generally have faster publication times so they are often included in pilots to garner more feedback in a shorter space of time, however, as researchers in different disciplines have such varying needs, it's important to have a more holistic perspective.

    Thieme offer Select Peer Review - https://www.thieme.de/en/thieme-chemistry/select-crowd-review-136859.htm

    F1000 - offer post publication peer review - https://f1000research.com/

    Wiley have summarized two years of transparent (open) peer review - https://www.wiley.com/network/researchers/researcher-blogs/transparent-peer-review-what-we-ve-learned?HootPostID=975d772a-96ee-4822-9462-32c33f18db81&Socialprofile=wileyinresearch&Socialnetwork=twitter

    BioMedCentral talk about innovative practices - https://www.biomedcentral.com/about/advancing-peer-review

    It's fascinating when you look at a service like Publons the number of verified reviews in the last 12 months - pretty much every journal at the top looks to be STM related - https://publons.com/journal/?order\_by=num\_reviews\_last\_one\_year
