      Gigatons carbon. Often biomass is measured or reported in terms of the dried organic weight of the species, or the amount of total carbon present. This ignores things such as variable amounts of water present, which would be the other main contribution to the mass of an animal, and so is probably a more consistent measure across different species.

    • Queen_Acicula

      In 2018 a paper came out in PNAS (linked below but I don’t know how to reddit so, may not come through.) by Bar-on, Phillips, and Milo titled ‘The Biomass distribution on Earth’. They came to the conclusion that animals are actually 6th in line for largest biomass percentage. That’s behind #1 Plants, then bacteria, fungi, archaea, and protists. Out of the animal biomass, the largest groups are the arthropods (represented by mostly krill and termites), and fish, then mollusks and annelids. Humans come in just behind livestock with much lower percentages.

      Quick note, I want to state I’m not 100% on board with all their methods, however I think it’s an incredible attempt at accomplishing such a feat. I think their methods are worth discussing and improving on!
