• baltinerdist

    Everything links together in one giant, neverending chainmaille mesh with tiny links.

    Because of the recession, your company bans flights for client travel. So the money you would have spent on the plane ticket doesn't get spent there. Combined with everyone else, the airline doesn't get as much revenue with which to buy a new plane. So the plane manufacturer orders fewer parts with which to construct the plane. So the airplane parts manufacturer has to lay off workers that don't have parts to build. So that laid off worker can't take her family out to dinner at the nice restaurant. So the nice restaurant doesn't order as many vegetables because there are fewer customers. So the local farmer sees their sales decrease and can't buy a new tractor. So the tractor manufacturer...

    Very few of these links lead straight one to another (one plane ticket lost does not equal one giant plane not ordered) but in aggregate when all the links start coming together, it just cycles throughout over and over again.

    This is a good point. It's "losing money" in an economic sense but not necessarily an accounting sense.

    Like when a company spends say $10k an hour on labor and all other costs to run and generates $20k an hour revenue doing so. If the whole factory stops you'll hear some manager complaining that they're losing $20k an hour but in reality they're losing the $10k in actual costs, the other $10k was just profit that could have been made but wasn't.

    Or let's say I bought $100k in stocks and they've gone down in value to $50k. Some would point to this as having lost $50k but I only lose that $50k if I sell at that price. If I hang onto it and they go back up to $200k Then I never lost $50k.
