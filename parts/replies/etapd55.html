  • TheGame2912

    The heat transfer goes as a constant * T\^4 in absolute temperature.

    To avoid confusing others, the equation is actually

    q = k[(Th)⁴ - (Tc)⁴]

    Where q is the heat transfer rate, k is a constant that includes the stefan-boltzman constant, the surface area of the object, and the emissivity factor (because objects are not perfect black bodies), Th is the hot obeject temperature and Tc is the ambient temperature.

    So while the radiation rate does indeed depend on the absolute temperature, it also depends on the temperature of the environment into which the object is radiating.

    An import thing to note is that for most instances involving objects at everyday average temperatures (not excessively hot) radiation is actually the least important effect to consider because of how small the Stefan boltzman constant is (5.67*10^(-8) W/m²K⁴). In a thermos, however, it becomes the main form of transfer because they are designed specifically to minimize conduction and convection.
