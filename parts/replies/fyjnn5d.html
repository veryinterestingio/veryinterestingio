      Light is absorbed by chromophores by causing electrons in the molecule to be exited. Oganic chromophores (like the two examples) absorb light though an excitation of a pi electron (double bonds basiclly) to its pi* anti-bonding state. In order for these molecules to absorb visible light, the pi system must be conjugated (lots of pi electrons connected to each other).

      So, to answer your question, for Organic chromophores (i.e. not metal based) conjugated pi systems like phenyl rings (or polyphenyl) or conjugated alkenes are needed to absorb visible light. Metal dyes absorb light by exciting the d electrons, so they don't contain these functional groups but rather electron donating or withdrawing groups bonded to the metal center to tune the energy gap between ground and exited states.

      I'm not an expert in dyes, but my guess would be the amine and sulfonic groups in those examples are added to make the dye soluble in water, rather than more expensive organic solvents.
