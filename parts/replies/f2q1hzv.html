    • eliminating_coasts

      The problem with a classic chemical battery is that all the parts are in the same place; for a petrol engine for example, you can have one engine, and just get a larger fuel tank if you want to go further.

      For a lithium battery, the fuel tank and the engine are the same thing, you can't scale up an individual cell to store more power by connecting it up to some fuel tank, you just have to build another cell next to it, that does all the charging, discharging and storage together.

      Most big long term storage systems work differently, they have something that connects to the electrical system, a pump/turbine system in the case of using water height for storage, and the energy store itself, the water kept in a reservoir up the mountain.

      Storage systems that work this way tend to have much higher capacities for energy storage relative to the power they supply each second, because they have to get all that water through a smaller turbine, but they are also much easier to expand; all you need to do is enlarge the "fuel tank", and they will store more energy.

      There are a lot of different energy systems that care about how long you can store energy, rather than caring about providing a lot of power in a short amount of time. Batteries and other similar chemical things like "supercapacitors" will probably always beat them for cancelling out very short term cycles in power, because you need fast response at high power, and care less about amounts of energy stored because you'll be charging again very soon anyway.

      For slower stuff, you'll probably see lots of battery techs developing that involve some machine connected to the grid, and some large reservoir or slow/static system that they work on.
