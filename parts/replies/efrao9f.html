      • kyrsjo

        Shorter and more compact particle accelerators, which can be cheaper to build and operate.

        Obviously great for high energy physics (you'll get 3-4 times the collision energy in the same space) which is what really drove the initial development, but there are also other applications such as smaller/lighter/cheaper/more flexible radiotherapy machines, and similar for XFELs (x-ray lasers for (bio-) chemical and materials research. Basically it's making high performance accelerators available outside of big national/international labs and for universities/hospitals/etc.

    • Peter5930

      Yes, at that field strength there's no material with a high enough dielectric strength to keep from being ripped apart into a plasma. Vacuum gets 20–40 MV/m, Teflon can manage 60–173 MV/m and the highest any material can manage is diamond at 2 GV/m. Your accelerator would be destroyed by uncontrollable electric discharge at 2.4 GV/m.

As the particles velocity is very high (very close to c), the strength of the magnetic field must not be as strong as an electric field which exerts the same force on the particle.
