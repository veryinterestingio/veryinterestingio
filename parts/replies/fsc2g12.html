    I think it's true that brighter, louder voices cut better via primitive recording equipment. However, I suspect that style was also a vestige of the pre-microphone era; those kinds of voices carry better across a crowded hall with no amplification at all. It's part of the same transition we see in mid-20th century cinema, from theatre actors with a big, loud style, to the more naturalistic actors like Brando, whose sound was often much smaller, because it could be picked up by soundstage mics in a way that it could never be perceived in a theatre.

    One misunderstanding I find common is that the engineers involved were “bad” and couldn’t capture the recordings in a high quality way. Look, if you were a radio engineer, you were given specifications that your broadcast had to meet to be sent and received. Those were set numbers. Your audio had a certain range to work in, in the radio wavelength. You took what was given in recordings or live audio and you made it fit into that space, capturing and delivering the best you could. This was given the assumptions of what the receiving equipment of the day might be. When there was very low bandwidths of audio available, the “best” sound you could get was to capture the middle part of the audio spectrum. The extremes of pitch, both high and low, are more challenging to both capture and reproduce. Many radios of early eras had one speaker about 1 1/2 inches across to play to a whole living room of people! Midrange audio contains the largest amount of musical audio, most vocal tones, so was what was most concentrated on in early audio. In those early days, no one listening could play your “bass drop”, as the tiny speaker would just self-destruct attempting to do so? So they started out with the important midpoint of the audio range kinda in the mid, but maybe just a bit higher than mid. Recording a violin or a flute was often a test early on. So recordings from that era sounds nasally and thin, but they were getting a fairly good representation of the source audio. Given their limitations.
