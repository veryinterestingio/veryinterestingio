      Not quite. Its more that they're in two separate orbits, but so close that the orbits interfere, such that once the inner orbit begins to catch up, the gravitational attraction of the outer moon speeds it up, which acts as a prograde burn and bumps it into a higher slower period orbit.

      Meanwhile the outer moon slows down due to the momentum exchange, and retrograde burns into a lower faster (in time) orbit.

      Essentially every time they meet they swap orbits from each other.
