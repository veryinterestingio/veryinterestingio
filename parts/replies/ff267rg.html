    • AxelFriggenFoley

      Saccades are much quicker. The limitation is visual latency. Let’s say you’re looking to your left and suddenly you hear a lion coming at your from your right. You want to look at the lion which means shifting your gaze, let’s say, 90 degrees. The fastest way to do that is to jump directly to where you think the lion is, then probably make a couple more smaller saccades to fixate on the lion. In order to do that though, you need to make the eye movement, then process the new visual scene, create a new motor plan to move your eyes to a new spot, and so on.

      I guess an analogy might be trying to get a small ball into a small hole 400 yards away. Would you roll the ball to the hole, constantly making fine adjustments as you go to get the perfect trajectory straight towards the hole? Or would you pull out your driver from your golf bag and hit that thing as close as you could get it and then maybe a wedge and then a putter to finish the job.

      It’s just faster to make a ballistic eye movement to your best guess and then follow up with smaller ones until you hit your target.

    • Furyful_Fawful

      It's the same idea behind looking at a bunch of objects. You want to look at the individual objects (say, trees/ferns/animals), not the space between them. You scan what you can looking at one spot, then you jump to the next and scan that. The brain can process multiple aspects of the same picture at a time, so it's more efficient to just scan from looking at one spot than scanning through the target in question. From there, it's trivial to apply the same concept to examining spots on an individual object (say, words or phrases in a paragraph).
