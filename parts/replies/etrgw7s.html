    • LeoRidesHisBike

      That's not true. The way digital volume works is by running a scale transform on the digital waveform. It's not that fewer bits are sent, it's that different bits are sent that represent quieter sounds.

      Volume scaling happens very late in the audio pipeline, so it doesn't "cut off" anything unless the levels clip.

      The reason that audiophiles who claim they can hear this (as a side note it would be interesting to see a proper double-blind experiment that shows they actually can) might be able to is because any transform at all is necessarily lossy. I.e. if you scale down, you have to sample (like shrinking a digital image), and if you scale up, you have to multiply (like blowing up a digital image).

    • space_fly

      Technically, it's quite simple. Audio is typically encoded in PCM, which is a series of numbers indicating the position of the speaker cone at a each moment in time (these numbers are called "samples"). A commonly used sample rate is 44.1khz, which means that there is one of these numbers for each 1/44100th of a second.

      The numbers themselves can be encoded in various ways, the most common being 16-bit integers (a number between 0 and 65535), 24-bit integers (a number between 0 and ~16 million) and 32-bit integers (a number between 0 and ~4 billion).

      The simplest way to change volume is to multiply all these numbers by a constant between 0 and 1, for example 0.5 for 50% volume. This will result in finer movements of the speaker cone, which will result in less volume.

      Theoretically, there is a small loss of information when you reduce the volume from software, because of how the numbers are encoded. Unless you are using floating point samples (rarely used outside professional audio/music production), you lose some bits when reducing volume. For example, for 16-bit samples at 50% volume, it means that you are reducing the range from 0..65535 to 0..32767, so you basically lose 1 bit. At 25% volume, you reduce the range from 0..65535 to 0..16383, so you lose 2 bits. At 12.5% volume, you lose another bit for a total of 3, and so on.

      If your music is encoded with 16-bit integers, you might notice a very small quality drop when significantly reducing volume (i.e. below 10% of original audio). At 24-bit and above, you probably won't notice anything.
