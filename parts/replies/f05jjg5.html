      • gugagore

        Yes, but to be clear, you have to do the filtering before you sample (that is, before you have output pixels).

        For example if I want to resize a 200x200 image into a 100x100 image, I need to low-pass filter the 200x200 image, get a blurrier 200x200 image, and then throw away every other row and column. Clearly you can optimize this by not computing blurry pixels you'll end up discarding.

        If your original signal doesn't start as samples, like a camera taking a photo, or a phone recording audio, the low pass filter must happen in the analog domain.

    • strutt3r

      Hertz is a measure of frequency within a second. With digital audio this is how often the waveform is sampled, with video this is how often the screen refreshes. More samples in audio means more fidelity to the waveform, with video it is more fidelity to a moving image. A low sample rate with audio leads to a tinny or choppy sound, just as a low refresh rate will give video a choppy look.

      Our bodies sensory organs and brain activity vary from one person to the next, so one person might be able to still discern video choppiness at a 70hz rate where another can’t tell the difference between 60-65hz. The average person’s body frequency itself varies throughout the day, from around 60-70hz.
