    Electric charge is always conserved. A black hole must have the same charge as the sum of all the charges of the matter from which it formed.

    I believe there are some specific phenomena predicted for charged black holes but to put it simply: a charged black hole will have an electric field.

    Since most macroscopic objects in the universe are roughly charge neutral, black holes are expected to be mostly charge neutral too.

      • random_pinkie

        In general, a charged black hole would be like any other charged object, with an electric field and a magnetic field. The interesting question is how it might affect the shape of the singularity inside the event horizon.

        The force of electric repulsion is far stronger than gravity and so the singularity might manifest itself as an infinitesimal ring or disc

        A perhaps notable result is that sufficiently charged objects (for example, an electron) cannot become black holes.

        None of this is considered particularly significant in the field of cosmology as black holes are expected to have very small or no net charge. This makes sense because a positively charged black hole would repel positively charged matter and attract negatively charged matter.
