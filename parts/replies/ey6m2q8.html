    “Insider trading” means trading while in possession of material nonpublic information about the issuer that the seller had access to as an insider.

    One also needs to take into account if the trading is out of the norm for that person. As an executive, with information about the company, I would never be able to sell stock, because I always know non-public information. I always sell on the first day (not pre-programmed, but manually sold) that I can sell, so that my activity looks like it's normal...always. Which means it can't be seen as insider trading. Now, if I decide to sell (or buy) outside of that normal activity, then it can be construed as insider trading. This is akin to the pre-programmed model that you've described, but it's based on activity, and not just setting up a regular trade. My window for trading changes every quarter, depending on when earnings comes out, so that we can't sell before bad earnings, for instance.

    This is the most correct answer. If an insider has a pre-scheduled, documented sales timeline, it normally will not be considered insider trading.

    For the sake of argument, if the said insider had a agreement for their broker to liquidate a net of 200k shares at a rate of 10k shares a week based on 1% of the volume weighted average price(VWAP) then they’ll simply sell a small portion in 2-5 trades per day. There could be a price to it as well, such as don’t sell if it’s below $45.

    These plans are well documented for obvious reasons.
