      Luckily the coronavirus cannot mutate in all of the same ways as the influenza virus.

      The biggest difference is in the construction of their genomes. Both viruses have a genome that is RNA (we, like all cell based life forms, use DNA), but influenza has a segmented genome.

      Segmented means that influenza has broken it's genome up into pieces, roughly one per gene (our genome is also kind of segmented into chromosomes). This segmentation is what allows for giant shifts in the circulating strains that cause pandemic influenza.

      On the surface of the influenza virus there are two proteins: HA and NA. Each of these can be one of many subtypes (you've probably heard of H1N1). If some creature gets infected with two strains of influenza at once (like H1N1 and H2N2) the strains can mix and match like it's a swingers party inside your cells and then you'll get H2N1 and H1N2 viruses.

      This is a really easy way for a segment from a virus that can't normally infect humans to suddenly appear in a human compatible virus! Bam! You've got pandemic influenza!

      Also, influenza doesn't have error correction built into its replication machinery so it is more likely to mutate. Coronaviruses do have some degree of proofreading.
