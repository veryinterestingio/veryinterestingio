    • malastare-

      What is it about influenza that is so unique, that it has these characteristics that no other virus family has?

      Explaining a complex system in simpler terms, according to a geneticist I've worked with:

      Influenza's spike protein, hemagglutinin, contains a long "neck" between the main body of the virus and the tip where it matches with cell proteins to bind to the cell. Lots of viruses have these spike proteins. Influenza's is particularly tolerant of having the length of that "neck" change without impacting the ability to bind to the cell. However, the length of the neck does impact your body's immune system and its ability to identify and bind the virus. This is combined with a fairly simple protein structure that forms that neck. It's easy for the virus to take on mutations that change the length of that neck.

      So, there is at least one system where mutations can be made that don't hurt the virus, but confuse the immune system. There are a couple other common mutation sites with more complex patterns, but similar results.

      Other viruses have spike proteins, but very few of them rely on a system that is as tilted in favor of viruses. In some ways, it suggests that it was inevitable that some virus would find and exploit this.
