    There are certain times from which a lot of records are preserved (old Rome) and other times from which a single new word would significantly increase what we know (early middle ages).

    So besides survivor bias in the sense of "what was preserved" it is also "what was preserved and survived until today"? For Europe the loss of books in the late antiquity is perhaps the most important. A huge part of what we know about the antiquity is not from originals but from copies made in the (early) middle ages and which have then been copied again and again.

    • StructuralEngineer16

      There's another example I find fascinating of this. A period we know very little about is the Bronze Age Collapse, mainly because the art of writing was lost, so there are no surviving accounts from the few hundred years afterwards, bits and pieces from Egypt excepted (which stayed more stable for longer, probably due to the presence of the Nile keeping a steady food supply going). The accounts we have don't tend to say 'this is what is happening' because everyone was too busy trying to survive in the chaos. One account we do have is the response to a request for aid from one minor king to another in the Levant that basically says 'sorry, we can't help, we're screwed too.' It was found in the remains of the kiln it was being hardened in (clay tablet), so some catastrophe overtook them between message being written and message being sent. I highly recommend the 'Extra Credits' YouTube channel's series on it.
