    To clarify, "elastic" describes how good something is at conserving energy during compression. Play Dough isn't elastic, it just splats when you drop it.

    Air and other gasses are very elastic. Under most circumstances if you compress air and then let it expand, you get almost all your energy back.

    Rubber balls are in between. It loses a fair amount of energy squishing on impact, and you don't get all that back when it bounces. That's one reason flatter tires are less fuel efficient, you lose energy to deforming the tire as it rolls.

    By filling up the ball, you're letting the air inside the ball store most of the energy and then give it back. And letting the ball squish less. Since the air is better at being elastic, less energy is lost and the ball bounces higher.

      • Emabott

        In some sense everything wears out. But you seem to be asking about a very ideal scenario where it's not leaking, and the materials holding it are perfect and don't degrade. Just focus on the air?

        The answer is mostly no.

        Rubber is a bunch of long, complicated molecules that can stretch and twist and tangle. That's where the bounciness comes from, they can bend and bounce back. But that also means repeated use can break, rearrange, untangle those long molecules, ruining the elasticity.

        Air is very simple. For example, nitrogen gas is most of our air and it's just two nitrogen atoms in a molecule. There isn't anything to break, deform, untangle.

        Air pressure is those molecules flying around (because they're not so cold they're frozen) and bouncing off the walls of the container. As you compress the gas, you store energy by making the gas hotter, making them fly faster and bounce harder, raising the pressure. And then as you expand you get that energy back.

        Because there's not much you can change about an N2 or O2 or CO2 molecule, it doesn't really break down. But it can leak, the molecules can occasionally break apart and then bind to the container, etc.
