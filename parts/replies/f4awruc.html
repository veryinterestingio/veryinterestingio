    • Chlorophilia

      So, possibly silly question, but if the heat that is normally circulated to the North Atlantic/Europe is disrupted, it's still going somewhere, right? So wouldn't the global average temperature be the same?

      Yes and no. The Younger Dryas was intensified in the North Atlantic so in many ways it's better to think of it as a regional event than a global event, which is a significant part of the reason why ocean circulation in the North Atlantic has been implicated. However, the climate system is extremely complex and interconnected so whilst changes in ocean circulation directly only redistributes heat, it can trigger other feedbacks that do alter global temperatures.

  • rakfocus

    I believe what happened (my partner had this in their project for Paleoclimatology class) was that as the ice sheet receded from near the Great lakes region, it left a large lake of melted fresh water. As they receded further, the melt water that had built up into a huge lake found a new path to the ocean that led it straight to a critical region of the Atlantic for oceanic temperature stability. More specifically, the part where warm waters sink down to the deeper levels of the ocean. These parts are critical for smaller scale temp changes because they remove tons of heat from the atmosphere. The huge influx of fresh water (which is less dense than salt water) in this particular location completely screwed up the cycling in this one critical area, and it affected the entire world.

    At least, this was the theory he presented - there are a number of other, older papers that have other theories
