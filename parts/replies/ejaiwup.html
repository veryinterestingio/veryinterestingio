      Conservation of energy is only guaranteed when the universe is static over time. This is a consequence of Noether's theorem. In general relativity, energy doesn't have to be conserved because the universe expands and therefore changes over time.

      You can see this with the much more mundane example of redshift. Light from distant galaxies is redshifted as the space it passes through stetches, and redshifted light has lower energy, but that energy doesn't go anywhere. It's just lost.

      In simple terms, yes, the expansion of the universe creates space. I'm not sure if matter moving outside the cosmic horizon counts as information loss because that matter still exists out there somewhere, but regardless, conservation of information is a rule of quantum physics, not general relativity. This is why the black hole information paradox is a thing. Going strictly by relativity, black holes destroy information because they are featureless, and despite several claimed resolutions, we can't really resolve it without a working theory of quantum gravity.
