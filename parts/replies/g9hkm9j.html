    +1 In addition to the pivot-point answers here, a major factor that exacerbates this effect is known as the "bore-axis" and it's relation to this "pivot-point" (your hand/wrist, or shoulder with a long gun).

    If you checkout competitive practical pistol shooters you'll notice a big lack of diversity in the handguns they shoot (practical meaning time-based shooting sports, IDPA, USPSA, etc and not bullseye or PPC). Most are 1911/2011, Glock, or CZ pattern pistols. The common feature among most of pistols is the very low bore-axis.

    A low bore-axis makes a pistol more controllable to shoot fast & accurately. Competitively, we describe that as a 'flat shooting' pistol design.

    This same feature is why you see very few pistols that go the other direction in compition, say a HK USP pistol, a higher bore-axis (and slide with more mass to boot), makes it a less competitive pistol when you're on the clock and keeping score. And even further down the 'high bore-axis' spectrum, look at a single-action revolver in 454 Casull for brutal recoil and pivot effect.
