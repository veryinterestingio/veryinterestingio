    • KevineCove

      It makes sense when you think about how some people can touch their toes and some can't, or how peoples' posture varies greatly. It's honestly less of a "default position" and more of a "path of least resistance" position. An easy way to think of it is that your muscles have a certain elasticity to them, and if enough tension is placed on a muscle, they'll naturally want to "snap back" to a less strenuous position. On a neurological level, your body also has things called muscle spindles which detect when a muscle is stretched to the point of injury, and will engage the antagonist muscle to pull the muscle back to a more neutral position (this is why you'll start shaking when doing a really deep stretch.)

      By the way, the difference in anatomical positions aren't a cultural thing so much as it's based on the terminology itself.

      In western medicine, "proximal" refers to how close something is to the heart, especially in the extremities ("distal" refers to the opposite.) So your elbow would be proximal to your wrist, and your wrist would be distal to your elbow. Since the arms and legs both extend downward in western medicine, you can usually think of proximal as being "higher" and distal being "lower" (obviously this changes as soon as you move your body into a different position.

      Traditional Chinese Medicine speaks in terms of chi/qi, which describes energy flow in terms if yin and yang. Yin energy comes upward "from the earth" and thus would travel from your feet up to your torso (yang energy comes "from the sun" and goes in the opposite direction.) However, because eastern anatomical position has you placing your arms above your head, yin energy also continues from your torso UP into your arms. If you were to use western terminology for this, you would say yin energy travels proximally through the legs, but distally through the arms. Using a different anatomical position for TCM keeps the terminology internally consistent.

      Anatomical position provides and absolute point of reference for describing the position of body parts. In that position, for example, the thumb is lateral to the index. Like how on a ship you don't say left or right, you use port or starboard.

      In addition to the functional resting position described above, there is also an 'open-packed' position for any given joint. This is the position in which there is the least stress on the joint and it is able to move most freely. In a lot of cases, the open-packed position would be a natural resting position.
