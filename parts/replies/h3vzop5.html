    • Cleistheknees

      A1C is honestly all over the place.

      The error bars on average RBC lifespan are large, generally it’s cited at 80 +/- 11 days, and the assumption that RBC synthesis itself remains static regardless of glucose levels is a big one. There’s also several different kinds of hemoglobin present in most people (>90% HbA1, HbA2, HbF), but the proportions themselves can vary, and we don’t know if they get glycated at the same rate, if they dissociate at different rates, etc.

      There’s also a long list of things which can alter hemoglobin concentration like thalassemias, which can significantly decrease the ratio of hemoglobin:glucose, meaning higher A1C for one person compared to another with more hemoglobin, but the same actual glucose levels between them. For example, beta thalassemia can make your RBCs substantially smaller, and increase the rate of reuptake by the spleen, giving you a lower A1C when you have the same glucose levels as someone with normal diameter RBCs.
