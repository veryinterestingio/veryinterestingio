    1 - not all companies/theaters/shows are AE and so the talent might not get paid before opening week, and

    2 - talent is not paid fir rehearsal after opening unless they work beyond the contracted hours in a week.

  • spader1

    You'd be surprised how little rehearsal time can be before the cast moves into the theatre and tech starts. Three or four weeks in the rehearsal studio is pretty common. Then they'll move to the theatre for tech, which will be another week or two. Though those weeks in tech aren't for the cast to be learning their tracks, they're there to add in scenery, lighting, and costumes.

    The other thing that I'm not seeing people touching on is the how of how cast and crew get paid. In theatre (so Broadway and every tour that I've worked on) the producers will set up an LLC for the show itself. If you're working on a show you'll be an employee of that LLC, and your paychecks and tax documents will come from the LLC.

      • Dear_Evan_Hansen

        I work as an actor for a regional theater, big enough to afford a couple union contracts per show. It’s unique in the sense that everyone’s schedule is affected by those actors who are in the union. We have to take a certain amount of breaks for certain amounts of time, and we get paid differently. I get a stipend based on how many rehearsals I’m required to attend, which can max out. So if I go to more than 30 rehearsals I believe, I’m basically working for free. Then from there I get paid per show, after the show opens.
