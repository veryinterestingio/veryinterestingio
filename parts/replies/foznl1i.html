      • PyroDesu

        Radiotherapy is very much not the same idea, at least not anymore, as far as I can tell. It's much more targeted - rather than dosing the whole body, it can be set up so that multiple beams of radiation can converge on where the tumor is so that there's not as much damage in the path of any individual beam, for instance. Or simply placing a sealed source nearby/into the tumor. The closest to chemotherapy is probably systemic radioisotope therapy, where a radioisotope can be attached to a particular molecule that the tumor is known to take up or that will attach itself to the tumor (or outright be an isotope of an element that will be taken up, in the case of the thyroid and iodine-131).

    • earthwormjim91

      How does it specifically target GI cells?

      It doesn't. For one, chemotherapy and radiation are two different things.

      Chemotherapy involes injecting certain chemicals into the body that kill rapidly dividing cells. Any rapidly dividing cells are attacked, not just cancer. That's a big reason why people lose their hair during chemo. Hair cells are rapidly dividing. As are your GI cells. A lot of people experience skin and nail damage during chemo as well because these cells are rapidly dividing. Anemia is another one due to the blood being full of rapidly dividing cells.

      Radiation therapy is generally very targeted. They just shoot a beam of radiation at specific areas.
