      Not at an atomic level really. Just think of it as throwing an egg at the wall. The sudden stop against the wall applies a lot of force to the egg and it just breaks.

      When an explosion goes off, the enormous pressure differential just hits you like an invisible fist. You don't scatter into your component atoms but your soft bits don't hold together very well under that kind of force. They just tear, rupture and suffer other damage.

    • CerddwrRhyddid

      Imagine hitting a bag of eggs with a baseball bat.

      ​

      The outside of the bag will probably look fine, but the eggs inside also recieve the force, and break.

      ​

      With most explosions, separation occurs at a macro level - it'll burst blood vessels, or organs will be broken apart.

      ​

      Separation at an atomic level generally requires that the tissue has been turned into gas - vaporised. This occurs in high energy explosions, such as nuclear explosions.
