      Well, now we're exiting ELI5 territory some. I'll still try and keep it fairly simple.

      Tesla is a startup, yes, as it experienced rapid growth for a period. Is Tesla still a startup? Probably, but again there isn't any hard rule. Growth doesn't have to be exactly linear, it can be really fast and then level off for a bit, and then fast again. One of the drivers of rapid growth like this would be to release new products, yes, once the rapid growth of your previous product slows. Or release your existing product in a new market (ex: Zoom for schools).

      Google was a startup but isn't anymore, their growth is too slow. But Google also doesn't sell "more people", they sell "more eyeball time". So even if you don't get more users, as long as you can get your existing users to use Google more often, that's still growth, as you now have more ad inventory to sell. They could make that happen through a lot of different things, like releasing a new product (Gmail, Google News, Amp, etc), or just making their existing product better so that people want to use it more often (improving the algorithm, the rich search results it provides beyond just links to pages, etc)

      Also its hard to know if you're in the rapid growth stage while you're standing in it - you can only really know for sure looking backwards. I mentioned the rapid growth and then leveling off - sometimes the leveling off is temporary and you go fast again, sometimes its permanent and you're out of your startup phase. You just can't know until you start to grow again - or don't, for a long enough time.
