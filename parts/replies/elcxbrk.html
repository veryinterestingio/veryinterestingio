    • evanberkowitz

      According to general relativity (GR), there is a singularity at the center of a black hole, which is surrounded by a horizon. The distance between the singularity and the horizon grows with the mass, so more massive black holes have bigger horizons.

      ​

      However, whether or not there are singularities in nature remains unknown, because it is unknown whether GR continues to hold to arbitrarily short distance scales, which is an experimental question. Many physicists expect that such a singularity is ultimately resolved (prevented from being 'really' a singularity) by quantum mechanical effects.

      ​

      Unfortunately, formulating a theory of quantum gravity is (so far) unaccomplished for cases where the universe looks like ours. In some mathematically simpler cases we can construct such theories, but whether a 'real' singularity persists remains unclear (not because the theory doesn't provide an answer, but because it is difficult to leverage the theory to make a prediction).

      ​

      So, the short answer to your question is "According to GR yes, in the real world nobody knows."
