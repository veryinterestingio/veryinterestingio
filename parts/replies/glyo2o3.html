    • haas_n

      Ultimately, ideas that cause the brains that house them to not pass on the genes that make up their bodies will start dying out, and those that encourage replication of those genes will spread.

      This is certainly largely true, but also not necessarily the case. If ideas spread from person to person via contact, we can model them like viruses. As you say, most viruses end up selecting themselves for non-lethality - because killing your host tends to be a bad way to survive. But even a lethal virus can survive in populations for quite a long time, as long as it kills you slowly enough to allow other people to have reproduced in the meantime.

      In the context of religion (itself a fantastically good idea at reproducing), celibate orders of monks and so forth have persisted in societies for quite a number of generations - because there was enough influx of people from other, non-celibate families, for the idea of celibacy to never run out of new 'converts'.

      But, crucially, such an idea can only ever infect a portion of the civilization.
