    • SlickMcFav0rit3

      Google mRNA decay

      But I'll give you the cliffs notes version:

      It gets chewed up by one of two things: Xrn1 or The Exosome

      Xrn1 is a 5'->3' exonuclease (front to back) that will destroy mRNAs once they lose their special cap.

      The Exosome is a complex of multiple proteins that, with help from the Ski7 complex, will degrade mRNAs from 3'->5' direction.

      Correct, for our genes there are poly A signals in each gene that recruit special machinery. This cuts the nascent transcript and adds a poly A tail.

      For some viruses, like SARS2, the tail is genetically encoded (in the RNA, since it's got no DNA)

      Do we have any idea why this happens?

      • WhoCaresWhyBother

        It’s protective and helps regulate translation to some degree. The poly-A tail is coated by an RNA binding protein named PABP that coordinates a lot of the cytoplasmic lifecycle of an mRNA from its regulation, translation, localization and eventual decay.
