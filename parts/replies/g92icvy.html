      The short answer is that within a single test you cannot. By doing multiple repeated tests in different variations, though, you can build a statistical model of the behavior of particles in a quantum system and, it turns out, the results we get would be impossible if the particles had a defined state before they were measured.

    • Bunslow

      https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bell%27s_theorem is the long answer.

      if the needle is really pointing to only North and East before we look, that's called a "local hidden variable theory", in that the needle's state-before-we-look is a "hidden" variable, but is fixed even before we look. But, weirdly enough, Bell's theorem is a mathematical proof of how such theories are different from theories where the needle indeed must vary away from the base states before we look, where there is no "hidden" base state but a truly varying non-base state instead before we collapse to the base state. And when we perform the experiments to test which category of theory describes the behavior of quantum particles, every such experiment to date has -- astonishingly -- categorically excluded the local hidden variable theories -- excluded the frameworks where the needle is secretly always stuck on a base state. It's impossible.
