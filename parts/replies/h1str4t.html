    • island_arc_badger

      The oxygen levels throughout most of the Mesozoic were around what it is now, never really significantly higher or lower.

      I’m not sure scientists ever had anything that actually pointed towards high oxygen levels during the Mesozoic, it was just one of (many many) ideas as to how gigantism happened in many dinosaurs.

      It also seems to get muddled with the Carboniferous, which was long before dinosaurs were around and genuinely did have some of the highest atmospheric oxygen levels the Earth has ever seen. This is in part what lead to the gigantism of certain arthropods, like the dragonfly ancestor Meganeura or the terrifying 6 ft long millipede ancestor Arthropleura.

      We have a better understanding of dinosaur physiology these days and the way that some species managed to get extremely large is like it’s more to do with particular adaptations like bone structure, heat regulation and metabolism. The fact that the late Mesozoic probably saw one of the highest levels of total biomass that the Earth has ever supported also helped.

  • Peter_deT

    More recently it's been proposed that sauropods were not height browsers but sweep browsers - they held their necks horizontal and swept a semi-circle around, then took a step and repeated (like using a line trimmer). The evidence is the attachments of the neck vertebra, which seem better to work this way than for height.
