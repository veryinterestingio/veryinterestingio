  • prappleizer

    Simple answer number 1: we know galaxies move in all directions (locally this has been measured), yet every large scale feature (distant galaxies and clusters) in the universe is always redshifted away from us by increasing amounts (i.e. farther objects are moving away faster) — the only explanations for this would be that our galaxy is sitting still at the center of a universe where everything is flying away from us, or, more reasonably, that space is uniformly expanding, which would lead to every galaxy or point in space observing the same effect. Our universe actually has an “observable horizon” because if you imagine a rubber band being stretched with dots on it for galaxies, and stretch it some set amount, the galaxy at one edge sees that at the other edge moving the fastest — eventually, the small stretch of space at every point along the way is enough that a galaxy far enough away is “moving away” faster than the speed of light. Thus its light never reaches us.
