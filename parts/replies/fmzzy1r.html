    • MarineLife42

      The tricky bit is that we don't really have a clear, universal definition of what 'sleep' actually is. And since we can't define it, we can't say why we require it either, if that makes sense. We know it is to do with rest and recuperation, memory consolidation and general cleanup, but what is it? Why do we do it for eight hours a day but a giraffe does it in 20 minutes? Why do we dream? Is that necessary, or a byproduct? And because we dream, we can say with conviction that our brains are still very much active while we sleep... so why can't we just stay awake?

      Sleep is still one of those questions that we keep grappling with and where seemingly simple questions have surprisingly elusive answers.

      • _thetimeismeow

        Did some research on cyanobacteria in college in a lab that studies circadian rhythms. They have a set of proteins that form something akin to a little molecular clock that runs with a roughly 24 hour period and controls gene expression. Thought to be one of the earlier examples of simple circadian systems. You might call "sleep" a period of cyclic gene down-regulation, at least for this organism.

        https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterial_circadian_rhythm

        See the section on clock proteins. Seems intuitive that there might be some evolutionary advantage to synchronizing photosynthesis/nitrogen fixation to the sun, perhaps ramping up protein synthesis just ahead of sunrise for example.

    • FRLara

      I think the simplest answer for "why do we sleep?" is "because we can". We have survived for millions of years sleeping, and evolution doesn't care for optimization. If we do sleep (for whatever primordial reason) and can survive and pass our genes, or descendants will also sleep. And the more complex our brain got, the more it relied on that behavior for basic functionality.

      If sleeping represented a major risk for survival, our ancestors wouldn't have developed that behavior.
