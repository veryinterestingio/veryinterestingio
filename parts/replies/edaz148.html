    I wonder why the peak changes as you increase temp; that seems counter intuitive

    • Ehcksit

      Shorter wavelengths require more energy, and a hotter object has more energy to give.

      At higher temperatures, subatomic particles move more quickly, and the photons they release also have a higher frequency. Frequency is the inverse of wavelength; a higher frequency means a shorter wavelength.

    • thats_handy

      /u/Ehcksit has given a good description of the why, but I thought you might like to know the rule, as well.

      The wavelength of the peak intensity is given by Wien's displacement law, λ = b/T, where b is Wien's constant.

      Edit: to satisfy your intuition, watch a hunk of metal warm up as it passes from dull red up through orange or yellow.
