    As I've said in another comment, the event horizon isn't a physical barrier, it's just the moment at which the gravitational force is strong enough that photons can no longer escape. You can think of the event horizon as the black hole's shadow.

    To get your head around things crossing the event horizon whilst they wouldn't appear to do so to an outside observer, we have to think of time as a personal experience. Your experience of time is unique to you and dependent on how you're moving through space. If it were you who was crossing the event horizon (of a particularly large black hole), your local time would run normally and you'd cross over no problem (apart from the problem of inevitable spaghettification and death). At this point, you're moving incredibly fast relative to an outside observer, which slows down your local time in comparison to theirs. They would see you approach the event horizon, slowing down until you stop entirely on the event horizon and fade away. You however would continue your journey unaware of the difference in time.

    Not the best explanation, perhaps someone else can offer something a little more eloquent.

  • rabbitwonker

    What you’re thinking of is the center point of the black hole — the “singularity.” That is the point where the infinities happen (theoretically), including time slowing to a virtual halt. The event horizon is just a demarcation along your journey to the center, and is only actually relevant to observers in the rest of the universe, because that’s the last point at which they will receive any light from you. The freezing and fading away part is an illusion that they see due to the behavior of light at the horizon.
