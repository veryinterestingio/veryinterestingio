    About a hundred years ago when we were first getting a sense of the existence of other galaxies Edwin Hubble came up with an idea for galaxy evolution. He thought that galaxies started round and eventually started to flatten out and form spiral arms. So he called the round and elliptical galaxies "early type" and spiral galaxies "late type" because the round ones were supposed to be formed first and later turn into spirals.

    It turns out that spiral galaxies are actually middle aged and elliptical galaxies are older. But Hubble's way of classifying galaxies was already widely used so astronomers still call old galaxies "early type" and newer galaxies "late type". I personally just avoid those terms and just say elliptical or spiral or dwarf.

    We know now that younger galaxies tend to be blue in color, and older galaxies tend to be more red. Usually galaxies transition from blue to red because of a major merger, but there could be other processes. What we know is that when galaxies get to a certain size they stop forming stars (or they form them very, very, slowly). After that they grow through mergers with other galaxies.

    Galaxies tend to get bluer the farther back in time we go, but we still see a mix of all types of galaxies.
