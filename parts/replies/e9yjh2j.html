      An object will accelerate or decelerate only when a force is applied to it. Gravity is the force which causes objects to fall, such as a ball tossed in the air. While the ball is held, it is subject to the same acceleration as the car and will match its velocity. In the absence of other forces, the ball's only acceleration will be due to gravity.

      As long as the car maintains a constant velocity (speed and direction) while the ball is in the air, it will appear to passengers in the car that the ball will fall straight back down into the hand. For the ball to not fall back into the hand, either the ball or the car needs to have a change in velocity (accelerate).

      If we assume that both the velocity of the system (in this case, the car) and the acceleration due to gravity are constant and that the throw results in a velocity vector purely opposite the direction of gravity, with nothing else acting on the ball, it can be thrown to any height and will eventually fall to its original position (the hand).
