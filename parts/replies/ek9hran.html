  • astrogator7

    Technically carbon monoxide is not considered a VOC. VOCs are heavier (relatively) organic compounds with high vapor pressures so they readily evaporate at room temperature. Basically stuff that gives of "fumes" like alcohol, acetone, gasoline, formaldehyde, CFCs, etc.

    While you are right that CO could not be filtered out with cartridges and requires a forced air system, most true VOCs can be filtered out with activated charcoal based systems.

      So carbon monoxide is not considered a VOC. VOCs actually CAN be readily filtered out using the those organic vapor cartridges you use. The particulate filter is just that fiber piece that snaps over the cartridge if it has an N rating. P rated particulate filters are oil proof and have that pink cover. The cartridge itself is basically just activated charcoal that absorbs the VOCs from the air.

      True VOCs as far as the EPA (air quality), OSHA (workplace safety), and chemists are concerned are organic (carbon/hydrocarbon based) chemicals that readily evaporate at room temperature, which makes them "volatile." So stuff that gives off fumes are volatile, like alcohol, acetone, gasoline, etc.
