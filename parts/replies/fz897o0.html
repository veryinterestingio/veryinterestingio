      In the earliest stages, cells decide what to be through the notochord. It's a chord that runs from head to butt, almost like a spine. Everything develops based on that.

      Then cells signal to each other what they are so adjacent ones know what to do. For example, skin cells will induce nearby cells to also become skin. That way, you don't have a bone cell growing nex to skin cell next to nerve cell.

  • Istalriblaka

    A part of this worth looking at is inter-cellular signalling.

    Especially during early development, cells will "claim" roles. For example, a cell may decide it's in a good spot to differentiate into a nerve cell. Outside of the brain, there's not a lot of nerve cells in one area (compared to, as an example, muscle cells which come in organized, dense bundles). This cell will release a chemical that's basically the cell saying, "Hey, I'm gonna be a nerve cell, nobody else be a nerve cell!" And nearby, the other cells will receive that signal and decide "Oh boy, nerve cell is taken. Maybe I'll be a muscle cell or something."

    To go a little deeper, it does this by locking that part of the DNA away. This process is called epigenetics, but basically it wraps the DNA up in layers of tight coils that the DNA reading and RNA producing proteins can't get through.
