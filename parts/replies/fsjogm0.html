  • RebelJustforClicks

    This is likely the main reason.

    For anyone not following, when you read music on a piano, you have 2 "clefs". Bass clef and treble clef.

    Bass is everything below and up to middle C, treble is everything above and sometimes including middle C.

    If you are playing another instrument, you generally only get one clef. This is for simplicity. Since the instrument has a limited range, the "inventor" will choose the proper clef that the majority of the notes fall into and use the "key" to fine tune things, basically.

    If you are playing say, trombone (key of C), you get a bass clef. So middle C is hanging above the clef and has a single stroke to denote that it is "middle C". D would have a note sitting "on" that stroke" and E would have 2 strokes, one thru the note and one below. This convention carries on and on. It also applies for notes "below" the chosen clef.

    After a while, say 4-5 strokes above / below, it just gets confusing. Instead of being able to instantly tell what you are seeing you have to start counting and that's just hard.

    So if an instrument has a 'general range' of 12-15ish notes that 80% of what it plays will fit into, and the center of that range is a "concert B4", it will likely be in the "key of C" and read music on the treble clef.

    My specific knowledge stops at trombone, bit of trumpet and piano. So I cannot really elaborate on Alto clef, sorry.
