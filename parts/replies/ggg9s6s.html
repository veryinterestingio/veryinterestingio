      There’s a few ideal strategies, depending on population size and density. What you describe - infectious and innocuous - is the type of strategy the herpes simplex viruses have. People can be infectious at any point after they contract it, most people have by symptoms, and symptoms are dangerous, just annoying. These viruses co-evolved with us in part bc the strategy works well for small populations that are not super dense and don’t come into contact with other groups that much. But when they do - they might be contagious at any point for the rest of their (not shortened) life and pass it on.

      Ebola has the opposite strategy. Very contagious but deadly. You need to spread it quickly bc the person with it dies quickly. In the example above, of a small tribe or hunter gatherer community, it would probably kill just about everyone and the few survivors wouldn’t have it. So when they mix with other groups, it won’t spread through those populations. Ebola relies on dense populations and rapid and frequent population mixing. Both effective strategies in an evolutionary sense.
