      Crater dating is complicated, especially for very young surfaces with a few small craters, and the age of Giordano Bruno is not settled. A study of inferred secondary craters, whose ages are estimated from their size and prominence, from the Giordano Bruno impact at the nearby Luna 24 site is consistent with the Kaguya-derived age of 1-10 million years. On the other hand, the craters in Giordano Bruno itself imaged by Kaguya could be self-secondary craters formed by material ejected at high angles from the primary impact that soon fell back into the newly formed crater. See the first paragraph of section 4 of this paper (PDF warning) for a very brief review with references of these estimates and considerations.
