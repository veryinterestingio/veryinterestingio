    It gets a little semantic. A gamecube disc is definitely ROM. It's a DVD-ROM. Read only memory encoded on optical disc. It's a series of bits, that happen to have a filesystem encoded on top of it. A disc image is a ROM in every sense of the word, with extra formatting applied at the next level. Otherwise you could save to the disc. It's just not a silicon chip.

    • Clewin

      A lot of old arcade cabinets either used solid state ROM or PROM (ROM programmed by a chip writer - you could then manufacture a cabinet entirely in-house) on the board. Cartridges were always solid state (a manufacturer is a necessity, where with a board you could solder it yourself). In any case, MAME (originally Multiple Arcade Machine Emulator, but now a variety of old hardware) popularized the term ROM because it was one of the first major project that supported multiple different old arcade games, but you needed a ROM dump to run them.
