• thesexydoc

    The major complications with such a cachectic or emaciated patient is refeeding syndrome and it is a beast all on it's own. Basically, the body realizes it's getting nutrition and tries to rebuild it's energy and muscle storages at the cost of electrolytes and other nutrients. This can prove to be rapidly fatal and needs aggressive supportive care. The goal is to go slowly based on metabolic demand and try to get the patient eating on their own (very challenging in anorexic patients) rather than rely on forced nutrition. It's best to limit parenteral feeding because this can shut down the GI system.

    • datta_dayadhvam

      This is correct. The main risk here is refeeding syndrome which is primarily an issue with phosphorous levels. Refeeding causes a massive shift of electrolytes especially phosphorous back into cells of the body which lowers the levels in your blood. When blood levels of phosphorous get very low you can die of respiratory failure from diaphragm weakness or cardiac failure primarily because there isn’t enough phosphorous in these muscles to make ATP or energy that cells can use.

      At centers where advanced anorexia is treated and patients are in similar states to the man in this photo, tube feeds are started slowly with close monitoring of electrolytes every few hours and gentle IV fluids are started. The process generally take 2-3 weeks to get from this point to a place where somebody could just eat a meal.

    • PeeyushMalik

      Feeding tube does not create a new opening, rather it just uses the existing body passages and acts as a direct connection between outside and stomach but through nasal cavity rather than mouth. Also, since our gastric lining is already acidic and adapted to exposure to foreign objects (food), it's very uncommon that feeding tubes cause infection, unless the same tube is kept inside unsupervised for a very long time. Regarding, why shake isn't administered orally is because in severe conditions, when a patient is unconscious or even semi-conscious, giving anything orally may do more harm than good because it can easily get into respiratory tract causing aspiration pneumonia or even death due to aspiration. This doesn't happen when we are conscious because our body reflexes prevent that by putting lid of epiglottis over the respiratory pipe when we are swallowing.

  • herman_gill

    You avoid IV fluids like the plague until you get the electrolytes balanced and get some thiamine into the patient. Oral/gastric fluids is fine.

    This a huge mistake people always make in the ED when they see someone who's "dehydrated", bolusing patients at risk for refeeding with large amounts of IV fluids, and then they develop florid heart failure.
