    My favorite non-technical explanation for autorotation is that the blades are gliding, just as an airplane will glide with the engine off. You can maintain a descent at a reasonable rate until close to the ground, then use the rotational energy of the blades to bring it to a brief halt just as you reach the ground.

    Of course, it's a complex skill and you have to get it right; it's an integral part of helicopter training.

        "no rotor power" isn't quite right. There's no engine power, but the air flowing up through the inside of the rotor disk drives the rotors and provides lift on the outside. You can control how fast the rotors go with their pitch. Lower pitch increases air flow up and makes them go faster and have more energy. Higher pitch makes them go slower and have less energy but slows your descent. Ideally, you autorotate from high enough and with enough airspeed to assume a steady state before nearing the ground.

        Just before touching down (and "just before" is very different for a 10 ton military helicopter than for a news chopper), you increase the pitch to trade the rotational energy for addressing your descent, cushioning your landing. But if you yank up on the collective like your friend's friend, you can pop yourself back up in the air and immediately assume the aerodynamic characteristics of a refrigerator. It happens. I've seen the flattened skids.
