  • spacetimecellphone

    I’m not a healthcare provider in any capacity, but this response actually addresses the question and makes more sense. Healthcare tends to have a “less is more” mentality as a general rule anyway (in absence of indications to suggest otherwise). So the idea of administering directly into a vein is more complicated/invasive and unnecessary for the desired/required effect. Way easier for someone to give a shot than finding an IV.

    The second part nobody seems to be addressing, is in regards to how blood flows. All living tissue needs an oxygen supply to survive, and blood flow is typically the way that is accomplished (the cornea of your eye being the exception since it exchanges oxygen directly with the air). Basically, if it’s not dead tissue, it’s exposed to blood. If blood is getting there somehow, either it keeps moving to somewhere else, or that area is going to start getting bigger. Assuming your vascular plumbing is in order, the blood cells are traveling on a one-way street. The heart pumps and there’s pressure involved pushing/pulling it from artery to capillaries and back to vein. Just looking at this logically.

    • pizy1

      The epinephrine pen is for convenience's sake. Your average Joe isn't gonna be able to find a vein and inject IV epinephrine correctly into that vein, especially when you're talking about someone going into anaphylactic shock, which is a panicky situation where the patient could still be moving around.

      Certainly if you could locate that vein and inject epinephrine there that'd be just as effective (if not more), but with the big caveat (in case anyone is getting any stupid ideas while reading this) that you'd need a much smaller dose (lower than how any epinephrine auto-injector comes, so you'd just have to have some epinephrine vials and a syringe around) and that it'd have to given very slowly (over at least a few minutes) or else you risk arrhythmias or other not fun side effects. So yeah, the quickness and ease of Epipens justifies their existence.

        You don't have to give it slow but a smaller dose and weaker concentration of adrenaline/epinephrine is used. Continuous infusions should really be given through a central line.
