  • BitOBear

    It's not just about the cars. Tesla's batteries on the small scale, and on the large industrial scale are in there too. So are the home solar installations. And the power wall.

    The stock market is largely a reputation lottery, and one of the questions in the entire equation is how much the assets are worth versus the stock price. Basically they're constantly asking the question how much would I get if the company closed today and we had to liquidate the assets.

    Then there's the volatility of a company. A company that's too stable ends up with an exactly stable stock price.

    Most car companies are also diversified. I can't remember which one but I think one of the major car companies also makes jet engines. I think GM maybe? And I know there's at least oneEuropean car company that makes jet engines as well.

    So you take the total value of the company and compare it to the total number of stocks issued and then factor in the likelihood of growth and that kind of gets you what the stock price is supposed to be.

    Of course the stock market is pure gambling and speculation. The only time a stock has any actual value is when the company decides to buy it back at a particular price. At any other moment you're just looking at The price you expect other people might buy your stock from you.

    The stock market is actually sort of a weird fiction on which we base our entire economy. In the initial stock offering people are making a loan to the company in exchange for a little ticket called a stock. The company only pays back that loan if they do a stock buyback. From the moment of initial sale to the moment of buyback The value of the stock is only in the value other people might discourage to acquire your particular ticket.

    In this way the stock market is just a reputation lottery. For every person who believes the stock price is going to go down there's another person who needs to believe that it's going to go up and both people need a willingness to exchange money for the ticket.

    That's why finding out a company CEO has had a sex scandal can cause a stock price to crash even though nothing about the company's actual business has changed at all.

    Preferred stock sometimes issues a dividend, so large old stable companies offer some value to actually owning the stock.

    And without the overall belief that the company is worth more whatever that means there's no reason to have the stock price go up except for the fact that it makes the stock price go up.

    The other part is that if the company suddenly folds collapses closes or otherwise shutters its doors a certain amount of the money goes to each stockholder based on the number of tickets they have. This rarely happens.

    So buying and selling stock isn't really a way to make money off of a company it's a way to make money off of other people who are buying and selling stock.

    It's a weird creepy game of poker.
