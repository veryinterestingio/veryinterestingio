  • ubik2

    If your load shifts, you’ll accelerate faster in the beginning, but more slowly a bit later. For a traditional geared ICE, you may or may not have enough power to end up overcoming that later slowdown (which requires more energy, since you’re exerting that force at a higher velocity).

    Whether the nails shift more, because they slide all over, or the cotton shifts more because it’s compressible is all up to variables we don’t know.

    • Mantheistic

      • ubik2

        It might actually be a couple seconds, but keep in mind the truck doesn’t accelerate at the same rate. Once it’s going faster, the acceleration is probably lower (though reality makes this more complicated because of gearing and optimal engine rpm).

        If we experienced a constant force that accelerated the truck, the initial velocity of the truck with a sliding cargo would be higher, but after the cargo slid to the back, both trucks would have the same velocity. Trucks don’t have a constant force, though.

        Edit: For clarity, the slower later is when that sliding cargo hits the wall at the back of the truck. At that point, your truck slows down and your cargo speeds up so they’re going the same speed. During the millisecond or so that the cargo is impacting the back of the truck, your acceleration is likely negative.
