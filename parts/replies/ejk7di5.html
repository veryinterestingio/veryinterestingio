      We can make LEDs of virtually any single frequency -- pure red, pure yellow, pure orange, pure green, etc. Some may be harder than others due to the band gaps involved, but for the most part it can be done.

      The human visual system, however, is based off of three pigments which react differently to light of different wavelengths. Each pigment is sensitive to a broad range of light, but the sensitivities vary based on the wavelength on the incoming light, and each pigment has a peak sensitivity in a different range. Conventionally, these three peaks are called "red", "green", and "blue".

      When we look at something like a sodium-vapor lamp, which emits 90% of its light at 590 nm wavelength, the light is sensed well by both the "red" and "green" pigments, but not the "blue" pigment. The rest of our visual system interprets this as "yellow". If we were to instead show a light that was a mix of red and green light so that it triggered the "red" and "green" pigments in our eye the same as the sodium-vapor lamp, that mix would also be interpreted as yellow.

      The color of everything we look at is determined by how well it affects those three pigments in our eyes -- which we can individually trigger by using lights of three different colors. Every color reproduction system we use, from color photography, to color television, to computer monitors, all works on recording and displaying a careful mix of "red", "green", and "blue" lights, not on a true mix of individual rainbow colors. They do this because mixing the three wavelengths suffice and because it is much easier to record/playback three different colors than an infinitude.

      Astronomers and other scientists do have techniques for recording many more colors than we can see. It is not uncommon for astronomers to take photographs using filters which only allow the specific wavelengths given off by hydrogen, or water, or carbon dioxide, or other specific chemicals, and thus "see" where those chemicals are in the sky. Similarly, people who need it can get LEDs that light things up in specific ways.

      You can get black lights which have a purple filter that eliminates most of the visible light emitted by the fluorescent tube. They look purple in operation. You can also get black lights which don't have the purple filter. They look blue in operation. The only different operationally is whether or not the purple filter is there. You can get black lights which use LEDs instead of a fluorescent tube.
