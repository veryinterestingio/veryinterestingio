    I like that analysis.

    But I have more to add to it. It pretty much goes that a steep mountain is never more than about 500 meters higher than its neighbors. The reason is that the unconfined strength of the bottom exposed rock at the bottom is the limiting cause. When the rock at the bottom of a cliff shatters outward, the rocks above it also shatter and collapse, and we have a rock-fall. If that rock were confined, it could support more weight. The mitigating feature of under sea, is that felsic rocks which typically weigh 2.8 gm/cc only weigh 1.8 gm/cc when in water due to buoyancy. But when you're talking unlithified sediments in the continental slope, then you get into special magick, in that environment, even a slope of less than 1 degree can have a slide.

It turns out that while the static angle of repose is steeper with lower g, the dynamic angle of repose shallower.
