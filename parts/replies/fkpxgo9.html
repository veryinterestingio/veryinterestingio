      • HeinzHeinzensen

        Exactly, sterilization with UV radiation is usually done with high-pressure mercury discharge lamps, but can be done just as well with UV-C LEDs, provided they have enough output power. The advantage of LEDs is that they offer a much more compact form factor and ruggedness, operate on low voltage (e.g. provided by a solar panel) as opposed to high-voltage PSUs for discharge lamps and can be modulated at high speeds (i.e. don't need any pre-heating or cooling time).

        For water and air purification, as well as surface sterilization, you would not necessarily favor microLEDs, as UV LEDs have quite a high power density to be dissipated. For this (and other reasons), you would probably use regular-sized LED chips, which are some 100 µm in dimension (this is still big for LEDs).

        If you want to know more about the general topic (applications, manufacturing and physics of UV LEDs), I can warmly recommend the excellent book "III-Nitride Ultraviolet Emitters: Technology and Applications" edited by Michael Kneissl and Jens Rass. If your university has access to Springer Link, you can download it free of charge. If not, just shoot me a PM.

  • FrozenPhoton

    Yep, this is totally correct. Another important caveat is that these “germicidal” mercury UV lamps often produce ozone (a pollutant harmful to human health) as a byproduct, so when using them make sure it is in a well ventilated space

    EDIT: just adding in for clarity that you can have UV lamps like those for seasonal effectiveness disorder that only put out Mostly UVA and a little UVB. These don’t generate the low wavelengths needed for it to be “germicidal” UV which are generally on the more expensive side and require care due to O3 generation.
