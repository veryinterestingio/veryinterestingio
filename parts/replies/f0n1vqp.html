      • UrbanPugEsq

        The laws are different, but not in a way that affects streaming. There's a compulsory license for music, but that only affects live performances.

        In the US, 17 USC 115 is a compulsory license for performances of musical compositions. Radio broadcast is a public performance of a composition. Having a few friends over to listen to a record at your house would not be a public performance. At the point where you start letting a lot of people listen, it becomes public performance.

        Section 115 was later modified to add digital delivery.

        For historical reasons, the law gives two copyrights to a song - the musical composition (i.e., notes and lyrics) and the sound recording of the performance of that song. Sound recordings were not subject to (federal) copyright before 1972.

        Sections 112 and 114 are about sound recordings, and they expressly address streaming.

        The whole point of this is that it is a combination of longstanding practice AND the law that results in the current state of streaming services, where music (e.g., spotify, pandora, iTMS) are much different than Netflix, HBO, Amazon Prime Video, etc.
