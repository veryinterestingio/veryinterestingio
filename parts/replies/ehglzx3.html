    • crashlanding87

      Allergy medicines aren't quite anti-inflammatoires. Inflammation is a process that can be triggered by a number of things. Anti-inflammatoires tune down inflammation, while allergy medicines block the signal that triggers the inflammation.

      That said, anti-inflammatoires will reduce some allergy symptoms (ie. inflammation and its secondary effects) but not all symptoms. You'd still sneeze loads, and would probably have a runny nose, but you might get a little less puffy and congested.

    • crashlanding87

      The role of inflammation in aging and long term disease is still being explored to be honest. For example, it's well established that people with recurring depression tend to have low-level systemic inflammation. This doesn't mean that the inflammation is the cause - it may be a side-effect of the actual cause. Anti-inflammatoires haven't shown much effect on depression.

      Also, inflammation can be extremely helpful in every day situations. Your small cuts and bruises would heal a lot slower and a lot less well without it, for example. There's also increasing evidence that long term use of anti-inflammatoires can be very damaging. I don't think it's clear yet whether it's the medicines themselves or the lack of inflammation that is culprit, though.
