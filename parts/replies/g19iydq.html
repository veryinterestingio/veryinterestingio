  • Coomb

    If the government is injecting a lot of money into the economy through a basic income, in order to avoid inflation it simultaneously must also increase the amount of money it's sucking out of the economy. That is, increase taxes. The whole point of a basic income scheme is to accomplish wealth redistribution. Nobody thinks the government can just print money, forever, as much as it wants, with no consequences re: inflation.

      • Coomb

        Taxes in no way decreased the money supply in the way you describe it as deferring inflation.

        Of course they do. Money that you have to give to the government is money that you can't spend on goods or services. An increase in taxes will, ceteris paribus, reduce inflation. If you increase government spending by the same amount as you increase taxes, of course, there will be no effect on inflation - which, in this case, would be the goal.

        If you want a Keynesian view on the individual income tax as a method of inflation control you can read one here, by Roy Blough, the Director of Tax Research at the Treasury Department from 1938 - 1946.

        I wouldn't call the current macroeconomic orthodoxy pure Keynesian by any means, but you're right that the Federal Reserve generally takes the position that a small, predictable rate of inflation is the ideal target because it incentivizes investment while allowing for relative price stability in the short term.

        Governments tend to be the largest spenders in any economy.

        I'm not sure what you mean by this. Total government spending (federal, state, and local spending combined) makes up about 40% of US GDP, so not a majority. As an individual entity, of course the federal government makes up the largest spender, as its spending is a deliberate policy tool to redistribute wealth in various ways, and it's also the largest single employer in the United States.
