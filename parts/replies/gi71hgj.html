    • tinySparkOf_Chaos

      A common one is the heat capacity.

      You start by slowly heating (or cooling) the substance while measuring the amount of energy used to heat it and the temperature.

      Graphing this, a sudden spike in energy used to heat with a very small temperature change indicates a phase change.

      This is used extensively with polymers and liquid crystals.

    • zebediah49

      At what scale can we define a state of matter?

      Any scale at which you can measure it, and convince Reviewer 2 that it's legit.

      Physicists in general love phase diagrams. For example, consider Figure 4(b), in which a phase diagram is assembled for simulated fish behavior. In this case, it's based on the "alignment" and "positional" parameters, also defined by the paper in question.

      Whether or not it counts as a "state of matter" is more of a philosophy question than a scientific one. The point is to characterize something interesting happening, by quantitatively different behavior occurring in different situations. The word "state", however, is used extremely widely, to express the.. well, state... of a system.
