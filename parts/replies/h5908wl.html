    • ToxiClay

      Hoo. I can try. We're getting into really weird science, though, so bear with me.

      So, four (really, three) quantum numbers define an individual electron's position around the atom, right?

      The first number, written as n, is called the principal quantum number (not related to the p shell), and it denotes, very roughly, how big the orbital is -- its average radius.

      The second number, our old friend ℓ, is only sort of related to angular momentum. Electrons can't really be said to "have" momentum in a truly classical sense, but we do the best we can to get a word that's "close enough." Suffice it to say that ℓ relates to how fast the electron is moving around the nucleus, and that changes the shape of the subshell.

      The third number, mℓ, is the magnetic quantum number, and fortunately it's a little easier to swallow. It defines the orientation of the orbital: which way it's pointing in space, roughly.

      So we've got the shell, the subshell, and the orbital as our three numbers. There is a fourth, and that's the spin of the electron. Don't make the mistake of thinking the electron is spinning like a top; it gets very confusing. If you were to paint a tiny dot on an electron, you would have to turn the electron all the way around twice to see the dot again.

      I told you it was weird.

      You can either have the spin "pointing" up or down in each orbital.

    • zebediah49

      I'll shoot a bit more ELI5:

      • n ("principal") number indicates how many interesting things the orbital can do. More energy == more interesting. n>0.
      • l ("angular") number indicates how bumpy it is. 0 is round. 1 has two bumps on opposite sides. 2 has four bumps. 3 and higher gets a bit weirder. There is a restriction that 0< l < n, because bumps are interesting features, and you're limited by n.
      • m ("magnetic") number indicates what direction the bumps point. -l <= m <= l. More bumps, more different ways of arranging them.

      The specific details can be worked out with some fairly exciting math, but you can kinda get a feel for how that relates via a diagram like this. N is on the right; notice how the l=0 case gains more complexity as you go to higher n. However, as you move right to l=1 (p), l=2(d), etc. that complexity is "spent" on having more "points".
