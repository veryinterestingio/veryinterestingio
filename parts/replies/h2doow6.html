        100% interest a year, but if you compound every 6 month, it’s not 100% a year any more is it?

        I think you're confusing interest rate with annual percentage yield. APY is how much you'll actually earn on an investment for a given interest rate that's compounded continuously. Because of that, the actual amount of money you'll get back on an investment is usually higher than what the interest rate is.

        In this context, the term "compound" essentially means

        when the bank calculates the interest payment on your investment.

        If a bank offers you 100% interest, compounded annually, they're saying, "If you invest your money with us, we will give you 100% more than the money you invested after one year."

        $100 invested at 100% interest for 1 year= $100 interest paid.

        We then have an interest rate of 100% and an APY of 100%.

        However, a bank could also offer you 100% interest, compounded biannually. Now you're getting half the interest rate every half year:

        We will give you 100% more than the money you invested after one year. We'll give you 50% after six months, and another 50% six months after that.

        Invest $100 and after six months the bank will pay you 50% of your annual rate, which means that you now have $150. Now when the bank calculates the remaining 50% they owe you, it won't just be on the original $100 you invested, but will also include the $50 they've already paid you for the first six months.

        $100 invested at 50% for the first half-year = $50 paid to you. You now have $150 invested.

        $150 invested at 50% for the second half-year = $75. You now have $100 + $50 + $75 = $225

        Out of a 100% interest rate, we get an APY of 125%.
