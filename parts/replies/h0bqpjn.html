    One of my math professors literally said to me once "You're not in calculus to learn calculus, you're in calculus to learn algebra." Calculus is applied algebra. Physics is applied Calculus.

    Calculus, imo, is intuitively understood as working with ~Infinite Infinitesimals. i.e. If you want to figure out the area of a sphere, and you cut it into more and more pieces, eventually you get a nearly infinite number of really thin slices. When you add the area of each slice up, it gives you the area inside the circle.

    Physicists invented calculus trying to solve real-world problems.

    • Holgrin

      Calculus, imo, is intuitively understood as working with ~Infinite Infinitesimals.

      I like to think of calculus as the mathematics of changes. It is (mathematically) trivial to calculate the distance of a straight line and hence the area and volume of any geometric shape with all straight edges. But curves are lines with changing slopes and calculus provides a method for calculating these.

      Additionally, it works with things that aren't obviously geometric, like acceleration.

      People have an intuitive understanding of position and, usually, velocity - if velocity is constant. Acceleration is a little bit different, but it follows a simple calculus pattern: Velocity is the derivative of position; i.e. how much the position is changing over time; acceleration, likewise, is the derivative of velocity; i.e. how velocity is changing over time.
