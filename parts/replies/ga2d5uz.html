  • Turkeydunk

    This is the best way to think about it. And, there is no ‘quantum weirdness’ that completely prevents the electron from interacting with off-resonant light. The electron completely interacts with the light. But, since light’s frequency is much faster than the transition rate of electrons between states, off-resonant light quickly cancels itself out before any transitions can occur.

      • Turkeydunk

        It doesn’t really have anything to do with neighboring electrons. It is actually about the ‘Linearity’ of the transitions. Basically meaning, any transition that happens in one direction, can also happen in the exact opposite direction the same way and with the same strength. So you can sort of just imagine that the “peaks” of the light’s sine wave exactly cancel out the “troughs” though it’s a bit more complicated than that.

        And yes, electrons DO produce their own radiation, but due to linearity they also absorb the exact same amount of radiation, so there is a net zero effect
