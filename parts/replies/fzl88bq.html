      The thing that makes Buddhism difficult for those raised around Western philosophical ideas is that Buddhism's end goal is completely different from the three main Western religions. In both Christianity and Islam the end goal is to be saved and go to heaven and live by God's side for all eternity. Judaism also believes in ever lasting life with God, it's just the route you take to get there is different.

      The goal in Buddhism is to escape Samsara completely and return to the Buddha nature never to be reborn again. Samsara is the wheel of life. It contains all the realms of life and reincarnation. So the goal is to never exist again. This is achieved by obtaining Nirvana. There are some sects of Buddhism that have a "heaven". This is known as pure land Buddhism. However this heaven-like space is not a permanent location but rather an intermediate step you take while waiting to return to the Buddha nature. This is because the branch of Buddhism in which pure land sects are found believes that no one can escape the Samsara until the entire world has achieved nirvana. So once the individual obtains nirvana it becomes his duty to help lead others to finding their own revelation.

      This lack of a soul is a key difference that separates Buddhist Samsara from the Samsara of Buddhism's parent religion, Hinduism. The Saṃsāra doctrine of Buddhism asserts that while beings undergo endless cycles of rebirth, there is no changeless soul that transmigrates from one lifetime to another. The Buddha attempted to explain this with the metaphor that moving between the realms of Samsara was like a flame being transferred from one candle to another.
