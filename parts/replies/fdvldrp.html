    • myalt08831

      I can think of a few reasons why your old power brick would be larger.

      A big one is, electronic components have been trending toward being smaller and more power-efficient, while performing the same function. Smaller saves space on the circuit, so you can fit the power circuit in a smaller box. Power efficiency means you can do the same job while producing less heat -- less heat means you need a less-beefy cooling solution, again letting the overall charger housing become smaller.

      Likewise, the components in the display he is using might be using more efficient components. Even the same overall display technology implemented with newer parts could have a smaller circuit. (So both the power brick and display might get a bit more efficient or smaller just from using newer parts.)

      What would make a huge difference would be which display technologies you are both using. If you have a big, blocky monitor, it's probably a CRT or some other technology that shoots electron beams at the front panel of your screen. These are really power-hungry. By contrast, flat-panel displays are all much more power-sipping. Flat panel screens usually have a backlight of LEDs instead of lighting the front with electron beams. (There's more to it than that which I'm not expert enough to explain, but Technology Connections on YouTube does a great job of explaining all the nitty-gritty details.)

      If his display is a lot more efficient, his display might not need to draw as much power in the first place, so his power brick can be a less heavy-duty design.

      Lastly, some older hardware was over-engineered to last a lifetime. Modern tech has been influenced by a trend toward the minimum expense of manufacturing (and in some ways the minimum quality) that consumers will buy. But this is a bit less technical and more "the economy" so I can't really be 100% sure how much that plays into it.

      On another note, almost every household device has to convert from AC wall power to DC power, and to a lower-voltage than wall power. This power conversion can happen anywhere between the wall and the device, usually at the wall plug in a small brick (often nicknamed a "wall wart")... On a brick with a cord on both ends (like many laptop chargers)... Or inside the device being powered.

      So it's possible your boyfriend's monitor has a little circuit that converts power inside the screen housing.
