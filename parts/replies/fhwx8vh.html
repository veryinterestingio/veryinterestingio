    Aside from sediment and salt, are there dangers from the water being contaminated with pesticides or other man made contaminates which contain nitrates and chemicals like phosphorus? I know where I live contaminates from the tobacco fields get into the river causing algae blooms leading to red tide. I wondered if there is something similar that can happen in a tsunami.

    • alleax

      Tsunamis have caused soil contamination due to anthropogenic contaminants in the past. The 2011 Japan tsunami led to spilled oil in the Gensui river which required an oil fence to be constructed to prevent the oil from floating to the surface and downstream followed by manual oil removal by humans.

      The 2004 Indian Ocean tsumani contaminated and destroyed 40,000 drinking wells in Sri Lanka. This potable water was contaminated with salt and also nitrates. Referring specifically to eutrophication, the ecological consequences are not fully understood because huge tsunamis are quite rare. Tsunamis alter the physico-chemical parameters (organic content / granulometry etc.) of the area they impact.

  • Kythorne

    Is this one of the reasons the oceans are so briny? Just eons of rainwater diluting the salt and running into the ocean?

    • alleax

      Partly yes, think of the oceans as the washbasins of our planet. A small percentage of the salinity in the oceans is a direct result of runoff from land that introduces a multitude of elements into the ocean system. These nutrients are essential for life and thus ocean ecosystems directly depend on chemical inputs from the lithosphere.
