      Further, in actual reality, extremely high precision is not all that important for most engineering applications. If I know that I need something to remain above or below a certain limit, using π = 3 or π = 4 is probably close enough, depending on whether you need to round up or down.

      This is why they teach significant figures in Science classes. If I establish my tool with the worst precision is only good to three "significant figures" (e.g., roughly the precision of using a yardstick or ruler, or anywhere I can say "two and a quarter inches", or "a cup and three-quarters"), I can use 22/7 or 3.14 for π, which was more than sufficient precision for human-scale applications, such as bridges or aqueducts. Tooling like /u/romgab describes will give you closer to 9 significant figures, which is plenty good enough for millimeter-scale precision, and plenty of people have memorized π to 9 digits, but at this point, you're using digital tools and computers anyways.

    Abstract - a perfect circle of radius 2, say, has an area of exactly 4pi. That is an exact area, and a perfectly good number.

    Equally abstract example: A circle with radius 1/(π^0.5 ) has area 1. So if OP's idea of an "exact number" is a number that can be expressed finitely, then just set the radius to something obscure that divides well with pi.
