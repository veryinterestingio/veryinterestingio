    • Astromike23

      Are we able to use this approach on Gas Giants, to "see" into their core or does the pressure/density become too great?

      Most spectroscopic imaging will be limited to detecting the cloud-tops on the gas giants, since that's where most wavelengths of light first encounter something opaque.

      A few wavelengths of lights will penetrate to different depths. At 889 nm, for example, just outside the visible range into the infrared, methane absorption is particularly strong, and you can only sample the stratosphere well above most cloud-tops before the methane haze gets thick enough to become opaque to that wavelength. On the other hand, certain regions of the microwave spectrum can penetrate below the first few layers of the cloud deck before being rendered opaque from the concentration of deeper water vapor (and is exactly why the Juno spacecraft currently in orbit of Jupiter includes a microwave detector).

      No wavelengths of light will go much deeper than that, so detecting the core or even the liquid metallic hydrogen mantle with this technique is basically a no-go.

I work on compiling IPCC inventories for developed countries, where each country is required to report on their annual emissions based off the best scientific guidelines published by the IPCC. What this means in practice is that countries extrapolate data based on "emissions factors" or proxy data to calculate emissions from each sector. Countries don't measure exact emissions, but take the best educated guess using the same methods and accounting approach year after year, while this is reliable to calculate changes between years, it is not as accurate as it could be and countries are constantly changing the methods and recalculating. It also depends on national statistics and government to publish this data, meaning it is not always the most reliable, transparency, or honest. Some countries (as you can imagine) don't want to be open about their national data.

      The three satellites are set to launch in 2025 in order to collate data for the United Nations’ (UN) global assessment of greenhouse gas emissions in 2028. Once the satellites are in orbit around the Earth, they will form the world’s first planet-wide emissions observation system: currently, CO2 emissions are measured by conducting ‘inventories’ of fuel burning on a country-by-country basis; which, while it can provide a workable estimate of global emissions figures, is affected by poor measuring and reporting protocols by a number of national governments.
