    • WRSaunders

      Trees aren't like people. You can cut a stem off a tree and grow a whole tree from it, you can't do that with the hair off a human.

      So, it's not the case that "the tree is dead" and "the tree is alive" are the only two alternatives. Trees with dead branches are still alive. There are many "partially alive in some parts" alternatives for a tree.

      We don't count humans as "dead" just because their heart stops. Many people have been saved every year by medicine after their heart stops. People are legally considered "dead" when their brain stops working, even though many will still have a beating heart at that point in time.

    • ap1msch

      Not an expert, like other folks posting, but while trying to save a plant, I had another Redditor explain that "plants store life", but only so much. Plants (trees) without leaves can still be alive, but the measure of their stored energy is difficult/impossible to quantify. If they consume that energy (not dormant) and fail to absorb new energy, they will die.

      I recently bought three Carolina reaper plants that were shipped...poorly. I got them from the mailbox and replanted them immediately. All leaves fell off. They were three brown sticks in dirt. Without Reddit, I might have tossed them. Instead, I listened to the posters and cared for the brown sticks.

      The stored energy in the plants enabled new leaves to form on 2 of the plants. On the 3rd one, you could see sprouts start, but eventually they stopped sprouting.

      In short, 2 of the plants had sufficient energy to recreate what they needed to absorb energy. 1 plant was on the cusp, but fell just shy.

      Back to your question, without the ability to measure the "energy" stored in a tree/plant, you don't really know.
