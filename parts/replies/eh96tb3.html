    Yep, many chemicals can act as oxidisers. A good example of an elemental oxidiser other than oxygen is fluorine, which is in fact a much better oxidiser than even oxygen itself, due to having the highest electronegativity of any element. Combustion (burning) is simply rapid exothermic oxidation, what the oxidiser is doesn't matter.

        If you want elements or compounds, there isn't one, as far as I know. Oxygen is the second strongest oxidiser after fluorine. However, if you accept things other than elements or compounds, I can give you a treat.

        IUPAC defines oxidation as "the complete net removal of one or more electrons from a molecular entity." Usually, that refers to a different molecular entity tearing it away. But what about complete annihilation of an electron by an anti-electron? I am, of course, talking about the mighty positron. If you do some fancy physics math (or read how it's been done here (seriously, read it)), you'll see that the effective reduction potential of a positron is +2166413 V, a bit over 773000 times stronger than fluorine. That's the strongest currently existing oxidising agent, stronger than any known stripped superheavy atom, and maybe the strongest one possible. A stripped atom of unsepttrium (element 173) would be stronger though, and that element may very well be synthesisable in the (very, very far) future
