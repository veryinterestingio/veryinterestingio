      You’re on the right track, but you seem to have a couple things backwards.

      Narcan is not inert when administered through the mucous membranes. In fact, that’s exactly how it is being absorbed when it is atomized into the nose. It is not inhaled into the lungs; if that were the case, then narcan would be totally ineffective against opiate overdoses as those who have overdosed are often not breathing. It is worth noting, however, that Narcan is more readily absorbed through the mucous membranes of the nose as compared to the mucous membranes of the sublingual area.

      Narcan is an added agent in the buprenorphine preparation known as Suboxone, which is typically administered via the sublingual route. The beauty of this preparation is that Narcan has a lower bioavailability via the sublingual route and the ratio of Narcan to buprenorphine is adjusted in such a way that the Narcan does not completely block the effects of the buprenorphine. Conversely, if a recovering addict where to attempt to melt down the Suboxone and inject it intravenously, where Narcan has a much greater bioavailability, the added Narcan would completely block its effects. The precursor to Suboxone, Probuphine, was a pure buprenorphine preparation and IV abuse was a common problem, thus, Suboxone was brought to the market.
