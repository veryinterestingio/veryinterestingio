    • CentiMaga

      The uncertainty principle is fundamental (because it’s trivially true).

      A SQL is specific to measurement situation. In the example you provided in the other thread, the “SQL” in question was the variance in measurements of the position observable of a traveling wave-packet. The proposed “contractive state” had a position uncertainty below that of the archetypical “minimum-uncertainty wave-packet” that’s described in textbooks, and indeed could be made arbitrarily small; ergo it was below the SQL for wave-packets.

      However notice that the uncertainty principle was never violated, because higher momentum uncertainty balanced it out (indeed over-balanced) at all times.

      The SQL is different from the uncertainty principle, tho often related. To the extent it‘s constrained by the uncertainty principle in a given situation, it will never violate it.

    • CentiMaga

      Well contractive states are just a particular state for a free-particle system that unusual property. I found a few papers on google explaining them (e.g. this). The “standard quantum limit” is an experimental physics term.

      But I’d caution: nothing in QM makes sense except in light of the math, and everything in QM makes perfect sense under the math. Learn QM if you can. Griffith’s “Introduction to Quantum Mechanics” is the gold standard today. (1 year of calculus-based physics, multivariable calculus, linear algebra, statistics, and differential equations are QM prereqs.)

      If you’re just casually interested, a good starting point is to no longer think of a particle as a point “x̅(t): ℛ → ℛ^(3)” with mass “m” that moves according Newton’s 2nd law given initial conditions x̅(0) and x̅'(0), but rather as a wavefunction “Ψ(x̅, t): (ℛ^(3), ℛ) → ℂ” with mass “m” that moves according to the Schrödinger equation (which is just an expression of energy conservation Galilean invariance) given an initial condition Ψ(x̅,0) subject to the restriction that ∭ Ψ*(x̅, t) Ψ(x̅, t) dV = 1.

        There are lots of ways to conserve energy, and while the Schrödinger equation in a static potential will, it's not a statement of it.
