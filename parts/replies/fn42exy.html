    Lucky, mechanisms to repair these damage such as excision repair or photoreactivation repair exist; the latter being unique to specific plants and prokaryotic species. (Ironically the photoreactivation repair uses light to repair DNA damaged by light)

    Photolyase, the enzyme responsible for photoreactivation, exists in most species, fungi and animals included. It's not active in placental mammals though, for whatever reason. Maybe our ancestors lived so much in underground burrows that a photolyase was superfluous and mutations weren't selected against.
