    • ElectronRain

      Do you have a source for that? I don't know about efficiency but blue LEDs usually have the lowest efficacy (lm/W).

      There are several related but distinct measures of efficiency or efficacy.

      Blue O/LEDs tend to have the highest quantum efficiency - that is photons emitted per charge injected - due to their relatively large bandgaps and the energy gap law. That property (larger bandgaps) works against them for power efficacy however, as they require larger driving voltages to function than diodes emitting lower energy photons. Additionally, lumens are a photometric unit and the human eye's decreased sensitivity to blue light is also working against the power efficacy. Thus blue O/LEDs perform comparatively worse when measured in lm/W.

      Edit: You also asked somewhere else about CIEy. I didn't look at the paper, but academia usually uses the 1931 CIE chromaticity diagram (x,y) to measure the color of LEDs and, for blue O/LEDs specifically, the y value is particularly difficult and important to get right for "true blue" emission.
