    • Steve_Jobs_iGhost

      For this explanation, you really need to drop the notion of photons altogether. Trying to have any sort of understanding of why this is happening from the perspective of particles will prevent you from having a true understanding of what is going on. A good thing to keep in mind in general is in reality, there is no such thing as a "wave-particle duality". There are only waves, but we can treat them as particles under certain conditions. This is one of the conditions where we cannot treat the wave nature of light as a particle. If that's at all confusing, consider Newtonian mechanics against General Relativity. General Relativity is always the case, but under some conditions (standard everyday earth conditions), Newtonian mechanics does just fine.

      Because the two composing waves are moving at different speeds, there are alternating times of constructive and destructive interference. It isn't just one or the other, and in fact it's that principle that allows the superposition wave to travel at a speed different than the two composing waves. If they were always constructive or always destructive, that would imply that both waves were moving at the same speed, and thus the combined wave too would be moving at the same speed. Please see the first video I posted to have a visual understanding of that.

      As far as possibly increasing the speed of light, the best I can tell you is physics says no. I don't have a good grasp on the effects of phase velocity on group velocity (Composing wave effects on superposition wave), I'm simply aware of the phenomenon. Not taking into consideration the limit of the speed of light, I would be inclined to say that it seems like it could be a possibility, however that fundamental limit is there for a reason. I highly encourage checking out that first video.

    • Steve_Jobs_iGhost

      Each type of atom responds differently to different wavelengths of light. In general, there are three responses to incoming light - absorb, reflect, refract/pass through.

      In the case of a transparent material, all visible wavelengths of light pass through. For glass specifically, ultraviolet light is absorbed by the atoms.
