      It can be confusing - particularly when talking about efficacy trials (normally controlled trials) and effectiveness trials (how it works in the real world)

      In this specific case (vaccines) they tend to talk about efficacy being purely "What percentage of exposed people didn't get the disease" and effectivness purrely on "what are the outcomes".

      Possibly influenza is an easier example - because the 2019-20 flu vaccine in the northern hemisphere didn't contain all the common varients, efficacy was low (around 40%) - so far more people than hoped who had been vaccinated caught flu. That said, among vaccinated people, the outcomes were far better than the unvaccinated people. DEspite catching flu, vaccinatedd people were less likely to be hospitalised, if they were the length of stays were less and overall there were fewer deaths.
