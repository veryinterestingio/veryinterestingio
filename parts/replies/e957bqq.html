    • dstarfire

      One of the key aspects of insider trading you keep missing is that the information is "nonpublic". You can act on the information, you just have to share that information with other investors first.

      You could call your stock broker (most trading is done through a brokerage these days) and tell them what you know. You could tell a reporter. You could publish an ad with information in a newspaper, magazine, message board, or whatever that's likely to be read by other investors, and, especially, the people you're selling your shares to. It's not your responsibility to convince anybody.

      Once you've made a reasonable effort to make that information public, you are free to act upon it.

      What separates the amateurs from the pro's isn't the amount or quality of information (at least, not anymore), it's the ability to analyze and interpret that information.

      In the example: Okay, the CEO is going to kill his or herself, you have to share that fact with the world. But, as an expert stock investor, you're not obligated to share your opinion that the CEO's been horribly mismanaging the company for years, that the most likely* replacement is a goldmine of untapped brilliance, so this personal tragedy will actually make the company better.

      * you know this candidate is most likely based on your assessment of the board members and other candidates.
