    The estimate I remember seeing, but don't ask me to cite it, is that 60% of viruses are "icosahedral", meaning they have a protein shell.

    All viruses have a "protein shell", the capsid. Its primary function is protecting the genome until it can be released into the host cell. There's different types of these capsid in terms of structure roughly divided into the terms "helical", consisting of a polymer of few structures with highly variable length, "icosahedral", consisting of 20 equilateral triangles and "complex" ones, that are, well, a bit more complex...

    They can optionally have an "envelope", a lipid membrane derived from the host cell, on top of that, altering the strategy used for infection of the given virus compared to viruses that don't have such a membrane.

    Strictly speaking the only obligatory features of a virus is this capsid and the genome.

    (I know that you are most likely aware of this, but I thought that that part of your comment was a bit unclear.)

      • p040502123

        Well, the lipid membrane came about as regular evolution of some ancient virus. It's not necessarily down to external threats, it was just, for some scenarios a better suited strategy for the virus' reproduction.

        The membrane isn't really about protecting the virus more; Actually, enveloped viruses are generally more susceptible to inactivation because the membrane is far more sensitive than the capsid itself.

        This issue comes with the fact that the outer most boundary of a virus is the part that has to make contact with the host's cells to infect them. In naked viruses, it's proteins of the capsid itself; in enveloped ones it's proteins on its membrane.

        Therefore, by disrupting the membrane, you have taken away the virion's (virus particle) mechanism to infect.

        So, to summarize: It's not really about external threats, some classes of viruses are enveloped others simply aren't. They don't "decide" to spontaneously take some mebrane with them; the virus just uses the strategy, that its genome has evolved to use, as each strategy requires very specific genes to deploy.

    TO add to your comment:

    The estimate I remember seeing, but don't ask me to cite it, is that 60% of viruses are "icosahedral", meaning they have a protein shell.

    Icosahedron is a shape - its a polyhedron with 20 faces. Icosahedral simply refers to the shape of the virus. Viruses who have outer coats of proteins can take various shapes, including icosahedron, in contrast to viruses with outer coat of lipids and proteins (like the SARS-COV2 we're discussing here) which tend to approximate spheres. Pure (or nearly pure) protein coats can hold a shape while lipid bags without a structure anchoring it naturally becomes a sphere. For esample, dissociated cells in suspension from humans (or any vertibrates) often appear spherical, while they have different characteristic shapes in the different tissues they are part of.
