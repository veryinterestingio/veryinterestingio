      • fiendishrabbit

        That would actually not be one of them. Modern ejection seats are safe for an upside down ejection down to a height of about 50m (the russian ones are supposedly safe down to a height of 35m-ish, but I wouldn't try that).

        But all ejection seats have what's called an "ejection envelope", the limits of when it's possible to use the ejection seat (safely, or at all). Speed, spin, g-forces, rate of descent all put limits on the ejection envelope, although modern aircraft use 0-0 ejectionseats (zero altitude, zero velocity) so that there at least are no minimum limits in terms of altitude (when ejecting upwards) or speed.

    The cable slide escape is pretty interesting. Theoretically you hop in this thing and it whips you down into a reenforced bunker where you can ride out the massive explosion of the rocket above. Practically though, a lot of people didn't think it would actually work. It was all installed and setup anyway, for peace of mind. Though I believe that was directed at people like backers in the government and regular citizens. I can't remember where I read it, but I'm certain that most people actually involved in the launch (including the astronauts) knew that if it ever came down to them having to use it they'd be toast. Rocket emergencies tend to happen very quickly. This thing would work well if there was like a fuel leak and you needed to get them away to stabilize the rocket. It would not work at all for something like a fire breaking out on the pad, there's just too many extremely flammable/explosive things in a rocket to allow for much escape time
