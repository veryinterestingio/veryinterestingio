    • dabenu

      This is true, but the air leaving the AC is still at 100% humidity.

      Say it takes in air of 30°c and 70% relative humidity, and output said air at 15°c.

      As you can read in a mollier diagram the incoming air contains almost 19g of water per kg of air. Once you cool to 15°, the max humidity is 11g/kg. So all of a sudden you have a "surplus" of 8g of water, that will end up in the condenser.

      Then the cold air mixes with ambient air and warms up to say 25°, but still has 11g/kg of water. As the temperature is now higher, the relative humidity is around 55%, so the relative humidity in the room drops even though the cold air from the AC is at 100%
