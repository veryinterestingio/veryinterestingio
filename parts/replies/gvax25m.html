    I think one of the hardest aspects of this is that infants/toddlers develop language when it is socially appropriate to learn from the bottom up, whereas the months of just listening, babbling, repetition, and limited meaningful conversation isn’t socially appropriate as an adult. Imagine sitting at a dinner party with native speakers - they’d have to be very kind and patient to keep inviting someone over who can’t really participate in any conversation for months and months.

    I am no linguist, but I did an immersive language program in Italy in college and I became partially fluent by 4 months only because daily language lessons went in tandem with speaking in the community and living with a very encouraging host family. If I didn’t have the courses to learn basic grammar, common phrases, and simple vocabulary to get started, I don’t think I would have done very well aside from maybe picking up common greetings in the grocery store or using constant context clues to understand the gist of what people were trying to say to me, let alone understand isolated words.
