  • Jabru08

    Depends on the context.

    In general, inflammation is simply the body's response to some sort of cellular injury (e.g. necrosis or infection). This is usually a good thing, since if your body never responded to something like a minor skin infection it would eventually eat you alive (literally). Inflammation also cleans up all of the necrotic cells and starts repair pathways. It can be harmful to you if the response is excessive (e.g. septic shock), prolonged (e.g. persistent infection like TB), or inappropriate (think autoimmune disease).

    The acute inflammatory response has five signs: redness/warmth, swelling, pain, and loss of function. Pain medication like NSAIDs are generally ok to take in this context because the inflammatory mediators it inhibits only really stops pain and fever, which makes you more comfortable without really messing with the body's immune response. There is some literature out there which advises against turning down the body's thermostat (bacteria and viruses are really picky about what temperatures they like to replicate in), but this is up for debate and really only relevant in the setting of critically ill patients.
