      • foramsgalorams

        To be fair, whilst sand can definitely be finely broken up shells, I think the vast majority of silica sand that you would find on beaches is produced by the weathering of continental material (given enough time, quartz - a form of silica - is the only stuff that sticks around), which then gets transported to the coast by rivers. There’s an excellent Minute Earth explanation here. The sand predominantly made from broken up shells tends to be calcium carbonate fragments, which give the sand a particularly bright look, like the golden white beaches of the Bahamas.

        The kind of silica I was thinking of - making up the microscopic shells of certain plankton like diatoms, radiolaria, silicoflagellates... those shells are already smaller than sand sized. Once the plankton dies, they rain down into the seafloor and build up sediments to form a siliceous ooze. This may then become lithified into some kind of siliceous clay or chert if it gets buried deep enough.
