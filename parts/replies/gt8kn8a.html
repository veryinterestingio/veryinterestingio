    • PhotoJim99

      Exactly. It's depth of field. The camera (or lens') diaphragm lets you adjust the size of the aperture.

      The f/ number of the aperture is the bottom of the fraction of the proportion of the size of the opening to the lens' focal length, so for example, an aperture of f/4 is a hole 1/4 of the focal length - on a 200 mm lens, that'd be a 50mm opening. (Well, the equivalent to a 50mm circle; diaphragm blades make not-quite-circular openings, usually hexagonal.)

      The human eye works the same way (physics is physics). And by making a small hole with your hand, your hand is acting as a diaphragm.

  • crumpledlinensuit

    It does, but it's not really correct.

    A pinhole works because light travels in straight lines and putting it through a tiny pinhole means that you are basically only letting a small angle pass through, which makes it less blurry.

    A lens, on the other hand, "corrects" the angles by refraction, so that they all meet up at one place (or "focus"). As you said, a smaller aperture gets more rays in focus than a big one, but the reasoning isn't quite right.

    If you have a small enough aperture, you don't even need a lens at all - which is how pinhole cameras and camerae obscurae work.

      • crumpledlinensuit

        Interestingly, the setup you described in the last part can be used to manipulate images too, via spatial filtering.

        Let's assume we have two identical lenses with focal length f. I'll represent lenses as L, the pinhole as P and the initial and final images as I and I' respectively.

        I<-----f----->L<-----f----->P<-----f----->L<-----f----->I'

        The setup above will result in I' being identical to I but with a low-pass filter applied. If you use a pinpoint rather than a pinhole, you get a high-pass filter. Using slits can limit the filtration to one dimension or another. If you use a series of parallel slits (or rings) you can do very interesting things, like increasing resolution.

        The reason that this works is that the light intensity at P is a 2D Fourier transform of I, so by removing certain components, you change I'.
