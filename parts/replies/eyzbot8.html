    I'm a molecular biologist - not to be arrogant, but pretty much every other answer on this post is wrong. The answer, as far as we know, is no. We can use viruses in medicine, either to make vaccines or in gene therapies - but that's not their natural behaviour, and they don't spread through populations. You could argue that the act of developing immunity to a virus could be a net benefit (like how immunity to cowpox conferred immunity to smallpox), but that is not really due to the actions of the virus itself. You could again argue that viruses have played an important role in evolution by allowing genomic rearrangements and horizontal gene transfer - again, not an immediate effect of the virus, and not likely good for the individual. Most of the other answers refer to these, but to call these processes a direct beneficial effect of a virus is misleading.
