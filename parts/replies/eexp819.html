      • Squee427

        Not OP, sorry. But there are levels of consciousness based on what the person reacts to.

        Alert - completely awake with no stimulus.

        Voice - will react/open eyes to voice.

        Pain - Will open eyes or react (at the most will open eyes and maybe vocalize sounds, at the least will grimace and/or contract some muscles) to pain.

        Unresponsive - Will not react to anything in any way, including severe pain.

        You can have someone unconscious (not alert at all) but reacting somewhat to pain.

        Also see part of the Glasgow Coma Scale, of which one component is Motor Response:

        6 - Obeys Commands

        5 - Localizes pain (not necessarily awake/eyes open)

        4 - Withdraws from pain

        3 - Flexion to pain

        2 - Extension to pain

        1 - No reaction

        As for narcotic induction, I believe OP means that instead of needing to use paralytics and possibly multiple sedatives for anesthesia, s/he can use strictly a narcotic such as fentanyl. I only see certain varieties of things we need to sedate or paralyze for, so I don't have as much expertise in that field. In my area there are times we only need to use a narcotic to continue sedation instead of something like versed or propofol on top of the narcotic, for example in some intubated patients.

        Source: Am ED nurse.

      • Lancet

        The autonomic system is part of the nervous system that is outside direct conscious control - it is responsible for the "fight or flight" response when you are scared or in pain, and the "rest and digest" response when the opposite is true. The "fight or flight" stress response keeps functioning even when the patient is fully unaware under anaesthesia.
