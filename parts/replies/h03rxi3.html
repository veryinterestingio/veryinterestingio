      Yuck, discovering math instead of inventing... missing the point completely.

      The fact that the tools we invented (math) describe the world accurately (~ thanks to physics) doesn't make it anything less than an invented tool. We never ever looked at nature and discovered numbers or functions in it : we invented mathematical representations of the things we saw in nature and managed to make them accurate enough to somehow predict their evolution.

      Lets take an example of basic mechanics to illustrate my point : you SEE a metal ball falling from a certain height, its speed is increasing as the fall continues, you suppose this is linked to its weight (you're a genius and just intuited Newton's second law). Then you INVENT a mathematical description of the relevant parameters using measures (measures are clearly invented, we never "found" an object of 1kg) of them :

      -mass "m" in kg of the metal ball

      -weight "w" equal to m times a constant called "g"

      -position "h" in meters from the original position

      -how fast it's falling : speed "v" in meters per second

      -how fast the speed is increasing : acceleration "a" in m.s^-2

      -time since the beginning of the fall in second "t"

      You suppose ma=mg => a=g. The tool we invented to describe the link between a and v is the derivative and primitive, and same for v and h, so we apply these tools to a and find that : v=gt+0 , h=(1/2)g(t^2 )+0. Now we can measure stuff, verify our hypothesis and that our tools work. All the stuff we measure is not a discovery, it is not the reality, just representations of it, wether they are accurate or not will never change this.

      Saying we discovered math implies that it describes the intrinsic nature of reality, there is a debate on that point. (But it is the most constringent so the weight of proof falls on people defending that point.) This debate became relevant with the birth of quantum physics, but not with what we were talking about (calculus, geometry, mechanics...).
