    • mb34i

      There are 4 fundamental forces in our universe: gravity, electromagnetism, weak nuclear force, strong nuclear force.

      As a star gets more and more mass, gravity increases, so it shrinks. The gases it's made of get compressed more. At some point the gravity is so strong that the nuclei of atoms in the gas (plasma) in the star are squeezed so close that it's possible for them to combine into one giant (star-sized) "nucleus" - a neutron star.

      When this happens, you can think of it as gravity (the squeeze) winning against electromagnetism (protons in the nuclei of atoms repelling each other).

      If the neutron star continues to accumulate matter and gravity continues to increase, eventually gravity will "overcome" even the much stronger nuclear forces.

      At that point, as far as we know from physics, there is no other force to oppose gravity, and the material of the star is squeezed "infinitely", until its "size" reaches 0.

      That's a singularity. An object with the mass of stars (planets can be in orbit around it just like with a star), but all of it squeezed into a "mathematical" point - size 0 (not even micron or nanometer scale, literally 0).

      Of course, we cannot see a singularity, because gravity bends space and time at quite a distance around this singularity "center", and light from it can't escape to reach us. We can observe black holes, but all we see is the region of black from where light can't escape.

      What happens inside this region is theorized, but impossible to observe. And that's why they use the term "singularity", which is more of a mathematical term. As far as we know there's no other force to oppose gravity, so logically the material of the star will shrink down to 0 size.

    In math, it refers to the point where a mathematical model or object ceases to follow the 'normal' rules of math - such as being finite or differentiable.

    I think you've got the right idea, but putting it as "violating the normal rules of math" might give people the wrong idea. A singularity is a point in a mathematical structure which is undefined or not well-behaved.

    I'd describe a singularity as "violating the normal rules of a mathematical object" rather than the whole "rules of math".
