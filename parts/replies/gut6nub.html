  • Droidatopia

    No. The simplifications here are crossing between genes and chromosomes.

    You don't pass genes down to your children. You pass chromosomes, or whole libraries of genes. And there are only 23 pairs to choose from.

    It is even possible, although statistically extremely unlikely, that you could have a grandchild that is 100% genetically unrelated to you (discounting mitochondria)

    But even just considering the average cases (ignoring mitochondria, the y chromosome, and distant cousin marriages):

    Gen 0 (you) - 46 chromosomes

    Gen 1 - 23 chromosomes

    Gen 2 - 11-12 chromosomes

    Gen 3 - 5-6 chromosomes

    Gen 4 - 2-3 chromosomes

    Gen 5 - 1-2 chromosomes

    Gen 6 - 0-1 chromosomes

    This means that if every generation were to inherit as evenly as possible from their parents, then your great-great-great-great-grandchildren might have none of your chromosomes.

        Well, no that's just the middle of the road case, like the hump of a bell curve.

        It's possible you're completely gone by generation 4.

        It's also possible by Generation 8 that there are 3 of your chromosomes going strong.

        But you also have to consider the spread.

        By generation 6, on average there is ~1 chromosome of you, but assuming no generational die-offs and average child bearing rates, there are 64+ members of that generation with ~1 chromosome of you. And sooner or later distant cousins marry, which means you could find yourself finding more of yourself in a distant descendant.
