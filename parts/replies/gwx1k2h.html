  • jpj007

    To expand on this a bit, Euclidean geometry is about geometry done on a flat (uncurved) plane. On such a plane, parallel lines never meet.

    However, we can also picture curved surfaces like a sphere, for example. On a sphere, parallel lines can meet.

    Think of the planet's Equator. A circle going around the middle of the globe. There are lines of longitude going north/south through the Equator, all at right angles to the Equator. These lines are parallel. However, they still meet at the poles. So, the surface of the Earth is non-Euclidean.

    You can't prove a postulate because a proof starts with your postulates. There isn't anything you can prove them with

    In fact whether you accept a postulate/axiom to be true is a free choice. Just as you can construct non-Euclidean geometries without the 5th postulate, you could also do the same thing with any of the other postulates as well. Fano's Geometry is an example of a geometry which doesn't use any of Euclid's postulates.
