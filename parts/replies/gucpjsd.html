    Anyone care for some etymology? Meteor comes from the greek work "μετέωρος", which is a composite word consisting of "μετά" (=after) and "αορ" (modern greek word equivalent would be αίρω, and it translates to lift/raise/set free).

    So μετέωρος in modern greek translates to something that hangs in the air, "hover" might be a good word to describe it. It can be used figuratively for something that is uncertain and it hovers between some positions/decisions.

    In ancient greek it was also used for things that were very tall (e.g. the famous Meteora Monasteries), and also could generally refer to celestial bodies.

      Pluto Eris and Ceres are our solar system's 3 officially recognized "Dwarf Plannets". These are objects large enough to force themselves into spheroids, but not so large as to clear their orbits of other stuff.

      Planetoid isn't really something with a strict definition, but yes, generally speaking, you wouldn't be wrong to use it while talking about a dwarf planet.

      Edit: There are certain to be many undiscovered dwarf planets, along with several likely dwarf planets that are still being classified (Haumea, Makemake, Quaoar, Sedna, Orcus and Gonggong)

      Edit 2: also worth noting, Pluto's largest "moon" Charon is actually so large that it's more accurate to say the two bodies orbit eachother, than that the moon orbits Pluto. Technically this should make them binary dwarf planets rather than simply a dwarf planet and its moon.
