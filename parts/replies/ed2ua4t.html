    • Natanael_L

      His graph would be slightly easier to read with a grid overlay.

      Think of that image as being a continous feed from a printer, like the graphs on a EKG measuring heart activity. This graph prints in a downwards direction.

      The satellite is transmitting on one small range of frequencies, and as it got closer to the receiver the radio waves was compressed so it looked like the frequency range moved sideways on the receiver side. That means the receiver has to adjust the range of frequencies it listens to in order to follow the signal.

    • JP8_And_Coke

      Look at some SDR software like SDRSharp.
       
      OK, first look up "SDR" (Software Defined Radio) on Amazon/whatever, and see that you can get one for $25. Then, once it's delivered, build a ghetto 2 meters antenna out of scrap wires (or sticks and coat hangers) laying around, plug it into your computer, and run an SDR app (like SDRSharp for Windows), and have a blast seeing and hearing all this stuff. Depending on your geographic location (and noise floor - away from the city is better), you can absolutely pick up a sat with a surprisingly small/cheap amount of equipment.
       
      Hit me up if you really think this stuff is neat (or head over to /r/amateurradio and/or /r/sdr ); we can help get you started for dirt-cheap. Amateur radio isn't just about 70 year olds bitching about their ingrown toenails; I got into it because I like the tech aspect.
