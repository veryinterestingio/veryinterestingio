      • shiny_roc

        If I may rant slightly, it bugs me when people say that 88% is only a modest difference relative to 94%. It's actually half as good. With the mRNA vaccines, you had a 1 in 17 chance to get the COVID from the original virus with its ID50, and you have a 1 in 8 chance to get COVID from the Delta variant with its ID50. The protections for severe disease are similarly diminished, though the protection was so amazingly good against severe disease that it still remains excellent.

        Of course there are confounding factors. In the US at least, the vaccinated population skews heavily toward older and sicker people, both because they were prioritized first for vaccination and because younger people have generally seen vaccination as less urgent because of their lower risk of death (never mind how incredibly awful Long COVID is and that it hits young people plenty often). The bulk of the people being hospitalized and dying from breakthrough Delta cases are elderly, suggesting that protection against hospitalization and death in younger people is still extremely robust, but unfortunately we don't have as much data to break it down by demographics because CDC stopped tracking non-hospitalization/death breakthrough infections. (I am overall still highly confident in CDC and laud their efforts and expertise. However, in my decidedly non-expert opinion, that was a mistake.)

        I would still love to see more data on Long COVID from breakthrough cases (at this point with Delta, though previously I wanted it for original and Alpha). If the same percentage of breakthrough cause Long COVID as as unvaccinated, that ends up being roughly 98-99% effectiveness against life-altering debilitation, which is very solid but still leaves substantial risk of life-altering disabilities.
