        So, instabilities in the atom as a whole can be solved a number of ways. I’m definitely no chemist, so take this with a grain of salt...

        Yes, I believe ionization is essentially the imbalance of charge between the nucleus and the electrons they “support”. This usually leads to easier chemical reactions, as it solves the imbalance through binding with another atom that can make it more stable. But this new binding, to ultimately reach some local stability, may take a little more outside energy to make happen. It’s kind of the basis for chemistry in my understanding. Atoms/molecules liking other atoms/molecules to achieve stability through reactions under different temperatures, pressures, light sources, It gets really complicated due to so many possibilities and the “strength” of each one, but it’s the reactions between atoms.

        Now, if we are talking about instabilities within the nucleus of an individual atom, this is where nuclear physics comes in (and I’m a little more familiar with, tbh).

        The nucleus is made up of protons and neutrons that are “glued” together by the strong force. The strong force ONLY works when a proton or neutron are very, very close together. Otherwise two protons will be repelled away from each other (as if you had two repelling magnets held very close together, then released. But if they almost touch they are “glued”). Neutrons are basically just filler to help “supply more strong force” while not holding a charge to make the whole nucleus want to fly apart. This balance of protons and “filler” neutrons is quite important too. Imbalances in this lead to radioactive decay in 3 main forms.

        Alpha decay, where a helium nuclei (2 protons, 2 neutrons) is ejected from the main nuclei to make a smaller, more stable nuclei. This usually throws off some extra neutrons too, which provides the initial energy for the chain reactions used in nuclear power plants...

        Beta decay, which itself has a couple forms and tons of intricate interactions, but is basically the switching of a proton to a neutron, and vice versa. Interestingly, this can sometimes create a positron (anti-electron/antimatter) that instantly gets destroyed with our regular matter and produces 2 pure energy gamma rays in opposite directions. Which is a great segue to...

        Gamma decay, where again, it gets extremely complicated, but can kind of be reduced to “the nucleus fired off some pure energy for some reason”. Gamma rays are an extremely broad class of high frequency electromagnetic energy. Visible light is the same exact type of energy, just a much lower frequency. So infrared is low frequency and non-visible to our eyes, then visible reds up through to violet, then non-visible ultra-violet, and then waaaay higher up the spectrum is gamma rays. The frequency (how fast it oscillates) combined with the amplitude (how far it oscillates) will give you the total energy. Gamma rays are thus relatively always potentially destructive, regardless of amplitude.

I believe this could prompt a beta decay where it creates an electron instead of a positron (as I previously mentioned). I would guess this is the case where a proton switches to a neutron, releasing its charge and flipping a quark(???). Anyways... it would be a case where the nucleus could create an electron to provide that outer structure for itself, but this would likely instantly lead to more instability in the nucleus once it received electrons again, which are very easy to steal through chemical reactions. So a very highly ionized atom never really exists since it can easily correct that imbalance through chemical reactions with its ever abundant neighbors. You can also have too many electrons giving structure and it kinda collapses in on itself and the neutron goes through “electron capture”, which is a form of beta decay.
