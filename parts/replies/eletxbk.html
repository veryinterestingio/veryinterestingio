    • mfb-

      IIRC atoms with greater mass than iron are energetically suitable for fission decay.

      Some of them but not all.

      Nickel has the peak in binding energy per nucleon: Something a bit heavier still has a very high binding energy, if it would split the products would be far away from the peak and have a lower binding energy. Niobium-93 is the first isotope where fission would release energy. Compare this to iron with a mass number of ~55: You need close to twice the mass of iron before fission releases energy.

    • diazona

      Did you mean "subatomic"? If so, then not really. While it is true that physical processes on such small scales are random and can be described by probabilities, they still do have to satisfy certain constraints. One of those constraints is conservation of energy, and that outright forbids the decay of an atomic nucleus if the decay products would require more energy than the original nucleus. Of course, something from outside the atom could provide the needed energy, but then we wouldn't really call it a decay, nor spontaneous.

    • mfb-

      The probability for a process that doesn't conserve energy is exactly zero (popular science articles get that wrong frequently). If every possible splitted state needs more energy than the original atom has then the atom cannot split.

  • Teeshirtandshortsguy

    Why is alpha decay considered a separate process? What kind of radiation is it when something is undergoing fission? Neutron?

    • mfb-

      Fission of large nuclei produces two nuclei that are typically ~1/3 and 2/3 the mass of the original nucleus. It only happens with very large nuclei and it is usually a rare process unless you go to the very short-living elements that we can produce in accelerators. Compare this to alpha decay, where one nucleus has just 4 nucleons (and always 4) and the other one can have more than 200. It can happen with smaller nuclei and it is a common process with many nuclei.
