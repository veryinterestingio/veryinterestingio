    • qwerty_ca

      Companies such as https://www.wikiwand.com/en/Global_Crossing and https://www.wikiwand.com/en/MCI_Inc. paid for them. A lot of cables currently in service around the globe were laid around the dotcom bubble (1995-2002ish). New cables are still being laid but the industry is nowhere near as frantic as it was back then. It takes around 2-3 years for the cable laying project end-to-end, but this involves things like permitting, finding financing, doing seafloor mapping etc. The laying of the cable itself takes in the weeks-to-few-months timeframe depending on the ship used, the weather, the length of the routes, the current, the type of cable etc.

  • SynthPrax

    How do the cables handle being deployed across expanding fault lines, like the mid-oceanic ridge in the Atlantic?

    • qwerty_ca

      It's not like the zone between the ridges is a bottomless pit haha. There's still a seabed there, even if it's deeper. They just plop the cable along the seabed at the bottom. Here's what the continental ridge looks like in Silfra, Iceland for example: https://thumbs-prod.si-cdn.com/vkCf-ItkQVGKXLZM2MxmXiS7vJA=/fit-in/1072x0/https://public-media.si-cdn.com/filer/6e/60/6e6091a8-fbf5-45ac-ad2a-79102bc3abf4/silfra-sw-5.jpg

      In this case it's very near the surface, but under the ocean it's the same thing, just with a lot more water on top and a lot more distance between the cliffs. You can simply drop cable from one edge of the cliff, run it along the bottom and bring it back up on the other side.

      And yes, before you ask, the cable will break in case of earthquakes (https://www.submarinenetworks.com/news/cables-cut-after-taiwan-earthquake-2006) or sharks biting it (https://www.independent.co.uk/life-style/gadgets-and-tech/news/sharks-are-eating-the-internet-in-vietnam-9962747.html).

      At least with the earthquakes part, there's no real cheap-yet-effective way to mitigate that yet. People just have to head out and fix it if it's broken. Here's a ship that does things like that https://www.wired.co.uk/article/subsea-internet-cable-ship-boat.
