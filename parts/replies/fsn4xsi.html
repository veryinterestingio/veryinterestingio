    • jumbledupname

      This is a very [very] good question.

      The commonly used image of an atom is very wrong. Electrons don't orbit a nucleus in the same was as planets orbit a star. Electrons are best thought of as excitation of an omnipresent "electron field" [not to be confused with an electric field]. This electron field has a base value everywhere. And wherever the field has an excess value over that base you get an electron (voila!).

      So now you have a field interacting with another field [specifically, an electron field interacting with an electromagnetic field]. And that influences where excitations happen, what momenta they carry, and what angular momenta they carry etc. Elementary quantum mechanics simplifies this picture by instead talking about probabilities of finding such excitations in a given region of spacetime --that's the "wave" nature of the particle in loose-speak.
