        I think it would be debated. For two reasons. First, just because it's a polarizing issue. There are a lot of people who would vehemently deny it's extraterrestrial, regardless of the evidence, and there are others who would immediately jump to it being extraterrestrial, again regardless of the evidence. And these groups can be very vocal, so even if they're in the minority, they will garner lots of attention and make it a topic of debate until the evidence is truly overwhelming. Just look at events like Tabby's star and Oumuamua, with all the people screaming aliens. People believe what they want and can make issues out of it. We still debate climate change, despite the evidence.

While you are right that there would likely be very noticeable differences, we likely won't be ABLE to notice these differences. Like, even just putting it under a microscope is difficult. We'd most likely be working off of just chemical analysis and very grainy photos to start with and gradually get more and more info as we sent better/different equipment for tests. This preliminary data may give a clear indication of origin, but going back to point #1, if there is ANY other explanation (which there almost always is) then people will argue it until we've completely ruled out those other explanations.

The vast majority of the equipment they send off is not scrubbed to the best of their abilities because it's just too expensive. They know this equipment has germs on it. Very few, within an "acceptable" margin, which is likely to die on the journey, but definitely still there. Attempting to get rid of all the germs is extremely difficult, can't be guaranteed, and isn't really necessary for most of their research. So they don't bother. That's part of why they again don't go to where martian life is most likely to be, because their own rules and regulations would require the most stringent sterilization procedures which they don't want to dedicate budget funds to. They can get so much more research done by dedicating those funds to the actual equipment instead.

  • JDFidelius

    We can't be sure that we aren't contaminating other places, but I would think that we would be able to tell the difference between earth life and life that evolved from a completely separate source. We aren't sure what sort of form DNA would take if life evolved differently, if DNA is even used for passing information from one generation to the next. Also, mitochondria have their own DNA since they likely descend from symbiotic internal bacteria. If we found something on Mars with mitochondria, we would know that it's from earth because it would be too much of a coincidence for extraterrestrial life to have organelles with ring-shaped DNA very similar to bacterial DNA. The fact that the mitochondria carry information, and that that information is in the specific form of DNA, and also happens to be ring-shaped, is one of thousands of litmus tests for telling if something we find was brought there recently by rovers or if it evolved on Mars for billions of years.
