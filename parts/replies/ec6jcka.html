    Note that this also wastes a significant amount of power, as there's a voltage drop over the diodes. Even if you use ideal diodes (circuits that approximate the behavior of a diode with no voltage drop), it still consumes something.

    Edit: The term "ideal diode" seems to make a lot of people upset. It has two definitions:

    • A theoretical circuit element that allows current to pass in one direction but not in the other, with no voltage drop, and no reverse breakdown at any voltage.

    • A real-life circuit which approximates this behavior. Because it's in the real world, it has some loss associated with it.

    EEs tend to talk about the second definition because they're a thing that we use, you can buy them on Digikey. I guess physicists talk about the first?

      • Davecasa

        Ideal diodes are generally implemented as a FET with a controller. When the controller senses a positive voltage (or sometimes even zero voltage), it turns the FET on. When it senses negative voltage, it shuts off. As FETs have no voltage drop, this looks a lot like a diode with no voltage drop. That's the ideal. In the real world:

        • FETs don't have a voltage drop, but they do have a resistance. The more current you pass through them the more power they consume, and it gets bad quickly as the consumption is I^2 x R rather than I x Vdrop of a diode. In some cases it can even be worse than the diode.
        • This circuit is very fast, under a microsecond normally, but not as fast as a diode (nanoseconds). Sometimes that matters.
        • Self-powered ideal diode controllers need to get their energy from somewhere - this is often done by turning the fet off for some portion of the duty cycle (~1%), letting the fet's body diode conduct current, and charging a cap from the difference. This decreases efficiency because you have the voltage drop of the diode some of the time, and introduces a bit of ripple in output voltage.
        • Etc.
