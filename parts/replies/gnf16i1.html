    • cos

      And this is an RNA virus. RNA is a single strand replication with little effective self-checking

      I believe one of the things that distinguishes coronaviruses from most other kinds of virus - including other kinds of RNA virus - is that they do have error checking. They encode their own replication proofreader that catches a large majority of replication errors. This means their mutation rate is much much lower than that of most other kinds of viruses, which is what allows them to have much longer RNA - which, in turn, is how they've been able to evolve more clever mechanisms for evading immune systems.

      On the opposite extreme you have something like HIV, which mutates too much for us to have ever been able to come up with a vaccine.

      However, coronavirus proofreading is still much worse than the normal DNA-to-mRNA process for our own proteins. So their mutation rate is still much much higher than our protein error rate. Edit: And of course our own mutation rate is even lower, since our mRNA doesn't itself get replicated, and our DNA replication is considerably more reliable.
