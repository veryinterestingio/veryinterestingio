    In addition to less time and energy being spent moving around and being awake, you also do not notice things while you are asleep. During the day, changes happen over a few hours are small enough that you might not notice, but being asleep for eight hours (plus however long before you fell asleep) allows those changes to add up.

    It is similar to how you might not notice how much a younger sibling grows when you see them every day, but if you are gone for a summer, you will notice the change.

    I don't think this effect can be overstated.

    Ironically, for RA the classic symptomology is that the symptoms are worst in the morning when the body has not been moving and immune complexes can form more in the joint spaces. Patients classically wake up with morning stiffness which then improves during the day as they are moving.

    EDIT: osteoarthritis is classically worst at night; however, depending on how old OP is, if the RA is long-standing such that there has been noticeable damage done to the joints, it would make sense that symptoms are now worse at night.
