    I'm not totally certain about my parsing here, but...

    If "occurring by random chance" means "occurring by chance alone," then that seems like a not-incorrect way to state that you've assumed a nil null hypothesis. And since you didn't correct it, I assume you meant "results" as a fudge for "at least as extreme results". So that becomes "the probability of at least as extreme results occurring under the (nil) null hypothesis" which is a correct definition of p-values.

    In other words, a p-value is not the probability that a result at least as extreme as yours did occur by chance alone, P(H|D). But it is the probability that a result at least as extreme yours would occur by chance alone, P(D|H).

    • peroleu

      It has nothing to do with random chance.

      For example, if you're comparing two blood pressure medications and your hypothesis is treatment with medication x will have a greater reduction in blood pressure than medication y, then your null hypothesis is medication x will have no effect on blood pressure compared to medication y. If your p-value of blood pressure reduction between the two groups is 0.01, then there is a 1% chance that you would find the same results if medication x had no effect on blood pressure compared to medication y.

      You are testing the null hypothesis against the alternative hypothesis.
