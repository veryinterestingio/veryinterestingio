    • EclecticDreck

      In order to be licensed as a therapist in the US, a person must attain a significant degree of education beyond what a layperson is likely to come upon in the normal course of life. The degree conferred is generally a Masters of some flavor or another. They must also spend hundreds of hours as an intern working directly in the role of therapist under the supervision of at least one licensed and experienced therapist. None of this confers magical powers; instead, it offers the trained therapist two things. First, they have knowledge of and exposure to the various therapeutic techniques which have demonstrated positive results in studies, as well as knowledge which will assist in diagnosing the would-be patient. Second, they have significant experience using the training and techniques against a much larger patient set. All that this really does is make the trained therapist more likely to select and correctly execute useful therapeutic techniques compared to the layperson.

    Another aspect is efficacy versus effectiveness. An efficacious technique produces the desired result in controlled research settings. If that therapeutic effect is preserved when they attempt to implement that program outside of the research settings (in clinical practices), it is said to have effectiveness.

    Of course I’m not saying that comparing a trained therapist to a random person would answer that (because that wouldn’t really make sense), but it’s another factor to take into consideration
