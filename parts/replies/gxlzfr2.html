    Pilot here (though it's been awhile)

    If you're getting a tuned for cruising, you typically want it to be efficient for the aircrafts designed cruising true air speed, and at a low RPM (relatively speaking)

    if you just want climb performance (e.g. STOL, or sky-diving plane), low TAS and (often) high RPM is the go.

    With high TAS, low rpm, the prop needs to have a much more aggressive twist in the blade.

    For extreme example, see reno air-race f1 class aircraft. 250MPH on a fixed pitch prop, you are really pushing the limits of what you can do with a fixed pitch. They have bonkers performance once at speed, but their take-off roll is crazy long

      Great summary. I think a critical point people often misunderstand is that the airspeed affects propeller efficiency and the pitch required for optimal efficiency. You mention it quite clearly, just wanted to emphasize it.

      Imagine that the propeller is an airfoil, a wing, that moves through the air. Since a specific angle between this wing and the air moving past it causes the highest amount of lift (thrust) to be generated, the airspeed of the aircraft is obviously a factor in selecting this angle.

      This explanation also shows why propellers are twisted - rotational speed is higher towards the tip of the propeller than near the center.
