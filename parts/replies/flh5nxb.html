  • harmonicr

    First, for any supply to sufficiently exist, demand is necessary. This means that step one for increasing scientific literacy is to promote interest in scientific literacy. NDT does a great job with Cosmos and his pop-sci videos, as did Carl Sagan before him. Answering the question "Why people should *be interested in* science?" is a challenge. How can it be made more fun, entertaining, and relatable?

    Second, people need to have awareness of what resources exist. Khan academy is one. Crashcours is another that I've personally liked for teaching science, especially to middle/high-schoolers. Richard Feynman's lectures and books are a great accessible way to break into advanced theoretical physics.

    Third is in regards to statistics. I slightly disagree with u/Link7280. 1) Metareviews are crucial and are gaining popularity in social sciences. These reviews synthesize findings/data from various articles on one topic to make a better assessment of what is going on. In this regard, Link is on point. However, 2) if the sample size is small in a study, but the statistics reach significance, then the effect is still meaningful. This is because inferential statistics take sample size into consideration when rejecting a null hypothesis. The question is essentially "What are the chances of gathering a sample of this size, using the same sampling procedures, and finding these results (which are generally an average based on some clearly measurable dependent variable, for example "Depression")." The distribution of sample means compares sampling distributions built on n-sizes, and larger n-sizes result in more robust distribution differences. See: http://onlinestatbook.com/stat\_sim/sampling\_dist/. If we find a sample that the statistics suggest we could have found easy enough by random chance alone, then we don't have confidence that our effect truly exists. Small samples *can* however impact whether we consider the results to have high external validity (do they apply to everyone? Or only to a select group of people. For example, college aged students at an affluent private school who were included in this study.). Finally, 3) some journals are indeed better than others. Checking their impact factors is one good start to addressing whether the journal has clout. Looking into the authors is the next step (h-index, CV, advisors, etc.).

    My main point here is that we need to stir up interest before anything else. We need to prove to the average person that science is enjoyable to digest and that it is relevant to their lives. Part of this involves dissolving the elitism that surrounds a lot of scientific circles.
