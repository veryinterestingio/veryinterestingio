      There is one other technical point that I want to make sure is clear. Y'all are discussing the fact that there cannot be a UNIFORM distribution on the natural numbers (this means they are all equally likely). I think this is what a lot of people mean when they say truly random, but this is not a technical term because if you, for example, rolled a die with six sides labelled 1,2,3,4,4,4, it would still be random. But it would be a different distribution than a standard six sided die.

      So you CAN have a nonuniform distribution on the natural numbers. For example P(k)= 1/2^k. So there is a 50% probability of picking 1, a 25% probability of getting 2, a 12.5% probability of getting 3, and so on. For this distribution the sum of the probabilities turns out to be 1, even though it is an infinite sum.

    • Lost_Geometer

      Yes. This is the common meaning of the informal (and formally nonsensical!) statement that a random natural has property x with probability y. To be precise let P(x,N) be the probability that a natural number uniformly selected from the range (0,N) has property x. Then if \lim_{N \to \infty} P(x,N)=y as shorthand one says that the probability of x is y.
