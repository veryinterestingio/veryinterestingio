    • Potato-Aim

      Artificial gravity is the sensation caused by the floor accelerating you toward the center of the circle, away from the straight line your inertia would otherwise take you. You can solve for the acceleration (a) you would experience at any radius (r) with a couple variables and equations. You need mass (m), force (F), and the RPM of the station.

      Given a fixed RPM and mass, we can compare the acceleration you would experience at different radii (i.e. at different distances from the middle of the station. The equation for centripetal force is:

      • FsubC = (mv^(2))/r

      To know your velocity at a given radius and RPM we need:

      • v = [2(pi)r]/t

      where t is time. We can then combine the two equations with

      • F = ma

      to yield

      • a = [m[(2(pi)r)/t]^(2)]/(rm)

      or

      • a = [(2(pi)r)/t]^(2)/r

      You'll notice that the experienced acceleration is not dependent on mass, just like real gravity! From this, we can get some theoretical values. The acceleration in the case of

      • 2 meter radius
      • 2 seconds per revolution

      would be 2pi^(2) meters per second squared. Now, reducing the radius to 1 meter, we get

      • 1 meter radius
      • 2 seconds per revolution

      yielding a = pi^(2) meters per second squared. In other words, if you reduce your radius by half, your acceleration is also reduced by half. In the case from your question, where you're in the middle of the station, your variables would be:

      • 0 meter radius
      • 2 seconds per revolution

      giving you an acceleration of 0 meters per second, or zero gravity. Hope this was helpful!
