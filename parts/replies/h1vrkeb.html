    • Appaulingly

      Yes you've got it. Liquids comprise of freely moving atoms/ molecules. And surface tension (caused by a net attraction normal to the surface) tends the cause the liquid to form fairly smooth interfaces.

      Solids that are shiny can be crystalline and therefore form fairly regular and smooth surfaces. But even amorphous solids can be shiny (e.g. plastics).

      In the end though it's about the surface roughness. Reflection will always occur at an interface to varying extents. It's just about whether or not it's diffuse or specular reflection.

      It's also why most conventionally dull materials can be sanded/polished to a shine. Most metals can be made to be mirror like, any non-porous thing can for the most part. You can make some of those porous things shiny by using something to get the surface more uniform, like stains and oils on wood. Shine = uniformity
