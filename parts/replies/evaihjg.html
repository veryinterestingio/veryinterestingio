    So, SpaceX went for a commodity strategy - build one really really good motor to serve first and second stage, rely on modern control electronics to regulate 9 motors operating together in the first stage, and engineer for reusability, and get your reliability that way, and dramatically cut costs to fly the rocket and use that money to pay for the new R&D.     • bubba-yo

      SpaceX wouldn't have even started if the aforementioned technological changes hadn't taken place. SpaceX was hardly running circles around existing launch capacity. They had a cheap rocket with a limited utility. That utility expanded considerably as the Merlin engine got better, as they developed the Heavy, and as they got reusability.

      The Atlas V was a more reliable rocket, with comparable capabilities. But they like everyone else were production limited. Reusability is what allowed SpaceX to dramatically up their cadence. But the RD-180 in the Atlas V is 20 years old, and there's no incentive to refine that motor because of that reliability - instead they want sales to pay off the R&D involved.

      Reusability provides a new incentive to continue to invest. It's a completely different approach to rocket building. Nobody gets up in the morning and builds the exact capacity car they need to get to work. I drive to work every day with 3 empty seats. That's fine, because the fuel cost to carry the extra seats is minimal, and when I do need 4 seats, I don't need to buy a different car. That's SpaceXs future. More rocket isn't a problem because fuel is extremely cheap. That guides their design. It doesn't guide anyone elses - at least not yet.

      Your argument would make sense if NASA was the only customer, but SpaceX didn't have them as a customer for a while. They had private customers - the same customers that ULA was going for. ULA could and should have built the Falcon 9, but they didn't, and that's not NASAs fault, it's ULAs fault.
