    No guarantee of that happening, though. Evolution does not work on neatly separated traits, it works on mutations which have multiple effects.

    E.g. a mutation can simultaneously increase the viral load, decrease the minimum infectious dose, and decrease your probability of survival.

    Decreasing the probability of survival from 99% to 98% is twice the mortality rate, but only 1% difference for the virus. Other effects of that mutation can make up for it.

    In case of flu, it has a very short incubation period, little asymptomatic spread, etc. Plus the 1918 flu was far more deadly than COVID-19 , with correspondingly higher impact of non survival.

    For an example of that evolution not happening, consider smallpox, which remained highly deadly. It was ultimately eradicated by deliberately spreading a similar virus, vaccinia, which rarely spreads between people naturally (so it was administered as a vaccine, in early days "arm to arm", see https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaccinia#History ). An earlier practice, variolation, used scabs from mild cases and may have resulted in selection of a less deadly strain, but not one that would spread naturally. Of course, smallpox is a very different virus with a very large DNA genome, so its not clear how much you can generalize from that other than to note that such evolution is not guaranteed.
