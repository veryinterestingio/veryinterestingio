    • juleznailedit

      The skin you're picking at is called the keratinized proximal nail fold, commonly mistaken for the cuticle. That dry skin you're finding underneath the fold is the cuticle.

      I'm not a dermatologist, so I can't tell you anything regarding the health of your nails. You said you're undergoing treatment, so I would ask the person treating you about the health of your skin and nails, unless they're someone like a psychologist/psychiatrist then I'm not sure they would know. My guess would be you're dealing with dry skin and you have a habit of picking. Softening the skin could probably help lessen the itch to pick.

      I can suggest you purchase a pair of sharp cuticle nippers (Tweezerman is the best brand in my opinion) to help nip those hangnails instead of pulling them. It can help reduce the parts that stick up that you tend to pick at. I would also highly recommend you purchase some jojoba oil and start using it to help keep the skin hydrated & supple. Jojoba is the best oil to use due to its molecular size being almost identical to that of our own sebum (body oil). Use it when you wake up, at least once during the day & before bed. Ideally, you want to use it after you wash your hands to help replenish moisture, but that kind of consistency comes with time. Start small with 3 times a day (morning, mid-day & before bed). Focus on rubbing it into your proximal nail fold, as this can stimulate blood flow. However, if the person you're being treated by tells you to do something else (if they're a dermatologist and not a psychologist/psychiatrist) please follow their recommendations before mine. If you're seeing a dermatologist, ask them about the suggestions I've made & see what they say.

    • juleznailedit

      Unfortunately, you can't change the shape of your nail bed. Both of those are absolutely due to genetics! The smile line (hyponychium), which is where the edge of the nail bed meets the nail plate, isn't always even or a perfect crescent shape. That's perfectly normal! Same goes for the c-curve, or lack thereof, that's genetic as well! Some people have super tight c-curves, other people have more relaxed curves and some, like your index, have none.
