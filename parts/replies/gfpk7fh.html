  • kilotesla

    to actually get temperature as a function of time you need to then solve these differential equations. Which can be difficult.

    But fortunately, this is an easy one.

    dT1/dt=k(T2-T1)/C1 dT2/dt=k(T1-T2)/C2

    Define ∆T = T1-T2

    Subtract equations

    d∆T/dt = -k(1/C1 + 1/C2)∆T

    Define 1/tau = k(1/C1 + 1/C2)

    In terms of tau and the initial temperature difference ∆T(0),

    ∆T(t) = ∆T(0) • e^(-t/tau)

    The final temperature, equal for both, is Tf = (T1•C1 + T1•C2)/(C1 + C2).

    You can then write the individual temperature trajectories,

    T1(t) = Tf + (T1(0)-Tf)e^(-t/tau).

    And the same for T2.

    This is an exact solution of that differential equation, but that differential equation is an approximation, as many comments have directly or indirectly discussed. The level of approximation is very similar to the level of approximation in Newton's law of cooling. A quick summary is that the two bodies are effectively isolated from the surroundings and that the temperature within each is uniform.

      Not quite: C1 and C2 are not specific heats (or even heat capacities) in the equations dT1/dt=k(T2-T1)/C1 and dT2/dt=k(T1-T2)/C2, as the units don't work out. They stand for (and it took me a good 10 minutes to work this out) cρxV/A, where c is specific heat, ρ is density, x is the distance corresponding to the temperature difference, V is the volume of the material being heated, and A is the cross-sectional area of heat flux. The units of 1/C are then m·K/J, which is consistent with the equations.
