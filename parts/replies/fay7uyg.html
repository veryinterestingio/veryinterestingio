      • Zhyrek

        This and an expanding universe sound like the same problem, but they are actually quite different!

        Consider two cases: The rubber band expanding 1km when its 1km long, and when its 2km long. In the first case, it doubles in length, while in the second, each 1km section only grows by 0.5km, meaning the "expansion" of each length of the rubber band actually slows down over time!

        For the universe, each section of the universe is expanding at a constant rate per unit length. So it would take the same about of time for the universe to grow from 10 billion light years to 20 billion, as it would for two 10b ly segments to do the same (which would go from 20b to 40b). Unlike the linear growth of the elastic band, this "linear growth per length" is actually exponential growth

      • rocketsp13

        It's not that it's accelerating linearly like we see in your example, it's that it's accelerating at an exponential rate. So, lets say that over a given distance, for a certain time, that length doubles. Then each part of that new area doubles over the next period of time, and so forth.

        Eventually you're guaranteed to get portions of the universe that are "moving" away from each other faster than the speed of light can catch up to them. Even if they aren't moving through space relative to each other, what that space does between them is what pushes it over the top.
