      • albule

        Funny that you say this because the Purkinje effect basically says that wavelengths closer to the blue end of the spectrum are easier to focus on at night. Has to do with the density of rods located outside of the fovea compared to the density of cones in this area. Cones are better at distinguishing color in high light whereas rods are better in low light.

        This is why it's easier to "see" the light from a star if you look slightly to the side of it instead of trying to focus on it directly.

    • tminus7700

      One thing that happens with normal monochromatic light sources (even spectral lines) is that the emissions are a Gaussian distribution about the central spectrum point. For instance an 800nm LED. Solidly in the IR, but you will see a dull red glow. There is still some light at the 700nm tail. This can make these devices harmful, because they don't look bright when you see them, but the main peak is very bright. There are similar effects for UV emitters. In addition, UV will make your cornea fluoresce. So you will see a general visible glow when looking at a UV source that is wholly in the UV.
