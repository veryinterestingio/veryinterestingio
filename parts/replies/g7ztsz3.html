  • jmalbo35

    The answer to your question 'can this platform be used' is yes it absolutely can - the technology can be 're-used' with simply new DNA 'cargo' to the new virus target.

    I'm not sure "absolutely can" is appropriate, it's a very legitimate concern that people with immunity to the vector might clear the viral vector too fast for it to work efficiently and thus not respond much to the vaccine antigen. That's the reason that the companies developing these vaccines often choose low seroprevalence adenoviruses (eg. chimpanzee adenoviruses) in the first place - to avoid pre-existing immune responses to the vector.

    It isn't incredibly clear how big of an issue this is, as some studies suggest that immunity can still develop reasonably well after the second use of a vector, whereas others have suggested that pre-existing antibodies against the adenovirus being used as a vector results in lower titers of antibody against the antigen being vaccinated against.

    • DaleYuzuki

      Of course - happy to.

      The Ad5 vector cannot replicate, and is well-suited for both mucosal cell entry (lining of your nasal and bronchial passageways) as well as other cells in the body.

      This virus then has to be produced in large billions quantity per individual, and the method to produce them industrially is known.

      Also in favor of Ad5 is storage, it doesn’t have -20C or even -80C storage (dry ice shipment) (like Moderna’s) requirements.

      The virus delivers a single payload to a single cell - say a fibroblast cell below the skin - and that cell will start pumping out SARS-CoV-2 spike protein and express it on the cell surface.

      Your immune system will recognize it and start generating both a general and a specific immune response - including those valuable CD4+ Th1 T-helper cells.
