    • iayork

      It’s a theoretical issue, but probably not a practical one for humans.

      Each time the immune system encounters an antigen, the T and B cells specific for that antigen first expand (double rapidly), and then contract (die off rapidly), leaving far more than the original number but fewer than the peak number. The final count of memory lymphocytes may be tens of thousands or millions of times higher than the starting count.

      That’s not sustainable, considering that you’re exposed to millions of antigens throughout your life. Eventually, your blood and spleen and lymph nodes would be choked with lymphocytes.

      But the immune system has a mechanism for roughly counting memory lymphocytes, and wants them to not get too numerous. So as new lymphocytes are added to the memory system, old memory lymphocytes are gradually weeded out. It’s not a clean last-in-first-out system but that’s roughly how it ends up working out. If you haven’t been exposed to an antigen for 50 years, you may well still have memory lymphocytes for it, but not nearly as many as you did 45 or 25 years ago.

      This does have implications for the nature of immunity in elderly people, but many other factors also alter elderly immune systems, so it’s not the only thing to worry about.
