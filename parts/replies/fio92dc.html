      • jermleeds

        It doesn't. In Hawaii, the hotspot lies under the Pacific plate, which is scooting by at ~10 cm/year. It's a conveyor belt moving to the northwest, which is why the oldest (and therefore most eroded) islands are Kauai and the smaller ones to the NW; the youngest islands are to the SE. Hawaii is the youngest island to break sea level, and is obviously where the volcanic action is nowadays. There is a younger seamount to the SE, if I recall correctly. The Iceland Hotspot lies under the mid-Atlantic ridge, which is a comparatively stable feature geographically, despite having the North American and Eurasian & African plates flowing away from it. So that weird combination of volcanic chemistries stays more or less there, making a big blob, instead of a chain.
