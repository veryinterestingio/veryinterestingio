    I believe, either manually or automatically, a scram was initiated in response to sudden rising temperatures, and that was what actually triggered the runaway thermal explosion. The RMBK control rods had a section of graphite moderator attached to the end of them, such that when the control rods were removed they were replaced with the graphite displacers to further increase power.

    Initiating the scram was, counter-intuitively, dangerous because the length of the graphite displacers was shorter than the height of the core. So when the control rods were fully removed, in their place was a section of graphite with 1.25m of water above and below it. The water acts as a neutron absorber, which reduces the rate of reaction. By reinserting the control rods, the graphite starts to displace the 1.25m column of water near the bottom of the reactor, and starts to spike power output locally. That spike causes water to boil, and power to increase in a feedback loop as described above.

      • ppitm

        since it is the most definitive source on the accident to date and would further add to your considerable knowledge.

        It is the most definitive source in English. It was written in the early 1990s and is outdated or questionable in several areas. For instance, INSAG-7 overlooked the rather large likelihood that ORM was 15 rods because the DREG computer had a glitch that did not account for the 7 LAR rods. The report also oversimplifies the power conditions in the bottom half of the core at 1:23:40.

        Also read the footnotes, INSAG here is just quoting an analysis done by the reactor's design bureau in 1986, which is a questionable source. Basically any Soviet analysis of the accident from the cover-up period needs to be corroborated by later work.

        What we do know is that the RBMK was labelled a nuclear hazard at less than 700 MW and 30 rods ORM (later increased several times over).
