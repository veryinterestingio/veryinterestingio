      • Babyy_blue

        This is not necessarily true. I just bought a house for cash, and after the inspection we asked for a lower price based on a few things that needed fixing. They were not major. But if the seller hadn’t been willing to come down, we would have been able to walk away and still get our earnest money back.

        From my understanding it’s more about the bank, not the buyer. Banks have certain requirements of a home before they are willing to approve the loan, and the seller may not want to fix those things. Example, in OR, all windows and doors must have molding around the exterior. Usually the bank will require the issue to be fixed before the sale, while a cash buyer may be willing to take money off the price to fix it themselves. So it’s not just the buyer that has to be okay with the inspection, but the bank as well.

        Without the bank, escrow can also be a lot shorter. We closed in about 3 weeks, which is what enticed the seller to go with our cash offer as opposed to a higher offer contingent on a loan.

        Edit: I should have said lender instead of bank, sorry about that!

        And appraisal contingencies are also a factor. This can be hard especially in the current market because homes are scarce and going for much more than their appraised value.
