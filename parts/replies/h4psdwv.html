    • cstheory

      This case is a little different. Infinity scroll pages are dynamically loaded. When the page first loads, it pulls maybe the first 20 items to show you, and then it monitors your scrolling and when you get to some point in the list it will fetch more items to show you, from the server, without reloading the whole page. It will edit the page on the fly and inject those new items at the bottom of the list. It might also remove some from the top of the list to keep the active page light and fast. When you navigate away and then back, the browser doesn’t have any idea how the developer set up their infinite scrolling. It doesn’t keep track of the internal state of the page and restore that when you go back. It just says oh you were at address www.foo.com, and it puts you there in that page’s initial state. On any infinite scrolling list where the back button takes you right back where you were, that’s because the developer of the page saved that internal state somewhere and then restored it when you went back, to make the experience better.

      Edit: I should also mention that if you’re writing this kind of page from scratch instead of using a framework, the restoring state when you go back feature is by far the hardest part of infinite scrolling to do well, and the developer might not have planned for it in the design because they didn’t realize until testing that going back would be a problem, which is why it’s often not implemented or implemented poorly (where you’re slightly off in position or the list is in a different order, for example).

    Fun tip for some people who might not know it (from a fellow Web Dev) if you right click the back arrow in your browser it'll show you the last like 10 pages you've been to.

    If a site is keeping you "stuck" in a bit of a loop you can right click up there and just click to the actual page you're trying to go back to. On mobile you can usually long press on the back button to achieve the same result.

    Not a mind blowing tip or anything, but it can really help if you're prone to just spam clicking the back button to try and get back. Or just giving up and retyping in the search bar haha.

    Edit: Additional note.. mileage may vary in different browsers. I know this exists in Chrome and Firefox. Haven't tested is specifically in all browsers but I'm sure most modern browsers support it by now.
