    • GaPSMAV

      Well, no. I'm hoping you don't get any snarky comments or get downvoted, because this is an excellent moment to learn something.

      Our current definition of "life," or the things that separate something "dead" from something "alive," is that living things have the capacity for growth, reproduction, functional activity, and continual change preceding death (source: Oxford Languages.)

      By that definition, an oak tree is just as alive as any person, even if the tree doesn't have consciousness while the person does.

      When it comes to describing the spectrum of intelligence in life, there are a few terms you need to know:

      1. Consciousness: an entity that is conscious means that it is aware of itself and the world around it.
      2. Sentience: sentience is the ability of a conscious entity to sense their environment or have the capacity to feel emotion. Essentially, it is subjective consciousness.
      3. Sapience: wisdom, or rather, knowledge and the ability to apply that knowledge.

      Now, which living things do or do not exhibit these traits can be debated, but if I'm not misunderstanding the concepts (and someone please do point it out if I've made a mistake,) the loose hierarchy goes like this:

      • Most humans are conscious, sentient, and sapient.
      • Most mammals are conscious, might be sentient, but probably aren't usually sapient.
      • Living things in the Animal Kingdoms of Plants, Fungi, Protista, and Monera probably aren't conscious, sentient, or sapient.
