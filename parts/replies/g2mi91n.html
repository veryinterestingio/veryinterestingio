    • agate_

      it would still make sense to have renewable options that partially work and fallback to fossil only when no renewable alternative is available and that could still help, right?

      Two problems here. First, a fossil fuel plant has a huge capital cost to build it in the first place, and if you run it only part of the time, it's harder to recoup that initial investment. The less you use a power plant, the more each kilowatt-hour costs.

      Second, fossil fuel plants can't turn on instantly: all the cost-competitive ones take hours to warm up before they can produce power. And that's literally warming up, burning fuel but producing no electricity. So turning them on and off in response to changing demand is something you want to avoid if you can: it's often not physically possible, and even when it is it's wasteful and expensive.

      Point being: yes, fossil fuel plants can and will be used as a backup system to deal with the unreliability of renewable energy. But when operating this way, they're much more expensive and inefficient.

    • Phage0070

      I'm guessing we're not actively doing that because it's a high investment that don't fully pay off due to the need to still burn fossil at important times?

      Exactly. They won't pay themselves off for several years at least and many people see it as trying to solve a problem they don't have; to the average person electricity is electricity, there is no difference where it comes from.

      There are also some legal and practical challenges to consider. Suppose the power company, a private business, decides that solar panels will save them some money over the long term and are willing to drop the cash to build out the infrastructure. They inform you, a homeowner, that they want to build a series of solar panels on your roof. You won't own those solar panels of course, and the power company will want to reserve the right to access your property and roof at any time for things such as maintenance on their equipment, as well as prohibiting you from doing work or modification on your roof which could interfere with them. Of course this will involve adding a few poles and suspended line across your property (burying power cables would be far too expensive) which again you cannot mess with and they can access at their discretion. This agreement will last for 10 years and anyone you want to sell the property to will also need to accept it. For this you get... nothing. Are you OK with that?

      For many people probably not. So even if a power company thought it might be a nice idea the chances they can actually pull it off as an overall transition are slim. The main issue is that the major benefit of renewable energy, benefits to the environment, are not actually tangible benefits to the entities making the decisions and paying the bills. If renewable energy was actually less expensive and these companies as greedy as commonly portrayed then nothing would be stopping them from making it happen.
