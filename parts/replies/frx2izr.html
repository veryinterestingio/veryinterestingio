    • Storgan_Manley

      Right. The days of "corporate raiding", where PE firms would buy a company using extreme amounts of debt (say 15x their annual earnings), sell off all the assets individually, and leave the company saddled with that debt were almost exclusively in the 1980s and 1990s. Barbarians at the Gate is about this - it's one of the most read books in finance circles.

      These days, PE funds are much more in the spotlight and are much more regulated (Obama passed a rule that essentially said PE funds could only borrow up to 6x a company's annual earnings - Trump has relaxed this). Additionally, PE fund investors who provide the PE capital to make investments are much more vigilant about good corporate governance practice. Not a PE apologist - there are certainly some weasels in the industry and firms known to be harder on the cost cutting side. But in my experience, overwhelming majority of PE professionals I have come across want to grow the business rather than cut their way to returns.

      • melograno1234

        Another thing worth noting here is that a lot of the shadier stuff like levering up at 15x or stripping down the assets is now impossible - lenders now have covenants that prevent PE shops from doing the more pirate-y stuff, and selling the debt on a very highly leveraged buyout is challenging.

        In my experience as an industry professional, most PE shops aim to put as much debt as possible while still maintaining a B2/B rating and hitting 20% IRR, and will only sacrifice the ratings if they strongly believe in the target and are willing to cope with the more challenging execution of a very leveraged deal.
