      Angina is caused by low blood flow and therefore oxygen to the heart. The coronary vessels feed the heart, if they are narrowed then this will cause pain. Typically the narrowing is caused by a plaque, which is called coronary artery disease.

      Exercise does also release catecholamines, but in different proportion, and the downstream effect is dilation of vessels including the coronary arteries to improve blood flow to your muscles, including your heart. This is why exercise does not typically cause pain in healthy individuals with no history of coronary artery disease.

      When people with severe coronary artery disease exercise their coronary artery that has the plaque cannot expand in that area to meet the new oxygen demand for the heart, and they feel that pain, which we define as angina.

      However, severe stress (including emotional) can cause such severe release of catecholamines and other hormones that make them potent, which can lead to constriction of blood vessels. When you constrict your blood vessels, you create additional strain on your heart, even if your heart is still getting enough blood flow so that no angina is felt. But sometimes, there can be such intense stress (including emotional) that the hormones force even your coronary arteries to spasm and constrict, in these cases patients do feel that same anginal pain. These can be young patients with no prior heart history or risk factors. I’ve seen a case for example of a mid-30s woman who came in one week after the loss of her father.

      Takatsubo cardiomyopathy has many names, and I’d agree with you that it’s most accurate and descriptive one is stress cardiomyopathy. One of the classic echocardiogram findings is apical ballooning, which is a consequence of a heart struggling to beat against a large systemic resistance from vasoconstriction. When the heart can’t move volume out of its chamber, it starts to balloon out, making it worse at contracting in the first place, leading to heart failure. This can all happen as a consequence of emotional trauma.

      I thought it was relevant to the OPs question because if we can damage our heart so much from feeling emotional stress, is it really hard to believe that when this process happens to you following less severe but still significant emotional trauma that our heart would ache?
