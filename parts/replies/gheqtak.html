  • AKADriver

    When actual tissue samples from the time period can't be found, there are two other ways that the viral species/strain involved in a past epidemic can be deduced.

    One is to study the immune responses in the blood of people who were alive at the time - "seroarchaeology". Their immune systems should still retain memory of the virus in the form of trace levels of antibodies that bind to it.

    The other is to study the genomes of contemporary endemic viruses to determine their evolutionary history and when they likely made their most recent zoonotic jump or genetic reshuffling, and line this up with historical events, called a molecular clock analysis.

    The 1889-90 Russian Flu pandemic is of interest, because seroarcheaeology points to an influenza H3 subtype (possibly H3N8) being more common in people who were born in the 1880-1900 time period, while molecular clock analysis of the human coronavirus species OC43 points to a common ancestor with bovine coronavirus around 1890.

    Seroarchaeology of H1N1 (also information about H3N8): https://www.pnas.org/content/pnas/111/22/8107.full.pdf

    Molecular clock analysis of OC43: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15650185/

      • AKADriver

        Some people mentioned cross-immunity with common-cold CoVs could have been the reason why children were almost unscathed by SARS-cov-2, with symptoms worsening with age.

        This, IMO, is unsupported by evidence now, even though it was a popular hypothesis back in the spring. We now have direct counter-evidence for cross-reactivity, eg: https://www.medrxiv.org/content/10.1101/2020.10.08.20209650v1

        A broader naive immune cell repertoire that's better equipped to respond and adapt to a novel pathogen explains the age stratification of SARS-CoV-2 much better, when we can even see that effect just looking at the variation in the population within an age group: https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674(20)31235-6?rss=yes

        This also fits with a virus like OC43 shifting from a pandemic virus to an endemic one without a major attenuating mutation, wherein people with previous exposure have long-term disease-attenuating immune responses but short-lived infection-preventing ones.
