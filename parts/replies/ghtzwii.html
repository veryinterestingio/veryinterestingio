      This answer is a little off in places. First off is that the color of light emitted by an object does not necessarily correlate directly to its temperature. There are two major types of spectra which a radiating body can have, an emission spectrum and a blackbody spectrum.

      Emission spectrums are due to electron transitions between energy levels, each energy level has a very specific energy associated and so the light emitted comes in very narrow bands of wavelength/colour.

      A gas burner is blue because of its emission spectrum, the excited electrons in the gas release blue light. This is not directly related to temperature at all. A small blue bunsen flame can have a cooler temperature than a large orange/white fire.

      Other light sources like candles, wood fires or the sun emit light due to blackbody radiation, here the spectrum emitted is directly related to the surface temperature. This has absolutely nothing to do with fusion other than the fusion in the sun is what provides the energy to keep its outer layers hot. So the sun emitting 'white' light as well as UV isn't because of fusion, its because it has a blackbody spectrum.

      A blue star doesn't emit only blue/uv light, increasing the temperature of the star increases how much light it puts out at all wavelengths, including the lower energy ones. A star which was exactly the same as our sun but hotter would emit more
