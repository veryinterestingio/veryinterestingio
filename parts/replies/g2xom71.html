    • MrNick4

      You don't test whether people have a protein of a certain structure, because everyone's antibodies are different. Rather, you include some parts of the virus on the test, and if something in the patient's blood binds to one or more parts, it has to be antibodies.

      • craigdahlke

        To clarify further, many antibody tests actually use a secondary antibody specific to IgG or IgM, so not only does something bind to the antigen in the test, but the secondary antibody then sticks to that (they often call this a sandwich ELISA for this reason, because the patient antibody is “sandwiched” between the antigen and the secondary antibody), and a fluorescent marker on the secondary ab gives you the positive result. This is how they can differentiate between IgG and IgM, and tell that it’s not just non-specific binding of some other non-antibody protein in the serum.
