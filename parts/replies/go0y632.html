      • PaintsWithSmegma

        Short answer it depends. The effects are compounded by how long and what type of gas you've absorbed as well as how fast you ascend. Worst case? The dissolved nitrogen in your body moves into your blood stream, forms into bubbles and you choke on your own blood while simultaneously having air embolisms in your spinal column, brain and joints. I've never seen it in person but have read and talked to people that have and its incredibly painful.

        Best case is you come up slower and under shoot your deco limit. You'll have the same problem but not as bad, joint pain, difficulty breathing and headaches.

        You can prevent this by spending time under pressure or "at depth" by coming up slowly and letting the compressed gas slowly exit you.

        Think shaking up a bottle of soda then opening it fast vs opening it up just a little at a time.

      • Nate12345678

        Scuba divers who venture beyond ~15m (basically anything other than introductory diving) level are trained to slowly return to the surface over the course on 10ish minutes, or wait 5m below for several minutes in a "decompression stop". Many divemasters will plan the route so that the stop is unnecessary.

  • Jtsfour

    Shallow water blackout is caused by a sudden decrease in the partial pressure of oxygen in your lungs & blood.

    Going deeper allows your body to absorb more oxygen from your lungs and when you ascend it can be very dangerous.

    When freediving on a breath hold you compress the air in your lungs and that increases the partial pressure allowing more oxygen to be used.
