      • Tikhon14

        Well to be clear, "immense gravity" by itself doesn't do much to you. Damage from a stellar mass black hole occurs because of the difference in acceleration due to gravity across your body.

        The tidal force is proportional to the length of your body over the distance from the black hole. (and of course the strength of gravity itself).

        You experience tidal forces, therefore, just standing up on earth. Your feet are closer to earth than your head, so your feet feel slightly more gravity than your head. Your feet are experiencing an acceleration due to gravity of about 9.8m/s^2, and your head is about 0.0000006 m/s^2 less.

        However, the cohesiveness of your body far exceeds this minuscule tidal force, so you are fine.

        In contrast, the distance between your feet and head is significant when entering a stellar mass black hole, and your feet would feel ~50,000 m/s^2 more acceleration due to gravity than your head. That exceeds the cohesiveness of your body, so you'd be pulled apart. And remember, because tidal forces are proportional to the length of your body, as you are pulled apart the effect amplifies rapidly. Within less than a second you'd be shredded, and as parts of you approached the singularity, even molecules could be torn apart.

        A supermassive black hole, however, has a much more gentle gradient at the event horizon. It's a few times more than the tidal forces on earth, but nothing you'd notice.

        Going even further, for an ultramassive black hole, you'd experience the same tidal forces as on earth. You'd have billions of kilometers to go after entering the event horizon before you'd experience significant tidal forces (assuming everything works like that within the black hole).
