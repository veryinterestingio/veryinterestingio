    It is actually even harder than that.

    Public transit cards like this are RFID cards - the reader powers a small chip when the card is "read". The chip powers on and, using one half of a public/private encryption key pair, sends the reader an encrypted message that can be deciphered by the other half of the key pair. If the keys match, the reader can decode the message and get a validation; if not, then the decoded message is gibberish and not validated.

    So, not only would you have to guess the card number, but you would also have to guess the encrypted data that card should produce - which is effectively impossible.

      Much harder. Even 128-bit encryption (which is still unbreakable but towards the lower end of strong encryption these days) has 3.402 x 10^38 possible keys. In comparison, the odds of winning the Mega Millions lottery is around 1 in 302.6 million, or 3.026 x 10^8.

      Assuming you, on average, guess the right key after going through about half of the possible keys, it still takes you going through 3.402 x 10^19 keys, still significantly more possible guesses than the odds of winning the lottery.
