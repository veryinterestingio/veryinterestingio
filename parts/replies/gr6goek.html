      • headsiwin-tailsulose

        You're both right and wrong. That study explores the effect of metabolic stress on hypertrophy, but nowhere does it say it is the only cause or most likely cause (spoiler alert: because it's not).

        Metabolic stress results in hypertrophy, but progressive overload (mechanical tension and volume) and eccentric overload (what OP asked about) both cause hypertrophy as well. So while lactic acid production does result in hypertrophy and growth, it is definitely not the only or main cause - progressive overload is.

    • CptHrki

      Real myofibrillar hypertrophy is probably some combination of CNS stimulation and metabolic/mechanical stress triggering certain pathways that end up signalling for protein synthesis. Exactly how this takes place is actually unknown. Needless to say it's a very complex topic, for example initial strength gain (first 8 weeks or so) when starting training is mostly nervous system adaptation to activate more muscle fibers rather than hypertrophy. There's also sarcoplasmic hypertrophy which is simple increase of fluid volume in muscle cells (which is why strength isn't strictly correlated with size).

      I'm really unqualified to go more in depth though, what I said is basically colloquial knowledge from reading and some personal experience.
