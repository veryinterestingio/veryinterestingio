    Q:

    If some animals can emit light, can they emit other forms of electromagnetic radiation in useful ways (obviously, excluding infrared as escaped heat)?

    dmmaus A:

    So there's a lot going on here. I'm a physicist, not a biologist, so I'm going to talk mostly about the physics involved.

    Visible light is very useful as a sensory medium because the spectrum of the sun peaks in the visible region. So the world is nice and bright in those wavelengths, at least during the day. This is why animal eyes have generally evolved to make use of those wavelengths. I know a few animals such as birds and some insects extend that range a little into the UV beyond what humans can perceive, but they don't go very far, because there's not much point having a sense organ sensitive to wavelengths that aren't commonly found in on Earth. Similarly with anything beyond short infrared. There are some species such as pit vipers who have infrared sensory organs to detect body heat of prey, but nothing detects microwaves or radio.

    There's a physical problem on the long wavelength end. To detect a given wavelength, your detector needs to be at least roughly the same size. Even if there were natural radio waves bright enough to be useful, no animal would be able to see them without a detector the size of an aerial, or a satellite dish.

    There's another physical problem too, in that microwaves, radio waves, x-rays, and gamma rays all pass through our bodies. If your body can't stop a photon passing through it, it basically can't detect it.

    Putting all this together explains why living beings can sense visible light and almost nothing else. We can feel the warmth of infrared, but because of the longer wavelength it doesn't have the fine resolution of visible light. So those pit vipers can detect roughly where a warm prey animal is, but they can't resolve it with any detail.

    If animals can't sense any given wavelength, then there's not much point being able to emit it, even if it were physically possible. A lot of wavelengths simply can't be generated by an organic being - radio for example takes a large transmitter, and is most efficiently built of metal. X-rays require too much energy input, and are dangerous besides.

    I'm fairly certain no organisms emit anything other than visible light, or possibly near UV/IR (as well as the thermal IR you mentioned). And I hope what I said explains why.

    HerraTohtori A:

    I'm going to focus on radiofrequencies, because infra-red and ultraviolet frequncies of the EM spectrum are obviously used by many animals (although I don't think any animal actively produces ultraviolet light, they just reflect it).

    In short:

    All animals produce bioelectric fields, and as a result, some electromagnetic radiation is also generated as radio frequencies in the gigahertz range. Some animals are further adapted into passively sensing these electric fields with specialized electroreception organs, and others still are further specialized into actively generating their own electric fields/discharges and sensing their surroundings based on how their own electric field is affected.

    So a lot of animals can sense and some can produce electric fields.

    But I don't think any species is evolved to specifically sense or produce electromagnetic waves at radio frequencies for useful purposes.

    Long answer:

    There are definitely animals that can emit electromagnetic radiation in the radio wavelengths, but I don't think any of them actually make use of that radiation - it's more of a byproduct of other things they do.

    For example, there are several aquatic animals which can produce high voltage electric discharges, which they can use to incapacitate nearby prey animals, or to deter predators.

    Electric current is movement of charges, and when you accelerate charged particles you produce electromagnetic waves. However, these animals don't generally have an antenna to select a certain wavelength to emit. Instead, an electric discharge typically produces a broad spectrum electromagnetic pulse. If you had an radio nearby, tuned to a "white noise" frequency on the AM band, you might be able to hear a series of faint "pops" for the pulse generated by each discharge, depending on how close you are to the source (the animal). In this video the electric discharges of an electric eel are demonstrated with a speaker hooked up the voltage probes on the aquarium, converting the electric discharges into sound in a more direct way, but theoretically this is also what you should be able to hear on the radio receiver.

    There are also animals that can sense electric fields, which is called electroreception. These, too, tend to be aquatic animals, and a lot of them are also animals that can produce the aforementioned electric discharges. In fact, part of the function of the electric discharges is sensing conductive and non-conductive objects nearby, because they interact differently with the electric field produced by the animal. This is called active electroreception, and it's used by animals such as electric eels, electric rays, and some sharks (like elephant fish).

    There is also passive electroreception, in which the animal senses weak bioelectricity generated by other organisms nearby. Most sharks can use passive electroreception.

    Now, again in theory if you have two electric eels producing their electric discharges, they would definitely be able to feel each other through their electroreception sense. And, in theory, if you can sense electric fields then you can also sense electromagnetic radiation, at least if it's powerful enough (and at suitable wavelength) to excite the sensory organs that can sense electric fields.

    However, I don't think the electromagnetic pulses produced by these animals are actually strong enough to travel very far through the water. This is because water tends to have all kinds of ions in solution, which makes it conductive, and conductive materials typically become opaque to EM-waves (though they can be transparent on some wavelengths). Case in point, radio waves that can easily go through air do not penetrate very far in water. Because of this, I think it's just the electric field that these animals sense, rather than the (very weak) electromagnetic pulses that would be produced.

    Then there are animals that can sense magnetic fields. Again, in theory, if an animal has a sensory organ that can sense magnetic fields, it could in theory be able to sense very long radio waves as a form of slowly oscillating magnetic field. However the required field strength to pick up these radio waves with a magnetoreception organ would probably require extremely powerful transmitter. It doesn't seem like any animal has a mechanism to actually produce radio waves of such intensity.

    Producing electromagnetic radiation at wavelengths shorter than ultraviolet would be problematic because at some point, EM-radiation becomes ionizing so the organism would be actively giving itself cancer. This might actually be a valid strategy at increasing random mutations in a species with extremely fast and effective reproductive cycle, but I don't know of any examples where such a thing would have occurred. And I don't know of any biological mechanism to produce short wavelength UV-radiation, never mind X-rays or gamma rays... except by absorbing radionuclides, but then you would just passively emit radiation from their decay.
