    Q:

    ELI5: I keep seeing people post amazing astronomy photos that took 18+ hours of exposure. How does that work exactly with daylight and the celestial bodies shifting ever so slightly from night to night?

    raptor567 A:

    Most astrophotography is done using cameras/telescopes with motorized mounts/tripods. Using computer software you can 'lock-on' to specific celestial objects, and the mount will track the object as it moves across the sky.

    Long exposures are done by capturing light over multiple nights and then using imaging software to 'stack' the images, combining their visual information.

    Source: I dabble in astrophotography

    HipstCapitalist A:

    First, you need to use special hardware (EQ mount + tracker) to make sure your telescope moves at exactly the same speed as the Earth rotates, so you always have the same stars in focus.

    Then, you take dozens of pictures that range from a few seconds to several minutes of exposure. The better you are aligned, the longer your exposure can be.

    That process can be repeated several nights in a row, which is how you can get total exposures longer than 18 hours at a time.

    ​

    The light you get is extremely faint, but by taking so many pictures, you can feed all that information into specific software to "stack" them and reveal all these details.

    To give an analogy, imagine you had done all that process using traditional film instead of a digital camera. You have a full roll of 24 faint pictures of your subject. You could expose each negative one at a time onto a single sheet of paper to reveal all the hidden details.
