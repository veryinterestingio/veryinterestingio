    kylco A:

    All three are chemicals associated with "rewards" or emotional states in the brain. I'm not a neurologist or even a medical professional, but I've looked in to this for my own awareness; if you have specific concerns I'd go to a better source than Reddit. That said, here is my understanding if each of these three (naturally occurring) chemicals and their effects on your brain:

    Serotonin: this neurotransmitter is actually mostly found in your gut! It affects your "satiation" - your sense of being full, satisfied, or content. Drugs that target this are SSRIs or SRAs, generally known as "antidepressants" or appetite suppressants which work by making your brain hold on to the serotonin longer or by dumping more into your system. It also has a lot of other uses and effects in your system, which is part of why these kinda of medicines can have lots of unusual side effects on things like sleep, sexual function, jaw clenching, etc.

    Endorphins: these are actually hormones your body secretes! They are natural opioids, which means they feel like and behave similarly to addictive narcotics like heroin, morphine, and the like. They make you feel really good, loose, and relaxed - euphoria. The key to understanding this hormone is that it makes pain go away - your body releases it when you are injured. However, exercise and extreme activity reads as muscle pain to the body, which releases the endorphins that make up a "endorphin rush" or "second wind" when conducting intense physical exercise or stretching after a strenuous activity. It also comes out to play after sex, while listening to music, enjoying really good food, or other kinds of euphoric activities.

    Dopamine: Dopamine is the messenger chemical that does a lot of different things depending on how and where it's used. Think of it as emails that tissues send each other - generally, "good job" or "reward" messages for a particular activity or behavior. Since no individual part of our bodies is actually terribly smart, we just know what we're doing when dopamine arrives, and associate that with "good job!" This is usually quite good for forming habits. Not all habits are good, however - this mechanism releases rewards for solving puzzles, but also for consuming cocaine or hurting yourself to get an endorphin push, for example. It's like scratching that perfect itch or perfectly accomplishing a difficult task.

    So, in short: serotonin is about feeling satisfied, full, or content. Endorphins make pain go away, and therefore make you feel "euphoric." Dopamine makes you feel rewarded, and can be used to regulate endorphins or otherwise form behaviors by triggering a "yes, do that!" reflex.

    MrFunsocks1 A:

    They are not "happy chemicals". It's a big misunderstanding people have of neurotransmitters and signals - they do not do one thing. For example, just the wikipedia intro description of serotonin: "modulating mood, cognition, reward, learning, memory, and numerous physiological processes such as vomiting and vasoconstriction. "

    The difference between them is they do different things. There are different neurons in the body that use them in different ways. The specifics... well, I could research and write a few pages on the effects and neural pathways of each one, but it wouldn't be that interesting, and anything shorter wouldn't tell anything like the real story. More of any one doesn't necessarily make you happier, just triggering the activation of certain pathways that might happen to use them does.

    One of the interesting ones to me is dopamine, specifically in regards to Parkinson's. One of the main sets of dopaminergic neurons are in the substantia nigra (SN for short), a tiny bundle of neurons in the back of the brain that sends a kind of "pacemaker" pulse to the parts of your brain that regulate movement. Basically, these neurons are constantly giving a little "boop" signal to the neurons that will tell your arm to move (or any other movement). Then, when another part of your brain says "arm, move now!", the neurons are already close to threshold and your arm moves. When you're asleep, these SN neurons stop firing, so when your dreaming brain tells your arm to move, it doesn't pass the threshold to move your arm.

    In Parkinson's, the SN neurons die (for reasons, not important now). So it becomes harder to will yourself to move, hence the paralysis and shaking. Of course, the treatment, L-dopa, is basically just taking a form of dopamine that goes everywhere in your brain, basically triggering these movement neurons a bit in the absence of the SN neurons. This can lead to a lot of swaying and uncontrolled movement, as suddenly the threshold for movement is really low, but it can also lead to schizophrenia. Why? Another, completely separate part of your brain, responsible for hearing and seeing, ALSO has a similar system, where it tells your brain you heard or saw something, but it has to pass a threshold, also run by dopaminergic neurons. So now you see and hear things that aren't necessarily there.

    Oh, and taking L-DOPA also leads to side effects like excess libido (since dopamine is also involved in sexual satisfaction pathways), as well as things like hypotension, anxiety, nausea, arrythmias, etc. etc. etc.

    Because we only have 7 main neurotransmitters, and they run our brain, spinal cord, and peripheral nerves. If 3 of them did "happy things" and nothing else, you'd run out of neurotransmitters well before you got to the neurotransmitter that let your arm move. Or made your heart beat. Or helped you learn, hear, understand writing, and so forth.
