    Q:

    If a transplanted organ develops cancer, can that cancer spread to the rest of the body even though it's DNA is different from the host DNA?

    dragons_scorn A:

    It's not only possible, it has happened. It's very rare, especially since organ transplant screenings tend to be rigorous. But some things slip through the cracks.

    It's not really the DNA that's the issue, it's what's on your cells. Everyone has these proteins on their cells that are unique to them, it helps your immune system identify what is or isn't your body. Yes, your DNA codes for these proteins so it's indirectly involved but that's the only involvement. When you get a transplant you are given immunosuppressive drugs that you take to ensure your immune system doesn't kill the new organ. This means that if cancer does arise in the organ it will be a lot harder for the body to fight it

    Not only yes, but that's not actually how cancer works. Cancer starts from a single cell that goes bad. That single cell multiples into more cells, that mass of cells is 'the cancer'. So when cancer spreads, it's that original cell's descendants spreading, with no care to the healthy other body cells.
