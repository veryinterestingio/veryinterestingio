    Q:

    ELI5: Humans suffer from muscle atrophy when they don't use muscles for extended periods of time. How come bears can hibernate for months and be just fine?

    VoilaVoilaWashington A:

    The method isn't clear, as others have said, but there's a key difference - humans are weird, in that our muscle needs can change very quickly. There's a benefit to being able to build up muscle mass in one are as needed, then lose it when it's not needed. So if an early human had to spend a month travelling, by the end of it, they'd have strong legs. Then they need to build a shelter, and if the leg muscles remained, they'd take a lot of calories to maintain. So upper body muscles would build, lower body would fade.

    It lets humans be efficient.

    Bears, on the other hand, gonna bear. Their muscle needs are relatively consistent, so there's no good reason to have the muscles atrophy during hibernation - those bears that remain strong through hibernation would have a better chance at surviving than those who do not.

    thumbnail_looks_like A:

    Human beings have evolved over time to fluctuate their muscle mass based on how much physical activity they engage in. This is beneficial because it lets us adapt to our environment while also avoiding wasting resources maintaining muscles that serves no purpose. In our case, muscle atrophy is actually an advantageous trait.

    Bears have evolved to retain their muscle mass regardless of hibernation, because it's more biologically expensive to create muscle than to maintain it. After every hibernation cycle, the bear will need its full strength relatively quickly and can't afford the time and cost to rebuild muscle. In the bear's case, muscle atrophy would be a disadvantageous trait.
