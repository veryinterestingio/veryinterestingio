    Q:

    If an antiparticle is the same mass as its associated particle, how can something like a neutron have an antiparticle? How would it be different?

    I started reading about particle physics earlier today for the first time and it really is like I'm five.

    Caucasiafro A:

    So proton and neutrons are composed of even smaller particles called quarks that have a charge.

    A proton is composed of two "Up" quarks and one "down" quark. An up quark has a charge of 2/3 and a down quark a charge of -1/3.

    2/3+2/3-1/3 =1 so a proton has a charge of 1

    ​

    A neutron is composed of two down quarks and one up quark: 2/3-1/3-1/3 = 0 no charge.

    ​

    Antiparticles are actually composed of anti-quarks. The quarks themselves are what have the opposite charges.

    ​

    So an antineutron is composed of two anti-down quarks and one anti-up. anti-down quarks have charge of +1/3 and anti-up quarks have a charge of -2/3 So you still end up with zero net charges.

    ​

    harlottesometimes A:

    A neutron is made of three small parts that behave together. Each of the three small parts that make up a neutron have opposites. An anti-neutron is what happens when the three opposite small parts get together, behave, and form a particle.

    Fizil A:

    It isn't really about being composite. It is about other "quantum numbers" besides electric charge that are opposite between matter/anti-matter pairs. The best example of this is neutrinos, which have anti-particle partners. In fact a lot of neutrinos produced in experiments, with beta decay for instance, are electron anti-neutrinos.

    What is the difference between a electrically neutral neutrino, and it's also electrically neutral anti-neutrino partner? Well the primary difference is simply lepton number (neutrinos and electrons are in a class of particles called leptons). It may seem silly at first glance, but in general the number of leptons and anti-leptons in a system of interacting particles is constant. So the electron lepton number of the electron and electron-neutrino is defined as +1, while positrons and anti-electron-neutrinos have lepton numbers of -1 (it is a little more complicated than this if you consider the other generations of leptons, but ignore that for now).

    So let's say a neutron is undergoing beta decay into a proton and electron. It starts out with a lepton number of 0: there are no leptons. It would end up with a lepton number of +1: there is now an electron. That is not allowed, the lepton number of the system has to remain 0. So the neutron doesn't decay into a proton and electron, it decays into a proton, electron, and anti-electron-neutrino. Now the interaction maintains it's original lepton number of 0, +1 from the electron, and -1 from the anti-neutrino.

    Neutrinos and anti-neutrinos also have opposite "weak iso-spin", a quantum number that determines how they interact with the weak force, like how electric charge determines how a particle interacts with the electromagnetic force.
