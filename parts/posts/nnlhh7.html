• Technology How are electric cars better for the environment? They're still charged by powerplants that mostly burn fossil fuels. Wouldn't an increase in electric cars result in an increase in fuel consumption by the power plants?

    NEW
    Q:

    I mean yes, your car itself would be better for the environment, but wouldn't that be offset by the increase in pollution by the power plants that have to charge your car now? Solar and wind technology isn't enough to power everyone's electric vehicles if the whole world switches.

    phiwong A:

    Offset yes, but not totally. The first thing you have to understand is that one unit of fuel used in a car engine does not generate the same power as one unit of fuel used in a power plant. A power plant generating electricity plus distribution losses is still way more efficient at producing power than a small car engine. The economies of large scale energy production lends itself to efficiencies that just cannot be reproduced reasonably in small car engines.

    It is this difference that matters. And that difference continues to increase as more renewable energy sources are implemented in the power grid.

    Riggald A:

    1) The electricity grid is sized for peak use. You can charge the electric vehicles without needing more power plants, just by running the existing plants more.

    2) Electricity needs to be used the moment it is generated. Wind farms get turned off if it is windy, but no one is using electricity.

    If you attach a lot of electric vehicles to the grid, with their big batteries, you can keep generating electricity from wind all the time, because there is somewhere to put it.

    3) It is more efficient to burn fossil fuels in a central, highly-efficient plant than it is to transport it to every place there is a car, then carry it about with you as you drive, then burn it in your car's less efficient engine.

    On a short trip, when the engine is cold, that inefficiency is even worse.

    4) Typically, central fossil-fuel generating plants use less-carbon intensive fossil fuels, burning 4 hydrogen per carbon. Cars burn 2.25 Hydrogen or 2.125 Hydrogen for every carbon - so the car is even worse in terms of greenhouse gas use.

    5) After a fossil fuel car is built, it gradually gets less efficient as the engine wears out and gets gets clogged up with dirt & soot. This also makes it less efficient in terms of greenhouse gasses.

    After an electric car is built, it gradually becomes more greenhouse gas efficient over time, as more and more renewable energy is added to the grid.

    (It is about 2 to 3 times cheaper, MWh for MWh, to add offshore wind to the grid than to add nuclear. Solar is even cheaper than offshore wind. On-shore wind is even cheaper than solar. And the price of renewables is continuing to drop, so renewables will likely continue to take the largest share of new & replacement capacity, as time goes by)

    6) Electricity gets used by fossil-fuel extraction, transport, & refinement & transport.

    Electric cars are often use energy more efficiently than the equivalent fossil-fuel car. Some electric cars can travel as far as a fossil-fuelled car with a full tank, on just the electricity that was used to get the refined fuel into the tank. (Comparing an efficient EV to a typical US pick-up truck)

    So you could even just about swap the fleet to electric cars without necessarily increasing the electricity you need to generate.
