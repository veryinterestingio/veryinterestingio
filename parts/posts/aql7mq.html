• Biology How do single celled creatures heal their wounds if at all?

    NEW
    Q:

    Multicellular creatures pretty much just replace the dead/damaged cells with new cells but what do the individual cells do to repair damage to their individual cell walls?

    Is it active or passive meaning do the molecules just link back together because of their physical qualities (polarity between water molecules and such) or do cells/Eukaryotes/prokaryotes/Archaebacteria have ways of actively repairing themselves?

    giantmessofbranes A:

    There are two major aspects to unicellular repair: wound healing and regeneration. Wound healing are the mechanisms for putting a stop to a wound itself, the thing that is causing cellular destabilization. For example, a cell stabbed with a needle, or bitten and partly swallowed by a predator. Regeneration is differentiated from wound healing in that it is building back up to equilibrium before the wound ever occurred.

    All of it involves complex chemical signalling pathways. There are ways to detect wounds in a single cell (change in the intracellular environment, such as the intracellular environment is filling up with molecules from the intercellular environment). If wounds are detected, genes and protein translation can be activated for wound healing. Pathways can also detect the loss of equilibrium in the intracellular environment, and through the use of these repair mechanisms, rebuild what was lost.

    As with all things it is a matter of thresholds. There's a point even for model organisms with good repair mechanisms where enough damage is just death. At the same time, a certain amount of leakage between the intra- and inter-cellular environment is normal and necessary. Wounds lie in the middle, and across species, there is likely variance in what each species "can handle."

    Some organisms can even split into two separate organisms from damage, almost like an externally activated cell division. This is possible if the given organism has enough nucleoplasm in both pieces, and the damage is moderate enough that both pieces can heal and regenerate into distinct organisms.

    Through layers of abstraction, you can see it's not all that different from how an earthworm, person, or (crewed) sailboat would heal wounds/damage. Those different examples have different tolerances, but it comes down to wound detection and acting on those detections with repair measures. With a single cell, it's just happening at the cellular and molecular scale.

    This Science Review article is both readable and goes into very nice detail.

    Depends on the oragnism and how big the injury is, but the luxury of being so simple is that wound healing depends almost completely on closing of the cell membrane before too much damage is done. The phospolipid bilayer is mostly self repairing so no work on the cells part there.

    The issue comes in when worrying about how much of the inside changed. Is the pH managable now or did vital proteins already get denatured? Did the DNA/Nucleus get damaged? Is the osmolarity too far out of balance to recover? And does it still have the capability to produce energy? If yes, than it will go ahead and continue homeostasis and re-regulate all functions. Ion and H+ pumps will correct hydrogen/ion levels to normal parameters, synthesis of carbohydrates to rebuild the cell wall will start, and etc.

    It all depends how bad the injury was

    rileewyliecoyote A:

    Interestingly, there's basically a "spectrum of death" for microbes. Depending on how intact their plasma membrane is and whether or not they can sporulate and remain dormant, microbes can't easily be declared "dead". Some are even non culturable in lab settings so our perception of dead and alive is really hard to define and apply in those situations.
