• Physics Why does looking through a tiny hole make things focus?

    NEW
    Q:

    When I forget my reading glasses and need to read small print I can curl up my forefinger (like making a fist, but only my forefinger) tight enough to leave only a tiny pinhole in the center of my curled finger. If I look through that tiny hole by putting the finger very close to my eye this makes the print come into focus.

    Why? How does this work?

    Just_for_this_moment A:

    Focusing just means taking light rays that have slightly different directions and making them all meet at the same point.

    When you look through a tiny hole, only the light rays that were already going to meet at the same point get through. The light is already focused.

    S_and_M_of_STEM A:

    Blurry images are caused by different portions of the lens focusing at different distances. The tiny opening only lets a small part of the lens function, so the spread in focus distances is very small. This allows your eye to generate a clear, though not as bright, image on your retina.

    lawpoop A:

    Imagine uncooked spaghetti sticks shooting out from a point source in all directions, circularly. That's light rays emanating from a light source.

    Now imagine you have a tall, slender jar that you catch some of those hard spaghetti sticks in. The mouth of that jar acts as a pinhole lens. The only light rays (spaghetti sticks) you're going to capture in the jar are the ones pointed in the same direction.

    The image you see in the tiny hole is from light rays that are all more or less aligned in the same direction.

    diabolical_diarrhea A:

    Your eye has a lens. When light enters it, the light at the edge of the lens is bent more than the light in the center. This is to create a focal point. Because your eye is an imperfect lens, it cannot create a perfect focus. This is pretty much true for all lenses, so don't feel bad. When you look through a small aperture, you cut out the light rays that need to be bent more, leaving only the more focused light raysb in the middle. Hence a darker, more focused image.
