    Q:

    If weight is the measured effect of gravity on an object, and gravity is stronger the closer to the object’s center of mass the measuring object is, do objects high in the atmosphere weigh less than those on the ground, from a physics standpoint?

    Rannasha A:

    Yes, but the difference isn't very big. The radius of the Earth is about 6400 km (depending on where on Earth you measure). So if you're 10 km high in the atmosphere (the cruising altitude for long haul commercial aircraft), you're only 0.15% further away from the center of mass.

    Since gravity is proportional to the square of the distance, that means there's a difference of about 0.31%. On a fairly average adult male of 80 kg, that's about a quater of a kg difference. This difference is certainly measurable, but it's also well within daily fluctuations of ones weight (a drink, a meal or a bathroom break could cause differences of similar magnitude).

    chubbygeodesic A:

    This is essentially correct, but there is one minor detail in your question that bears analyzing: for an extended spherical body like a planet, the maximal gravitational force you can feel would be at the surface. The gravitational field strength actually becomes lower if you go closer to the earth's center of mass than the surface (i.e. tunnelling down). You can think of it like this: there's some mass "above" you that pulls you "up" a little and thus reduces your weight. This effect can also be explained by a principle known as Gauss's Law.

    In fact, if you somehow found yourself at the very centre of the earth and somehow prevented yourself from being crushed/ionized due to the enormous pressure and temperature, you'd be weightless (but in perhaps the least cool way imaginable).

    Henry_Darcy A:

    Yes, weight will change ever so slightly. In fact, a rough approximation from the first derivative of Newtons law of gravity with respect to r gives a value of about -.308 milligals per meter elevation increase. A gal is a cm per second squared. We use a device in geology/geophysics to detect small changes in gravity called a gravimeter. Some are designed almost exactly like spring scales except there are a few differences including a quartz spring and vacuum chamber. Elevation changes and terrain changes are typically removed from the gravitational anomalies so that changes in density of the subsurface rocks can be detected. This helps geologists find ore deposits, caverns, changes in crust thickness, etc.
