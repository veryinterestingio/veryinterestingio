    Sounds like a crap science teacher. How crap of a teacher he is depends on what year your class is and what foundational knowledge you are supposed to have at this point.

    If you want to screw with him, ask what the speed is. Engine size also matters, as does the configuration of the truck bed. Is it segmented? Can the nails move around? Is the cotton compressed?     A thought I'm playing with is that the two trucks would be different in that the load is going to shift to the back of the truck as it starts driving, and that more dense load (nails) is going to shift more mass because it has more room to shift into.

    Though I'm not 100% sure whether shifting load actually affects the momentum of the truck. I do know that load shifting to the front of the truck extends your breaking time, so the inverse would suggest that load shifting to the back of the truck would lower your acceleration.

    If I'm right about this then that would imply that the truck full of nails is going to accelerate slower and thus take longer to reach the finish line. And after the finish line it'll hold on to its momentum longer (so take longer to break).

    Robathor777 A:

    If the trucks start at the same time, from the same place, and travel with the same velocity (speed and direction) then they both arrive at the end point at the same time.

    The only difference in the trucks is their cargo. This data is irrelevant, assuming the trucks have the same velocity. It's not a question of inertia, drag, air resistance etc. Even if we assume those things are different, your teacher stated they are moving at the same speed. (Even if the cotton has more drag, lets say, it doesn't matter. If the speed is the same, then it is the same. Force, inertia, mass, density are irrelevant.)

    Simple Newtonian physics.

    ​

    Your teacher is either leaving something out, or you are. Or, your teacher is just plain wrong. It happens!
