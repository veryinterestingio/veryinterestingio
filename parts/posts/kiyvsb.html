    NickWarrenPhD A:

    According to your article and the US CDC, mucormycosis is most common in patients who have weakened immune systems, like those taking corticosteroids for severe COVID symptoms. The fungi that cause mucormycosis are fairly common, but an intact immune system normally keeps it at bay.

    And yes, mucormycosis is potentially life threatening.

    sciolycaptain A:

    The species of mold that cause mucormycosis are ubiquitous in the environment. We are exposed to them all the time.

    It's only pathologic in patients with compromised immune systems, classically that would be severe diabetes with history of DKA, solid organ transplant, bone marrow transplant, and other hematologic cancers. Oh and it's been associated with iron chelation therapy. Typically infection starts in the sinuses or lungs, and spread to adjacent areas. It can also cause skin infections, this is thought to be related to contaminated dressings.

    The causative mold is angioinvasive. It likes blood vessels, and spreads inside blocking blood flow, so it can cause a lot of necrosis, and spreads quickly.

    It's a terrible infection. Treatment is antifungal drugs and requires aggressive, repeated, frequent, and early surgical debridement. You need to cut away everything you can until you get to healthy appearing bleeding tissue. The earlier you start the better the chances of survival. That also depends on what form you have,best we can do is probably 50% survivi, and usually you end up with a lot of disfigurement due to the surgical debridements.

    This isn't that common an infection though. maybe 1000 reported cases over 50 years? I've only taken care of 5 or 6 patients with confirmed diagnosis over the course of 10 years.

    The association with getting mold infections after COVID is something that we have noticed in the at risk population. Though we see far more aspergillus than mucor.

    adviceneeder1 A:

    The condition is more than "a bit dangerous," as it kills more than half of people who have it. I've seen 3 patients with it in the last couple years. Ophthalmology and ENT are usually at the forefront of care as the fungus causes rapid necrosis usually involving in the sinuses, nasal cavity, and orbit before affecting the brain. Patients were, and usually are extremely poorly controlled diabetics here in the US. These post COVID patients are probably diabetics and additionally on high dose steroids making them further immunocompromised.
