• eli5: Does saying “off the record” before spilling the beans to a reporter, actually give you any recourse if they use your statement in their article?

    NEW
    Q:

    I can’t count how many times I’ve heard “off the record” in a bad TV drama, just curious to know where it came from and if it holds any weight. Don’t worry, your claims will be considered “off the record”.

    bozosonthebus A:

    Long time radio journalist here (yes: we still exist) who rarely if ever agrees to go off the record. It makes you part of their conspiracy to withhold information from the public and you can end up dealing with gossip instead of facts with no accountability or attribution.

    From a practical standpoint, going off the record doesn’t really shield the source. The people involved can usually figure out who is leaking.

    One more thing. A source cannot simply announce something is off the record and then spill the beans. The reporter has to agree to go off the record. Can’t tell you how many times I’ve had to explain that to politicians. Even had one try it in the middle of a one-on-one interview while I was holding the microphone up to his mouth. When he announced after the fact that his last answer was “off the record,” I assured him it was not and when the story aired, he accused me of recording him out of context.

    Chel_of_the_sea A:

    Not directly. But powerful people often want to guide media people towards particular pieces of information that they don't want to be quoted on and want to maintain good relationships with the press, and the press benefits by getting information they couldn't get in a quote. So neither party wants to break that agreement, because it's beneficial to both.

    mostlygray A:

    My mom and dad were reporters for a local paper. They both tried to do decent stories for a small town. It was mostly silly stories but sometimes it was big ones locally. Government corruption, pollution issues, economic development scams, things like that. Even small towns can have big stories for the region.

    Off the record is not off the record. It's "A little bird told me" kind of stuff where you conceal the source and the details so that they can't be identified. Then you get corroborative information so that the original snitch can't be found from your story.

    You code your notes so that only you know whom you spoke to.

    To sum up: It's not off the record, but the reporter will conceal your identity and will not identify you unless you intend to commit a crime or are a really bad person. You have to be really, really bad for a reporter to say who you are. You always protect your sources. No one likes a snitch.
