• Why do numbers 11 through 19 have a different syntax to every other number?

    NEW
    Q:

    As they don't follow the same formatting as twenty-(1-9), or one-hundred [and] (1-9) as examples in speech. Shouldn't they be named Tenthy-five, as an example. It's also partially true in other languages, such as French, why do we do this?

    BillWoods6 A:

    Eleven and twelve are from old, old words probably meaning something like 'one/two left [after you take away ten]'.

    Thirteen -- nineteen are basically 'three-ten' -- 'nine-ten'. You can think of them as being said with a strange accent.

    Skusci A:

    Basically the more commonly a number is used the more likely it is that they get their own special name. And until fairly recently people just generally didn't need to be able to count super high. Or if you did need to you would just use multiples of smaller units. Like say 5 dozen instead of sixty, and tack on whatever was left over as an extra number. And language was in use for a long time before people started caring about large numbers.
