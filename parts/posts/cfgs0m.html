    PepurrPotts A:

    Hey, I'm grieving too. I'm not a scientist, but I can share a little bit of what I know as a mental health professional.

    Grief can mess with our mood-related hormones. It can reduce serotonin levels (which help with energy), and increase our cortisol levels (which can zap energy with they're consistently high).

    Additionally, the brain sort of "defragments" itself while we sleep, like a computer. It processes, prioritizes, and consolidates information- including our emotions. Some studies suggest that people with high anxiety need more sleep because of this. (I am NOT stating that as a fact, just as a suggestion.) But if it's true, then it makes more sense that grieving would require additional time to "defrag" (i.e. sleep) as well.

    Other studies show that we literally phsically hurt when we're in emotional pain. That's taxing on the body, even if it's not a truly physical injury, and addressing that takes additional energy.

    I hope you're taking care of yourself, and I hope someone else can give you a more scientific response. HUG.
