    Some vaccines appear to have non-specific (or heterologous) effects. Specifically, the two live-attenuated BCG and measles vaccines seem to grant protection against things beyond what they were designed for.

    Meanwhile, the DTP vaccine, which is not live-attenuated, may have a detrimental non-specific effect, although the evidence here looks weaker. No reason to start questioning vaccines, people.)

    NatAttack3000 A:

    I currently research in this space! (PHD in immunology). I know of a few lines of research on vaccinated groups that could help us know more when they are finished, and I imagine there are other studies underway that I am not aware of.

    Basically, we don't know. The difference seems to be that live vaccines have beneficial nonspecific effects (NSE) and killed/non-live could have 'detrimental' NSE (I am working to understand why this could be). It's probably worth mentioning that these effects are often seen in specific populations that have a high burden of disease - the effects may not be present in most adults.

    There are different covid vaccines - some are live, like Astrazeneca, some are dead (like SinoVac). mRNA vaccines (Moderna, pfeizer) are sensed in a similar way to a live virus in your body, so there could be similar NSE to other live vaccines, but we don't know yet.
