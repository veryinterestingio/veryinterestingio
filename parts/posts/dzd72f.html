    Rannasha A:

    Normally, microwave ovens are shielded. However with very old or defective microwave ovens, this shielding might not be that great and there might be some noticeable leakage. Since microwave ovens operate at the same frequency as some wifi networks (2.4 GHz), a poorly shielded microwave oven can interfere with wifi reception (note: many wifi networks use 5 GHz signals, which wouldn't be affected).

    But it only takes a tiny amount of leakage for a microwave oven to interfere with a wifi signal, given that an active microwave oven is about 10,000 times more powerful than a wifi router and a wifi router is often tucked away somewhere making the actual strength of the wifi signal even lower. It doesn't take much to interrupt a wifi signal.

    marcan42 A:

    Some microwaves can leak and this can disrupt WiFi signals, because they use several thousand times more power internally than a WiFi router puts out. A small leak can be enough to overwhelm the WiFi signal. Microwaves are cheap consumer devices without high-spec RF sealing at the door, so they are not guaranteed to be completely leak-proof.

    However, you shouldn't be worried about any health effects. A small amount of leakage isn't any different from the WiFi signal itself. RF radiation at the frequencies that microwaves use just does what it does inside a microwave: heat things up. It doesn't cause cancer, because it is non-ionizing radiation. As long as you don't make a habit of having your head right up against the door seal of a microwave while you cook every time, you're going to get more RF exposure out of talking on a phone than from a nearby microwave, and that amount RF exposure isn't going to do anything to your health.

    The test that the video demonstrates is reasonable. If you put a phone inside a closed microwave (without turning the microwave on, of course) and it loses the WiFi signal (on 2.4GHz - this is not guaranteed to work if you have a 5GHz network), then it is doing at least a decent job of shielding when the door is closed. It's not a terribly accurate test, but it should at least detect if there are any big issues with the shielding.

    AberrantCheese A:

    It sounds like you are confusing ionizing and non-ionizing radiation. Ionizing radiation is the scary stuff you think about when you hear of nuclear bombs and X-Rays etc. That's the stuff that damages your DNA, and kills you dead in scary ways. Non-ionizing radiation can of course burn you, but it's a thermal burn. Ultimately, you aren't much different than the pot roast you put into the microwave oven, so if you feel suddenly quite warm near the oven then by all means turn the damn thing off and have it checked, but no need to reach for the iodine pills.
