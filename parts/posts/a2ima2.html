    Ipride362 A:

    Water is in higher volumes during warmer weather. This water, or humidity, creates a super conductive “gel” so to speak that stretches from the upper atmosphere down to the ground.

    The water on the ground and the heat creates a friction and charge that attracts the instability of the cloud formation. A static connection is made and a discharge of energy stretches between the colder, dry atmosphere and warmer, wetter ground. This discharge is a flash of light and it creates a shockwave across the entire stratosphere and troposphere. The light is called lightning.

    This shockwave presents to humans as a pressure differential. Closer to the discharge, it will shake things and will be felt. Farther away, not so much. But within a few miles of the discharge, you’ll hear a rumbling or loud noise. Closer experience will hear a loud crashing noise. Farther away, more a rumbling. This is thunder.

    Anyways, in the winter the ground dries up and the moisture in the air recedes to the point where the friction between troposphere and stratosphere is too low to sustain a discharge between the two.

    While you can have thunder with a snowstorm, it usually happens as a rain storm experiences a sudden drop on tropospheric temperature. This transition allows you to have the two things that would scare the poop out of Anyone.
