• Physics Did we just "invent" that between a sound frequency and its double there are exactly 12 evenly spaced subdivisions, 7 of which are particularly important, and then tuned all instruments to this and got used to this arbitration so that things out of that order sound strange?

    NEW
    Q:

    I'm talking of course about the musical scale. Is there a parallel universe (or maybe a particular culture) where there are actually 16, or 9, or 5 subdivisions between octaves? I get the concept of an octave (a sound frequency and its double sound similar), but the rest seems somewhat arbitrary to me, even though it sounds perfect.

    atoheartmother A:

    Yes, the 12-tone system is an 'invention'. People can (and do) divide the octave into different numbers. BUT every system is a conpromise that tries to preserve as many pleasing intervals (combinations of two notes) as possible. Intervals that have simpler ratios generally sound better - some of the most universally pleasing are 1/2, 2/3, 3/4, etc - but culture has a large influence on this as well.

    So if you want to divide the octave into evenly spaced notes, you want to do so in a way that appproximates those pleasing intervals while also being actually useful. The 12-tone system seems to do a pretty good job of that. Other systems might be more harmonically accurate, but might not be as versatile or might be overly complicated.

    So while it is an invention, it is also a compromise between the culturally evolved idea of what sounds 'good', and the constraints of math.

    transmutethepooch A:

    Dividing an octave into 12th's gives you notes that are really close to the harmonies from integer ratios of frequencies, while keeping a reasonably small number of notes.

    16 notes is close, but misses the 4th and 5th by more than the set of 12 notes.

    You can add more notes to get closer, but 12 suffices.

    Step n 2^(n/12) Harmony
    1 1.059 16/15 = 1.067, Minor 2nd
    2 1.122 9/8 = 1.125, Major 2nd
    3 1.189 6/5 = 1.200, Minor 3rd
    4 1.260 5/4 = 1.250, Major 3rd
    5 1.335 4/3 = 1.333, 4th
    6 1.414
    7 1.498 3/2 = 1.500, 5th
    8 1.587 8/5 = 1.600, Minor 6th
    9 1.682 5/3 = 1.667, Major 6th
    10 1.782 16/9 = 1.778, Minor 7th
    11 1.888 15/8 = 1.875, Major 7th
    12 2.000 2/1 = 2.000, Octave

    FortuitousPost A:

    Yes. Sounds that vibrate in different multiples of a particular frequency sound pleasing together. The ancients noticed that this was the same as dampening vibrating strings at nice fractional amounts.

    The simplest fractions sound best, and in early Christian chants only these were allowed. Gradually, more intervals were added. Eventually, the list expanded to 12 intervals, roughly equally spaced.

    The composer Bach and the mathematician Euler worked together to form a mathematical theory of the "Well-Tempered Scale" that we use today. Rather than using fractions, it uses the 12th root of 2 to produce the intervals, so this is definitely a modern invention. The intervals are close to the original ones, but noticeably wrong.

    The advantage of the tempered scale is that it allows instruments to not be limited to only certain keys. An instrument tuned to the G scale in the untempered system sounds great on songs written in G, and okay on songs in the keys of C or D, but much worse on songs in keys other than these ones. The tempered instrument sounds equally good (or bad) in every key.
