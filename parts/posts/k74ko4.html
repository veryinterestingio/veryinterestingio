    NixChicMirages A:

    Believe it or not, there were no "Plagues" to catch!

    (A YouTuber by the name of CPGGrey did a video on this, called "Americapox: The Missing Plague". This comment is based on that research.)

    Most of the bad diseases are zoonotic, which means they're originally animal diseases which learn to live inside humans. These diseases are so deadly because they think they're inside of animals. A disease doesn't want to kill its host, but instead, keep the host alive and reproduce inside of it. When zoonotic diseases do what would be "normal" inside of a cow or pig, it can kill a human.

    In Afro-Eurasia, there were several different types of livestock! There were pigs, cows, chickens, horses, sheep, lots of species which we raised in large concentrations, in unsanitary conditions. This is the perfect opportunity for a zoonotic disease to 'jump' from animals to humans. That's what happened with SARS and the H1N1 Virus, for some modern examples.

    What livestock exists on the American continents? Really only llamas and alpacas. Because of a few reasons, it was difficult to raise these species in industrial quantities. Without the huge concentrations of these animals, there was almost no opportunity for zoonotic disease to spread to humans in the first place!

    TL;DR: The really terrible diseases are the ones that humans catch from livestock. The American Continents didn't raise livestock in the same way that Europeans did, and therefore didn't have any plagues to transmit!

    GaidinBDJ A:

    They did. Very notably is syphilis.

    As for the reason for the disparity: Europeans were more technologically advanced and had widespread agriculture and animal husbandry meaning that more diseases had already developed and spread over the millennia. American Indians at the time the Europeans arrived were predominantly hunters and gatherers and had not developed agriculture or animal husbandry to the same level, so fewer diseases were commonplace.

    So when Europeans showed up and diseases started spreading, they didn't understand why normal non-fatal diseases were suddenly killing people. A few hundred years later, the germ theory of disease came around and explained it.
