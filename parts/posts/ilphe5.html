• Biology What causes the feeling that somebody is looking at you?

    NEW
    Q:

    You know when you’re sitting in traffic or at a restaurant and you can actually sense that somebody is looking at you even though they’re not in your direct or even peripheral vision? What is that?

    TheProfessaur A:

    There is no extra sense for this. It's usually a result of confirmation bias.

    Long story short, people can't actually tell when someone is looking at them unless they have seen that person looking at them. Your eyes pick up a lot of information and not all of it goes into your immediate awareness. We have super sensitivity to gaze and even a glimpse of someone or something looking at you can trigger it. On the rare occasion that you do catch it, you are more likely to remember that than every time you don't.

    DoranMoonblade A:

    Answer: This phenomenon can be explained using the theories of selective attention. Our brain is bombarded with sensory input and the brain can only send a tiny fraction of it to your conscious to keep things coherent. Like in a computer there are so many tasks running in the background but if all of it were displayed/brought to your attention it would not make sense.
    The sensory input received from the eye is greater than what our conscious brain sees. Selective attention lets us focus on what we need and discard any excess information unless you consciously bring your attention to that information. Example: you are probably so focused reading this that you are discarding the rest of the information being picked up by your eyes. If you however notice some movement in your peripheral vision or consciously bring your attention to what's happening outside of your phone/computer screen without moving your eyes away from the screen, you will notice new information pour into your conscience.
    So, that feeling that someone is staring at you may be information that you eyes sent to your brain but which you didn't notice till you drew your attention over it.

    tpriddy A:

    Your peripheral vision tracks motion and it sees/calculates when that unfocused motion is tracking your motion, a head or eyes track your motion, following you; you're being watched, that rock is falling right toward you, a predator is stalking you. It is so different from your primary, central vision that it is a different sense; you "feel" like you're being watched.
