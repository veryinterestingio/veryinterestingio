    DONOTPOSTEVER A:

    It will depend on what kind of associations the animals have developed in response to this kind of stimuli.

    I recall a study done on birds where researchers painted white spots above the male’s beaks. They repeated this study each breeding season. In the first year, the white-faces males were largely unsuccessful in finding breeding partners. However, in the following year, the white-faced males were strongly preferred over the natural colour!

    This is an example of sexual selection, and it occurs naturally in the wild all the time as the result of normal mutations.

    ZAFJB A:

    I have observed, first hand on a small game reserve in South Africa.

    We have a small herd of blesbok, normally a blue brown colour. We added 5 white blesbok (not sure if they are true albino or a white mutation).

    The white animals seemed to immediately recognise the normal coloured animals as being the same. That may have been because that had mixed with normal colour animals before we got them.

    The normal colour animals initially ran away from the white animals, but within a week or so they had completely adjusted to the white ones being part of the herd.

    I guess shape, smell, movements and behaviour are sufficient for them to make the connection.

    Edit for context: These animals are wild, no human interaction. Also, no predators on our reserve.
