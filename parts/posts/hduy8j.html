    Bananas are more fibrous which keeps ice crystals small, which is how it stays creamy while frozen. Ice cream is churned during the manufacturing process which achieves this same result. Other fruits that are less fibrous will turn into slushes when frozen (like popsicles instead of ice cream)

    flurpleberries A:

    When you bite into a frozen fruit, you may not like the texture because all of the cell walls have been broken. Cell walls are something plants have that usually help them to stay rigid, and you can think of them like a water bottle. If you freeze a water bottle the water expands and may burst the bottle. Cell walls are like that too. Freezing the fruit causes many of those individual cells to freeze and break down, so the texture is weird and full of little ice crystals.

    The change in texture and the way temperature affects flavor are why you may perceive the fruits as being flavorless. Fruits higher in water content may have more difference in texture before and after being frozen. Fruits higher in sugar are easier to taste after being frozen because sugar tastes similar cold.

    Try this: blend your frozen fruit (particularly berries) with a few splashes of orange juice, just enough juice as you need to blend it up. You'll get a thick, creamy sorbet that tastes really good. That's because you're eliminating that weird, busted frozen cell texture and boosting the sour frutiness with the orange juice.

    Alternatively, blend your fruit up and freeze it as ice pops. Also good!
