• Engineering How do non-plugin hybrid cars actually use less fuel?

    NEW
    Q:

    I know that the basics of a hybrid involves using the electric motor at low speeds, and the ICE at higher speeds. And when the car is using the ICE, it charges the electric motors battery, so that it can be used again.

    But how does this save fuel? A basic understanding of the first law of thermodynamics is that energy cannot be created nor destroyed, only transferred. Wouldn’t this mean that the ICE would use more fuel while it is charging the electric engine, negating whatever gain it would have gotten? I feel like it would actually be counterintuitive, because the transfer isn’t likely to operate at perfect efficiency.

    Anyone have more insight into this?

    mnvoronin A:

    Many commenters here talk about regenerative braking, and that ICE is better at a certain RPM. While these points do have their merits, they are not totally correct.

    First of all, regenerative braking, while helping with efficiency somewhat, is not the largest contributor. Taking my example from another comment, the city drive during heavy traffic conditions (morning commute to work in Auckland - probably the best conditions for recovering the braking energy with slow-moving traffic and frequent stops), I have used 1.74 kWh of power over 14 km, and 0.182 kWh were recovered by regenerative braking. That's just over 10%, and would not explain a 50%+ efficiency difference with traditional ICE cars.

    Second, while ICE is better at certain RPM, the engine load (requested torque as a percentage of the maximum possible at given RPM) is even more important. For example, take this graph. It shows the engine's brake-specific fuel consumption (i.e. the amount of fuel required to produce 1 kWh of mechanical energy) depending on RPM and engine load for the Gen 3 Prius. You will see that the best BSFC of 215 g/kWh is achieved at about 2000 RPM and 80% load. Thus, if your current driving conditions require 2000 RPM and, say, 25% load, the engine is extremely inefficient and burns more than double the fuel to produce the same amount of energy. You can also see that BSFC at under 50% load does not change much with RPM, so reducing the speed of the engine won't help much, though shifting to higher gear will increase the load and alleviate the problem somewhat. Hybrid cars get around this by alternating between running at full electric (discharging battery) and running the engine at high (optimal) load and storing the excess in the battery.

    It's also worth noting that Toyota hybrids, in particular, can always use the best possible RPM/load for any required power (red line on the graph) because of the design of the power split device. True series hybrids (the ones where the engine is only used to spin the generator and wheels are 100% electric) can also achieve that, but the double conversion (mechanical-electric-mechanical) can be quite lossy at the lower power outputs, so you will mostly see this design in diesel-electric train locomotives where it's insanely effective.
