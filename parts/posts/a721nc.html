• If California has yearly fires, the shouldn't the state just cut a crap load of fire breaks to protect communities?

    NEW
    Q:

    A fire break is when you cut EVERYTHING within X feet/yards of a property so that if a fire happens, your home is safe.

    Looking through some topographical maps I do not see this happen often.

    JudgeHoltman A:

    Because if it's an outlier, that's how you turn forest fire problems into flood problems.

    Every tree you cut down reduces the amount of water that the forest drinks. Watershed designers bank on the forest drinking up rain before spilling into creeks and rivers.

    Fire breaks are wide enough that they'll also create some natural drainage paths for the forest too. So now the forest drains even faster when it rains, which means the trees remaining get have less time to drink the water they do have access to, making the forest even drier and more susceptible to fire.

    Basically, it's not that simple, and might make the problem worse.

    Edit: Drought can make flooding worse. Grass and shrubs hold down topsoil and drink water. Without water, they die and thin out, and stop holding the topsoil down. When a rainstorm does come around, the water sheets off the soil, turning it into a mudslide which is also bad.

    FiveDozenWhales A:

    This is currently a big part of the California's Department of Forestry & Fire Management's plan; to improve safety by protecting inhabited areas, mostly through fire breaks and observation. This was adopted as part of a cohesive state-wide plan for 2010, and reaffirmed for 2018. Implementation is left to individual jurisdictions and varies from place to place.

    "Jurisdiction" varies greatly - in many places, the critical location for a fire break is on private land. While it's possible that California or the federal government could flex Eminent Domain and force landowners to surrender their land and/or clear trees, in practice this is avoided as much as possible.

    Floating embers are the leading cause of structural damage from wildfires in California. Fire breaks don't work great against embers. In these cases, building resistant structures is a much better strategy; again, this is left to private landowners in most cases.
