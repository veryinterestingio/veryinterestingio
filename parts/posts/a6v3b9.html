    Q:

    ELI5: why is carbon monoxide (CO) poisonous while carbon dioxide (CO2) is mostly harmless, despite only differing by one oxygen molecule?

    varialectio A:

    The shape of carbon monoxide molecule and the electron bonds holding it together mimic the oxygen molecule well enough for it to take its place in the large molecule that carries oxygen in the blood. If carbon monoxide blocks the site, the oxygen doesn't get to the cells.

    Carbon dioxide is completely the wrong shape and bonding to do the same.

    Salindurthas A:

    despite only differing by one oxygen molecule?

    Rather than tackling the specific question, I'll tackle this general idea you have, which seems to hint at a misconception.

    I think it is a mistake to even think of it as differing by "only" one oxygen atom.

    Yes, one oxygen atom is small, but it is big compared to the rest of the molecule.

    Think of it this way - that oxygen atom is like a third of the carbon dioxide molecule!
    Imagine if I took a third of your DNA away, or added half-again the heat of a fire, or removed quarter of a car's wheels.
    Those would result in huge changes in how those things behave!

    With that in mind, taking away a third of a molecule seems like it very well could be a big deal.

    jaksblaks A:

    because CO will displace the oxygen in your blood stream and that is bad for you. It has a greater affinity for hemoglobin than oxygen does. CO2 does not do this.

    There are two equilibrium reactions of binding oxygen and becoming oxygenated hemoglobin:

    Hb(aq)+4O2(g)⟶Hb(O2)4(aq)

    Hb(aq)+4O2(g)⟵Hb(O2)4(aq)

    And carbon monoxide binding equation at equilibrium:

    Hb(aq)+4CO(g)↽−−⇀Hb(CO)4(aq)
