    Q:

    We share ≈96% of our genes with gorillas, ≈50% with bananas, so if we added all the specific shared genes what percentage is purely human?

    shannamae90 A:

    It’s hard to know for sure. We don’t have a solid count for how many genes humans have to start with because there is some discussion about what counts as a gene, and not all sequences that could be considered a gene have been fully studied. Out of the 20,000-25,000 genes in our genome, 23 have been identified only in humans and not in any other currently living animals.

    beingtall A:

    There are maybe 6 genes that are only found in humans. That's about 0.002% of the human genome. But the answer is more complicated than that. What makes us human is not just the genes but how they are regulated. We share 98-99% genes with chimps but when looked closely the genomes are all filled with structural rearrangements. This results in a very different regulatory system in both. So the actual similarity may be 60-70%.

    bobbot32 A:

    JTD783 A:

    To add to this, consider that the amount of copies of a gene present in the genome, or the arrangement of genes, or the expression of a gene, could be unique in humans. Genetic diversity is much more complex than just which genes exist. As the other responses have said, it’s really hard to measure. But I don’t think very much is unique to humans, to make an understatement.
