• Biology why do our brains associate neat edges and squares with tidiness and order?

    NEW
    Q:

    To be more specific, why do we percieve a blanket folded in a square as neat and tidy rather than a blanket tossed in a pile in the same place? Why does a shirt folded in a neat square look more appealing than one folded "sloppily"? Or a bed made with tight lines and corners vs a bed that isn't made?

    Is this strictly a cultural phenomenon or is there something scientific that makes our minds decide what is tidy, satisfying, and proper and what isn't?

    jimbowolf A:

    The world, in general, just isn't made of straight lines. Humans are extremely visual creatures with excellent eyesight, and something being straight or precisely shaped is simply very noticeable to us as a species. It's very easy to pick out a straight line in a world that's otherwise formed with extremely random patterns.

    We like straight lines because they actually give us a survival advantage. A straight spear is easier to use and less likely to break. A clean, flat surface is easier to work with than a bumpy, dirty floor. Boxes can more uniformly fit objects inside than an awkwardly shaped container.

    We've adapted as a species to like straight lines, because straight lines literally help us survive.
