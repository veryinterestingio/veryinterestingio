    OBLIVIONDEMISE A:

    The best way to explain this is to first start with why we hear sound in the first place.

    Sound is a translation of vibrations into nervous impulses that get sent to the brain. Strong vibrations transmit faster in solids and slower in gases (this is important for later). However, the intensity of those vibrations impact how well they are heard.

    In order to translate those vibrations into sounds, we need way to carry on the message (almost like a game of telephone). That medium is actually a series of 3 bones in the ear connected to each other. When sound vibrations come from the air, they shake the bones (which are solid) and thus carry on the shaking before it dies out.

    At some point, the vibration of the bones physically hit something called a hair cell. Note: hair cells are named NOT because they make hair on our skin, but because they have an extrusion(s) that look like tiny hairs. These extrusions are mechanically operated, meaning that once the hairs are physically hit, they open up a bunch communication responses inside the hair cell. That leads to the neighbor of these hair cells, the nerve cells, picking up the signal started by the vibration, and sending it to the brain.

    There are millions of signals like this that the brain must filter out to avoid overloading. Therefore the brain will distinguish only the most new, strange, unique, and changing sounds. If a sound is soft and repeats itself enough times, the brain will ignore it (like our own heartbeat). Now if we are in a quiet environment, the amount of ambient (surrounding) noises is not loud enough to overshadow the sound of our heartbeat. Thus we can then hear it.

    Finally to be more specific, the heartbeat is audible because the pressure it causes from blood flow is a vibration itself. If the pressure is high enough (or if the ambient noise is low enough), then the vibrations will be detected by the ear bones and will be translated into sound.

    TLDR: Sounds are translations of vibrations into electric signals picked up by the brain. Loud, variable vibrations are easily detected. Heartbeats are usually soft and constant, thus not easily detectable. Given a quiet environment or a change in heartbeat, we can hear the heart as it makes vibrations that are translated to sound.
