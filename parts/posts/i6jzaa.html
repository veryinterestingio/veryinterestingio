• Earth Sciences Earthquakes when there are no nearby fault lines?

    NEW
    Q:

    We just recently had 5.1 magnitude earthquake in Sparta NC, the nearest fault line is in the middle of the Atlantic Ocean. Any explanation to why this could happen?

    CrustalTrudger A:

    This is the earthquake for those unaware and it definitely occurred on a fault. This earthquake occurred in the Appalachian mountains, so there are without a doubt multitudes of faults that would have formed during one of several mountain building events which are recorded in the geology there, though none of them are active in the traditional sense. Earthquakes in the Appalachians don't occur that frequently, but they do happen, e.g. the 2011 Mineral VA quake. These can best be thought of as intraplate earthquakes, i.e. an earthquake that occurs in the interior of a tectonic plate (as opposed to most earthquakes which occur along the edges of tectonic plates). While a simple view of a tectonic plate is a completely rigid block of lithosphere which does not deform internally, this isn't quite true and strain that is being transmitted throughout the plate can cause pre-existing faults to rupture and generate an earthquake.

    There is a difference between plate boundaries and faults. The North American plate does extend out to the Mid-Atlantic Ridge like you say, and most plate motion is focused at the boundaries. But geology is messy, and plates are chock full of smaller faults.
