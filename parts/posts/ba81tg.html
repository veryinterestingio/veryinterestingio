    After processing, pasteurization, and storage, orange juice loses of its flavor and aroma so they add "flavor packs" (made of orange product such as oil and pulp) to reflavor it so it always has a consistent taste. There are different breeds of oranges which contribute to different tastes along with picking and eating them whether they are ripe enough or not.

    youkai1 A:

    It's statistics. The same reason all garbage trucks in your town smell the same.

    The Law of Large Numbers means that the more times you do something, the closer you'll get to it's statistical probability. If you flip a coin you have a 50/50 chance of getting heads, but it's really not all that uncommon to get 3 or even 5 heads in a row. As you flip the coin more and more times the distribution of heads and tails over the course of all of your flips gets closer and closer to 50/50. Fruit juicers juice so many fruits that the large batch juice all tastes like the statistical average of each of the individual fruits. Just like the garbage truck.

    lellololes A:

    The orange juice you buy from the store doesn't even taste much like actual orange juice.

    When it says it is not from concentrate, it does not mean that the OJ is fresh squeeze in to a bottle. It is stored in oxygen free vats. Components of the OJ are separated and then later re-added to the liquid to make what they call "OJ"

    The combination of pasteurization, the amount of time the juice is stored for, and the way the flavors are reintroduced results in the flavor being what it is.

    Many of the flavors in fresh squeezed OJ go away after a short time. Store bought OJ is still missing a lot of them.

    Different brands tend to have different characteristic flavors, but none are close to fresh squeezed. They're all pretty similar.
