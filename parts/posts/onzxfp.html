    EatTheBeez A:

    There are different kinds of sleep, called 'stages'. Slow wave sleep is the stage where your body takes care of itself. If you're sick, you need more slow wave sleep. If you work out a lot during the day, or get very hot, you need more of it too. And it's the most important kind of sleep, from a survival perspective. The other stages (like REM, where you do your best dreaming) help you learn things and keep sane, but they don't heal your body the same way.

    Now, our bodies evolved a certain way, and one trick that happened was that we evolved to get most of our slow wave sleep in the first half of the night. In fact, an adult past their 30s probably doesn't get much more than half an hour of slow wave sleep in the second half of their night! But they might get 3 hours of it in the first 4 or 5 hours of sleep.

    So what does this mean for waking up tired? Well if you sleep 8 hours, you'll get that slow wave sleep early in the night, then spend the rest of the night in 'stage 2' and 'REM' sleep, while your brain does other important work. But REM sleep actually uses up energy. It's a bit tiring, while your brain is cleaning things up in there. And if you keep sleeping, and snoozing, and dreaming, and sleeping, you get more and more REM, but not more slow wave sleep.

    So you actually tire yourself out a bit by sleeping!

    Interestingly, people who can sleep for more than 10 hours regularly tend to hit another slow wave sleep period after the 10 hour mark. But for the rest of us who aren't long sleepers, we just wake up tired after too much REM.
