• Biology Do insects have a micro-biome, and if so could eating live insects effect the human micro-biome?

    NEW
    Q:

    Do insects have a micro-biome, and if so could eating live insects effect the human micro-biome?

    That_Biology_Guy A:

    Insects most definitely do have microbiomes, and this has been a fairly rapidly growing area of research in recent years. For example, termites completely rely on gut symbiotes (including both eukaryotes and bacteria) to be able to digest wood, and there's been some interesting studies on how the evolution of these microbes tracks the evolution of their hosts (or rather, how it doesn't; see Bourguignon et al. 2018). Plenty of other insects have also been the subjects of microbiome studies, including bees, which have relevant microbiomes both within their bodies and within their food provisions. The effect of various pesticides on bee microbiomes has been the subject of a fair number of studies (e.g. Motta et al. 2018).

    As for the second part of your question, there isn't that much available research on this yet, but I did find one interesting study by Stull et al. 2018. These researchers did find significant differences in microbiome composition in their treatment group of humans who ate cricket flour, mostly just due to increases or decreases in the proportions of certain types of bacteria compared to their control group. However, these are all bacteria that naturally occur within humans, so the implication isn't that the subjects got new bacteria from crickets, but rather that certain gut bacteria just do better or worse under that particular diet. Though obviously in this case the crickets were heavily processed and quite far from being alive, so I'm not sure if that would change anything.

    number65261 A:

    Not exactly related, but even plants have a microbiome of sorts. There are special fungi fungal relationships called mycorrhizae around the roots of certain trees/higher order plants that facilitate nutrient uptake. At a certain point you find out biology is just a nesting doll that builds on whatever works, with no regard for whether the advantage comes through hosting another organism. Thus, we find an insane amount of these examples in nature, gut microbiomes being one of the many. It is suspected even mitochondria started as a prokaryotic/bacterial symbiont that has now genetically degraded to the point that it is more like an organ within each cell. Another case of one creature "feeding"/supplying "nutrients" to another, just on a more microscopic scale.

    Oznog99 A:

    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolbachia

    Part of insects and nematodes. It is often symbiotic.

    A lot of the danger of nematode (worms) infection in mammals may be an immune response to wolbachia in the worms.

    One strategy of killing nematodes like heartworms inside mammals is to use an antibiotic that targets wolbachia. Some nematodes cannot survive without it in their microbiome and will decline and eventually die off.

    This avoids using more toxic chemicals that kill the nematodes directly, and may prevent a crisis where the nematodes die off suddenly and decay, exposing the immune system with a flood of wolbachia.
