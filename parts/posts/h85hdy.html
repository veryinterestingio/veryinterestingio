• When and How Did "Stop, Drop, and Roll" Become a Huge Priority in Schools?

    NEW
    Q:

    Basically I was led to believe that catching on fire was going to be a pretty constant threat during adult life. Teaching us what to do when we found ourselves on fire was a higher priority than a large number of actually critical life skills accessible to young children. I was 25 before someone bothered to teach me how to recognize the signs of abusive behavior, but I was ready to survive a clothing fire before I graduated from Kindergarten. Poor mental health and suicide? Crippling debt from financial illiteracy? Eh, those problems will work themselves out so long as we keep these highly combustible youngsters safe from the real danger!

    I have not, to date, been on fire. I have never met someone who has been on fire. I've never read a newspaper article about a person who found themselves on fire. I have never seen a person catch fire, for any reason, in real life. I can only assume there was a reason for this focus on stop, drop, and roll. There must have been a time when kids were just bursting into flames left and right, until parents pushed schools to implement an aggressive anti-fire policy. Is this real? Did our grandparents' generation sit around the kitchen table, shake there heads, and say "Lost another one today. Burnt to cinders, he did."?

    Basically, what is the history of this initiative, and was it ever essential enough to warrant its current prominence?

    EDIT: Thanks everyone for contributing to this wonderful discussion. I've learned a lot here today. First, I learned that many of you have caught fire. Multiple times. Like, an alarming number of you. Second, I learned that I hate fire safety, and want it removed from schools, to be replaced by Quickbooks tutorial sessions or something. I had no idea I believed something so stupid, so I really must thank you all for setting me straight on that issue. Finally, I learned that the high priority of stop, drop, and roll training happens because, yes, we did once live in a world made of crepe paper and lighter fluid. Toasters were ungrounded, smokers happily flicked ashes onto davenports, and pajamas were made of a type of sawdust called rayon. Bless you, Dick Van Dyke, and bless you, friendly schoolroom fire fighter. But most of all, bless you whoever decided that the 50s was a fucking terrible idea and we should just put a third prong on our electrical plugs like not-insane people.
