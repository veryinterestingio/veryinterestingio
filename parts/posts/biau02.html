• Biology How can rain forests have infertile soil yet such huge growth?

    NEW
    Q:

    I've seen many documentaries that talk about rain forests having infertile soil. When they cut them down to grow soya beans, etc the problem becomes that the soil doesn't have enough nutrients.

    So how can rain forests have such infertile soil yet have the biggest plants and trees in the greatest of volume of plants and trees in the first place?

    I'm quite perplexed! Looking forward to your answers!

    Jak1977 A:

    Because the trees pull up the nutrients as rapidly as they're deposited, there is no buildup of nutrients in the soil. The nutrient layer is very much in the surface, there's very little underneath. The trees are living on recycled nutrients of all the trees around them. The same matter goes around and around the cycle. Trees take in the nutrients, grow, make leaves, drop leaves, die, compost down in the top layer of soil, and the sequence repeats. If you have a crop which involves taking nutrients AWAY in trucks to be sold in towns, then there is a NET LOSS of nutrients. They have left the cycle.

    Another reason the Amazon specifically has so much growth, is because The Saharan desert provides massive amounts of nutrition across the ocean, and deposits it into the Amazon.

    I can't explain it better than NASA, but suffice it to say dust from the Saharan, which used to be a lakebed, so it contains quite a lot of various minerals and dead microorganisms and such, is carried by wind and dropped all over south and Central America, allowing things to grow.

    I've heard that most of the dead stuff (leaves, fruit, animals) doesn't stick around long enough to be composted into soil before it's picked clean by scavengers. Their nutrients don't really make it back into dirt, so lots of rainforest trees have those surface roots that look like big ribbons to get any nutrients that just hang out near the surface. There's not much below the surface and therefore really tough to farm crops.

    Pedogenic A:

    Other answers are generally good here, but to clarify about the mineral soil (below the top soil): When it rains as much as it does in a rainforest, the minerals in soil fall apart into more simple minerals (we call this weathering). Rainforests are the extreme end of this.

    In a nice grassland, like the Great Plains of the USA, minerals are like a sticky lunchbox. The can store nutrients both inside themselves and have all kinds of good stuff stuck to their edges.

    But in a rainforest, most minerals in the soil are like your dad’s wallet. They are smaller, and the stuff stuck inside is not what you’d want to have for a snack. And they can’t hold any nutrients around the edges, either. Weirdly enough, soils in the rainforest are super deep compared to grasslands, but it doesn’t matter because they’re filled with the wrong stuff for plants to be happy. That’s why the topsoil is so important—see other comments about organic matter and nutrients cycling between plants and topsoil.
