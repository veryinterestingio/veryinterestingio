    Q:

    Why is the effect of tinnitus a high pitched ring instead of a low hum or any other sound effect?

    wbeaty A:

    Tinnitus is not just a high pitch ringing. Its explanation was a mystery until recent decades, since it also arises as white-noise rushing, hums, etc. "Tinnitus is commonly described as a ringing in the ears, but it also can sound like roaring, clicking, hissing, or buzzing. It may be soft or loud, high pitched or low pitched. You might hear it in either one or both ears." Keywords to try: low-frequency tinnitus.

    The unknown sources of tinnitus are starting to fall before modern research. In the last two decades it was discovered that ears are not like passive microphones, but instead involve sound emitters and active feedback. Human hearing employs a mechanical amplifier in the cochlea, and the brain regions involving tinnitus are starting to be explored.

    As with misbehaving audio systems, ears with too much gain at the wrong frequencies can break into oscillation. Cornell's infamous Thomas Gold mentioned the original mystery which attracted his own attention in the 1950s: some tinnitus is a genuine sound, today known as OAE Otoacoustic Emission and can be recorded using a microphone inserted into the ear canal. The WP article notes that most humans don't detect their own spontaneous OAE sounds, but 1-9% do perceive it as tinnitus.
