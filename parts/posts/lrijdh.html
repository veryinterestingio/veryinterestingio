    cactusmittens A:

    Vaccines as a rule do not provide longer immunity than a natural infection, though they can.

    https://www.chop.edu/centers-programs/vaccine-education-center/vaccine-safety/immune-system-and-health

    When vaccines are doing a particularly good job, it can come down to:

    • dose and timing. Some viruses are very good at hiding from the immune system or only produce a pretty low infection titre. A vaccine can give a high dose of concentrated antigen in a place that your immune system can easily see and respond to. Having a high concentrated dose all at once is also seen as more urgent by the immune system and more inflammatory response cells may be activated (an "oh, shit! Look at all that bad stuff!" kind of reaction)
    • pathogen interaction with the immune system. This is less common for viruses which are relatively simple creatures, but many bacteria or pathogenic eukaryotes are able to modulate the immune system and make cells do anti-inflammatory things. These are absent from vaccines which often even contain adjuvants designed to make sure the immune response is activated. Similarly, the infection itself can cause real harm which stresses a body's ability to do anything productively and efficiently. Everything in life takes energy, and a natural infection may have to make trade offs a vaccine does not.

    When natural infections are doing better, it's usually due to:

    • a wider range of epitopes are presented, allowing multiple ways to recognize a pathogen
    • viral dose is overall higher as infection takes hold
    • all immunity processes are fully engaged (per the way our immune systems evolved)
    • immunity reflects the actual viral/pathogen strain that was contracted, which may be a strain that evolved since the vaccine was designed. Wide range of epitopes is also a player here since immunity to multiple antigens (mutating and unmutated) is expected
