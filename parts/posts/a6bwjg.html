    Vaccines are not 100% effective, and they do wear off sometimes.

    The best argument, though, in my opinion, is that herd immunity, the prevention of outbreaks by limiting the number of potential hosts, protects babies too young to be fully vaccinated. Babies are also far more likely to die from many of these diseases, be it whooping cough or just the plain old flu.

    Gromky A:

    Absolutely yes. Got hit with whooping cough (multiple people in the family did) a while back. It could have killed my daughter, who was too young for the vaccine at the time.

    I was vaccinated against it, but it generally wears off over time. Same reason you often have to take your pet in every year for a rabies vaccine. Tetanus lasts something like...7 years I think.

    In the case of whooping cough, the very young can't get the vaccine and the disease has a good chance of killing them. Once you can get the vaccine it protects you for quite a while. As an adult it is not likely to kill you, but you can spread it to infants who can't be vaccinated or children who have issues with vaccinations. And kill them. Kill them.

    Edit: And sometimes vaccines just don't take. The immune system is weird. As an example, I am one of the rare number of people who got chicken pox twice due to the fact that my immune system fought it off the first time but didn't develop the antibodies long-term.
