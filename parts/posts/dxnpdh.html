    WRSaunders A:

    There is a lot, but not so much that we can't get through it, the so called Kessler Limit. Many things have been hit by tiny bits, they have to be tough enough to take it. The larger pieces are tracked, and paths chosen to fly around them. Eventually we're going to have to start picking up the trash, but NASA projects to do that still seem too expensive.

    We watch for the big ones and avoid them. The little ones tend to come from a similar source as the big ones so there's some clustering but even if you spread them all out evenly there really isn't that much debris compared to the amount of space out there.

    Space is big! Mindbogglingly big, yadda yadda yadda.

    There are about 128 million pieces of space debris with over 99% being smaller than 1 cm. For easier math lets consider 1 Billion pieces that are each 1 meter wide floating in an orbit of 400 km(about the height of the ISS). That makes 1 billion square meters of that shell unusable, but that that orbital shell is 183 trillion square meters so that generous assumption of debris takes up 0.00055% of the orbital real estate even in this conservative estimate.

    In reality, satellites and the debris are spread around in a thick shell over Earth so they're quite spaced out. Similar to flying through the asteroid belt, if you pick any random straight path the odds of you hitting something are very low.

    jherico A:

    There are about 128,000,000 500,000 pieces of debris, many of them no larger than a grain of sand. But space is big.

    Now imagine you actually had that much sand. It would fit into a cube about 2 14 cm across. You could easily hold it in your hand a bucket. Now imagine that you spread that sand evenly across the entire United States. If you had to drive from New York to Los Angeles, how worried would you be about running into one of those grains of sand.

    Keep in mind that space is actually much larger than the United States... Not just because it covers the whole globe but also because it is a volume, not a surface. You might cross paths with a grain of sand but be a mile below it or a mile above it, easily.

    On top of this, the large objects up there are constantly tracked, and an impact with a sand grain sized object is probably going to be survivable (it's happened before)
