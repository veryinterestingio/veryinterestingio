    Wrathchilde A:

    The ice first freezes at the surface, and can result in a small "hole" of liquid at the surface, through which interior water is forced as more ice forms. The water so expelled forms the spike as it freezes.

    MagnusText A:

    Veritasium recently did a video on this, but basically, it occurs through a couple steps.

    1) top of the water freezes, except for a small hole that forms as the water doesn't freeze evenly. (Edited cause of hole as I misremembered)

    2) water expands as it freezes, so the inside of the water Rises a little as it is pushed out of the hole.

    3) the water on the outside of the water pushed out freezes first, allowing the inside water to rise further

    Steps 3 and 4 can repeat for a long while, until finally

    4) the inside of the water freezes entirely or the tip of the spike closes, ending the column's rise.

    jcla A:

    It is an "Ice spike". Although scientists believed they knew the mechanism, it is only recently that serious study with lab conditions and camera monitoring has happened.

    Here is a paper from CalTech about them from 2003: http://www.its.caltech.edu/~atomic/snowcrystals/icespikes/icespikes.pdf

    You'll see ice spikes form when there aren't any dissolved solids to inhibit their growth. Try putting distilled water in your ice cube tray next time and see what you get.

    Wiki entry for Ice Spikes: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_spike

    And a good explainer from the University of Toronto: https://www.physics.utoronto.ca/~smorris/edl/icespikes/icespikes.html
